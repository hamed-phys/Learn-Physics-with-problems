Problem 5: A Point Moving on a Plane

Express the plane constraint in vector notation, then differentiate to prove the velocity is orthogonal to the plane’s normal vector.

Plane 4x − 3y − 2z = 6 Position r(t)=⟨x(t),y(t),z(t)⟩ Goal r′(t) ⟂ n

Quick Facts

Normal vector
\( \vec n=\langle 4,-3,-2\rangle \)
Constraint form
\( \vec n\cdot \vec r(t)=6 \)
After differentiating
\( \vec n\cdot \vec r'(t)=0 \Rightarrow \vec r'(t)\perp \vec n \)

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Conceptual Setup

Restatement (in plain words)

A point \(P(t)\) moves in 3D with position vector \(\vec r(t)\). If it always stays on the plane \(4x-3y-2z=6\), rewrite that condition using vector notation with the plane’s normal vector. Then differentiate the condition to show the velocity \(\vec r'(t)\) is perpendicular to the normal.

Given • Unknowns • Must Prove

  • Given: plane \(4x-3y-2z=6\), position \(\vec r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle\).
  • Unknowns: vector constraint equation; relationship between \(\vec r'(t)\) and \(\vec n\).
  • Must show: (a) vector form; (b) \(\vec r'(t)\perp \vec n\).
Why these principles apply

For a plane \(Ax+By+Cz=D\), the vector \(\langle A,B,C\rangle\) is normal to the plane (it’s the gradient of the linear function). If the point stays on the plane, the scalar constraint remains constant for all \(t\). Differentiating a constant yields zero, giving a dot-product condition that encodes perpendicularity.

Approaches (compare & choose) ›
  • Vector-dot approach: write \(\vec n\cdot \vec r(t)=6\), then differentiate \(\Rightarrow \vec n\cdot \vec r'(t)=0\). Cleanest and matches the prompt.
  • Component approach: keep \(4x(t)-3y(t)-2z(t)=6\), differentiate to get \(4x'(t)-3y'(t)-2z'(t)=0\), then recognize it as a dot product.
  • Geometric approach: velocity is tangent to the plane; normal is perpendicular to the plane; therefore velocity ⟂ normal. Great intuition but still needs an equation-based proof.

Best choice: the vector-dot approach—most direct and uses exactly the requested objects (\(\vec r\) and \(\vec n\)).

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Plan + Pitfalls
Extract the plane’s normal vector

Goal: identify \(\vec n\).
Tool: \(Ax+By+Cz=D \Rightarrow \vec n=\langle A,B,C\rangle\).

Write \(\vec r(t)\) in components

Goal: connect vector form to \(x(t),y(t),z(t)\).
Tool: \(\vec r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle\).

Convert the plane equation into a dot-product constraint

Goal: express “on the plane” as \(\vec n\cdot \vec r(t)=6\).
Tool: dot product expansion.

Differentiate the constraint with respect to time

Goal: relate \(\vec r'(t)\) to \(\vec n\).
Tool: \(\frac{d}{dt}(\vec n\cdot \vec r)=\vec n\cdot \vec r'\) (since \(\vec n\) constant).

Use orthogonality from dot product

Goal: conclude perpendicularity.
Tool: \(\vec a\cdot \vec b=0 \iff \vec a\perp \vec b\).

Common mistakes

  • Writing \(\vec n\cdot \vec r(t)=0\) instead of \(6\).
  • Forgetting \(\frac{d}{dt}(6)=0\).
  • Treating \(\vec n\) as time-dependent (it’s fixed for a fixed plane).

Quick tips

  • The coefficients of \(x,y,z\) are the normal’s components.
  • “Stays on the plane” means the constraint holds for all \(t\).
  • Dot product equals zero is your “perpendicular” detector.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Derivation + Checks
Physical / geometric intuition

If \(P(t)\) is constrained to move on a plane, its instantaneous motion (velocity) must lie within the plane (a tangent direction). A plane’s normal vector is perpendicular to every in-plane direction. So we expect \(\vec r'(t)\) to have no component along \(\vec n\), meaning \(\vec n\cdot \vec r'(t)=0\).

(a) Express the plane condition in vector notation

The plane is \(4x-3y-2z=6\). For a plane \(Ax+By+Cz=D\), a normal vector is \(\vec n=\langle A,B,C\rangle\). Therefore,

\[ \vec n=\langle 4,-3,-2\rangle. \]

The position vector is \(\vec r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle\). Compute the dot product:

\[ \vec n\cdot \vec r(t) =\langle 4,-3,-2\rangle\cdot \langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle =4x(t)-3y(t)-2z(t). \]

The statement “\(P(t)\) lies on the plane” means \(4x(t)-3y(t)-2z(t)=6\) for all \(t\). Hence the vector form is:

Final (a) Constraint n·r(t)=6
\[ \boxed{\;\vec n\cdot \vec r(t)=6,\quad \vec n=\langle 4,-3,-2\rangle\;} \]

(b) Differentiate to show \( \dfrac{d\vec r}{dt} \perp \vec n \)

Start from the constraint obtained in (a), which holds for every time \(t\):

\[ \vec n\cdot \vec r(t)=6. \]

Differentiate both sides with respect to time \(t\). The right-hand side is constant:

\[ \frac{d}{dt}(6)=0. \]

On the left-hand side, \(\vec n\) is constant (the plane is fixed), so differentiation passes through the dot product:

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\big(\vec n\cdot \vec r(t)\big) = \vec n\cdot \frac{d\vec r}{dt}. \]

Therefore we obtain:

\[ \vec n\cdot \frac{d\vec r}{dt}=0. \]

A dot product of zero means the vectors are perpendicular. Hence:

Final (b) Orthogonality n·r′(t)=0
\[ \boxed{\;\vec n\cdot \vec r'(t)=0\;\;\Longrightarrow\;\;\vec r'(t)\perp \vec n.\;} \]
Sanity checks (units • limiting cases • interpretation) ›

Units

The plane equation is geometric. If \(x,y,z\) are lengths, the coefficients are dimensionless and \(6\) has length units only if the coefficients carry reciprocal-length units; in standard math geometry, everything is taken dimensionless or consistently scaled. After differentiating, \(\vec r'(t)\) has velocity units (length/time) and \(\vec n\) is constant, so \(\vec n\cdot \vec r'(t)\) has velocity units and can validly equal \(0\).

Limiting / special cases

  • If the point is stationary, \(\vec r'(t)=\vec 0\), then \(\vec n\cdot \vec r'(t)=0\) holds trivially.
  • Any velocity direction confined to the plane must be orthogonal to \(\vec n\), consistent with \(\vec n\cdot \vec r'(t)=0\).

Physical / geometric interpretation

\(\vec n\cdot \vec r(t)=6\) says the component of \(\vec r\) along the normal direction is fixed in time. Differentiating gives \(\vec n\cdot \vec r'(t)=0\), meaning the velocity has no normal component—the point cannot “leave” the plane. So the motion is purely tangent to the plane at every instant.
