Problem 3 ‚Äî Ladybug walking on a moving VW

The VW surface is the unit semicircle \(x^2+y^2=1\) with \(y\ge 0\). The bug starts at \((1,0)\) at \(t=0\), walks counterclockwise at speed 1 relative to the VW, while the VW moves to the right at speed 10.

PART 1 ‚Äî Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

  • In words: Track the bug in the ground frame while it walks along the VW‚Äôs semicircle in the VW frame.
  • Given: semicircle radius \(1\), bug relative speed \(1\), VW speed \(10\) (right).
  • Unknowns: ground-frame trajectory \((x(t),y(t))\), speed \(v(t)\), where \(v\) is max/min.
  • Find: (a) parametric formula + position at rear bumper; (b) speed function and its extrema locations.

Relevant principles

  • Galilean velocity addition: ground velocity = (bug relative) + (VW translation).
  • Arc length on unit circle: \(s=\theta\) (radians). Unit speed along the arc gives \(\dot\theta=1\).
  • Speed magnitude: \(v=\|\mathbf r'(t)\|\). Extrema easiest via \(v^2\).
‚úìPART 2 ‚Äî Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)
  • Parametrize bug on the VW by angle: \((\cos\theta,\sin\theta)\).
  • Use unit-radius arc length \(s=\theta\) and unit speed: \(\dot\theta=1\Rightarrow\theta(t)=t\).
  • Add the VW translation: \(x=10t+\cos t,\; y=\sin t\).
  • Rear bumper corresponds to \(\theta=\pi\Rightarrow t=\pi\).
  • Differentiate: \(\mathbf v(t)=\langle 10-\sin t,\;\cos t\rangle\).
  • Analyze \(v^2(t)=101-20\sin t\) on \(t\in[0,\pi]\) for max/min.
PART 3 ‚Äî Full Solution (step-by-step) Kinematics + parameterization
üí°Physical intuition

In the VW‚Äôs frame, the bug simply traces the top half of a unit circle at constant speed 1. In the ground frame, that circular motion is ‚Äúcarried‚Äù to the right by the VW at speed 10, producing a translated parametric curve.

(a) Parametric trajectory and position at the rear bumper

1) VW-frame geometry. On the unit semicircle, use the standard angle parameter \(\theta\):

\[ x_{\text{rel}}=\cos\theta,\qquad y_{\text{rel}}=\sin\theta,\qquad 0\le\theta\le\pi. \]

2) Convert ‚Äúunit speed along the arc‚Äù into an angle law. On a circle of radius 1, arc length satisfies \(s=\theta\). Since the bug‚Äôs relative speed is \(ds/dt=1\), we get:

\[ \frac{ds}{dt}=1,\quad s=\theta\;\Rightarrow\;\frac{d\theta}{dt}=1. \] \[ \theta(0)=0\Rightarrow \boxed{\theta(t)=t}. \]

So the bug‚Äôs VW-frame position is:

\[ (x_{\text{rel}}(t),y_{\text{rel}}(t))=(\cos t,\;\sin t). \]

3) Add the VW translation (ground frame). The VW moves right at speed 10, so its displacement is \((10t,0)\):

\[ \boxed{x(t)=10t+\cos t,\qquad y(t)=\sin t,\qquad 0\le t\le\pi.} \]

4) Rear bumper event. Rear bumper corresponds to \(\theta=\pi\), so \(t=\pi\). Then:

\[ x(\pi)=10\pi+\cos\pi=10\pi-1,\qquad y(\pi)=\sin\pi=0. \] \[ \boxed{\mathbf r(\pi)=\langle 10\pi-1,\;0\rangle.} \]
Sanity check (initial condition): \(\mathbf r(0)=\langle 1,0\rangle\) matches the front bumper.
Geometric check: relative motion stays on the unit semicircle since \(\cos^2 t+\sin^2 t=1\).

(b) Speed and where it is largest/smallest

1) Differentiate to get velocity.

\[ x'(t)=10-\sin t,\qquad y'(t)=\cos t \] \[ \mathbf v(t)=\langle 10-\sin t,\;\cos t\rangle. \]

2) Compute speed squared \(v^2\) (cleaner than \(v\)).

\[ v^2(t)=(10-\sin t)^2+\cos^2 t =100-20\sin t+\sin^2 t+\cos^2 t =101-20\sin t. \] \[ \boxed{v(t)=\sqrt{101-20\sin t}}. \]

3) Extremes on \(0\le t\le\pi\). Over this interval, \(\sin t\in[0,1]\). Since \(v^2(t)=101-20\sin t\) decreases as \(\sin t\) increases:

\[ \sin t=1 \text{ at } t=\frac{\pi}{2}\Rightarrow v_{\min}=\sqrt{101-20}= \sqrt{81}=9. \] \[ \sin t=0 \text{ at } t=0,\pi\Rightarrow v_{\max}=\sqrt{101}. \]
‚òÖBoxed results (Problem 3)
\[ \boxed{\mathbf r(t)=\langle 10t+\cos t,\;\sin t\rangle,\quad 0\le t\le\pi} \] \[ \boxed{\mathbf r(\pi)=\langle 10\pi-1,\;0\rangle} \] \[ \boxed{v(t)=\sqrt{101-20\sin t},\quad v_{\min}=9 \text{ at } t=\frac{\pi}{2},\quad v_{\max}=\sqrt{101} \text{ at } t=0,\pi} \]
Units: Speeds are consistent with the given 10 and 1 (same units).
Limiting case: If VW speed \(\to 0\), then \(v=\sqrt{\sin^2t+\cos^2t}=1\), as expected.
Interpretation: Speed is smallest at the top because the bug‚Äôs motion most reduces the rightward component.

Problem 4 ‚Äî Determinant, inverse entries, solve \(M\mathbf r=\mathbf b(t)\)

Given \(M\) and a partially specified \(M^{-1}\), compute \(\det(M)\), determine \(a,b\), solve the system for \(\mathbf r(t)=\langle x,y,z\rangle\), and compute \(d\mathbf r/dt\).

PART 1 ‚Äî Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

  • In words: Use determinant/inverse facts to complete \(M^{-1}\), then solve a linear system depending on \(t\).
  • Given: \(M\) and \(M^{-1}=\tfrac1{12}(\cdots)\) with unknown \(a,b\).
  • Unknowns: \(\det(M)\), \(a,b\), \(\mathbf r(t)\), and \(d\mathbf r/dt\).
  • Find: explicit formulas and a clean derivative vector.

Relevant principles

  • Invertibility: \(\det(M)\neq 0\) iff \(M^{-1}\) exists.
  • Identity condition: \(MM^{-1}=I\) determines unknown inverse entries.
  • Solve via inverse: \(\mathbf r=M^{-1}\mathbf b(t)\).
  • Differentiate: derivative is componentwise; only \(t\)-terms change.
‚úìPART 2 ‚Äî Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)
  • Compute \(\det(M)\) by cofactor expansion (or row reduction).
  • Use \(MM^{-1}=I\) to solve for \(a,b\) (mind the factor \(1/12\)).
  • Rewrite the system as \(M\mathbf r=\mathbf b(t)\) with \(\mathbf b(t)=\langle 0,t,3\rangle\).
  • Compute \(\mathbf r(t)=M^{-1}\mathbf b(t)\) via matrix-vector multiplication.
  • Differentiate \(\mathbf r(t)\) componentwise for \(d\mathbf r/dt\).
PART 3 ‚Äî Full Solution (step-by-step) Determinant ‚Ä¢ Inverse ‚Ä¢ Linear system

(a) Compute \(\det(M)\)

\[ M=\begin{pmatrix} 1&2&3\\ 3&2&1\\ 2&-1&-1 \end{pmatrix} \]

Expand along the first row:

\[ \det(M) =1\begin{vmatrix}2&1\\-1&-1\end{vmatrix} -2\begin{vmatrix}3&1\\2&-1\end{vmatrix} +3\begin{vmatrix}3&2\\2&-1\end{vmatrix}. \]

Compute the \(2\times2\) determinants:

\[ \begin{vmatrix}2&1\\-1&-1\end{vmatrix}=2(-1)-1(-1)=-2+1=-1 \] \[ \begin{vmatrix}3&1\\2&-1\end{vmatrix}=3(-1)-1(2)=-3-2=-5 \] \[ \begin{vmatrix}3&2\\2&-1\end{vmatrix}=3(-1)-2(2)=-3-4=-7 \]

Substitute back:

\[ \det(M)=1(-1)-2(-5)+3(-7)=-1+10-21=-12. \] \[ \boxed{\det(M)=-12} \]

(b) Find \(a\) and \(b\) in \(M^{-1}\)

We are given:

\[ M^{-1}=\frac1{12}\begin{pmatrix} 1&1&4\\ a&7&-8\\ b&-5&4 \end{pmatrix}. \]

Using the defining property \(MM^{-1}=I\), the integer matrix \(12M^{-1}\) must be:

\[ 12M^{-1}= \begin{pmatrix} 1&1&4\\ -5&7&-8\\ 7&-5&4 \end{pmatrix}. \]
‚òÖBoxed result
\[ \boxed{a=-5,\qquad b=7.} \]

(c) Find \(\mathbf r=\langle x,y,z\rangle\) as a function of \(t\)

Write the system in matrix form:

\[ M\mathbf r=\mathbf b(t), \qquad \mathbf r=\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\\z\end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf b(t)=\begin{pmatrix}0\\t\\3\end{pmatrix}. \]

Then \(\mathbf r=M^{-1}\mathbf b(t)\):

\[ \mathbf r =\frac1{12}\begin{pmatrix} 1&1&4\\ -5&7&-8\\ 7&-5&4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}0\\t\\3\end{pmatrix}. \]

Compute each component (matrix‚Äìvector multiplication):

\[ x=\frac1{12}\big(1\cdot0+1\cdot t+4\cdot3\big)=\frac{t+12}{12}=1+\frac{t}{12} \] \[ y=\frac1{12}\big((-5)\cdot0+7\cdot t+(-8)\cdot3\big)=\frac{7t-24}{12}=-2+\frac{7t}{12} \] \[ z=\frac1{12}\big(7\cdot0+(-5)\cdot t+4\cdot3\big)=\frac{-5t+12}{12}=1-\frac{5t}{12}. \]
‚òÖBoxed result
\[ \boxed{\mathbf r(t)=\left\langle 1+\frac{t}{12},\ -2+\frac{7t}{12},\ 1-\frac{5t}{12}\right\rangle.} \]

(d) Compute \(\dfrac{d\mathbf r}{dt}\)

Differentiate componentwise:

\[ \frac{dx}{dt}=\frac1{12},\qquad \frac{dy}{dt}=\frac7{12},\qquad \frac{dz}{dt}=-\frac5{12}. \]
‚òÖBoxed result
\[ \boxed{\frac{d\mathbf r}{dt}=\left\langle \frac1{12},\ \frac7{12},\ -\frac5{12}\right\rangle.} \]
Consistency: \(\det(M)=-12\neq 0\) confirms the inverse exists.
Interpretation: \(\mathbf r(t)\) is linear in \(t\) because only the RHS depends linearly on \(t\).
