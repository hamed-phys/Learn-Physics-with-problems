Core Idea

One theorem • two forms

Circulation Form

\[ \oint_C (P\,dx+Q\,dy)=\iint_R\left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA \]

Perfect when your integral looks like \(P\,dx + Q\,dy\).

Flux (Normal) Form

\[ \oint_{\partial R}\vec F\cdot \hat n\,ds=\iint_R(\nabla\cdot \vec F)\,dA \]

Perfect for total outward flux through a closed boundary.

Problem 6 — Convert \(\displaystyle \oint_C M\,dx\) to an Area Integral + Density/Mass Matching

Given: \(C\) is a simple closed curve (counterclockwise), enclosing region \(R\), and \(M=M(x,y)\).
Structure: Analysis → Strategy → Solution
Green’s Theorem (circulation form)
PART 1 Problem Analysis (no solving yet)
Conceptual setup

Rewrite the problem (in my words)

(a) Rewrite the boundary integral \(\oint_C M\,dx\) as a double integral over the interior region \(R\).
(b) Choose \(M(x,y)\) so that \(\oint_C M\,dx\) equals the mass of \(R\) if the density is \(\delta(x,y)=(x+y)^2\).

Given

  • Curve: \(C\) simple, closed, counterclockwise
  • Region: \(R\) enclosed by \(C\)
  • Field: scalar \(M(x,y)\)
  • Density (b): \(\delta(x,y)=(x+y)^2\)

Unknowns / what must be found

  • (a) A double-integral expression for \(\oint_C M\,dx\)
  • (b) A function \(M\) such that \(\oint_C M\,dx = \iint_R \delta\,dA\)

Relevant principle (and why it applies)

We use Green’s theorem (circulation form) because the integral is around a positively-oriented simple closed curve, and the integrand matches the pattern \(P\,dx+Q\,dy\) once we choose \(P\) and \(Q\).

\[ \oint_C (P\,dx+Q\,dy)=\iint_R\left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA \]
Key sign warning: If you set \(Q=0\), the right-hand side becomes \(\iint_R(0-\partial P/\partial y)\,dA\). That minus sign is the #1 source of errors.
PART 2 Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)
5–10 steps max
  1. Goal: Match \(\oint_C M\,dx\) to \(\oint_C (P\,dx+Q\,dy)\). Tool: Choose \(P=M,\;Q=0\).
  2. Goal: Convert to area integral. Tool: Apply Green’s theorem (circulation form).
  3. Goal: For mass matching, relate \(\oint_C M\,dx\) to \(\iint_R \delta\,dA\). Tool: Match integrands.
  4. Goal: Solve for \(M\). Tool: Integrate \(\partial M/\partial y\) with respect to \(y\), include \(g(x)\).
  5. Goal: Verify quickly. Tool: Differentiate your \(M\) w.r.t. \(y\) to recover the required density relation.
Quick tip: The “constant of integration” after integrating in \(y\) can be any function \(g(x)\), because \(\partial g(x)/\partial y = 0\).
PART 3 Full Solution
Derivation + checks

Physical intuition

Green’s theorem trades a boundary measurement for an “accumulation over area.” Here, \(\oint_C M\,dx\) is a special case of a circulation integral where only the \(dx\)-term appears. The theorem tells us exactly which partial derivative must be integrated over \(R\).

6(a) Convert \(\displaystyle \oint_C M\,dx\) to a double integral over \(R\)

Start from Green’s theorem:

\[ \oint_C (P\,dx+Q\,dy)=\iint_R\left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA \]

Choose \(P(x,y)=M(x,y)\) and \(Q(x,y)=0\). Then: \(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}=0\) and \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}=\frac{\partial M}{\partial y}\).

\[ \oint_C M\,dx =\iint_R\left(0-\frac{\partial M}{\partial y}\right)dA =-\iint_R \frac{\partial M}{\partial y}\,dA \]
Final (6a): \[ \boxed{\displaystyle \oint_C M\,dx=-\iint_R \frac{\partial M}{\partial y}\,dA} \]

6(b) Choose \(M\) so \(\displaystyle \oint_C M\,dx\) equals mass with \(\delta(x,y)=(x+y)^2\)

Mass is given by:

\[ \text{Mass}=\iint_R \delta(x,y)\,dA=\iint_R (x+y)^2\,dA \]

From part (a), \(\displaystyle \oint_C M\,dx=-\iint_R \frac{\partial M}{\partial y}\,dA\). To make this equal to mass for any region \(R\), match integrands:

\[ -\frac{\partial M}{\partial y}=(x+y)^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{\partial M}{\partial y}=-(x+y)^2 \]

Integrate with respect to \(y\):

\[ M(x,y)=\int -(x+y)^2\,dy \]

Let \(u=x+y\), so \(du=dy\). Then:

\[ M(x,y)=-\frac{(x+y)^3}{3}+g(x) \]
Final (6b): \[ \boxed{\displaystyle M(x,y)=-\frac{(x+y)^3}{3}+g(x)\quad (\text{any function }g)} \]
A simplest choice is \(g(x)=0\), so \(M(x,y)=-\frac{(x+y)^3}{3}\).
Sanity check: \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(-\frac{(x+y)^3}{3}\right)=-(x+y)^2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad -M_y=(x+y)^2=\delta(x,y) \] Perfect match.

Problem 7 — Outward Flux of \(\vec F=(1+y^2)\hat{\jmath}\) from a Curved Triangular Region

Region \(R\) enclosed by \(y=0\), \(x=1\), and \(y=x^3\). Compute total flux and flux through each side.
Tool: Flux form of Green’s theorem
Divergence controls net outward flow
PART 1 Problem Analysis (no solving yet)
Flux setup

Rewrite the problem (in my words)

(a) Use Green’s theorem in flux form to compute the total outward flux of \(\vec F=(1+y^2)\hat{\jmath}\) across the entire boundary of \(R\).
(b) Compute the outward flux across the two “straight” sides: the bottom edge \(C_1\) (on \(y=0\)) and the right edge \(C_2\) (on \(x=1\)).
(c) Use subtraction to find the flux through the curved side \(C_3\) (the graph \(y=x^3\)).

Given

  • Region: \(0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le x^3\)
  • Field: \(\vec F=\langle 0,\ 1+y^2\rangle\)
  • Boundary pieces: \(C_1:y=0\), \(C_2:x=1\), \(C_3:y=x^3\)

Unknowns / must find

  • (a) Total outward flux \(\Phi_{\text{total}}\)
  • (b) \(\Phi_{C_1}\), \(\Phi_{C_2}\)
  • (c) \(\Phi_{C_3}\)

Relevant principle (and why it applies)

We use the flux (normal) form of Green’s theorem because we want net outward flux through a closed curve. It converts boundary flux to an area integral of divergence.

\[ \oint_{\partial R}\vec F\cdot \hat n\,ds=\iint_R(\nabla\cdot \vec F)\,dA \]
Normal-direction warning: On \(C_1\) (the \(x\)-axis), the region lies above the boundary, so the outward normal points down. That flips the sign of the flux there.
PART 2 Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)
Minimal plan
  1. Goal: Write bounds for \(R\). Tool: \(0\le x\le 1\), \(0\le y\le x^3\).
  2. Goal: Compute \(\nabla\cdot \vec F\). Tool: If \(\vec F=\langle P,Q\rangle\), divergence is \(P_x+Q_y\).
  3. Goal: Compute total flux. Tool: \(\iint_R (\nabla\cdot \vec F)\,dA\).
  4. Goal: Compute \(\Phi_{C_1}\). Tool: Use outward normal \(-\hat{\jmath}\) on \(y=0\).
  5. Goal: Compute \(\Phi_{C_2}\). Tool: Outward normal \(+\hat{\imath}\) on \(x=1\); note \(F_x=0\).
  6. Goal: Get \(\Phi_{C_3}\). Tool: \(\Phi_{C_3}=\Phi_{\text{total}}-(\Phi_{C_1}+\Phi_{C_2})\).
Shortcut insight: If the field has no \(x\)-component, then any boundary segment with normal purely \(\pm\hat{\imath}\) contributes zero flux. That immediately kills \(\Phi_{C_2}\).
PART 3 Full Solution
Math + checks

Physical intuition

The field \(\vec F=(1+y^2)\hat{\jmath}\) points upward everywhere, and it gets stronger as \(y\) increases. So the bottom edge (with outward normal downward) should contribute negative flux, while the curved boundary at higher \(y\) must provide enough positive flux for the total to match the net “source strength” inside given by divergence.

Region description

The boundary is \(y=0\), \(x=1\), and \(y=x^3\). Therefore:

\[ R=\{(x,y)\,:\,0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le x^3\} \]

7(a) Total outward flux using flux-form Green’s theorem

Write \(\vec F=\langle P,Q\rangle=\langle 0,\ 1+y^2\rangle\). Then:

\[ \nabla\cdot\vec F=\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} =0+\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(1+y^2)=2y \]

Apply the flux form:

\[ \Phi_{\text{total}} =\iint_R(\nabla\cdot\vec F)\,dA =\int_{0}^{1}\int_{0}^{x^3}2y\,dy\,dx \]

Compute the inner integral:

\[ \int_{0}^{x^3}2y\,dy=\left[y^2\right]_{0}^{x^3}=x^6 \]

Now integrate in \(x\):

\[ \int_{0}^{1}x^6\,dx=\left[\frac{x^7}{7}\right]_{0}^{1}=\frac{1}{7} \]
Final (7a): \[ \boxed{\displaystyle \Phi_{\text{total}}=\frac{1}{7}} \]

7(b) Flux through \(C_1\) (horizontal) and \(C_2\) (vertical)

\(C_1\): \(y=0,\ 0\le x\le 1\)

Along \(C_1\), \(\vec F=\langle 0,1\rangle\). The region lies above the \(x\)-axis, so outward normal is \(\hat n=-\hat{\jmath}\). Also, \(ds=dx\).

\[ \Phi_{C_1}=\int_{0}^{1}\vec F\cdot \hat n\,dx =\int_{0}^{1}\langle 0,1\rangle\cdot\langle 0,-1\rangle\,dx =\int_{0}^{1}(-1)\,dx=-1 \]
Final: \(\boxed{\displaystyle \Phi_{C_1}=-1}\)

\(C_2\): \(x=1,\ 0\le y\le 1\)

On the right edge, outward normal is \(\hat n=+\hat{\imath}\). But \(\vec F=\langle 0,1+y^2\rangle\) has no \(x\)-component, so \(\vec F\cdot \hat n=0\) everywhere.

\[ \Phi_{C_2}=\int_{C_2}\vec F\cdot \hat n\,ds=0 \]
Final: \(\boxed{\displaystyle \Phi_{C_2}=0}\)

7(c) Flux through the curved side \(C_3\) (the curve \(y=x^3\))

Total flux equals the sum over the three sides:

\[ \Phi_{\text{total}}=\Phi_{C_1}+\Phi_{C_2}+\Phi_{C_3} \]

Solve for \(\Phi_{C_3}\):

\[ \Phi_{C_3}=\Phi_{\text{total}}-(\Phi_{C_1}+\Phi_{C_2}) =\frac{1}{7}-(-1+0)=\frac{1}{7}+1=\frac{8}{7} \]
Final (7c): \[ \boxed{\displaystyle \Phi_{C_3}=\frac{8}{7}} \]
Sanity checks
  • Sign check: On \(C_1\), outward is downward but \(\vec F\) points upward ⇒ negative flux. We got \(-1\).
  • Divergence check: \(\nabla\cdot\vec F=2y\ge 0\) in \(R\) ⇒ total flux positive. We got \(1/7>0\).
  • Interpretation: Curved boundary must “compensate” for negative bottom edge ⇒ \(\Phi_{C_3}=8/7\) is positive and large enough.
  • Units: If \(\vec F\) has units of (quantity/length·time), flux has units (quantity/time), consistent with area integral of divergence.
