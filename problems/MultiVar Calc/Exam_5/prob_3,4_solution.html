Full Structured Solution: Line Integral & (u,v) Change of Variables

We compute the work \(\int_C \vec F\cdot d\vec r\) along a curved path, then transform a curved planar region into a rectangle using the substitution \(u=\frac{x^2}{y}\), \(v=xy\) and evaluate the area via the Jacobian.

ⅠPART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Restate What the problem is asking

(4) For \(\vec F(x,y)=(yx^3)\hat{\imath}+(y^2)\hat{\jmath}\), compute the line integral \(\int_C \vec F\cdot d\vec r\) along the curve \(y=x^2\) from \((0,0)\) to \((1,1)\).
(5) In the first quadrant, region \(R\) is bounded by \(y=x^2\), \(y=\frac{x^2}{5}\), \(xy=2\), \(xy=4\). With \(u=\frac{x^2}{y}\), \(v=xy\): (a) find \(dx\,dy\) in terms of \(du\,dv\), (b) compute the area of \(R\) using a double integral in \((u,v)\).

Given / Unknowns Inventory
  • Given (4): \(\vec F=\langle yx^3,\; y^2\rangle\), curve \(C:\ y=x^2\), endpoints \((0,0)\to(1,1)\).
  • Find (4): \(\displaystyle \int_C \vec F\cdot d\vec r\).
  • Given (5): boundaries \(y=x^2\), \(y=x^2/5\), \(xy=2\), \(xy=4\), with \(x>0,y>0\).
  • Find (5a): the Jacobian factor for \(dx\,dy\) in terms of \(du\,dv\).
  • Find (5b): \(\text{Area}(R)=\iint_R 1\,dx\,dy\) using \((u,v)\).
Principles Why these tools apply
  • Work by a force field: \(\displaystyle W=\int_C \vec F\cdot d\vec r=\int_C (P\,dx+Q\,dy)\). This accumulates the force component along the displacement.
  • Change of variables (Jacobian): \(\displaystyle dx\,dy=\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right| du\,dv\), because the Jacobian measures local area scaling under the mapping.
Approaches Options and best choice
  • (4) Parametrize \(C\) (fast, direct) vs. checking conservativeness (unnecessary here).
  • (5) Use Jacobian + boundary mapping (turns curves into constants) vs. area in \(xy\) (messier).
Best plan: parameterize for (4); compute \(\partial(u,v)/\partial(x,y)\) and map boundaries to a rectangle for (5).

ⅡPART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Roadmap (4)Line integral along \(y=x^2\)
  1. Parametrize: set \(x=t\), \(y=t^2\), \(t\in[0,1]\).
  2. Compute differentials: \(dx\) and \(dy\) from \(t\).
  3. Substitute into \(P\,dx+Q\,dy\) with \(P=yx^3\), \(Q=y^2\).
  4. Integrate over \(t\in[0,1]\) to get work \(W\).
Common mistakes: forgetting \(dy=(dy/dt)\,dt\); dot product is \(P\,dx+Q\,dy\), not \(P+Q\).
Roadmap (5)Area via \((u,v)\)
  1. Compute \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)}\) from partial derivatives.
  2. Invert to get \(\displaystyle dx\,dy=\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|du\,dv\).
  3. Transform boundaries: \(y=x^2\), \(y=x^2/5\), \(xy=2\), \(xy=4\) into \(u,v\) constraints.
  4. Write \(\text{Area}(R)=\iint_S \frac{1}{3u}\,du\,dv\) over the mapped region \(S\).
  5. Evaluate the rectangle integral.
Common mistakes: swapping Jacobians; forgetting absolute value; mis-mapping \(y=x^2\) (it becomes \(u=1\)).

ⅢPART 3 — Full Solution

Problem 4 \(\displaystyle \int_C \vec F\cdot d\vec r\) along \(y=x^2\), \((0,0)\to(1,1)\)

Physical intuition: \(\vec F\cdot d\vec r\) is the incremental work. Along the first-quadrant path, the field and displacement generally point “forward” (positive components), so the work should come out positive.

Let \(\vec F=\langle P,Q\rangle\) with \(P(x,y)=yx^3\), \(Q(x,y)=y^2\). Then \[ \int_C \vec F\cdot d\vec r=\int_C (P\,dx+Q\,dy). \]

Parameterize the curve \(y=x^2\) by \(x=t,\ y=t^2\), for \(0\le t\le 1\). Then \[ dx=dt,\qquad dy=2t\,dt. \]

Substitute into \(P\) and \(Q\): \[ P(t)=y x^3=(t^2)(t^3)=t^5,\qquad Q(t)=y^2=(t^2)^2=t^4. \] Hence \[ P\,dx+Q\,dy=t^5(dt)+t^4(2t\,dt)=t^5dt+2t^5dt=3t^5dt. \]

Integrate: \[ \int_C \vec F\cdot d\vec r=\int_0^1 3t^5\,dt =3\left[\frac{t^6}{6}\right]_0^1 =\frac{1}{2}. \]

✓Final Result (Problem 4)

\(\boxed{\displaystyle \int_C \vec F\cdot d\vec r=\frac{1}{2}}\)
Sanity checks (units • sign • interpretation)
  • Units: \(P\,dx\) and \(Q\,dy\) each have “force × length” units (work). Result is consistent (treated dimensionless here).
  • Sign: integrand \(3t^5\ge 0\) on \([0,1]\), so work must be \(\ge 0\); \(\tfrac12\) matches.
  • Physical meaning: field assists motion in the first quadrant, giving positive work.
Problem 5 Region \(R\), substitution \(u=\frac{x^2}{y}\), \(v=xy\)

Physical intuition: The boundaries are of two types—parabolic (\(y\propto x^2\)) and hyperbolic (\(xy=\text{const}\)). The chosen \(u\) and \(v\) “measure” exactly these families, so the curved region becomes a rectangle in \((u,v)\). The Jacobian then converts area elements correctly.

5(a)Compute \(dx\,dy\) in terms of \(du\,dv\)

We compute the Jacobian \(\dfrac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)}\). Given \[ u=\frac{x^2}{y},\qquad v=xy, \] the partial derivatives are \[ u_x=\frac{2x}{y},\quad u_y=-\frac{x^2}{y^2},\qquad v_x=y,\quad v_y=x. \]

Therefore, \[ \frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)} = \begin{vmatrix} u_x & u_y\\ v_x & v_y \end{vmatrix} = u_xv_y-u_yv_x = \left(\frac{2x}{y}\right)(x)-\left(-\frac{x^2}{y^2}\right)(y). \]

Compute each term: \[ \left(\frac{2x}{y}\right)(x)=\frac{2x^2}{y},\qquad \left(-\frac{x^2}{y^2}\right)(y)=-\frac{x^2}{y}. \] So \[ \frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)} = \frac{2x^2}{y}-\left(-\frac{x^2}{y}\right) = \frac{3x^2}{y} =3u. \]

Hence \[ dx\,dy = \left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|du\,dv = \frac{1}{\left|\frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)}\right|}du\,dv = \frac{1}{|3u|}du\,dv. \] In the first quadrant \(u=\frac{x^2}{y}>0\), so \(|3u|=3u\). Therefore \[ \boxed{\displaystyle dx\,dy=\frac{1}{3u}\,du\,dv.} \]

✓Final Result (Problem 5a)

\(\boxed{\displaystyle dx\,dy=\frac{1}{3u}\,du\,dv}\)
5(b)Area of \(R\) in \((u,v)\) coordinates

Transform each boundary:

  • \(y=x^2 \Rightarrow u=\dfrac{x^2}{y}=1\).
  • \(y=\dfrac{x^2}{5} \Rightarrow u=\dfrac{x^2}{x^2/5}=5\).
  • \(xy=2 \Rightarrow v=2\).
  • \(xy=4 \Rightarrow v=4\).

So \(R\) maps to the rectangle \(S\) in the \(uv\)-plane: \[ 1\le u\le 5,\qquad 2\le v\le 4. \]

The area is \[ \text{Area}(R)=\iint_R 1\,dx\,dy = \iint_S 1\cdot\frac{1}{3u}\,du\,dv = \int_{v=2}^{4}\int_{u=1}^{5}\frac{1}{3u}\,du\,dv. \]

Evaluate the inner integral: \[ \int_{1}^{5}\frac{1}{3u}\,du = \frac{1}{3}[\ln u]_{1}^{5} = \frac{1}{3}\ln 5. \] Then the outer integral: \[ \int_{2}^{4}\frac{1}{3}\ln 5\,dv = \left(\frac{1}{3}\ln 5\right)(4-2) = \frac{2}{3}\ln 5. \]

✓Final Result (Problem 5b)

\(\boxed{\displaystyle \text{Area}(R)=\frac{2}{3}\ln 5}\)
Sanity checks (positivity • scaling • geometry)
  • Positivity: \(\ln 5>0\Rightarrow \text{Area}>0\).
  • Scaling: area is proportional to the width in \(v\): \((4-2)\). That matches the integral structure.
  • Geometry: substitution straightens \(y\propto x^2\) into constant \(u\) and \(xy=\text{const}\) into constant \(v\), yielding a rectangle.
