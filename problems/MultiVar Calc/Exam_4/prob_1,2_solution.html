This article presents a structured, rigorous, beginner-friendly walkthrough of the problems shown. We treat the scalar field \(f(x,y)\) like a ‚Äúpotential/height‚Äù field and use tools common in physics: gradients, local linear models, directional rates of change, and contour-map geometry.

Quick Context What these mean in physics

Gradient
\(\nabla f\) points in the direction of steepest increase of \(f\). Its components are sensitivities: \(f_x\) and \(f_y\).
Tangent Plane / Linearization
Near a point, the surface \(z=f(x,y)\) is well-approximated by a plane: \(z \approx f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b)\).
Directional Derivative
\(D_{\mathbf u}f=\nabla f\cdot \mathbf u\) is the rate of change if you ‚Äúwalk‚Äù in direction \(\mathbf u\) (must be a unit vector).
Contours
Level curves are \(f=\text{constant}\). The gradient is perpendicular to contours and points toward larger labels.

PART 3 ‚Äî Full Solution

Intuition ‚Üí Math Step-by-step Sanity checks
üå°Ô∏èPhysical intuition

Treat \(f(x,y)\) like a potential/height field over the \(xy\)-plane. The gradient \(\nabla f\) points ‚Äúuphill,‚Äù and the tangent plane is the best local flat model of the surface \(z=f(x,y)\). The directional derivative is the ‚Äúslope you feel‚Äù when walking in a specific direction. On a contour map, higher labels mean higher field value; gradients point perpendicular to the contours toward increasing labels.

üß©Problem 1: \(f(x,y)=x^2y^2-x\)

(a) Find \(\nabla f\) at \((2,1)\)

\[ f_x=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2y^2-x)=2xy^2-1 \] \[ f_y=\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2y^2-x)=2x^2y \]

Evaluate at \((2,1)\):

\[ f_x(2,1)=2(2)(1)^2-1=4-1=3 \] \[ f_y(2,1)=2(2^2)(1)=2(4)=8 \]
‚úÖFinal (a)
\[ \boxed{\nabla f(2,1)=\langle 3,\;8\rangle} \]

(b) Tangent plane to \(z=f(x,y)\) at \((2,1,2)\)

First confirm the surface height at \((2,1)\):

\[ f(2,1)=2^2\cdot 1^2-2=4-2=2 \]

Tangent plane formula: \[ z-z_0=f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b) \] with \((a,b,z_0)=(2,1,2)\) and \(\nabla f(2,1)=\langle 3,8\rangle\).

\[ z-2=3(x-2)+8(y-1) \] \[ z=2+3(x-2)+8(y-1) \]
‚úÖFinal (b)
\[ \boxed{z=2+3(x-2)+8(y-1)} \]

(c) Use linear approximation to estimate \(f(1.9,1.1)\)

Linearization at \((2,1)\): \[ L(x,y)=f(2,1)+f_x(2,1)(x-2)+f_y(2,1)(y-1) \] Using \(f(2,1)=2\), \(f_x(2,1)=3\), \(f_y(2,1)=8\):

\[ L(x,y)=2+3(x-2)+8(y-1) \]

Compute the small changes:

\[ dx=1.9-2=-0.1,\qquad dy=1.1-1=0.1 \]

Plug in:

\[ L(1.9,1.1)=2+3(-0.1)+8(0.1)=2-0.3+0.8=2.5 \]
‚úÖFinal (c)
\[ \boxed{f(1.9,1.1)\approx 2.5} \]

(d) Directional derivative at \((2,1)\) in direction \(-\hat i+\hat j\)

Direction vector and unit vector:

\[ \mathbf v=\langle -1,\,1\rangle,\qquad \|\mathbf v\|=\sqrt{(-1)^2+1^2}=\sqrt2 \] \[ \mathbf u=\frac{\mathbf v}{\|\mathbf v\|}= \left\langle -\frac1{\sqrt2},\,\frac1{\sqrt2}\right\rangle \]

Directional derivative:

\[ D_{\mathbf u}f(2,1)=\nabla f(2,1)\cdot \mathbf u =\langle 3,8\rangle\cdot \left\langle -\frac1{\sqrt2},\,\frac1{\sqrt2}\right\rangle \] \[ =\frac{-3+8}{\sqrt2}=\frac{5}{\sqrt2} \]
‚úÖFinal (d)
\[ \boxed{D_{\mathbf u}f(2,1)=\frac{5}{\sqrt2}} \]
üß™ Units
No explicit physical units were provided. All derivatives are ‚Äúfield change per coordinate change,‚Äù consistent across parts.
üìâ Limiting / local behavior
At \((2,1)\), the field increases more strongly with \(y\) than with \(x\) because \(f_y(2,1)=8>3=f_x(2,1)\).
üß≠ Physical interpretation
Moving in direction \((-x,+y)\) gives a positive rate \(\frac{5}{\sqrt2}\), so that motion increases the field locally.
üó∫Ô∏èProblem 2: Contour plot ‚Äî where on \(f=2000\) is \(\partial f/\partial y\ge 0\)?

Key geometric facts:

  • \(\nabla f\) is perpendicular to a contour \(f=\text{constant}\).
  • \(\nabla f\) points toward larger labeled values (the ‚Äúuphill‚Äù side).
  • \(\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y}\ge 0\iff (\nabla f)_y\ge 0\), meaning the gradient has a nonnegative \(y\)-component.
\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\ge 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad (\nabla f)_y\ge 0 \]

How to decide on the plot: pick a point on the \(f=2000\) curve. Look which side of the curve leads to higher contour labels (values \(>2000\)). The gradient points to that side. If that ‚Äúuphill‚Äù direction points at least partly upward (positive \(y\)), then \(\partial f/\partial y\ge 0\) there.

The shown contour map has a ‚Äútwo-lobe / two-peak‚Äù structure: higher levels sit nearer the centers of the lobes, so the gradient points inward toward those centers. Therefore, on the \(f=2000\) curve, \(\partial f/\partial y\ge 0\) on the portions where the ‚Äúinward/uphill‚Äù normal tilts upward.

‚úÖFinal (Problem 2)

Mark the portions of the \(f=2000\) contour where the next higher contours lie above it (equivalently: where the uphill normal points upward, so \((\nabla f)_y\ge 0\)). On this figure, that corresponds to the lower arc of the upper \(2000\) loop and the lower arc of the lower \(2000\) loop.

üß™ Units
A contour plot is unit-agnostic unless units are labeled. The sign of \(\partial f/\partial y\) is purely geometric here.
üìâ Limiting case
If you move straight up at a point and immediately cross into larger labeled contours, then \(\partial f/\partial y>0\). If you cross into smaller labels, it‚Äôs \(<0\).
üß≠ Interpretation
\(\partial f/\partial y\ge 0\) means a small increase in \(y\) does not decrease the field; ‚Äúnorthward‚Äù motion is not downhill.
