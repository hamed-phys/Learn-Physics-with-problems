Problem 3 — Linear Algebra (15 points)

Given \[ A=\begin{pmatrix}1&3&2\\2&0&-1\\1&1&0\end{pmatrix},\quad \det(A)=2,\quad A^{-1}=\frac12\begin{pmatrix}1&a&b\\-1&-2&5\\2&2&-6\end{pmatrix}. \] (a) Find \(a,b\). (b) Solve \(AX=B\) for \(B=(1,-2,1)^T\). (c) Replace \(2\) by \(c\) to form \(M\); find \(c\) making \(M\) singular and a nonzero solution of \(MX=0\).

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Goal: understand + choose tools

Restatement. We are given a specific \(3\times 3\) matrix \(A\) and a proposed formula for its inverse containing unknown entries \(a\) and \(b\). We must determine those entries, then use the inverse to solve \(AX=B\). Finally, we modify one entry of \(A\) to form \(M\), find the parameter value that makes \(M\) non-invertible, and produce a nontrivial vector in the null space of \(M\).

Given
  • \(A=\begin{pmatrix}1&3&2\\2&0&-1\\1&1&0\end{pmatrix}\)
  • \(\det(A)=2\neq 0\Rightarrow A\) is invertible
  • \(A^{-1}=\dfrac12\begin{pmatrix}1&a&b\\-1&-2&5\\2&2&-6\end{pmatrix}\)
  • \(B=\begin{pmatrix}1\\-2\\1\end{pmatrix}\)
Unknowns / What to find
  • (a) \(a,b\)
  • (b) \(X=(x,y,z)^T\)
  • (c) \(c\) such that \(\det(M)=0\), and one \(X\neq 0\) with \(MX=0\)

Key principles and why they apply.

  • Inverse definition: \(AA^{-1}=I\). This directly imposes equations that determine \(a,b\).
  • Solving linear systems: If \(A\) is invertible, \(X=A^{-1}B\).
  • Singularity: \(M\) is not invertible iff \(\det(M)=0\).
  • Orthogonality hint: \(MX=0\) means each row of \(M\) has dot product \(0\) with \(X\).

Approaches.

  • Direct multiplication \(AA^{-1}=I\): fastest for (a), minimal overhead.
  • Row reduction: works but longer since the inverse is already almost given.
  • Cofactor/adjugate: valid but computation-heavy.
Chosen approach Use \(AA^{-1}=I\) to solve for \(a,b\), then compute \(X=A^{-1}B\). For (c) compute \(\det(M)\) in terms of \(c\), then use the orthogonality hint (via a cross product of two rows) to construct a null vector.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Focus: clean plan, no algebra
  1. Step 1
    Goal: Lock down \(a,b\).
    Tool: Multiply \(A\) by the proposed \(A^{-1}\) and set the result equal to \(I\).
  2. Step 2
    Goal: Solve \(AX=B\).
    Tool: Use \(X=A^{-1}B\) and compute a matrix–vector product.
  3. Step 3
    Goal: Find \(c\) making \(M\) singular.
    Tool: Compute \(\det(M)\) as a function of \(c\); set it to zero.
  4. Step 4
    Goal: Produce a nonzero solution of \(MX=0\).
    Tool: Interpret rows as vectors \(U,V,W\). Use a cross product \(U\times V\) to get a vector orthogonal to both, then verify orthogonality to the third row.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting the overall factor \(\tfrac12\) in \(A^{-1}\).
  • Not enforcing off-diagonal entries of \(AA^{-1}\) to be \(0\).
  • In part (c), finding a vector orthogonal to two rows but not checking the third.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Includes: derivation + checks

Intuition. An inverse matrix “undoes” the action of the original matrix. Therefore, applying \(A\) and then \(A^{-1}\) must return every vector unchanged, which algebraically means \(AA^{-1}=I\). This gives concrete equations that uniquely fix the unknown entries.

3(a) Find \(a\) and \(b\)

Let \[ K=\begin{pmatrix}1&a&b\\-1&-2&5\\2&2&-6\end{pmatrix} \quad\Rightarrow\quad A^{-1}=\frac12K. \] Then \[ AA^{-1}=A\left(\frac12K\right)=\frac12(AK). \]

Compute \(AK\) by row–column dot products.

Row 1 of \(A\): \((1,3,2)\)

\[ (1,3,2)\cdot(1,-1,2)=2,\quad (1,3,2)\cdot(a,-2,2)=a-2,\quad (1,3,2)\cdot(b,5,-6)=b+3. \]

So row 1 of \(AK\) is \((2,\ a-2,\ b+3)\).

Row 2 of \(A\): \((2,0,-1)\)

\[ (2,0,-1)\cdot(1,-1,2)=0,\quad (2,0,-1)\cdot(a,-2,2)=2a-2,\quad (2,0,-1)\cdot(b,5,-6)=2b+6. \]

So row 2 of \(AK\) is \((0,\ 2a-2,\ 2b+6)\).

Row 3 of \(A\): \((1,1,0)\)

\[ (1,1,0)\cdot(1,-1,2)=0,\quad (1,1,0)\cdot(a,-2,2)=a-2,\quad (1,1,0)\cdot(b,5,-6)=b+5. \]

So row 3 of \(AK\) is \((0,\ a-2,\ b+5)\).

Thus, \[ AK= \begin{pmatrix} 2 & a-2 & b+3\\ 0 & 2a-2 & 2b+6\\ 0 & a-2 & b+5 \end{pmatrix} \quad\Rightarrow\quad AA^{-1}=\frac12AK= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \frac{a-2}{2} & \frac{b+3}{2}\\ 0 & a-1 & b+3\\ 0 & \frac{a-2}{2} & \frac{b+5}{2} \end{pmatrix}. \]

Set \(AA^{-1}=I\). Off-diagonal entries must be \(0\):

\[ \frac{a-2}{2}=0\Rightarrow a=2, \qquad \frac{b+3}{2}=0\Rightarrow b=-3. \]

Final (3a)

\[ \boxed{a=2,\qquad b=-3.} \]

3(b) Solve \(AX=B\)

Because \(\det(A)=2\neq 0\), \(A\) is invertible, so \[ X=A^{-1}B. \] Substitute \(a=2,\ b=-3\): \[ A^{-1}=\frac12 \begin{pmatrix} 1&2&-3\\ -1&-2&5\\ 2&2&-6 \end{pmatrix}. \]

Compute \(KB\) first, where \(K=\begin{pmatrix}1&2&-3\\-1&-2&5\\2&2&-6\end{pmatrix}\) and \(B=(1,-2,1)^T\):

\[ KB= \begin{pmatrix} 1(1)+2(-2)+(-3)(1)\\ (-1)(1)+(-2)(-2)+5(1)\\ 2(1)+2(-2)+(-6)(1) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -6\\ 8\\ -8 \end{pmatrix}. \] Therefore, \[ X=\frac12(KB)= \begin{pmatrix} -3\\ 4\\ -4 \end{pmatrix}. \]

Final (3b)

\[ \boxed{X=\begin{pmatrix}-3\\4\\-4\end{pmatrix} \ \Longrightarrow\ x=-3,\ y=4,\ z=-4.} \]
Sanity check
Verify \(AX=B\) quickly:
\[ \begin{aligned} (1,3,2)\cdot(-3,4,-4)&=1\\ (2,0,-1)\cdot(-3,4,-4)&=-2\\ (1,1,0)\cdot(-3,4,-4)&=1 \end{aligned} \]
Interpretation
Since \(A\) is invertible, the solution is unique. Multiplying by \(A^{-1}\) is the most direct “undo” operation.
Common pitfall
Missing the \(\tfrac12\) factor would double every component of \(X\).

3(c) Find \(c\) making \(M\) not invertible, then a nonzero solution of \(MX=0\)

Replace the upper-right entry \(2\) by \(c\): \[ M=\begin{pmatrix} 1&3&c\\ 2&0&-1\\ 1&1&0 \end{pmatrix}. \]

Compute \(\det(M)\). Expand along the first row:

\[ \det(M)= 1\cdot\det\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix} -3\cdot\det\begin{pmatrix}2&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix} +c\cdot\det\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\1&1\end{pmatrix}. \] Evaluate: \[ \det\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}=1,\quad \det\begin{pmatrix}2&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}=1,\quad \det\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\1&1\end{pmatrix}=2. \] Hence: \[ \det(M)=1-3(1)+c(2)=2c-2=2(c-1). \]

\(M\) is not invertible when \(\det(M)=0\):

\[ 2(c-1)=0\Rightarrow c=1. \]

Now find \(X\neq 0\) with \(MX=0\) for \(c=1\). With \(c=1\), the rows are

\[ U=(1,3,1),\quad V=(2,0,-1),\quad W=(1,1,0). \] The hint says \(MX=0\) iff \(X\) is orthogonal to \(U,V,W\). A vector orthogonal to both \(U\) and \(V\) is \(U\times V\): \[ U\times V= \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\ 1&3&1\\ 2&0&-1 \end{vmatrix} = (-3,3,-6)=3(-1,1,-2). \] Check orthogonality to \(W\): \[ W\cdot(-3,3,-6)=(-3)+3+0=0. \] So a simple nonzero solution is \[ X=(-1,1,-2)^T. \]

Final (3c)

\[ \boxed{c=1,\qquad X=\begin{pmatrix}-1\\1\\-2\end{pmatrix}\ \text{is a nonzero solution of }MX=0.} \]
Sanity check
Row dot-products are zero:
\[ U\cdot X=0,\quad V\cdot X=0,\quad W\cdot X=0. \]
Meaning
At \(c=1\), the rows become linearly dependent, so the null space is nontrivial.
Units
Pure linear algebra here — entries are dimensionless; the check is consistency of equations rather than physical units.

Problem 4 — Ladder Midpoint Parametric Equations (15 points)

A ladder of length \(L\) rests on the floor and against a vertical wall. As the bottom slides away, the ladder angle with the floor is \(\theta\). Find parametric equations for the midpoint \(P\) using \(\theta\) as the parameter.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Model: right-triangle geometry

Restatement. The ladder endpoints always touch the coordinate axes (floor and wall), forming a right triangle. The angle \(\theta\) at the bottom determines the triangle. The midpoint is the average of endpoint coordinates.

Given
  • Fixed ladder length \(L\)
  • Angle \(\theta\) between ladder and the floor
  • Wall vertical, floor horizontal (right angle)
Find
  • Parametric equations \((x_P(\theta),y_P(\theta))\)

Principles. The ladder is the hypotenuse of a right triangle, so \(\cos\theta\) and \(\sin\theta\) give the adjacent and opposite legs relative to \(L\).

Best approach Place the corner at the origin. Use trig to express the endpoint coordinates, then take the midpoint.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Goal: parametric form
  1. Step 1
    Goal: Choose coordinates.
    Tool: Wall = \(y\)-axis, floor = \(x\)-axis.
  2. Step 2
    Goal: Write endpoints.
    Tool: Bottom \((x,0)\), top \((0,y)\).
  3. Step 3
    Goal: Express \(x,y\) using \(\theta\).
    Tool: \(x=L\cos\theta\), \(y=L\sin\theta\).
  4. Step 4
    Goal: Find midpoint \(P\).
    Tool: Midpoint formula.
Quick tips
  • \(\theta\) is measured at the bottom, between ladder and floor.
  • Physical range: \(0<\theta<\frac{\pi}{2}\).
  • The midpoint trajectory will be a quarter-circle of radius \(L/2\).

PART 3 — Full Solution

Includes: checks + interpretation

Physical intuition. The ladder length stays constant, so all configurations lie on the constraint of a right triangle. Using the angle \(\theta\) automatically satisfies the geometry: trigonometry converts the angle directly into endpoint coordinates.

Coordinate setup

Place the corner at the origin. The ladder touches the floor at \((x,0)\) and the wall at \((0,y)\). The ladder length is \(L\), so it is the hypotenuse of a right triangle.

Use trigonometry

\[ \cos\theta=\frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}=\frac{x}{L}\Rightarrow x=L\cos\theta, \qquad \sin\theta=\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}=\frac{y}{L}\Rightarrow y=L\sin\theta. \]

Midpoint of the ladder

The midpoint \(P\) of the segment joining \((x,0)\) and \((0,y)\) is

\[ P=\left(\frac{x+0}{2},\frac{0+y}{2}\right)=\left(\frac{x}{2},\frac{y}{2}\right). \] Substitute \(x=L\cos\theta\), \(y=L\sin\theta\): \[ P(\theta)=\left(\frac{L}{2}\cos\theta,\ \frac{L}{2}\sin\theta\right). \]

Final (Problem 4)

\[ \boxed{ x_P(\theta)=\frac{L}{2}\cos\theta,\qquad y_P(\theta)=\frac{L}{2}\sin\theta,\qquad 0<\theta<\frac{\pi}{2}. } \]
Units
\(L\) is length; \(\sin,\cos\) are unitless → \(x_P,y_P\) have length units.
Limiting cases
\(\theta\to 0^+\): \(P\to(\tfrac{L}{2},0)\) (ladder nearly flat).
\(\theta\to \tfrac{\pi}{2}^-\): \(P\to(0,\tfrac{L}{2})\) (ladder nearly vertical).
Interpretation
The midpoint traces a quarter-circle: \[ x_P^2+y_P^2=\left(\frac{L}{2}\right)^2(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta)=\left(\frac{L}{2}\right)^2. \]
