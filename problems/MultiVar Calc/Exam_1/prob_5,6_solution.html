PART 1 — Problem Analysis no solving yet

Problem 5 — What it asks

  • Given \(P_0=(2,1,0)\), \(P_1=(1,0,1)\), \(P_2=(2,-1,1)\).
  • (a) Find the area of the triangle \(P_0P_1P_2\).
  • (b) Find the plane through \(P_0,P_1,P_2\).
  • (c) Intersect that plane with the line through \(S=(-1,0,0)\) parallel to \(\langle 1,1,1\rangle\).
Why vectors? A single cross product gives both (i) a plane normal and (ii) the parallelogram area—then triangle area is half.

Problem 6 — What it asks

  • Let \(\vec R(t)\) be position; define \(\vec V=\dot{\vec R}\), \(\vec A=\dot{\vec V}\).
  • (a) Compute \(\dfrac{d}{dt}(\vec R\cdot\vec R)\) using intrinsic vector notation.
  • (b) Prove: if \(|\vec R|\) is constant then \(\vec R\perp \vec V\).
  • (c) Still assuming \(|\vec R|\) constant, express \(\vec R\cdot\vec A\) using only \(\vec V\).
Physical picture: constant \(|\vec R|\) means motion on a sphere; velocity is tangent, acceleration has inward (centripetal) component.

Principles / laws used (and why they apply)

  • Cross product magnitude: \(\|\vec a\times \vec b\|\) gives area of the parallelogram spanned by two side vectors.
  • Plane point–normal form: with normal \(\vec n\), the plane is \(\vec n\cdot(\vec r-\vec r_0)=0\).
  • Line parametrization: \(\vec r(t)=\vec r_S+t\vec V\) for a line through \(S\) with direction \(\vec V\).
  • Dot-product differentiation: \(\dfrac{d}{dt}(\vec A\cdot\vec B)=\dot{\vec A}\cdot\vec B+\vec A\cdot\dot{\vec B}\).

Problem 5 Plan

1
Form side vectors from a common vertex: \(\vec a=P_1-P_0\), \(\vec b=P_2-P_0\).
2
Compute normal \(\vec n=\vec a\times\vec b\).
3
Area \(A=\tfrac12\|\vec n\|\).
4
Plane \(\vec n\cdot(\vec r-\vec r_0)=0\) using \(r_0=P_0\).
5
Line \(\vec r(t)=S+t\langle1,1,1\rangle\); substitute into plane to solve \(t\).
6
Intersection point from that \(t\); verify it satisfies plane and line.
Common mistakes: forgetting the \(\tfrac12\) for triangle area; mixing sign in cross product (sign flips direction but not magnitude).

Problem 6 Plan

1
Differentiate \(\vec R\cdot\vec R\) using dot-product product rule.
2
If \(|\vec R|\) constant, set derivative to zero \(\Rightarrow \vec R\cdot\vec V=0\).
3
Differentiate again \(\vec R\cdot\vec V=0\) and substitute \(\dot{\vec R}=\vec V\), \(\dot{\vec V}=\vec A\).
4
Solve for \(\vec R\cdot\vec A\) in terms of \(\vec V\cdot\vec V\).
Quick tip: \(\vec V\cdot\vec V = |\vec V|^2\) is a scalar; keep it distinct from the vector \(\vec V\).

PART 3 — Full Solution rigorous + beginner-friendly

Physical intuition (big picture)

  • Problem 5: two edges of the triangle define a parallelogram; the cross product measures its “oriented area”.
  • Problem 6: \(\vec R\cdot\vec R = |\vec R|^2\). If the radius is constant, motion is tangent, and acceleration must have an inward component related to speed.

Problem 5 — Geometry in 3D

Given points \(P_0=(2,1,0)\) \(P_1=(1,0,1)\) \(P_2=(2,-1,1)\)
\[ \vec a=P_1-P_0=(1-2,\;0-1,\;1-0)=(-1,-1,1) \] \[ \vec b=P_2-P_0=(2-2,\;-1-1,\;1-0)=(0,-2,1) \]
(a) Triangle area using \(\tfrac12\|\vec a\times\vec b\|\) expand

Compute the cross product \(\vec a\times\vec b\). Its magnitude is the parallelogram area; the triangle is half of that.

Cross product
\[ \vec a\times\vec b= \begin{vmatrix} \hat i & \hat j & \hat k\\ -1 & -1 & 1\\ 0 & -2 & 1 \end{vmatrix} \] \[ =\hat i\big((-1)(1)-(1)(-2)\big) -\hat j\big((-1)(1)-(1)(0)\big) +\hat k\big((-1)(-2)-(-1)(0)\big) \] \[ =\hat i(1) -\hat j(-1) + \hat k(2)=(1,1,2) \]
Magnitude and area
\[ \|\vec a\times\vec b\|=\sqrt{1^2+1^2+2^2}=\sqrt6 \] \[ A=\frac12\|\vec a\times\vec b\|=\frac{\sqrt6}{2} \]

Final (5a)

\(\boxed{A=\dfrac{\sqrt6}{2}}\)

  • Units: length\(^2\) (as expected for area).
  • Note: reversing cross product order flips sign but not magnitude, so area stays the same.
(b) Plane equation through the three points expand

The normal vector to the plane is \(\vec n=\vec a\times\vec b=(1,1,2)\). Use point–normal form with \(P_0\).

Point–normal form
\[ \vec n\cdot\big((x,y,z)-(2,1,0)\big)=0 \] \[ (1,1,2)\cdot(x-2,\;y-1,\;z)=0 \] \[ (x-2)+(y-1)+2z=0 \;\Rightarrow\; x+y+2z=3 \]

Final (5b)

\(\boxed{x+y+2z=3}\)

  • Sanity check: plugging \(P_0,P_1,P_2\) into \(x+y+2z\) gives 3 each time.
(c) Line–plane intersection expand

Parametrize the line through \(S=(-1,0,0)\) with direction \(\langle1,1,1\rangle\), then substitute into the plane.

Line parametrization
\[ \vec r(t)=(-1,0,0)+t(1,1,1) \] \[ x=-1+t,\quad y=t,\quad z=t \]
Substitute into plane \(x+y+2z=3\)
\[ (-1+t)+t+2t=3 \] \[ -1+4t=3\;\Rightarrow\;4t=4\;\Rightarrow\;t=1 \] \[ (x,y,z)=(0,1,1) \]

Final (5c)

\(\boxed{(0,1,1)}\)

  • Check: on plane: \(0+1+2(1)=3\). On line: \((-1,0,0)+1(1,1,1)=(0,1,1)\).

Problem 6 — Vector Kinematics Identities

Definitions \(\vec V=\dot{\vec R}\) \(\vec A=\dot{\vec V}\)

We avoid coordinates and use only intrinsic vector differentiation rules.

(a) Differentiate \(\vec R\cdot\vec R\) in vector form expand
Dot-product product rule
\[ \frac{d}{dt}(\vec R\cdot\vec R)=\dot{\vec R}\cdot\vec R+\vec R\cdot\dot{\vec R} \] \[ =2\,\vec R\cdot\dot{\vec R}=2\,\vec R\cdot\vec V \]

Final (6a)

\(\boxed{\dfrac{d}{dt}(\vec R\cdot\vec R)=2\,\vec R\cdot\vec V}\)

  • Units: LHS \(\sim L^2/T\), RHS \(\sim L(L/T)=L^2/T\).
(b) Constant \(|\vec R|\) ⇒ \(\vec R\perp\vec V\) expand

If \(|\vec R|\) is constant, then \(|\vec R|^2=\vec R\cdot\vec R\) is constant, so its time derivative is zero.

Set derivative to zero
\[ \frac{d}{dt}(\vec R\cdot\vec R)=0 \;\Rightarrow\; 2\,\vec R\cdot\vec V=0 \;\Rightarrow\; \vec R\cdot\vec V=0 \]

Final (6b)

\(\boxed{\vec R\cdot\vec V=0\;\Rightarrow\;\vec R\perp\vec V}\)

  • Physical meaning: velocity is tangent to the sphere \( |\vec R|=\text{const}\).
(c) Express \(\vec R\cdot\vec A\) using only \(\vec V\) expand

Differentiate \(\vec R\cdot\vec V=0\) and apply product rule.

Differentiate the orthogonality condition
\[ \vec R\cdot\vec V=0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{d}{dt}(\vec R\cdot\vec V)=0 \] \[ \dot{\vec R}\cdot\vec V + \vec R\cdot\dot{\vec V}=0 \] \[ \vec V\cdot\vec V + \vec R\cdot\vec A=0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \vec R\cdot\vec A = -\,\vec V\cdot\vec V = -|\vec V|^2 \]

Final (6c)

\(\boxed{\vec R\cdot\vec A=-\,\vec V\cdot\vec V=-|\vec V|^2}\)

  • Units: both sides \(\sim L^2/T^2\).
  • Limiting case: if \(\vec V=\vec 0\), then \(\vec R\cdot\vec A=0\).
  • Physical meaning: negative sign indicates inward radial acceleration component (centripetal behavior).
