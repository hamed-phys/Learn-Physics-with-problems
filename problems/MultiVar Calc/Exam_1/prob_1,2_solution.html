Problem 1 (unit cube in first octant)

  • Given: Unit cube aligned with axes, vertex at \(O=(0,0,0)\), edges length \(1\).
  • Unknowns: \(\overrightarrow{OQ}\) (space diagonal), \(\overrightarrow{OR}\) (to a face center), and \(\cos\theta\) between them.
  • Must find: Vector expressions in \(\hat{\mathbf i},\hat{\mathbf j},\hat{\mathbf k}\) and \(\cos\theta\).
  • Why these tools: Coordinates give direct components; dot product gives angles.

Problem 2 (motion in 3D)

  • Given: \(\vec R(t)=3\cos t\,\hat{\mathbf i}+3\sin t\,\hat{\mathbf j}+t\,\hat{\mathbf k}\).
  • Unknowns: \(\vec v(t)\) and speed \(|\vec v(t)|\).
  • Must find: Differentiate position to get velocity; magnitude for speed.
  • Why these tools: Velocity is derivative of position; speed is magnitude of velocity.
Approach options (click to expand)
  • Problem 1: (i) Coordinates + dot product (fast, unambiguous), (ii) symmetry-only geometry (elegant but risk of face confusion), (iii) edge-walk vector sums (similar to coordinates but less direct).
  • Problem 2: (i) Component differentiation (simplest), (ii) interpret as helix then compute constant speed (still needs derivatives), (iii) cylindrical coordinates (unnecessary overhead here).

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Plan → Avoid mistakes → Keep it clean
1

Set coordinates for the unit cube: \(O=(0,0,0)\), edges along \(x,y,z\). Tool: Cartesian setup

2

Locate \(Q\) as the opposite vertex: coordinates become \((1,1,1)\). Tool: cube geometry

3

Locate \(R\) as center of the right face \((y=1)\): midpoint in \(x\) and \(z\). Tool: face center

4

Write vectors \(\overrightarrow{OQ}\), \(\overrightarrow{OR}\) from coordinates. Tool: \(\overrightarrow{OP}=\langle x,y,z\rangle\)

5

Angle cosine via dot product: \(\cos\theta=\frac{\vec a\cdot\vec b}{\|\vec a\|\|\vec b\|}\). Tool: dot product

6

Differentiate \(\vec R(t)\) to get \(\vec v(t)\). Tool: component derivatives

7

Compute speed \(|\vec v|\) using \(\sqrt{\vec v\cdot\vec v}\) and simplify with \(\sin^2t+\cos^2t=1\). Tool: magnitude + trig identity

Common mistakes (avoid these)

  • Wrong face center: the figure places \(R\) on the right face \(\Rightarrow y=1\), not \(x=1\) or \(z=1\).
  • Speed vs velocity: velocity is a vector; speed is its magnitude (always \(\ge 0\)).
  • Missing \(\hat{\mathbf k}\) term: \(\frac{d}{dt}(t\,\hat{\mathbf k})=\hat{\mathbf k}\).
  • Forgetting \(\sin^2t+\cos^2t=1\) when simplifying speed.

Problem 1 — Unit Cube Vectors & Angle

Coordinates + dot product

Intuition

  • The space diagonal from the origin goes equally in \(x,y,z\): it “heads to” \((1,1,1)\).
  • The center of the right face has \(y=1\) and is halfway in \(x\) and \(z\): it “heads to” \((\tfrac12,1,\tfrac12)\).
  • The angle between them is measured by how strongly they align (dot product).

(a) Vector forms in \(\hat{\mathbf i},\hat{\mathbf j},\hat{\mathbf k}\)

\[ O=(0,0,0) \]

Since the cube is unit length and aligned with axes, coordinates range from 0 to 1.

\[ Q=(1,1,1) \quad\Rightarrow\quad \overrightarrow{OQ}=\langle 1,1,1\rangle = \hat{\mathbf i}+\hat{\mathbf j}+\hat{\mathbf k}. \]

The point \(R\) is at the center of the right face. The right face has constant \(y=1\), and its center is halfway in \(x\) and \(z\).

\[ R=\left(\tfrac12,\,1,\,\tfrac12\right) \quad\Rightarrow\quad \overrightarrow{OR}= \left\langle \tfrac12,\,1,\,\tfrac12\right\rangle =\tfrac12\hat{\mathbf i}+\hat{\mathbf j}+\tfrac12\hat{\mathbf k}. \]

Final (Problem 1a)

\[ \boxed{\overrightarrow{OQ}= \hat{\mathbf i}+\hat{\mathbf j}+\hat{\mathbf k}} \qquad \boxed{\overrightarrow{OR}= \tfrac12\hat{\mathbf i}+\hat{\mathbf j}+\tfrac12\hat{\mathbf k}} \]

(b) Cosine of the angle between \(\overrightarrow{OQ}\) and \(\overrightarrow{OR}\)

Use \(\cos\theta=\dfrac{\vec a\cdot\vec b}{\|\vec a\|\,\|\vec b\|}\) with \(\vec a=\overrightarrow{OQ}\) and \(\vec b=\overrightarrow{OR}\).

\[ \vec a=\langle 1,1,1\rangle, \qquad \vec b=\left\langle \tfrac12,1,\tfrac12\right\rangle. \]

Step 1 — Dot product

\[ \vec a\cdot\vec b = 1\cdot\tfrac12 + 1\cdot 1 + 1\cdot\tfrac12 = \tfrac12 + 1 + \tfrac12 = 2. \]

Step 2 — Magnitudes

\[ \|\vec a\|=\sqrt{1^2+1^2+1^2}=\sqrt{3}. \] \[ \|\vec b\| =\sqrt{\left(\tfrac12\right)^2+1^2+\left(\tfrac12\right)^2} =\sqrt{\tfrac14+1+\tfrac14} =\sqrt{\tfrac32}. \]

Step 3 — Compute \(\cos\theta\) and simplify

\[ \cos\theta =\frac{2}{\sqrt3\;\sqrt{\tfrac32}}. \] \[ \sqrt{\tfrac32}=\frac{\sqrt3}{\sqrt2} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \sqrt3\;\sqrt{\tfrac32}=\sqrt3\cdot\frac{\sqrt3}{\sqrt2}=\frac{3}{\sqrt2}. \] \[ \cos\theta=\frac{2}{3/\sqrt2}=\frac{2\sqrt2}{3}. \]

Final (Problem 1b)

\[ \boxed{\cos\theta=\frac{2\sqrt2}{3}} \]

Problem 2 — Velocity and Speed

Differentiate + magnitude

Intuition

  • \((3\cos t, 3\sin t)\) is circular motion of radius \(3\) in the \(xy\)-plane.
  • \(z=t\) rises at constant rate, so the path is a helix.
  • Constant circular rate + constant vertical rate suggests a constant total speed.

Given \(\vec R(t)=3\cos t\,\hat{\mathbf i}+3\sin t\,\hat{\mathbf j}+t\,\hat{\mathbf k}\).

Step 1 — Differentiate to get velocity

\[ \vec v(t)=\frac{d\vec R}{dt} =\frac{d}{dt}\big(3\cos t\big)\hat{\mathbf i} +\frac{d}{dt}\big(3\sin t\big)\hat{\mathbf j} +\frac{d}{dt}(t)\hat{\mathbf k}. \] \[ \frac{d}{dt}(3\cos t)=-3\sin t,\qquad \frac{d}{dt}(3\sin t)=3\cos t,\qquad \frac{d}{dt}(t)=1. \] \[ \Rightarrow\; \vec v(t)= -3\sin t\,\hat{\mathbf i}+3\cos t\,\hat{\mathbf j}+\hat{\mathbf k}. \]

Final (Velocity)

\[ \boxed{\vec v(t)= -3\sin t\,\hat{\mathbf i}+3\cos t\,\hat{\mathbf j}+\hat{\mathbf k}} \]

Step 2 — Compute speed as the magnitude

\[ |\vec v(t)|=\sqrt{\vec v\cdot\vec v} =\sqrt{(-3\sin t)^2+(3\cos t)^2+(1)^2}. \] \[ |\vec v(t)|=\sqrt{9\sin^2 t+9\cos^2 t+1} =\sqrt{9(\sin^2 t+\cos^2 t)+1}. \] \[ \sin^2 t+\cos^2 t=1 \;\Rightarrow\; |\vec v(t)|=\sqrt{9(1)+1}=\sqrt{10}. \]

Final (Speed)

\[ \boxed{|\vec v(t)|=\sqrt{10}\;\;\text{(constant)}} \]

Sanity Checks (units • limiting cases • interpretation)

Trust but verify
Problem 1 checks (cosine range + geometry)
  • Cosine range: \(\frac{2\sqrt2}{3}\approx 0.943\in[-1,1]\) ✓
  • Angle size: \(\cos\theta\) close to 1 means a small angle—reasonable since both vectors point generally outward into the first octant. ✓
  • Units: vectors here are in “cube-length units”; cosine is dimensionless. ✓
Problem 2 checks (spot check + physical meaning)
  • Spot check at \(t=0\): \(\vec v(0)=0\,\hat{\mathbf i}+3\,\hat{\mathbf j}+\hat{\mathbf k}\Rightarrow |\vec v(0)|=\sqrt{10}\). ✓
  • Interpretation: planar circular speed is \(3\); vertical speed is \(1\); total speed is \(\sqrt{3^2+1^2}=\sqrt{10}\). ✓
  • Units: if \(\vec R\) is in meters and \(t\) in seconds, then \(\vec v\) is m/s and \(|\vec v|\) is m/s. ✓
