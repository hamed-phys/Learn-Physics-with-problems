Double Integrals Change of Order Mass & Center of Mass Polar Coordinates

Fully Worked Solution: Regions of Integration & a Half-Annulus Lamina

This article solves two linked multivariable-calculus/physics-lamina problems: (1) interpreting and reversing a double integral, and (2) computing mass and reasoning about the x-coordinate of the center of mass on an upper half-annulus with nonuniform density.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Rewrite the problem (in my own words)

Problem 1

(a) Interpret \(\int_{0}^{1}\int_{x}^{2x} dy\,dx\) as a region \(R\) in the \(xy\)-plane and sketch it.
(b) Reverse the integration order to \(dx\,dy\); the new description must be split into two \(y\)-ranges.

Problem 2

(a) Find mass \(M\) of the upper half of the annulus \(1<x^2+y^2<9\) (\(y\ge 0\)) with \(\delta=\dfrac{y}{x^2+y^2}\).
(b) Write \(\bar x\) as an iterated integral and justify \(\bar x=0\) without evaluating.

Given, unknowns, and what must be found/proved

  • Problem 1 given: \(\displaystyle \int_{0}^{1}\int_{x}^{2x} 1\,dy\,dx\)
  • Problem 1 find: region \(R\) and the reversed-order integral(s) in \(dx\,dy\)
  • Problem 2 given: \(R=\{(x,y):1<x^2+y^2<9,\ y\ge 0\}\), \(\delta=\dfrac{y}{x^2+y^2}\)
  • Problem 2 find: mass \(M=\iint_R \delta\,dA\), and \(\bar x=\dfrac{1}{M}\iint_R x\,\delta\,dA\), then explain why \(\bar x=0\)

Relevant physical principles/laws (and why they apply)

  • Mass of a lamina: \(M=\iint_R \delta(x,y)\,dA\). We are given an areal density over a planar region.
  • Center of mass (x-coordinate): \(\bar x=\dfrac{1}{M}\iint_R x\,\delta(x,y)\,dA\). This is the definition via first moments.
  • Symmetry: If \(R\) is symmetric about the \(y\)-axis and \(\delta\) is unchanged under \(x\to -x\), then \(x\delta\) is odd and integrates to zero \(\Rightarrow \bar x=0\).

Possible approaches (compare briefly)

  • Problem 1: inequalities + sketch (best) vs. compute first (less direct for limits)
  • Problem 2: polar coordinates (best, circular region) vs. Cartesian (messy square-roots)
Chosen approach: Use geometry/inequalities to reverse the integral in Problem 1, and use polar coordinates in Problem 2 because the annulus and \(x^2+y^2\) naturally simplify in \((r,\theta)\).

PART 3 — Full Solution

Start with physical intuition

Problem 1: the integrand is \(1\), so the double integral equals the area of region \(R\).
Problem 2: mass is “density summed over area.” Because the region is circular and \(\delta\) contains \(x^2+y^2\), polar coordinates convert geometry and density into clean factors.

Problem 1

(a) Region of integration

The integral \[ \int_{0}^{1}\int_{x}^{2x} dy\,dx \] means the region \(R\) satisfies: \[ 0\le x\le 1,\qquad x\le y\le 2x. \] So \(R\) lies between the lines \(y=x\) (lower) and \(y=2x\) (upper) in the first quadrant, capped by the vertical line \(x=1\).

Key boundary points: \((0,0)\) from \(x=0\); and at \(x=1\), \(y\in[1,2]\) giving \((1,1)\) and \((1,2)\).
This forms a wedge/trapezoid-like region between \(y=x\) and \(y=2x\), cut off at \(x=1\).

(b) Reverse order to \(dx\,dy\) — two pieces

Start from the inequalities: \[ x\le y\le 2x. \] Solve for \(x\) in terms of \(y\): \[ x\le y \Rightarrow x\le y,\qquad y\le 2x \Rightarrow x\ge \frac{y}{2}. \] So for a fixed \(y\), the “natural” \(x\)-range is \(\dfrac{y}{2}\le x\le y\), but we must also keep \(0\le x\le 1\).

Determine the full \(y\)-range: along \(y=2x\) with \(0\le x\le 1\), we reach \(y=2\). Also \(y\ge 0\). Hence \(0\le y\le 2\).

Now split based on whether the cap \(x\le 1\) cuts off the upper limit \(x\le y\):

  • If \(0\le y\le 1\): then \(y\le 1\), so \(x\le y\) automatically stays \(\le 1\). Thus \(\frac{y}{2}\le x\le y\).
  • If \(1\le y\le 2\): then the bound \(x\le y\) would exceed \(1\), so the true upper bound becomes \(x\le 1\). Thus \(\frac{y}{2}\le x\le 1\).

Final (reversed-order) integral

\[ \boxed{ \int_{0}^{1}\int_{y/2}^{y} dx\,dy\;+\;\int_{1}^{2}\int_{y/2}^{1} dx\,dy } \]
Sanity check: Two pieces are necessary because the vertical boundary \(x=1\) only becomes relevant once \(y>1\).

Problem 2

(a) Mass \(M\) of the upper half-annulus

Region: \[ 1<x^2+y^2<9,\quad y\ge 0, \] density: \[ \delta(x,y)=\frac{y}{x^2+y^2}. \] Use polar coordinates \(x=r\cos\theta,\ y=r\sin\theta,\ dA=r\,dr\,d\theta,\ x^2+y^2=r^2.\)

Step 1: Convert the region to polar

\[ 1<r^2<9 \Rightarrow 1<r<3,\qquad y\ge 0 \Rightarrow 0\le\theta\le\pi. \] So: \[ 1\le r\le 3,\quad 0\le\theta\le\pi. \]

Step 2: Convert the density

\[ \delta=\frac{y}{x^2+y^2}=\frac{r\sin\theta}{r^2}=\frac{\sin\theta}{r}. \]

Step 3: Compute the mass integral

\[ M=\iint_R \delta\,dA =\int_{0}^{\pi}\int_{1}^{3}\left(\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\right)\,(r\,dr\,d\theta) =\int_{0}^{\pi}\int_{1}^{3}\sin\theta\,dr\,d\theta. \]

Evaluate the inner integral: \[ \int_{1}^{3}\sin\theta\,dr=(3-1)\sin\theta=2\sin\theta. \] Then: \[ M=\int_{0}^{\pi}2\sin\theta\,d\theta =2\left[-\cos\theta\right]_{0}^{\pi} =2\bigl(1-(-1)\bigr)=4. \]

Final mass

\[ \boxed{M=4} \]

Sanity checks (mass)

  • Units: \(M\) has units of mass if \(\delta\) is mass/area. (The formula gives mass in consistent units.)
  • Sign: on \(y\ge 0\), \(\delta\ge 0\), so \(M>0\). We got \(4>0\).
  • Limiting behavior: increasing outer radius would increase \(M\) linearly in \((r_{\max}-r_{\min})\) here, matching the simplified integrand’s \(r\)-independence after cancellation.

(b) Write \(\bar x\) as an iterated integral; explain why \(\bar x=0\)

By definition, \[ \bar x=\frac{1}{M}\iint_R x\,\delta\,dA. \] In polar: \(x=r\cos\theta,\ \delta=\frac{\sin\theta}{r},\ dA=r\,dr\,d\theta\). Therefore \[ x\,\delta\,dA=(r\cos\theta)\left(\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\right)(r\,dr\,d\theta) =r\cos\theta\sin\theta\,dr\,d\theta. \]

Iterated-integral expression for \(\bar x\)

\[ \boxed{ \bar x=\frac{1}{M}\int_{0}^{\pi}\int_{1}^{3} r\cos\theta\,\sin\theta \;dr\,d\theta } \]
Why \(\bar x=0\) (no evaluation): The region is symmetric about the \(y\)-axis: \((x,y)\in R \Rightarrow (-x,y)\in R\). The density \(\delta=\dfrac{y}{x^2+y^2}\) is unchanged by \(x\to -x\) (depends on \(x^2\)), but the factor \(x\) changes sign. So \(x\delta\) is odd in \(x\), and its integral over a left-right symmetric region cancels to zero. Hence \(\boxed{\bar x=0}\).

Physical interpretation

The density increases with \(y\) (heavier near the “top” of the semi-annulus), so the center of mass shifts upward (in \(y\)), but it remains exactly centered left-right, giving \(\bar x=0\).
