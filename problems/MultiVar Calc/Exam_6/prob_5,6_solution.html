Problem 5 — Flux of a Vector Field through the Boundary

Green’s Theorem (Flux Form)

Problem statement (clean rewrite)

  • \(R\) is the rectangle with vertices \((0,0),(1,0),(1,4),(0,4)\).
  • The boundary \(C\) consists of four edges \(C_1,C_2,C_3,C_4\) in order around the rectangle.
  • Vector field: \[ \mathbf F(x,y)=\big(xy+\sin x\cos y\big)\,\hat{\mathbf i}-\big(\cos x\sin y\big)\,\hat{\mathbf j}. \]
  • (a) Find the outward flux of \(\mathbf F\) across the closed curve \(C\).
  • (b) Compare flux through \(C_1\cup C_2\cup C_3\) with flux through all of \(C\).

What’s given / what’s unknown

  • Given: \(0\le x\le 1\), \(0\le y\le 4\), and \(\mathbf F=\langle P,Q\rangle\).
  • Unknowns: net outward flux \(\Phi_C\), and comparison statement in (b).
  • Goal: evaluate \(\displaystyle \Phi_C=\oint_C \mathbf F\cdot \mathbf n\,ds\).
Physics intuition: Flux is “net flow leaving the region.” Green’s theorem converts boundary flux into an area integral of divergence — the “source strength” inside \(R\).
Why Green’s theorem applies here
The boundary \(C\) is a simple, closed, positively oriented curve enclosing the region \(R\), and the field components are smooth. Therefore the flux form of Green’s theorem holds: \[ \oint_C \mathbf F\cdot \mathbf n\,ds=\iint_R\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}\right)\,dA. \]

5(a) Compute total outward flux \(\Phi_C\)

Divergence → Area Integral

  1. Identify components \(P(x,y)\) and \(Q(x,y)\)

    Write \(\mathbf F=\langle P,Q\rangle\): \[ P(x,y)=xy+\sin x\cos y,\qquad Q(x,y)=-(\cos x\sin y). \]

  2. Compute the 2D divergence \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf F=\partial P/\partial x+\partial Q/\partial y\)

    Differentiate carefully: \[ \frac{\partial P}{\partial x}=y+\cos x\cos y,\qquad \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}=-\cos x\cos y. \] The trig terms cancel, so \[ \nabla\cdot\mathbf F = y. \]

  3. Apply Green’s theorem (flux form)

    \[ \Phi_C=\oint_C \mathbf F\cdot \mathbf n\,ds =\iint_R (\nabla\cdot\mathbf F)\,dA =\iint_R y\,dA. \]

  4. Integrate over the rectangle \(0\le x\le 1,\;0\le y\le 4\)

    \[ \iint_R y\,dA =\int_{0}^{1}\int_{0}^{4} y\,dy\,dx =\int_{0}^{1}\left[\frac{y^2}{2}\right]_{0}^{4}dx =\int_{0}^{1} 8\,dx = 8. \]

Final (5a)

\[ \boxed{\Phi_C=\oint_C \mathbf F\cdot \mathbf n\,ds = 8.} \]

Units

Flux has the units of “field · length” in a line-flux setting; Green’s theorem preserves consistency because area integral of divergence matches boundary flux dimensions.

Limiting behavior

Since \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf F=y\ge 0\) on \(R\), net outflow must be positive. The result \(8>0\) agrees.

Interpretation

The divergence depends only on \(y\), meaning “source strength” increases with height in the rectangle. Integrating \(y\) over the area yields the total net outflux.

5(b) Compare flux through \(C_1+C_2+C_3\) with flux through \(C\)

Additivity + Edge Test
Flux over a piecewise boundary is additive: \[ \Phi_C=\Phi_{C_1}+\Phi_{C_2}+\Phi_{C_3}+\Phi_{C_4} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \Phi_{C_1+C_2+C_3}=\Phi_C-\Phi_{C_4}. \] So we only need the flux across the missing edge \(C_4\) (the left side \(x=0\)).

  1. Evaluate the field on the left edge \(x=0\)

    On \(C_4\), \(x=0\), so \[ \mathbf F(0,y)=\langle 0\cdot y+\sin0\cos y,\;-(\cos0\sin y)\rangle=\langle 0,\;-\sin y\rangle. \]

  2. Use outward normal direction on \(x=0\)

    The outward normal points to decreasing \(x\), so \(\mathbf n=\langle -1,0\rangle\). Then \[ \mathbf F(0,y)\cdot\mathbf n = \langle 0,-\sin y\rangle\cdot\langle -1,0\rangle=0. \] Hence \(\Phi_{C_4}=0\).

  3. Conclude the comparison

    \[ \Phi_{C_1+C_2+C_3}=\Phi_C-\Phi_{C_4}=8-0=8. \]

Final (5b)

\[ \boxed{\Phi_{C_1+C_2+C_3} = \Phi_C \text{ (they are equal, both }=8\text{)}.} \]

Problem 6 — Volume Enclosed by \(z=4\) and \(z=(2x-y)^2+(x+y-1)^2\)

Change of Variables + Polar

Problem statement (clean rewrite)

  • Top surface: \(z_{\text{top}}=4\).
  • Bottom surface: \(z_{\text{bot}}=(2x-y)^2+(x+y-1)^2\).
  • Find the volume where the plane lies above the surface.
  • Suggested: use a change of variables.
Geometric idea: The bottom is a “sum of squares” bowl. The inequality \((2x-y)^2+(x+y-1)^2\le 4\) becomes a circle after the right substitution.

Key formula

\[ V=\iint_D \big(z_{\text{top}}-z_{\text{bot}}\big)\,dA =\iint_D \left(4-\left[(2x-y)^2+(x+y-1)^2\right]\right)\,dA, \] where \[ D=\left\{(x,y): (2x-y)^2+(x+y-1)^2\le 4\right\}. \]

Direct integration in \((x,y)\) is messy because \(D\) is “tilted.” We rotate/transform it into a disk.

Full derivation

Jacobian Matters

  1. Find the footprint \(D\): where the plane is above the surface

    Enclosure requires \(4\ge (2x-y)^2+(x+y-1)^2\), so \[ D=\{(x,y): (2x-y)^2+(x+y-1)^2\le 4\}. \]

  2. Use the substitution suggested by the structure

    Let \[ u=2x-y,\qquad v=x+y-1. \] Then the bottom surface becomes \(z_{\text{bot}}=u^2+v^2\), and the region becomes \[ u^2+v^2\le 4, \] which is a disk of radius \(2\) in the \(uv\)-plane.

  3. Invert the transformation to compute the Jacobian

    From \(v=x+y-1\Rightarrow x+y=v+1\) and \(u=2x-y\), solve: \[ x=\frac{u+v+1}{3},\qquad y=\frac{-u+2v+2}{3}. \] Now compute \[ \left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right| = \begin{vmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial v}\\[4pt] \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} \frac13 & \frac13\\[4pt] -\frac13 & \frac23 \end{vmatrix} =\frac{1}{3}. \] Hence \(dA=\dfrac{1}{3}\,dudv\).

  4. Rewrite the volume integral in \((u,v)\)

    \[ V=\iint_{u^2+v^2\le 4}\Big(4-(u^2+v^2)\Big)\left(\frac13\right)\,dudv. \]

  5. Switch to polar coordinates in the \(uv\)-plane

    Let \(u=r\cos\theta,\ v=r\sin\theta\). Then \(u^2+v^2=r^2\) and \(dudv=r\,dr\,d\theta\). The disk \(u^2+v^2\le 4\) becomes \(0\le r\le 2,\ 0\le\theta\le 2\pi\). So \[ V=\frac13\int_{0}^{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2} (4-r^2)\,r\,dr\,d\theta. \]

  6. Evaluate the integral

    Compute the \(r\)-integral: \[ \int_{0}^{2} (4-r^2)\,r\,dr =\int_{0}^{2}(4r-r^3)\,dr =\left[2r^2-\frac{r^4}{4}\right]_{0}^{2} =8-4 =4. \] Then \[ V=\frac13\int_{0}^{2\pi}4\,d\theta =\frac13\cdot 8\pi =\frac{8\pi}{3}. \]

Final (Problem 6)

\[ \boxed{V=\frac{8\pi}{3}.} \]

Units

Volume is “height × area.” The integrand is \(4-r^2\) (a height) times \(dA\), so the result has volume units.

Limiting cases

If the plane were lowered to \(z=0\), there would be no enclosed volume (since the surface is \(\ge 0\)). With \(z=4\), a positive finite volume is expected.

Interpretation

In \((u,v)\), the bowl is radially symmetric: \(z=u^2+v^2\). The region is a disk, and the Jacobian scales the area by \(1/3\).

Quick “method map” (what to remember for exams)
  • Flux out of a closed curve: use Green’s theorem (flux form) \(\oint_C \mathbf F\cdot\mathbf n\,ds=\iint_R(\partial P/\partial x+\partial Q/\partial y)\,dA\).
  • When you see a sum of squares: try a substitution that turns it into \(u^2+v^2\), then use polar.
  • Never forget: Jacobian for area scaling, and the extra factor \(r\) in polar coordinates.
