PART 1 Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Conceptual + Setup

Rewrite the problems (in my words)

  • Problem 5: The surface \(S\) satisfies \(\;z=(x^2+y^2+z^2)^2\). (a) Show \(z\ge 0\) on \(S\). (b) Convert to spherical coordinates. (c) Use that form to set up a triple integral for the enclosed volume (do not evaluate).
  • Problem 6: For the patch of the surface \(z=xy\) above the disk \(x^2+y^2<1\), compute the upward flux of \(\mathbf{F}=\langle y,x,z\rangle\) by reducing to a double integral over the disk.

Given / Unknown / Must find

  • Given (P5): \(z=(x^2+y^2+z^2)^2\).
    Find: (a) prove \(z\ge 0\); (b) spherical equation; (c) correct volume integral bounds + integrand.
  • Given (P6): \(z=xy\), disk \(D: x^2+y^2<1\), \(\mathbf{F}=\langle y,x,z\rangle\), upward orientation.
    Find: \(\displaystyle \iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS\).

Relevant principles (and why)

  • Nonnegativity of squares: \((\cdot)^2\ge 0\) \(\Rightarrow\) RHS of P5 is \(\ge 0\).
  • Spherical coordinates: \(x^2+y^2+z^2=\rho^2\), \(z=\rho\cos\phi\) \(\Rightarrow\) P5 becomes algebraic in \(\rho,\phi\).
  • Flux for a graph surface: For \(z=g(x,y)\) upward, \(\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}=\iint_D \mathbf{F}(x,y,g)\cdot\langle -g_x,-g_y,1\rangle\,dA\). This is exactly what P6 requests.
  • Symmetry: Over a disk centered at the origin, \(\iint_D xy\,dA=0\), simplifying P6.

Approaches & best choice

  • P5: Spherical coordinates are best because \(x^2+y^2+z^2\) becomes \(\rho^2\).
  • P6: Use the graph-surface flux reduction (shortest, matches instructions). Parameterization is equivalent but more algebra; divergence theorem adds unnecessary closure steps.

PART 3 Full Solution

Intuition → Derivation → Checks

Problem 5

Physical intuition why it must be \(z\ge 0\)
The right-hand side \((x^2+y^2+z^2)^2\) is a square, so it can never be negative. Since the equation sets \(z\) equal to that square, the surface cannot dip below the plane \(z=0\).

(a) Show that \(S\) lies in \(z\ge 0\)

The surface \(S\) is defined by \[ z=(x^2+y^2+z^2)^2. \] For any real number \(u\), \(u^2\ge 0\). Let \(u=x^2+y^2+z^2\). Then \[ (x^2+y^2+z^2)^2 \ge 0. \] But on \(S\), this quantity equals \(z\), so \[ z \ge 0. \]

Result \[ \boxed{S \subseteq \{(x,y,z): z\ge 0\}.} \]

(b) Surface equation in spherical coordinates

In spherical coordinates \((\rho,\phi,\theta)\): \[ x^2+y^2+z^2=\rho^2,\qquad z=\rho\cos\phi,\qquad \rho\ge 0. \] Substitute into \(z=(x^2+y^2+z^2)^2\): \[ \rho\cos\phi = (\rho^2)^2=\rho^4. \] Rearranging: \[ \rho\cos\phi=\rho^4 \;\Longrightarrow\; \rho(\rho^3-\cos\phi)=0. \] So either \(\rho=0\) (the origin) or \[ \rho^3=\cos\phi \;\Longrightarrow\; \rho=(\cos\phi)^{1/3}. \] For \(\rho\) to be real and nonnegative we require \(\cos\phi\ge 0\Rightarrow \phi\in\left[0,\frac{\pi}{2}\right]\).

Spherical form \[ \boxed{\rho=(\cos\phi)^{1/3},\quad 0\le \phi\le \frac{\pi}{2},\quad 0\le \theta<2\pi\quad (\text{plus }\rho=0).} \]

(c) Iterated integral for enclosed volume (do not evaluate)

The spherical volume element is \[ dV=\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta. \] “Inside the surface” means \(0\le \rho \le (\cos\phi)^{1/3}\), with \(\phi\in[0,\pi/2]\) and \(\theta\in[0,2\pi)\). Therefore:

Volume setup \[ \boxed{ V=\int_{0}^{2\pi}\int_{0}^{\pi/2}\int_{0}^{(\cos\phi)^{1/3}} \rho^{2}\sin\phi \, d\rho\, d\phi\, d\theta.} \]
Sanity checks geometry consistency
  • Upper half-space: \(\phi\in[0,\pi/2]\) matches \(z=\rho\cos\phi\ge 0\).
  • Axis check: at \(\phi=0\), \(\rho=(\cos 0)^{1/3}=1\), so the surface reaches \(\rho=1\) along \(+z\).
  • Equator pinch: at \(\phi=\pi/2\), \(\cos\phi=0\Rightarrow \rho=0\); the surface meets the origin in the \(xy\)-plane.

Problem 6

Physical intuition sign + symmetry
We want the net “flow” of \(\mathbf{F}\) through the tilted surface \(z=xy\) with an upward normal. Over the symmetric unit disk, mixed terms like \(xy\) cancel out. The remaining contribution behaves like \(-\iint (x^2+y^2)\,dA\), which is negative—so the upward flux should be negative.

Step 1 — Write the surface as a graph over a region

The surface is the graph \[ z=g(x,y)=xy \] over the disk \[ D=\{(x,y): x^2+y^2<1\}. \]

Step 2 — Build the upward normal-area vector

Compute partial derivatives: \[ g_x=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xy)=y,\qquad g_y=\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(xy)=x. \] For an upward orientation, the surface element vector is \[ d\mathbf{S}=\langle -g_x,\,-g_y,\,1\rangle\,dA =\langle -y,\,-x,\,1\rangle\,dA. \]

Step 3 — Evaluate \(\mathbf{F}\) on the surface and take the dot product

Given \(\mathbf{F}=\langle y,\ x,\ z\rangle\), substitute \(z=xy\): \[ \mathbf{F}(x,y,xy)=\langle y,\ x,\ xy\rangle. \] The flux is \[ \iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} =\iint_D \mathbf{F}(x,y,xy)\cdot\langle -y,\,-x,\,1\rangle\,dA. \] Compute the dot product: \[ \langle y,\ x,\ xy\rangle\cdot\langle -y,\,-x,\,1\rangle =-y^2-x^2+xy. \] Hence \[ \iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} =\iint_D (xy-x^2-y^2)\,dA. \]

\[ \iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}=\iint_D (xy-x^2-y^2)\,dA. \]

Step 4 — Use symmetry and polar coordinates on the disk

By symmetry over the disk centered at the origin, \[ \iint_D xy\,dA=0. \] Therefore, \[ \iint_D (xy-x^2-y^2)\,dA =-\iint_D (x^2+y^2)\,dA. \] Convert to polar: \(x=r\cos\theta,\ y=r\sin\theta\), so \(x^2+y^2=r^2\) and \(dA=r\,dr\,d\theta\). Over the unit disk: \(0\le r<1,\ 0\le\theta<2\pi\). Then \[ \iint_D (x^2+y^2)\,dA =\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r^2(r\,dr\,d\theta) =\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r^3\,dr\,d\theta. \] Evaluate the integrals: \[ \int_0^1 r^3\,dr=\left[\frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^1=\frac14,\qquad \int_0^{2\pi} d\theta = 2\pi, \] so \[ \iint_D (x^2+y^2)\,dA = 2\pi\cdot\frac14=\frac{\pi}{2}. \] Hence the flux is \[ \iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}=-\frac{\pi}{2}. \]

Flux \[ \boxed{\Phi_{\text{up}}=\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS=-\frac{\pi}{2}.} \]
Sanity checks units + sign
  • Sign: Negative flux matches the dominant \(-x^2-y^2\) contribution in the integrand for an upward normal.
  • Symmetry: The \(xy\) term cancels over the disk because it is odd under \(x\mapsto -x\) (or \(y\mapsto -y\)).
  • Scaling: If the disk radius were \(R\), the flux would scale like \(-R^4\), tending to 0 as the patch shrinks—physically reasonable.
