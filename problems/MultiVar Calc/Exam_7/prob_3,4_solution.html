3 Problem 3 — Spherical Cap, Boundary Circle, and Stokes’ Theorem

Let \(S\) be the part of the spherical surface \(x^2+y^2+z^2=2\) lying in \(z>1\). Orient \(S\) upwards and give its bounding circle \(C\) (in \(z=1\)) the compatible orientation.

(a) Parametrize \(C\) and evaluate the line integral \[ I=\oint_C xz\,dx + y\,dy + y\,dz. \] (b) Compute \(\nabla\times\mathbf F\) for \(\mathbf F=xz\,\mathbf i + y\,\mathbf j + y\,\mathbf k\). (c) Write a flux integral through \(S\) computable using the value of \(I\).

P1 Problem 3 — PART 1: Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

We identify geometry, orientations, the implied vector field, and the theorem that links the boundary integral to a surface flux.

Restatement (in my words)

  • Take the spherical cap of radius \(\sqrt{2}\) above the plane \(z=1\).
  • The boundary is the circle where the sphere cuts the plane \(z=1\).
  • Compute a closed line integral around that circle with the correct orientation.
  • Compute the curl of the vector field.
  • Write the corresponding curl-flux integral across the cap using Stokes’ theorem.

Given / Unknown / Find

  • Given: \(x^2+y^2+z^2=2\), region \(z>1\), upward orientation.
  • Boundary: \(C \subset \{z=1\}\).
  • Implied field: \(\mathbf F=(P,Q,R)=(xz,\;y,\;y)\).
  • Find: (a) \(I\) via parametrization of \(C\), (b) \(\nabla\times\mathbf F\), (c) flux form over \(S\).

Relevant laws & why

  • Stokes’ theorem: connects circulation \(\oint_C \mathbf F\cdot d\mathbf r\) to curl flux \(\iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf F)\cdot\mathbf n\,dS\), perfect for parts (a)–(c).
  • Orientation rule: upward normal on \(S\) implies the boundary is CCW when viewed from \(+z\) (right-hand rule).

Approaches (compare)

  • Direct parametrization of \(C\): fastest here because \(z\) is constant on \(C\Rightarrow dz=0\).
  • Stokes first: compute curl then integrate over the cap; valid but may require more surface geometry.
  • Exact differential trick: sometimes \(\oint d(\cdot)=0\); indeed \(x\,dx+y\,dy\) hints at this structure.
  • Best choice: direct parametrization for (a), then Stokes to express (c) cleanly.

P3 Problem 3 — PART 3: Full Solution

★
Physical intuition

The closed line integral measures the circulation (net tangential “swirl”) of \(\mathbf F\) around \(C\). If the tangential contributions cancel over a full loop, the circulation is zero—then Stokes says the net curl flux through the cap must also be zero.

(a) Parametrize \(C\) and evaluate \(I\)

Step 1 (Find \(C\)): Intersect the sphere with \(z=1\):

\[ x^2+y^2+z^2=2,\quad z=1 \;\Rightarrow\; x^2+y^2+1=2 \;\Rightarrow\; x^2+y^2=1. \]

So \(C\) is the circle of radius \(1\) in the plane \(z=1\).

Step 2 (Orientation): “Upward” orientation means the compatible boundary direction is counterclockwise when viewed from \(+z\) (right-hand rule).

Step 3 (Parametrization):

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle \cos t,\ \sin t,\ 1\rangle,\qquad 0\le t\le 2\pi. \] \[ dx=-\sin t\,dt,\quad dy=\cos t\,dt,\quad dz=0. \]

Step 4 (Evaluate \(I\)): Start from \[ I=\oint_C xz\,dx+y\,dy+y\,dz. \] On \(C\), \(z=1\Rightarrow xz=x\), and \(dz=0\Rightarrow y\,dz=0\). Therefore \[ I=\oint_C x\,dx+y\,dy. \]

\[ x=\cos t,\ y=\sin t,\ dx=-\sin t\,dt,\ dy=\cos t\,dt \] \[ I=\int_0^{2\pi}\big(\cos t(-\sin t)+\sin t(\cos t)\big)\,dt =\int_0^{2\pi} ( -\sin t\cos t+\sin t\cos t )\,dt =\int_0^{2\pi}0\,dt=0. \]

Result (a)

\[ \boxed{I=0.} \]

(b) Compute \(\nabla\times\mathbf F\) for \(\mathbf F=xz\,\mathbf i+y\,\mathbf j+y\,\mathbf k\)

Identify components \( \mathbf F=(P,Q,R) \) with \[ P=xz,\quad Q=y,\quad R=y. \] Use the curl formula:

\[ \nabla\times\mathbf F= \left\langle \frac{\partial R}{\partial y}-\frac{\partial Q}{\partial z},\ \frac{\partial P}{\partial z}-\frac{\partial R}{\partial x},\ \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right\rangle. \]

Compute each component carefully:

\[ \frac{\partial R}{\partial y}= \frac{\partial (y)}{\partial y}=1,\qquad \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}= \frac{\partial (y)}{\partial z}=0 \;\Rightarrow\; (\cdot)_x=1. \] \[ \frac{\partial P}{\partial z}= \frac{\partial (xz)}{\partial z}=x,\qquad \frac{\partial R}{\partial x}= \frac{\partial (y)}{\partial x}=0 \;\Rightarrow\; (\cdot)_y=x. \] \[ \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}=0,\qquad \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}=0 \;\Rightarrow\; (\cdot)_z=0. \]

Result (b)

\[ \boxed{\nabla\times\mathbf F=\langle 1,\ x,\ 0\rangle.} \]

(c) Write a flux integral through \(S\) computable using \(I\)

By Stokes’ theorem (with compatible orientation): \[ \oint_C \mathbf F\cdot d\mathbf r = \iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf F)\cdot \mathbf n\,dS. \] Since \(\oint_C \mathbf F\cdot d\mathbf r = I\) and \(\nabla\times\mathbf F=\langle 1,x,0\rangle\), the flux integral is:

\[ \iint_S \langle 1,\ x,\ 0\rangle\cdot \mathbf n\,dS = I. \] Using part (a), \(I=0\), so: \[ \iint_S \langle 1,\ x,\ 0\rangle\cdot \mathbf n\,dS = 0. \]

Result (c)

\[ \boxed{\iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf F)\cdot \mathbf n\,dS =\iint_S \langle 1,\ x,\ 0\rangle\cdot \mathbf n\,dS =0.} \]

Sanity checks (Problem 3)

  • Units: If \(x,y,z\) have length units, then \(dx,dy,dz\) also length → each term like \(xz\,dx\) scales like \(L^3\). Consistent.
  • Orientation: Reversing \(C\) changes the sign of \(I\), but \(0\) stays \(0\) → robust result.
  • Interpretation: Zero circulation means no net “swirl” of \(\mathbf F\) around \(C\), so net curl-flux through the cap is also zero (Stokes).

4 Problem 4 — Divergence Theorem Flux Through \(x^4+y^4+z^4=1\)

Use the divergence theorem to compute the outward flux of \(\mathbf F=\mathbf i+\mathbf j+\mathbf k\) across the closed surface \(x^4+y^4+z^4=1\).

P1 Problem 4 — PART 1: Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Restatement

  • Compute \(\displaystyle \iint_{\partial V}\mathbf F\cdot\mathbf n\,dS\) for the closed surface \(x^4+y^4+z^4=1\).
  • Use the divergence theorem to replace a surface integral by a volume integral.

Given / Unknown / Find

  • Given: \(\mathbf F=(1,1,1)\), closed surface \(x^4+y^4+z^4=1\).
  • Unknown: net outward flux through the surface.
  • Find: numeric flux value via \(\iiint_V (\nabla\cdot\mathbf F)\,dV\).

Relevant law & why

  • Divergence theorem: converts outward flux over a closed surface into a volume integral of \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf F\) over the enclosed region \(V\). Ideal since \(\mathbf F\) is constant.

Tool: \(\nabla\cdot(1,1,1)=\partial_x 1+\partial_y 1+\partial_z 1\).

Common mistake

Overthinking the geometry of \(x^4+y^4+z^4=1\). The divergence theorem makes the shape irrelevant if \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf F=0\).

P3 Problem 4 — PART 3: Full Solution

★
Physical intuition

A constant field has no built-in sources or sinks. If \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf F=0\), the net flow out of any closed surface is zero: whatever enters must leave somewhere else.

We want the outward flux across \(\partial V:\ x^4+y^4+z^4=1\):

\[ \iint_{\partial V}\mathbf F\cdot\mathbf n\,dS. \]

Apply the divergence theorem:

\[ \iint_{\partial V}\mathbf F\cdot\mathbf n\,dS = \iiint_V (\nabla\cdot\mathbf F)\,dV. \]

Compute the divergence for \(\mathbf F=(1,1,1)\):

\[ \nabla\cdot\mathbf F= \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(1)+\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(1)+\frac{\partial}{\partial z}(1)=0+0+0=0. \]

Therefore the volume integral is:

\[ \iiint_V 0\,dV=0. \]

Result (Problem 4)

\[ \boxed{\iint_{\partial V}\mathbf F\cdot\mathbf n\,dS=0.} \]

Sanity checks (Problem 4)

  • Units: Flux has units of \([\mathbf F]\cdot[\text{area}]\). Divergence has \([\mathbf F]/[\text{length}]\). Zero divergence implies zero net flux.
  • Limiting cases: Same answer for a sphere, cube, or any smooth closed shape.
  • Interpretation: No net “source strength” inside the volume ⇒ no net outward flow.
