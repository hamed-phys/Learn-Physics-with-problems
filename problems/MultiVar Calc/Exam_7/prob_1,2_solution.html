Quick Definitions

Conservative field: \(\mathbf{F}=\nabla\phi\) for some scalar potential \(\phi\). In simply connected regions, \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=\mathbf{0}\Rightarrow\) conservative.
Divergence theorem: \[ \iint_{\partial V}\mathbf{F}\cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS=\iiint_V (\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F})\,dV. \] Flux out equals total “source strength” inside.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Setup & Concepts

Problem 1 (15)

Given: \[ \mathbf{F}=\langle e^x yz,\; e^x z+2yz,\; e^x y+y^2+1\rangle, \qquad \mathbf{G}=\langle y,\;x,\;y\rangle. \]

  • (a) Show \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative.
  • (b) Find a potential \(\phi\) with \(\nabla\phi=\mathbf{F}\).
  • (c) Show \(\mathbf{G}\) is not conservative.
Key idea: In \(\mathbb{R}^3\) (simply connected), the fastest test is the curl: \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=\mathbf{0}\Rightarrow\) conservative. If curl \(\neq \mathbf{0}\), it’s not conservative.

Problem 2 (20)

Let \(S\) be the part of the sphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=4\) lying in \(x^2+y^2>1\) (outside the cylinder of radius 1 about the \(z\)-axis). Vector field: \[ \mathbf{F}=\langle y,\;-x,\;z\rangle. \]

  • (a) Compute outward flux through \(S\).
  • (b) Show flux through any part of the cylinder is zero.
  • (c) Use divergence theorem to compute the volume between \(S\) and the cylinder.
Why this is “physics-flavored”: Flux is like flow rate; divergence is local source density. If \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}=1\), the total outward flux equals the enclosed volume (up to units/scaling).
Approaches (compare quickly)
  • Problem 1: Curl test + integrate to find \(\phi\) (fast + systematic).
  • Problem 2: Compute \(\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{n}\) on the sphere (simplifies), show cylinder flux is 0, then use divergence theorem for volume.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Plan (no algebra)
  1. Goal: Prove \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative.
    Tool: Compute \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}\) and check it equals \(\mathbf{0}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\).
  2. Goal: Start constructing potential \(\phi\).
    Tool: Integrate \(P=\phi_x\) with respect to \(x\), add \(C(y,z)\).
  3. Goal: Determine \(C(y,z)\).
    Tool: Differentiate candidate \(\phi\) w.r.t. \(y\), match to \(Q\).
  4. Goal: Finish the potential completely.
    Tool: Differentiate w.r.t. \(z\), match to \(R\), solve for remaining function \(D(z)\).
  5. Goal: Show \(\mathbf{G}\) is not conservative.
    Tool: Compute \(\nabla\times\mathbf{G}\); any nonzero component proves non-conservative.
  6. Goal: Compute flux through the spherical patch \(S\).
    Tool: On sphere \(r=2\), use \(\hat{n}=\frac{\langle x,y,z\rangle}{2}\) and evaluate \(\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{n}\); convert bounds using \(x^2+y^2>1\).
  7. Goal: Show cylinder flux is zero.
    Tool: Parametrize cylinder \(x=\cos\phi, y=\sin\phi\); outward normal is radial \(\langle\cos\phi,\sin\phi,0\rangle\); compute dot product.
  8. Goal: Get volume by divergence theorem.
    Tool: Compute \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}\) and use flux = volume when divergence is 1 and cylinder flux is 0.
Common mistakes:
  • Forgetting the “constant of integration” can depend on remaining variables (e.g., \(C(y,z)\)).
  • Using the position vector instead of the unit normal on the sphere.
  • Missing the correct angular bounds for \(x^2+y^2>1\) on the sphere \(r=2\).

PART 3 — Full Solution

Derivations
Physical intuition: A conservative field has no local “swirl” (curl \(=0\))—like a force derived from potential energy. Flux measures outward “flow” through a surface; divergence measures flow created per unit volume (source density).

Problem 1

Conservative Fields

(a) Show that \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative

\[ \mathbf F=\langle P,Q,R\rangle =\langle e^x yz,\; e^x z+2yz,\; e^x y+y^2+1\rangle. \] \[ \nabla\times \mathbf F= \left\langle \frac{\partial R}{\partial y}-\frac{\partial Q}{\partial z},\; \frac{\partial P}{\partial z}-\frac{\partial R}{\partial x},\; \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right\rangle. \]

Compute each component:

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial R}{\partial y}=\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(e^x y+y^2+1)=e^x+2y\), \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}=\frac{\partial}{\partial z}(e^x z+2yz)=e^x+2y\). So \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial R}{\partial y}-\frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}=0.\)
  • \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial P}{\partial z}=\frac{\partial}{\partial z}(e^x yz)=e^x y\), \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial R}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(e^x y+y^2+1)=e^x y\). So \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial P}{\partial z}-\frac{\partial R}{\partial x}=0.\)
  • \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(e^x z+2yz)=e^x z\), \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}=\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(e^x yz)=e^x z\). So \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}=0.\)
Conclusion
\[ \nabla\times\mathbf F=\mathbf 0. \] Since \(\mathbf F\) is defined everywhere in \(\mathbb R^3\) (simply connected), \[ \boxed{\mathbf F\ \text{is conservative.}} \]

(b) Find a potential \(\phi\) for \(\mathbf{F}\)

We want \(\nabla\phi=\mathbf F\), i.e.

\[ \phi_x=e^x yz,\qquad \phi_y=e^x z+2yz,\qquad \phi_z=e^x y+y^2+1. \]

Step 1: Integrate \(\phi_x\) with respect to \(x\):

\[ \phi(x,y,z)=\int e^x yz\,dx = e^x yz + C(y,z). \]

Step 2: Differentiate this \(\phi\) with respect to \(y\) and match \(\phi_y\) to the given \(Q\):

\[ \phi_y = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(e^x yz + C(y,z))=e^x z + C_y(y,z). \] Set \(\phi_y=Q=e^x z+2yz\) to get: \[ C_y(y,z)=2yz. \]

Step 3: Integrate \(C_y=2yz\) with respect to \(y\):

\[ C(y,z)=\int 2yz\,dy = y^2 z + D(z). \]

Step 4: Now match \(\phi_z\) with \(R\):

\[ \phi = e^x yz + y^2 z + D(z) \quad\Rightarrow\quad \phi_z = e^x y + y^2 + D'(z). \] Set \(\phi_z=R=e^x y+y^2+1\): \[ D'(z)=1\Rightarrow D(z)=z+C_0. \]
Potential Function
\[ \boxed{\phi(x,y,z)=e^x yz+y^2 z+z+C_0.} \]

(c) Show that \(\mathbf{G}=\langle y,x,y\rangle\) is not conservative

Compute the curl. Let \(\mathbf G=\langle P,Q,R\rangle=\langle y,x,y\rangle\).

\[ \nabla\times \mathbf G= \left\langle \frac{\partial R}{\partial y}-\frac{\partial Q}{\partial z},\; \frac{\partial P}{\partial z}-\frac{\partial R}{\partial x},\; \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right\rangle. \]
  • \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial R}{\partial y}=1\), \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}=0\) ⇒ first component \(=1\).
  • \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial P}{\partial z}=0\), \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial R}{\partial x}=0\) ⇒ second component \(=0\).
  • \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}=1\), \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}=1\) ⇒ third component \(=0\).
Non-Conservative Verdict
\[ \nabla\times\mathbf G=\langle 1,0,0\rangle\neq \mathbf 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \boxed{\mathbf G\ \text{is not conservative.}} \]

Problem 2

Flux & Volume

Sphere: \(x^2+y^2+z^2=4\) (radius \(2\)). Cylinder: \(x^2+y^2=1\) (radius \(1\)). Surface \(S\): part of the sphere with \(x^2+y^2>1\). Field: \[ \mathbf F=\langle y,\;-x,\;z\rangle. \]

(a) Compute the outward flux through \(S\)

Step 1: Outward unit normal on the sphere \(r=2\) is

\[ \hat{\mathbf n}=\frac{\langle x,y,z\rangle}{2}. \]

Step 2: Dot with the field:

\[ \mathbf F\cdot \hat{\mathbf n} =\langle y,-x,z\rangle\cdot\frac{\langle x,y,z\rangle}{2} =\frac{yx-xy+z^2}{2} =\frac{z^2}{2}. \]

Step 3: Convert to spherical coordinates on \(r=2\): \(z=2\cos\theta\), and

\[ dS=r^2\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi = 4\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi. \]

Step 4: Translate region condition \(x^2+y^2>1\). On the sphere:

\[ x^2+y^2=(2\sin\theta)^2=4\sin^2\theta>1 \Rightarrow \sin\theta>\frac12 \Rightarrow \theta\in\left[\frac{\pi}{6},\frac{5\pi}{6}\right], \quad \phi\in[0,2\pi]. \]

Step 5: Flux integral:

\[ \iint_S \mathbf F\cdot \hat{\mathbf n}\,dS = \int_{0}^{2\pi}\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6} \frac{(2\cos\theta)^2}{2}\,\big(4\sin\theta\big)\,d\theta\,d\phi. \] Simplify the integrand: \[ \frac{4\cos^2\theta}{2}\cdot 4\sin\theta =8\cos^2\theta\sin\theta. \] So: \[ =\int_{0}^{2\pi}\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6} 8\cos^2\theta\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi =16\pi\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}\cos^2\theta\sin\theta\,d\theta. \] Let \(u=\cos\theta,\;du=-\sin\theta\,d\theta\): \[ 16\pi\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}\cos^2\theta\sin\theta\,d\theta = 16\pi\int_{-\sqrt3/2}^{\sqrt3/2} u^2\,du. \] Compute: \[ \int_{-a}^{a}u^2\,du=\frac{2a^3}{3},\quad a=\frac{\sqrt3}{2} \Rightarrow \int_{-\sqrt3/2}^{\sqrt3/2}u^2\,du=\frac{\sqrt3}{4}. \] Therefore: \[ \boxed{\iint_S \mathbf F\cdot \hat{\mathbf n}\,dS = 16\pi\cdot\frac{\sqrt3}{4}=4\sqrt3\,\pi.} \]

(b) Show flux through any part of the cylinder is zero

Parametrize the cylinder \(x^2+y^2=1\) by \(x=\cos\phi\), \(y=\sin\phi\). Its outward unit normal is radial:

\[ \hat{\mathbf n}_{\text{cyl}}=\langle \cos\phi,\sin\phi,0\rangle. \] On the cylinder: \[ \mathbf F=\langle y,-x,z\rangle=\langle \sin\phi,-\cos\phi,z\rangle. \] Dot product: \[ \mathbf F\cdot \hat{\mathbf n}_{\text{cyl}} =\sin\phi\cos\phi + (-\cos\phi)\sin\phi + z\cdot 0 =0. \]
Cylinder Flux
\[ \boxed{\text{Flux through any cylindrical surface patch} = 0.} \]

(c) Using divergence theorem, compute the volume between \(S\) and the cylinder

Compute divergence:

\[ \nabla\cdot\mathbf F= \frac{\partial y}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial (-x)}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial z} = 0+0+1=1. \]

Let \(V\) be the region inside the sphere and outside the cylinder. Its boundary \(\partial V\) consists of the spherical piece \(S\) and a cylindrical piece \(C\). Divergence theorem gives:

\[ \iint_{\partial V}\mathbf F\cdot \hat{\mathbf n}\,dS = \iiint_V (\nabla\cdot\mathbf F)\,dV = \iiint_V 1\,dV = \mathrm{Vol}(V). \] Since \(\iint_C \mathbf F\cdot\hat{\mathbf n}\,dS=0\) from part (b), \[ \mathrm{Vol}(V)=\iint_S \mathbf F\cdot \hat{\mathbf n}\,dS. \] Using part (a): \[ \boxed{\mathrm{Vol}(V)=4\sqrt3\,\pi.} \]

Final Results (boxed)

Answers
Problem 1
\[ \boxed{\nabla\times\mathbf F=\mathbf 0\ \Rightarrow\ \mathbf F\ \text{is conservative}} \] \[ \boxed{\phi(x,y,z)=e^x yz+y^2 z+z+C_0} \] \[ \boxed{\nabla\times\mathbf G=\langle 1,0,0\rangle\neq \mathbf 0\ \Rightarrow\ \mathbf G\ \text{not conservative}} \]
Problem 2
\[ \boxed{\iint_S \mathbf F\cdot \hat{\mathbf n}\,dS=4\sqrt3\,\pi} \] \[ \boxed{\text{Cylinder flux}=0} \] \[ \boxed{\mathrm{Vol}(\text{region})=4\sqrt3\,\pi} \]

Sanity Checks & Physical Interpretation

Quality Control
Units (conceptual): These are math fields; if interpreted physically, flux has units of field × area. Here \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf F=1\) acts like constant source density, so total flux equals enclosed volume (in consistent units).
Limiting/geometry check: The full sphere volume is \(\frac{32}{3}\pi\approx 33.51\). We remove a cylindrical “core,” so the remaining volume must be smaller. Our result \(4\sqrt3\pi\approx 21.77\) fits that expectation.
Why the spherical flux simplifies beautifully

On the sphere, the unit normal is proportional to the position vector. The tangential “swirl” part of the field \(\langle y,-x,0\rangle\) is orthogonal to the radial direction \(\langle x,y,0\rangle\), so it contributes zero to \(\mathbf F\cdot \hat n\). Only the \(z\)-component contributes, yielding \(\mathbf F\cdot \hat n=z^2/2\).
