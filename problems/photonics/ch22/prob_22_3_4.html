Quick Summary

  • What this problem is about: Find the quantities that stay constant during χ(3) four-wave mixing (FWM) as power transfers among four optical waves.
  • Key physics idea: In a lossless parametric interaction, the nonlinear polarization redistributes energy between waves but does not create/destroy total energy; additionally, photons are exchanged in fixed ratios set by the frequencies.
  • Governing equations: Coupled-mode (slowly-varying envelope) equations for the complex wave amplitudes Aj(z) with phase mismatch Δk.
  • Manley–Rowe relations: The photon flux changes satisfy (1/ω1) dP1/dz = (1/ω2) dP2/dz = −(1/ω3) dP3/dz = −(1/ω4) dP4/dz for the process ω1+ω2→ω3+ω4 (signs depend on which waves are “inputs” vs “outputs”).
  • Energy conservation: Because ω1+ω2=ω3+ω4, the total power satisfies d(P1+P2+P3+P4)/dz = 0 in the lossless steady-state model.
  • Physical meaning: FWM converts photons: each “event” annihilates one photon at ω1 and one at ω2, creating one at ω3 and one at ω4.
  • Output: Fully symbolic invariants + how to use them to sanity-check any FWM solution.
Final deliverable
A clean derivation of (i) energy conservation and (ii) photon-number (photon-flux) conservation laws for four-wave mixing (Manley–Rowe relations), expressed in terms of optical powers or intensities.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 What is four-wave mixing?

Four-wave mixing (FWM) is a third-order nonlinear optical process (χ(3)) where four monochromatic waves exchange energy through the medium’s nonlinear polarization. In its most common nondegenerate form, two “input” waves at frequencies ω1, ω2 generate (or amplify) two “output” waves at ω3, ω4 such that: ω1 + ω2 = ω3 + ω4.

0.2 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • ωj — angular frequency of wave j (rad/s).
  • kj — propagation constant (rad/m). In a simple medium, kj=n(ωj)ωj/c.
  • Δk — phase mismatch (rad/m). For the process ω1+ω2→ω3+ω4: Δk = k1 + k2 − k3 − k4.
  • Aj(z) — slowly varying complex amplitude (units depend on normalization).
  • Pj(z) — optical power carried by wave j (W). Often Pj ∝ |Aj|2.
  • Φj(z) — photon flux (photons/s), Φj=Pj/(ħωj).
  • χ(3) — third-order susceptibility (SI: m2/V2 in one convention; tensor in general).

0.3 Physical meaning of key quantities

  • Complex amplitude Aj: contains both magnitude (related to power) and phase (crucial for constructive vs destructive energy transfer).
  • Phase mismatch Δk: measures how well the nonlinear polarization “stays in step” with the generated waves. Large |Δk| causes the energy exchange to average out.
  • Photon flux Φj: the most natural variable for Manley–Rowe laws, because FWM is literally photon conversion subject to frequency constraints.
Validity conditions (assumptions)
  • Lossless medium (no absorption or gain other than the parametric exchange).
  • Continuous-wave (or quasi-CW) / steady-state along z so a 1D propagation model makes sense.
  • Slowly varying envelope approximation (SVEA): Aj changes slowly over an optical wavelength.
  • Single spatial mode per wave (or fixed effective area so power ∝ field intensity).
  • Neglect higher-order nonlinearities and strong dispersion effects beyond Δk (for the conservation derivation).
Common models/approximations (why used)
  • Coupled-mode equations: compress Maxwell’s equations + nonlinear polarization into simple ODEs for Aj(z).
  • Perfect phase matching: Δk≈0 maximizes net conversion; but conservation laws hold even when Δk≠0.
  • Equal coupling coefficients (pedagogical): keeps algebra transparent. The invariants do not depend on the specific value of the coupling constant, only on the symmetric structure of the interaction.

0.4 Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • Photon bookkeeping: If one photon at ω1 and one photon at ω2 disappear, then one photon at ω3 and one at ω4 appear. So photon fluxes must change in equal-and-opposite pairs.
  • Energy check: The energy change per “event” is ħ(ω3+ω4−ω1−ω2), which is zero if the frequency sum matches. That is why the total optical power can stay constant.
  • Role of phases: Even if conservation allows conversion, the direction and rate depend on relative phases: a phase shift can reverse energy flow (parametric amplification vs deamplification).
Deeper note: why “photon number” is subtle, yet Manley–Rowe still works

Strict “photon number” is not always conserved in nonlinear media in a fundamental quantum sense because the medium can exchange energy. However, in an ideal lossless parametric process with a classical undepleted medium (no excitations created), the photon flux relations emerge as robust invariants of the coupled-mode equations. They are the classical shadow of underlying quantum selection rules for the interaction Hamiltonian.

0.5 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Sign conventions: which frequencies are “inputs” vs “outputs” determines plus/minus signs in the Manley–Rowe relations.
  • Normalization: |Aj|2 is not automatically “power” unless you define it so. Always translate to physical power Pj (W) before stating conservation laws.
  • Loss/gain: absorption, linear gain, Raman effects, or free-carrier loss break the invariants.
  • Multiple modes: if power leaks to other modes/frequencies, conservation within the chosen four waves no longer holds.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

1.1 Restate the problem

Derive the invariants (conserved quantities) of four-wave mixing that express conservation of energy and conservation of photon number, known as the Manley–Rowe relations. In practice, we want equations that relate the rates of change (with propagation distance z) of the powers (or intensities) of four interacting waves.

1.2 Given quantities (implicit in the topic)

  • Four waves at frequencies ω1, ω2, ω3, ω4 in a χ(3) medium.
  • Frequency relation for FWM: ω1+ω2=ω3+ω4.
  • Propagation constants kj and phase mismatch Δk.
  • Lossless, SVEA coupled-wave description.

1.3 Unknowns (what we must find/prove)

  • Conserved combinations of powers Pj (energy conservation statement).
  • Conserved combinations of photon fluxes Φj=Pj/(ħωj) (Manley–Rowe relations).
  • A compact “rate form” showing how changes in Pj are linked through the frequencies.

1.4 Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Maxwell + nonlinear polarization: the χ(3) polarization drives coupling between waves.
  • Poynting theorem: in a lossless medium the net electromagnetic power flow is conserved; nonlinear exchange redistributes power between frequency components.
  • Parametric selection rule: time-translation symmetry implies frequency sum conservation ω1+ω2=ω3+ω4, enforcing energy conservation per conversion event.
Assumptions we will use explicitly
  • Lossless propagation (no absorption/scattering), so any change in one wave’s power must be balanced by others.
  • Single four-wave interaction (no other generated frequencies included).
  • SVEA coupled-mode equations with the standard symmetric FWM structure.

1.5 Possible approaches (compare, then choose)

  • Approach A — Coupled-mode amplitude algebra (best for this problem): Write the standard FWM coupled equations for Aj, compute d|Aj|2/dz, and show specific linear combinations vanish.
  • Approach B — Poynting theorem / energy flow: Start from the electromagnetic energy continuity equation and show total power conservation; then connect to photon flux.
  • Approach C — Quantum/Hamiltonian picture: Use an interaction Hamiltonian Hint∝a1a2a3†a4†+h.c. and extract conserved number operators.

We choose Approach A because it gives the Manley–Rowe relations directly and cleanly in the classical coupled-wave framework.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Fix the process direction (1+2→3+4) and define Δk.
  2. Write the coupled-envelope equations for Aj(z).
  3. Relate amplitudes to power via Pj=αj|Aj|^2.
  4. Differentiate |Aj|^2 and identify the common exchange term.
  5. Convert to photon flux Φj=Pj/(ħωj) to reveal equal-and-opposite rates.
  6. Integrate the rate equalities to get constants (invariants).
  7. Multiply by ħωj and use ω-sum matching to obtain energy conservation.
Common mistakes (quick tips)
  • Dropping the complex-conjugate term when computing d|A|^2/dz.
  • Forgetting that photon flux is P/(ħω), not just power.
  • Thinking Δk=0 is required for Manley–Rowe (it is not, in the lossless four-wave model).

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Intuition

In ideal nondegenerate FWM, the elementary conversion event is: one photon at ω1 + one photon at ω2 ↔ one photon at ω3 + one photon at ω4. Therefore photon fluxes must change in lockstep (with opposite signs between the input pair and the output pair).

3.2 Definitions

Frequency matching and phase mismatch for the process 1+2→3+4:

Key definitions (copyable) 
ω1 + ω2 = ω3 + ω4
Δk = k1 + k2 − k3 − k4

3.3 Coupled-mode equations (SVEA)

A standard symmetric lossless FWM set is:

Coupled-envelope equations (copyable) 
dA1/dz =  i κ A2* A3 A4  exp(+iΔk z)
dA2/dz =  i κ A1* A3 A4  exp(+iΔk z)
dA3/dz =  i κ A4* A1 A2  exp(−iΔk z)
dA4/dz =  i κ A3* A1 A2  exp(−iΔk z)

3.4 Power and photon flux

Define power and photon flux:

Definitions (copyable) 
Pj(z) = αj |Aj(z)|^2
Φj(z) = Pj(z)/(ħ ωj)

3.5 Derive common exchange rate

Use d|A|^2/dz = A*(dA/dz) + A(dA*/dz). Define the mixing product: M = A1A2A3*A4*e−iΔkz. Then the derivatives reduce to a single real exchange term:

Amplitude-squared rate relations (copyable) 
Let Q(z) = 2κ Im(M*) (real).

Then:
d|A1|^2/dz = +Q
d|A2|^2/dz = +Q
d|A3|^2/dz = −Q
d|A4|^2/dz = −Q

Physically: the two “input-side” waves change together and the two “output-side” waves change together with opposite sign.

3.6 Manley–Rowe relations (photon-flux and power forms)

Converting to photon flux (the invariant-friendly quantity) gives:

Manley–Rowe rate form (copyable) 
Φj = Pj/(ħ ωj)

Lossless FWM (ω1+ω2→ω3+ω4):
dΦ1/dz = dΦ2/dz = −dΦ3/dz = −dΦ4/dz

Equivalent power form:
(1/ω1) dP1/dz = (1/ω2) dP2/dz = −(1/ω3) dP3/dz = −(1/ω4) dP4/dz

3.7 Integrate to get invariants

Equalities of derivatives imply conserved combinations:

Invariants (copyable) 
Φ1 − Φ2 = const
Φ3 − Φ4 = const
Φ1 + Φ3 = const   (equivalently Φ2 + Φ4 = const)

3.8 Energy conservation (total power)

Using Pj=ħωjΦj and letting dΦ1/dz=dΦ2/dz=−dΦ3/dz=−dΦ4/dz=S(z):

Energy conservation (copyable) 
Ptot = P1 + P2 + P3 + P4 = ħ(ω1Φ1 + ω2Φ2 + ω3Φ3 + ω4Φ4)

dPtot/dz = ħ(ω1 + ω2 − ω3 − ω4) S(z) = 0  since ω1 + ω2 = ω3 + ω4

⇒ d(P1+P2+P3+P4)/dz = 0
FINAL ANSWER (copyable)
Copy final answer 
For lossless four-wave mixing with ω1+ω2=ω3+ω4 (process 1+2 → 3+4), define photon flux Φj = Pj/(ħ ωj).

Manley–Rowe (rate) relations:
dΦ1/dz = dΦ2/dz = −dΦ3/dz = −dΦ4/dz,
equivalently (1/ω1)dP1/dz = (1/ω2)dP2/dz = −(1/ω3)dP3/dz = −(1/ω4)dP4/dz.

Invariants (constants along z):
Φ1 − Φ2 = const,
Φ3 − Φ4 = const,
Φ1 + Φ3 = const (equivalently Φ2 + Φ4 = const).

Energy (total power) conservation:
d(P1+P2+P3+P4)/dz = 0  (follows from the Manley–Rowe rate form and ω1+ω2=ω3+ω4).

3.9 Sanity checks

  • Units: Φ is photons/s. The Manley–Rowe equalities compare photon-flux change rates, so they are naturally dimensionally consistent.
  • Limiting case: if coupling → 0, then all derivatives vanish and invariants hold trivially.
  • Sign logic: if waves 3 and 4 grow, waves 1 and 2 must lose photon flux at the same rate (ideal lossless conversion).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 What each invariant “means”

  • Φ1 − Φ2 constant: photons in waves 1 and 2 are removed/added together; their difference cannot change.
  • Φ3 − Φ4 constant: the same “pairing” holds for the generated pair.
  • Φ1 + Φ3 constant: photons removed from wave 1 reappear in wave 3 one-for-one (flux-wise), and similarly Φ2 + Φ4.

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Frequencies: power changes scale with ω, photon-flux changes do not. So equal photon conversion implies unequal power conversion if frequencies differ.
  • Phase mismatch: changes efficiency (how much transfer you get over a length), but not the invariant structure in the ideal model.
  • Loss: breaks invariants; then you must add sink/source terms to the power equations.

4.3 Alternative derivation (Hamiltonian idea)

With an interaction Hamiltonian Hint∝a1a2a3†a4†+h.c., number-operator combinations like N1−N2 and N1+N3 commute with H, giving the same conserved quantities (in expectation) as the classical Manley–Rowe invariants.

4.4 Concept checks (Q & A)

  • Q: Does equal photon-flux transfer imply equal power transfer? A: No; power is photon flux times ħω.
  • Q: Is Δk=0 required for Manley–Rowe? A: No; it affects efficiency, not the invariant bookkeeping in the lossless model.
  • Q: Can wave 3 grow without wave 4 in ideal FWM? A: Not in the four-wave closed system; the generated pair changes together.
