Quick Summary

  • What this problem is about: judging six common claims in fiber-optic communications and stating when each is valid.
  • Key physics idea: system performance is governed by loss (attenuation), dispersion (pulse spreading), and device noise—and the “best” wavelength depends on which limit dominates.
  • Governing link relation (power budget): received power decreases exponentially with distance; in dB, losses add: P_R(dBm) = P_T(dBm) − α(dB/km)·L(km) − (connector/splice margins).
  • Governing dispersion idea: pulse broadening is tied to the dispersion parameter D(λ) and source spectral width Δλ: ΔT ≈ |D(λ)|·Δλ·L (chromatic dispersion contribution).
  • Material/device constraint: wavelength choices are coupled to available sources/detectors (Si vs InGaAs, etc.) and component ecosystem (amplifiers, filters, WDM).
  • Result type: conceptual/qualitative verdicts with conditions—not a single numeric answer.
  • Main conclusions: 870 nm can be best for short, low-cost links; 1300 nm often balances low dispersion with moderate loss; 1550 nm minimizes loss and enables EDFAs/WDM but has nonzero dispersion unless managed.
  • Industrial engineering lens: “preferred wavelength” depends on total cost of ownership (laser + detector + packaging), yield, reliability, testing, and installed-base compatibility—not just physics minima.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

Wavelength, λm (often nm)

Optical carrier wavelength in vacuum (e.g., 870 nm, 1300 nm, 1550 nm). Determines fiber loss, dispersion, and which materials can emit/detect efficiently.

Attenuation, αdB/km or 1/m

Power loss per unit length. In dB/km it adds linearly with distance: total loss = α·L.

Length, Lkm

Link distance (the dominant scaling factor): both loss and dispersion typically grow with L.

Dispersion, D(λ)ps/(nm·km)

Chromatic dispersion parameter: how group delay changes with wavelength. Drives pulse broadening for finite source linewidth.

Spectral width, Δλnm

Source linewidth (LEDs: broad; lasers: narrow). Larger Δλ increases chromatic pulse spreading.

Modal dispersiontime spread

In multimode fibers, different modes take different paths and arrive at different times; can dominate over chromatic dispersion in MMF.

0.2 Physical meaning & intuition

  • Loss (attenuation): if loss is high, you run out of photons → receiver sensitivity and SNR limit the distance.
  • Dispersion (pulse spreading): even if power is sufficient, pulses can smear into each other → intersymbol interference (ISI) limits bit rate × distance.
  • Noise & detection: detector noise and shot noise determine required received power for a target BER.

0.3 Key laws/principles & validity conditions

Principle A — Exponential loss (Beer–Lambert form)

In a uniform fiber, optical power decays approximately exponentially: P(z) = P(0) exp(−α_lin z). In engineering practice we use dB: Loss(dB) = α(dB/km)·L(km) + extra losses.

Validity: linear propagation (no strong nonlinearities), no catastrophic bends, and reasonably stable splices/connectors.

Principle B — Chromatic dispersion causes time spread

Different wavelengths travel with different group velocities. For a source with spectral width Δλ, the pulse broadening contribution is often modeled as ΔT ≈ |D(λ)|·Δλ·L.

Validity: small-signal approximation, relatively narrowband sources, and when polarization-mode dispersion (PMD) is not dominant.

Principle C — Multimode vs single-mode dispersion mechanisms differ

In multimode fiber (MMF), modal dispersion can dominate because rays/modes take different paths. In single-mode fiber (SMF), modal dispersion is absent, so chromatic and polarization dispersion matter most.

0.4 Common models/approximations (and why engineers use them)

  • Link budget in dB: simplifies multiplicative losses into additive terms; ideal for quick feasibility checks and margin accounting.
  • Dispersion as linear broadening: ΔT ∝ L for chromatic effects with fixed Δλ; helps estimate the bit-rate–distance product.
  • “Windows” (850/1310/1550 nm): practical wavelength bands where fiber loss and device technology are favorable (industry standardization reduces cost).

0.5 Mini intuition examples (conceptual)

  • Short link, cheap optics: If L is tens to hundreds of meters, attenuation differences between 850 and 1310 nm barely matter, so cost/availability can dominate.
  • Long link: At tens of kilometers, even 0.2 dB/km vs 2 dB/km is a huge power difference → the low-loss wavelength becomes crucial.
  • High bit rate: Even with plenty of power, dispersion can smear fast pulses; managing D(λ) can matter more than minimizing α.

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls & hidden assumptions)

Common pitfalls

  • Assuming a single “best wavelength” exists for all systems (it does not).
  • Confusing material dispersion with total dispersion (waveguide + material + PMD + modal effects).
  • Believing “zero dispersion” means “no pulse spreading” (source linewidth, waveguide effects, PMD, and MMF modal dispersion still exist).
  • Equating “single-mode is better” with “single-mode has lower attenuation” (loss depends on glass + design, not only modal count).
  • Overlooking the device ecosystem: detectors, lasers, amplifiers, packaging, and test infrastructure.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Why this topic matters in real engineering

Fiber-optic systems are engineered products: performance targets (bit rate, reach, BER) must be met with constraints on cost, reliability, supply chain, manufacturability, and standards compliance. The physics (loss/dispersion/noise) sets the feasible region; industrial engineering chooses the optimum point.

Where it appears in industry (concrete areas)

  • Data centers & enterprise networks: short-reach interconnects (meters to a few km), often prioritizing low-cost transceivers and high volume.
  • Telecom metro & long-haul: tens to thousands of km, prioritizing low loss, optical amplification, and dispersion management.
  • Access networks (FTTx): passive optical networks and last-mile distribution—cost and standard wavelengths/components dominate.
  • Industrial sensing & instrumentation: fiber links in harsh environments; reliability, connector losses, and testing matter heavily.

Why engineers care (industrial metrics)

Performance

Reach, BER, jitter/ISI, power margin, temperature drift tolerance.

Cost

Laser/detector material cost, packaging (hermetic vs non), test time, yield, and field returns.

Reliability

Connector contamination sensitivity, aging of lasers, fiber bending losses, and environmental robustness.

Standards & interoperability

Fixed wavelength bands and channel plans enable multi-vendor ecosystems; deviating increases integration risk.

Typical measurement/validation methods (brief)

  • OTDR (Optical Time-Domain Reflectometry): measures distributed loss and reflections to locate splices/bends.
  • Insertion loss & return loss testing: validates connectors/splices and reflections that can destabilize lasers.
  • Dispersion measurement: phase shift or time-of-flight methods; used to qualify fiber for high-speed links.
  • Receiver sensitivity & BER testing: validates end-to-end performance with real modulation formats.

Design trade-offs & constraints (non-idealities)

  • Bend sensitivity: some fibers/components have tighter bend radius limits; this affects installation and reliability.
  • Laser safety and power handling: higher optical power improves SNR but increases nonlinear effects and safety constraints.
  • Component availability: 1550 nm ecosystem includes EDFAs and dense WDM filters; 850 nm benefits from low-cost VCSELs and Si photodiodes.
  • Dispersion management cost: moving to 1550 nm often requires dispersion compensation or advanced modulation/DSP for high bit rates over long distances.
From lab model to product: what changes?
  • Lab: treat α and D as clean constants. Product: add margins for temperature, aging, connector contamination, and manufacturing spread.
  • Lab: focus on “best physics window.” Product: prioritize standardized wavelengths, qualified parts, and field-proven packaging.
  • Lab: ignore installation. Product: bend losses, splicing quality, and technician variability are part of the design envelope.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Identify what “preferred” means: decide whether the dominant limit is power (attenuation/noise) or bandwidth (dispersion/ISI).
  2. Compare attenuation at the stated wavelengths using the qualitative silica loss curve (850/870 worse than 1310; 1550 typically best).
  3. Compare dispersion behavior: near 1310 nm standard SMF has near-zero chromatic dispersion; at 1550 nm dispersion is nonzero (often significant at high bit rates).
  4. Account for fiber type: MMF introduces modal dispersion; SMF removes it.
  5. Account for device technology: Si detectors work well near 850–900 nm; InGaAs detectors for 1310/1550 nm; amplifiers (EDFAs) are centered around 1550 nm.
  6. Evaluate each statement by checking whether it improperly claims “for all systems” or ignores a dominant mechanism.
  7. State conditions where the statement becomes approximately true (short vs long links, low vs high bit rates, SMF vs MMF, availability of amplification/DSP).
  8. Sanity-check by testing extremes: very short links (cost dominates) vs long-haul (loss dominates) vs very high bit rate (dispersion dominates).

Common mistakes (quick tips)

  • Taking “no pulse spreading” literally at one wavelength—real systems have finite linewidth, PMD, and (in MMF) modal dispersion.
  • Assuming SMF always has lower attenuation than MMF—attenuation is mostly material + fabrication + wavelength dependent.
  • Dismissing APDs because they’re “noisy”—gain can still improve sensitivity in receiver-noise-limited regimes.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Physical intuition (before verdicts)

Three wavelength “windows” are common in silica fiber practice: near 850–870 nm (low-cost sources/detectors but higher fiber loss), near 1310 nm (low dispersion in standard SMF, moderate loss), and near 1550 nm (lowest loss and strong amplifier/WDM ecosystem but nonzero dispersion in standard SMF). Therefore, any statement claiming one wavelength is preferred for all systems is immediately suspect.

Key idea to use repeatedly

A wavelength can be “preferred” for one dominant constraint but not another: power-limited links prefer lower α; dispersion-limited links prefer smaller |D| (or better dispersion management).

(a) “The wavelength λ₀ = 1300 nm is preferred to λ₀ = 870 nm for all optical fiber communication systems.”

Verdict: Not valid as stated (the phrase “for all systems” makes it false).

Physics comparison: In silica, attenuation at ~870 nm is typically higher than at ~1310 nm. That favors 1300 nm for longer distances because the link budget improves (more received power for the same launch power).

Link budget dependence (dB form):

Received power decreases with α·L:
P_R(dBm) = P_T(dBm) − α(dB/km)·L − Σ(losses) − margin

Why the statement fails “for all systems”: for short-reach links (e.g., inside buildings/data centers), L is small, so the extra fiber loss at 870 nm may be negligible. Then cost and device simplicity can dominate: low-cost 850–870 nm sources (often VCSEL-class emitters) and silicon photodiodes are widespread and economical.

When (a) becomes approximately true

  • Medium-to-long reach where fiber attenuation matters strongly (L large enough that α differences create multiple dB of extra loss).
  • When device cost is not the dominant constraint, or when 1310-nm components are already standardized/available.
  • When dispersion in SMF is a concern: 1310 nm can also reduce chromatic dispersion in standard SMF, improving bandwidth-distance.

(b) “The wavelength λ₀ = 1550 nm is preferred to λ₀ = 1300 nm for all optical fiber communication systems.”

Verdict: Not valid as stated (again, “for all systems” is too strong).

Physics comparison: 1550 nm typically has the lowest attenuation in silica, which is a major advantage for long distances. However, in standard SMF, chromatic dispersion at 1550 nm is generally nonzero (often much larger than near 1310 nm), which can limit high-bit-rate transmission unless dispersion is compensated or advanced modulation/DSP is used.

Chromatic pulse spread model:

ΔT_chromatic ≈ |D(λ)| · Δλ · L

Engineering implication: for high bit rates over moderate distances without heavy dispersion management, 1310 nm can be “preferred” because it reduces chromatic broadening in standard SMF (near the zero-dispersion region).

When (b) becomes approximately true

  • Long-haul / metro links where attenuation dominates and optical amplification (e.g., around 1550 nm) is available.
  • Systems using dispersion compensation or DSP (making 1550 nm dispersion manageable).
  • WDM-heavy systems where the 1550 nm ecosystem (filters, amplifiers, components) is the practical optimum.
Applied-physics note: why 1550 nm is so dominant in long-haul

The industrial “killer advantage” of the 1550-nm band is not only low fiber loss but also the mature amplification and WDM component ecosystem. This shifts system design from “how far can one transmitter go?” to “how many amplified spans and channels can we operate reliably and cheaply?”

(c) “Single-mode fibers are superior to multimode fibers because they have lower attenuation coefficients.”

Verdict: Generally false reasoning (the stated reason is wrong), though SMF is often “superior” for other reasons.

Key correction: Single-mode versus multimode primarily changes dispersion behavior (modal dispersion), not necessarily attenuation. Attenuation is mostly controlled by glass purity, wavelength window, and fabrication quality.

Why SMF is often superior in communications: SMF eliminates modal dispersion, enabling much higher bandwidth-distance products. MMF can be excellent for short reach but tends to be modal-dispersion-limited at longer distances or very high bit rates (depending on MMF type, launch conditions, and equalization).

Correct “superiority” statement (more accurate)

Single-mode fibers are often superior for long reach and/or high bit rate because they avoid modal dispersion and support tighter spatial mode control, not because they inherently have lower attenuation.

(d) “There is no pulse spreading at λ₀ ≈ 1312 nm in silica-glass fibers.”

Verdict: False as stated (too absolute). There is a useful “near-zero chromatic dispersion” idea, but it is not “no pulse spreading.”

What is partly true: In standard silica SMF, the total (material + waveguide) chromatic dispersion can cross zero near ~1310 nm. Around that region, D(λ) is small, so chromatic broadening can be minimized.

Why “no pulse spreading” is wrong:

  • Finite source linewidth: even small |D| can produce spreading if Δλ is large (e.g., LEDs).
  • Polarization-mode dispersion (PMD): causes spreading independent of chromatic D and can matter in long links.
  • Multimode fibers: modal dispersion can dominate even if chromatic dispersion is near zero.
  • Not universal at 1312 nm: the exact zero-dispersion wavelength depends on fiber design and manufacturing tolerances.

When (d) becomes approximately valid (carefully stated)

  • Standard SMF near its zero-dispersion wavelength and narrow-linewidth laser (small Δλ), so chromatic broadening is minimal.
  • Short enough distances that PMD is negligible and other impairments (e.g., nonlinearity) are not dominant.

(e) “Compound semiconductor devices are required for optical fiber communication systems.”

Verdict: False (not required universally), though often essential for certain wavelength bands.

Device-physics reason: Efficient light emission and detection depend on semiconductor bandgap. Many long-haul telecom wavelengths (1310/1550 nm) commonly rely on compound semiconductors (e.g., InP-based lasers, InGaAs detectors), but short-wavelength systems can use silicon detectors (and in some cases different emitter technologies).

Engineering reality (conditional)

  • At ~850–870 nm: silicon photodiodes are excellent; many cost-optimized systems use them.
  • At 1310/1550 nm: silicon is a poor detector; InGaAs (compound) photodiodes/APDs are widely used.
  • Sources: practical lasers for telecom bands are typically compound semiconductors, but the statement “required for all systems” overreaches.

(f) “APDs are noisier than p–i–n photodiodes and are therefore not useful for optical fiber communication systems.”

Verdict: False (the conclusion does not follow). APDs can be very useful despite excess noise.

What is true: APDs introduce avalanche gain but also excess noise (stochastic multiplication). So compared to a p–i–n diode, an APD generally has additional noise mechanisms and requires high bias and careful temperature control.

Why APDs can still improve performance: The internal gain can increase the effective signal level before certain downstream electronic noise sources, improving sensitivity in receiver-noise-limited regimes. In many practical designs, APDs are chosen when extra sensitivity is worth added complexity and power.

When APDs are useful (typical conditions)

  • When receiver electronics noise would otherwise dominate (APD gain boosts signal above that noise floor).
  • When extra dB of sensitivity extends reach without adding an optical amplifier or higher launch power.
  • When system complexity is acceptable: high-voltage bias, temperature stabilization/compensation, and careful design.
Optional deeper note: “noisier” vs “better sensitivity”

“Noisier” is not the same as “worse.” An APD can have more intrinsic multiplication noise yet still provide better overall sensitivity because it moves the signal to a level where later-stage electronic noise matters less. The right comparison is total BER vs received power, not noise in isolation.

Final Answer (copy-ready verdicts with conditions)

(a) 1300 nm preferred over 870 nm for all systems: FALSE.
    • Often preferred for longer reach because silica loss is lower near 1310 nm than near 870 nm.
    • Not universal: short, cost-driven links can prefer ~850–870 nm due to cheap sources + Si photodiodes and small L.

(b) 1550 nm preferred over 1300 nm for all systems: FALSE.
    • Often preferred for long-haul/metro because silica attenuation is lowest near 1550 nm and the 1550-nm ecosystem (amplification/WDM) is strong.
    • Not universal: standard SMF has near-zero chromatic dispersion around ~1310 nm; for high bit rates without dispersion management, 1300 nm can be preferable.

(c) SMF superior to MMF because SMF has lower attenuation: GENERALLY FALSE (wrong reason).
    • Attenuation is mainly material/fabrication/wavelength dependent, not inherently “SMF vs MMF.”
    • SMF is often superior for high bit rate/long reach because it eliminates modal dispersion.

(d) No pulse spreading at ~1312 nm in silica fibers: FALSE as stated.
    • Chromatic dispersion can be near zero around ~1310 nm in standard SMF, minimizing one spreading mechanism.
    • Pulse spreading can still occur from finite linewidth, PMD, and (in MMF) modal dispersion; the exact zero-dispersion wavelength depends on fiber design.

(e) Compound semiconductor devices are required: FALSE.
    • 1310/1550 nm systems commonly use compound semiconductors (e.g., InP/InGaAs).
    • But short-wavelength links can use silicon photodiodes and different low-cost source technologies; “required for all” is too strong.

(f) APDs are noisier than p–i–n and therefore not useful: FALSE.
    • APDs have excess multiplication noise and added bias/thermal complexity.
    • They can still improve receiver sensitivity when electronic noise limits performance, extending reach without higher launch power or amplifiers.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpreting the core formulas

  • Loss term (α·L): every extra km adds a fixed dB penalty (for fixed α). This is why small α differences become decisive at long reach.
  • Dispersion term (|D|·Δλ·L): pulse spreading scales with distance and linewidth; narrowing Δλ (laser vs LED) can “buy back” bandwidth.

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative)

Increase distance L

Loss increases linearly in dB; chromatic broadening increases roughly linearly. Long links increasingly favor low α (often 1550 nm) and/or amplification.

Increase bit rate

Shorter bit slots tolerate less ΔT. Dispersion-limited systems may favor wavelengths with smaller |D| (near 1310 nm) or require compensation/DSP.

Increase linewidth Δλ

Chromatic broadening increases; LEDs suffer more than lasers. This can make “near-zero dispersion” wavelength choices much more valuable.

Choose MMF vs SMF

MMF adds modal dispersion (often the main limitation). SMF removes it, making chromatic/PMD the key dispersion mechanisms.

4.3 Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of discussing “preferred wavelength” qualitatively, you can formalize it using two budgets: a power budget (minimum received power for BER) and a timing budget (maximum allowable pulse spread). For each wavelength, compute feasibility: (i) P_R ≥ P_min and (ii) ΔT_total ≤ fraction of bit period. The preferred wavelength is the one that satisfies both with the largest margin and best cost/reliability profile.

4.4 Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: If you halve Δλ (narrower laser), what happens to chromatic pulse spread?
    A: It halves, since ΔT ∝ Δλ.
  • Q: Why can 850–870 nm still be used despite higher fiber attenuation?
    A: For short links, α·L is small; cost and Si detector advantages can dominate.
  • Q: Does “zero chromatic dispersion” guarantee no ISI?
    A: No—PMD, modal dispersion (MMF), finite linewidth, and other impairments can still cause spreading.
  • Q: Why is 1550 nm attractive beyond low loss?
    A: Strong component ecosystem (amplification, WDM filtering, standardized parts) reduces system cost per bit for long reach.
  • Q: Why might an APD outperform a p–i–n receiver?
    A: Gain can overcome downstream electronic noise, improving sensitivity even though multiplication adds excess noise.
