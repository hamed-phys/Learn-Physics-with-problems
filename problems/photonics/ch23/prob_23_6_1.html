Quick Summary

  • What this is about: measuring an ultrashort Gaussian optical pulse using an intensity autocorrelator, then intentionally stretching one arm with dispersive fiber.
  • Key physics idea: an intensity autocorrelator measures a time-overlap integral of intensities; for Gaussians, the correlation is also Gaussian.
  • Governing equation: intensity autocorrelation (or cross-correlation if pulses differ):
    G(τ) = ∫ I₁(t) I₂(t−τ) dt.
  • Gaussian width rule: convolution of Gaussians adds variances: σ_G² = σ₁² + σ₂², so FWHM_G = √(FWHM₁² + FWHM₂²).
  • (a) Result: for identical pulses, the measured intensity autocorrelation is Gaussian with FWHM = √2 · (pulse FWHM).
  • (b) Result type: numeric fiber length using dispersion parameter Dλ. For a 50 fs transform-limited Gaussian at 800 nm, bandwidth is ~18.8 nm (FWHM).
  • (b) Fiber length: to stretch by factor 5, needed length is about 0.12 m (≈12 cm) for |Dλ|=110 ps/(km·nm).
  • (c) Modified correlation width: cross-correlation of 50 fs and 250 fs Gaussians gives FWHM ≈ √26 · 50 fs ≈ 255 fs.
  • Engineering meaning: deliberate chirp/stretch can widen and stabilize overlap signals, but introduces calibration needs, phase distortions, and bandwidth/acceptance constraints—especially in nonlinear interferometers.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • Time t (s) and delay τ (s).
  • Optical field U(t) (arbitrary field units). Often written U(t)=A(t) e^{j2πν₀t}.
  • Intensity I(t)=|U(t)|² (W/m² in absolute terms; often normalized).
  • FWHM (full width at half maximum): duration where a function is ≥ half its peak (s).
  • Intensity autocorrelation (2nd-order intensity correlation): G_I(τ)=∫ I(t) I(t−τ) dt (units: intensity²·time).
  • Dispersion parameter Dλ (ps/(km·nm)): slope of group delay vs wavelength.
  • Optical bandwidth Δλ (m or nm) and Δν (Hz).

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • G_I(τ) measures “how much two copies of the pulse overlap” when one is delayed by τ.
  • At τ=0 the overlap is maximal; as |τ| grows beyond the pulse duration, overlap → 0.
  • For Gaussians, overlap vs delay is also Gaussian—this is a major reason Gaussians are analytically convenient.
  • Fiber dispersion stretches pulses because different wavelengths travel at different group velocities, producing a time “spread” proportional to |Dλ| L Δλ.
Overlap integral Gaussian-in → Gaussian-out Dispersion broadening

Key laws/principles + validity conditions

Assumptions behind the standard formulas
  • Pulses are well-modeled as Gaussian in time (intensity) and transform-limited unless otherwise stated.
  • Autocorrelator is an intensity correlator: output ∝ ∫ I(t)I(t−τ)dt (not a field interferometer unless specified).
  • Fiber stretching uses Dλ and assumes dispersion is approximately constant over the pulse bandwidth and higher-order dispersion is negligible.
  • Nonlinear conversion (e.g., SHG gating) is assumed fast compared with pulse duration and within its phase-matching bandwidth.

Common models/approximations (and why they’re used)

  • Gaussian pulse model: analytic integrals; many lasers are close to Gaussian near transform-limit.
  • Linear dispersion broadening: treat stretching as RMS addition: output duration depends on input duration and dispersive delay spread.
  • Small-bandwidth approximation for Δν ↔ Δλ: Δν ≈ (c/λ²) Δλ (valid when Δλ ≪ λ; here it’s moderate but still commonly used for estimates).

Mini intuition examples (conceptual)

  • Example 1 (overlap): Two identical pulses: if you slide one by one pulse-width, overlap drops strongly; that drop defines the correlation width.
  • Example 2 (Gaussian property): Multiplying two Gaussians in time gives another Gaussian; integrating over time preserves Gaussian dependence on delay.
  • Example 3 (dispersion): If your pulse has 20 nm bandwidth and fiber causes 100 ps/nm delay spread, different spectral components arrive separated by ~2 ns → huge stretch (unless the fiber is short).

What to watch for (pitfalls)

Pitfalls & misconceptions
  • Autocorrelation ≠ pulse: an autocorrelation trace is broader than the pulse; you need a deconvolution factor that depends on pulse shape.
  • Intensity vs field interferometry: intensity autocorrelation loses optical phase; nonlinear interferometers can contain phase information but require more care.
  • FWHM vs RMS (σ): Gaussian algebra is easiest in σ; converting back to FWHM must be consistent.
  • Dispersion parameters: Dλ (ps/km/nm) is not the same as β₂ (s²/m), though they are related.
  • Higher-order dispersion & bandwidth: very short pulses can be wideband; constant-Dλ may be only an estimate.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (3+ areas)

  • Ultrafast laser manufacturing & metrology: verifying pulse duration for micromachining (glass, polymers, metals) and semiconductor scribing.
  • Telecom & datacom R&D: characterizing short pulses and chirp for high-speed optical links and coherent systems.
  • Biophotonics & microscopy: two-photon / multiphoton imaging depends critically on pulse width at the sample (dispersion in optics matters).
  • Materials processing & LIDT (laser-induced damage threshold): peak power scales inversely with pulse duration, affecting safety margins and reliability.

Why engineers care (performance, cost, reliability)

  • Performance: peak intensity, nonlinear efficiency, and processing quality depend on accurate pulse width.
  • Reliability: unknown chirp/dispersion can drift with temperature, fiber routing, and component aging.
  • Cost & manufacturability: intensity autocorrelators are simpler and cheaper than full phase-retrieval tools, but provide less information.
  • Safety: peak power estimation from average power requires correct pulse duration; errors can be costly.

Typical measurement/validation methods in practice (brief)

  • Intensity autocorrelation (this problem): quick “is it ~50 fs or ~200 fs?” check; robust and common in production labs.
  • SHG-FROG / SPIDER: full characterization (amplitude + phase) for R&D and critical systems; more complex calibration.
  • Spectral + dispersion audit: spectrum analyzer + known optics/fiber dispersion budgets to predict pulse broadening at the target plane.

Design trade-offs and constraints (real-world non-idealities)

  • Finite phase-matching bandwidth: SHG crystal may not support the full bandwidth of a 50 fs pulse, distorting the trace.
  • Alignment & polarization: correlator signals are sensitive to polarization and beam overlap.
  • Dispersion variability: Dλ depends on wavelength, fiber type, and temperature; higher-order terms matter for ultrashort pulses.
  • Interpretation risk: different pulse shapes can produce similar autocorrelation widths; “Gaussian assumption” must be justified.
From lab to product: how idealized models are adapted
  • Production settings often use autocorrelation as a process-control metric (pass/fail window) rather than a full diagnostic.
  • Engineers build a calibration curve by comparing autocorrelation width to a “gold standard” (FROG/SPIDER) for representative units.
  • Dispersion compensation in real systems is designed with tolerance margins, because component-to-component variations and routing changes are unavoidable.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement (in plain words)

A transform-limited Gaussian optical pulse has 50 fs FWHM and central wavelength 800 nm. It is measured using an intensity autocorrelator (a standard two-arm setup with a nonlinear gate and slow detector).

(a) Find the shape and FWHM of the measured autocorrelation trace.
(b) Improve the measurement by stretching one arm’s pulse by a factor of 5 using a silica-glass fiber with dispersion coefficient Dλ = −110 ps/(km·nm) at 800 nm. Find the required fiber length and the width of the modified correlation function.
(c) Apply the stretching idea to a nonlinear interferometer and discuss merits and difficulties for pulse measurement.

Given

  • Pulse is Gaussian, transform-limited
  • Pulse intensity FWHM: Δt = 50 fs
  • Central wavelength: λ₀ = 800 nm
  • Stretch factor: S = 5
  • Fiber dispersion parameter: Dλ = −110 ps/(km·nm)

Unknowns

  • (a) Autocorrelation shape and FWHM: Δτ_AC
  • (b) Required fiber length L and modified correlation width Δτ_mod
  • (c) Merits/difficulties in a nonlinear interferometer (qualitative, applied)

Relevant principles (and why)

  • Gaussian convolution: intensity correlation is a convolution-like integral; Gaussians remain Gaussian.
  • Transform-limited time–bandwidth product: connects 50 fs duration to optical bandwidth needed for dispersion calculation.
  • Dispersion-induced broadening: stretching due to group-delay slope Dλ.
  • Cross-correlation width rule: when the two pulses differ (one stretched), the correlation width depends on both widths.
Assumptions we will use
  • Pulse intensity is Gaussian: I(t) ∝ exp(−t²/(2σ²)).
  • Autocorrelator measures G(τ)=∫ I(t)I(t−τ)dt (no phase retrieval in parts a–b).
  • Fiber broadening can be estimated by delay spread: Δt_out ≈ √(Δt₀² + (|Dλ| L Δλ)²) (Gaussian FWHM-based estimate).
  • Use small-bandwidth conversion: Δν ≈ (c/λ₀²)Δλ.

Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

  • Approach A (σ-domain Gaussian algebra): convert FWHM → σ, do integrals, then convert back. Pros: clean, minimal mistakes. Cons: requires conversion discipline.
  • Approach B (known deconvolution factors): recall standard results (Gaussian autocorrelation FWHM = √2 times pulse FWHM). Pros: fast. Cons: easy to mix up intensity vs field autocorrelation.
  • Approach C (frequency-domain dispersion model): use β₂ and Fourier transforms. Pros: rigorous. Cons: longer; needs parameter conversion from Dλ.

Choice: Use Approach A for (a) and correlation widths, plus the standard dispersion broadening estimate for (b). This is accurate enough and keeps the physics transparent.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Model the pulse intensity as Gaussian in time and define its σ from the given FWHM.
  2. Write the measured intensity autocorrelation integral G_I(τ)=∫ I(t)I(t−τ)dt.
  3. Evaluate the integral using Gaussian completion of the square to show G_I(τ) is Gaussian in τ.
  4. Extract the correlation σ and FWHM and express it in terms of the pulse FWHM.
  5. Compute the transform-limited bandwidth from the time–bandwidth product for a Gaussian pulse.
  6. Relate bandwidth in frequency to wavelength near 800 nm to obtain Δλ.
  7. Use dispersion broadening to solve for fiber length L such that Δt_out = 5Δt₀.
  8. Compute modified correlation width as a Gaussian cross-correlation: Δτ_mod = √(Δt₀² + Δt_out²).
  9. Discuss nonlinear interferometer implications: where stretching helps, and where it creates new measurement/phase issues.
Common mistakes (quick tips)
  • Using the field autocorrelation factor instead of the intensity autocorrelation factor.
  • Mixing RMS widths (σ) with FWHM without converting consistently.
  • For dispersion: forgetting the bandwidth term Δλ (a short pulse with broad bandwidth stretches quickly).
  • Sign of Dλ does not affect width; only the magnitude matters for stretching.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition before calculating

An autocorrelator essentially asks: “If I overlap the pulse with a delayed copy, how strong is the nonlinear signal?” For a Gaussian, overlap falls off smoothly with delay, and we expect a Gaussian-shaped trace centered at τ=0. Because overlap involves two copies, the correlation must be broader than the original pulse. Stretching one arm should broaden the correlation even more, roughly like the quadrature sum of widths.

(a) Shape and FWHM of the measured intensity autocorrelation

Let the pulse intensity be a Gaussian: 

I(t) = I0 · exp( − t^2 / (2σ^2) )

Here σ (s) is the intensity standard deviation; FWHM and σ are related for a Gaussian.

The intensity autocorrelation measured by the correlator is: 

G_I(τ) = ∫_{−∞}^{∞} I(t) · I(t−τ) dt

Substitute the Gaussian form:

G_I(τ) = I0^2 ∫ exp( − t^2/(2σ^2) ) · exp( − (t−τ)^2/(2σ^2) ) dt

Combine exponents: 

t^2 + (t−τ)^2 = 2(t − τ/2)^2 + τ^2/2

Therefore 

G_I(τ) = I0^2 · exp( − τ^2 / (4σ^2) ) · ∫ exp( − (t − τ/2)^2 / (σ^2) ) dt

The integral over t is a constant (independent of τ), because it is just the area of a Gaussian. So the τ-dependence is: 

G_I(τ) ∝ exp( − τ^2 / (4σ^2) )

Conclusion: the measured intensity autocorrelation is also Gaussian in τ.

Compare exp(−τ²/(4σ²)) with a standard Gaussian form exp(−τ²/(2σ_G²)). We identify: 

1/(2σ_G^2) = 1/(4σ^2)  ⇒  σ_G = √2 · σ

For a Gaussian, FWHM = 2√(2 ln 2) · σ. Hence: 

Δτ_AC = 2√(2 ln 2)·σ_G = 2√(2 ln 2)·(√2 σ) = √2 · (2√(2 ln 2)·σ) = √2 · Δt
(a) Final result 
Autocorrelation shape: Gaussian
Autocorrelation FWHM:  Δτ_AC = √2 · Δt

With Δt = 50 fs ⇒ Δτ_AC = √2 · 50 fs ≈ 70.7 fs

(b) Fiber length to stretch one pulse by a factor of 5 (and modified correlation width)

Step 1: transform-limited bandwidth of a 50 fs Gaussian

For a transform-limited Gaussian pulse, the time–bandwidth product (FWHM form) is: 

Δν · Δt = 0.441   (Gaussian, FWHM)

With Δt = 50 fs = 50×10^−15 s: 

Δν = 0.441 / (50×10^−15 s) = 8.82×10^12 Hz  (≈ 8.82 THz)

Convert frequency bandwidth to wavelength bandwidth near λ₀ = 800 nm using: 

Δν ≈ (c / λ0^2) · Δλ   ⇒  Δλ ≈ (Δν λ0^2) / c

Numerically (with c = 2.9979×10^8 m/s, λ₀ = 800 nm): 

Δλ ≈ (8.82×10^12)(800×10^−9)^2 / (2.9979×10^8)
    ≈ 1.883×10^−8 m ≈ 18.8 nm  (FWHM)
Note on validity of Δν ↔ Δλ conversion for 50 fs at 800 nm

Here Δλ/λ₀ ≈ 18.8/800 ≈ 2.35%, which is not extremely tiny but still within the regime where the linear approximation is widely used for engineering estimates. A more exact mapping would slightly adjust the number but not the main conclusion: wide bandwidth means only a short length of dispersive fiber is needed.

Step 2: dispersion broadening to achieve a stretch factor S = 5

The fiber’s dispersion parameter is Dλ = −110 ps/(km·nm). The group-delay spread across the pulse bandwidth is approximately: 

Δt_disp ≈ |Dλ| · L · Δλ

A common Gaussian estimate for output FWHM after dispersive broadening is: 

Δt_out ≈ √( Δt0^2 + (Δt_disp)^2 ) = √( Δt0^2 + (|Dλ| L Δλ)^2 )

We want a stretch factor S = Δt_out/Δt0 = 5. Therefore: 

5Δt0 = √( Δt0^2 + (|Dλ| L Δλ)^2 )
⇒ (|Dλ| L Δλ)^2 = (25−1) Δt0^2
⇒ |Dλ| L Δλ = Δt0 √24

Solve for L: 

L = (Δt0 √24) / (|Dλ| Δλ)

Insert numbers (use ps, km, nm consistently): Δt0 = 50 fs = 0.05 ps, Δλ ≈ 18.8 nm, |Dλ| = 110 ps/(km·nm). 

L ≈ (0.05 ps · √24) / (110 ps/(km·nm) · 18.8 nm)
  ≈ (0.05 · 4.899) / (2068) km
  ≈ 1.18×10^−4 km ≈ 0.118 m
(b1) Required fiber length (stretch by factor 5) 
Pulse bandwidth (TL Gaussian, 50 fs @ 800 nm):  Δλ ≈ 18.8 nm
Required silica fiber length:  L ≈ 0.12 m  (≈ 12 cm)

Step 3: width of the modified correlation function

After the fiber, one arm has width Δt2 = Δt_out = 5Δt0 while the other remains Δt1 = Δt0. The correlator now measures a cross-correlation: 

G_mod(τ) = ∫ I1(t) I2(t−τ) dt

For Gaussian intensities with FWHMs Δt1 and Δt2, the cross-correlation is Gaussian with: 

Δτ_mod = √(Δt1^2 + Δt2^2)   (Gaussian intensity cross-correlation, FWHM)

Here Δt1 = 50 fs, Δt2 = 250 fs, so: 

Δτ_mod = √(50^2 + 250^2) fs = 50 √26 fs ≈ 255 fs
(b2) Width of modified correlation function 
After stretching one arm by S=5:
Modified correlation shape: Gaussian
Modified correlation FWHM:  Δτ_mod = √(Δt^2 + (5Δt)^2) = √26 · Δt

With Δt = 50 fs ⇒ Δτ_mod ≈ 255 fs

(c) Applying the stretching idea to a nonlinear interferometer (merits & difficulties)

A nonlinear interferometer (e.g., with a squaring/SHG element before detection) can produce a signal that depends not only on intensity overlap, but also on phase-sensitive interference terms of the optical field. Placing dispersive fiber in one arm intentionally makes one replica chirped/stretched. That changes how phase information maps into the measured delay-dependent signal.

Possible merits (why it could help)
  • Wider, smoother envelope: broader overlap can make alignment less finicky and improve SNR with slow detectors.
  • Temporal “shear” of frequencies: chirped pulse maps frequency to time, so interference vs delay can encode spectral-phase trends more visibly (conceptually similar to “time-stretch” diagnostics).
  • Stability in industrial setups: wider correlation features are less sensitive to small mechanical jitter in delay stages (lower sensitivity to micrometer-scale drift).
Difficulties (what gets harder)
  • Calibration burden: you must know the fiber’s dispersion accurately (including higher-order terms) over the pulse bandwidth.
  • Phase distortions: nonlinear interferometers are sensitive to phase; fiber adds frequency-dependent phase that can complicate interpretation.
  • Bandwidth limits: SHG phase-matching and detector response can filter the signal, especially for broad 50 fs bandwidth.
  • Ambiguities: without full phase retrieval, different input phases can yield similar interferometric signatures when combined with chirp.
  • Polarization & PMD: standard fiber can introduce polarization-mode dispersion and polarization rotation, destabilizing interference.
Applied takeaway for “lab-to-line” measurement design

In many industrial contexts, engineers use dispersion insertion to condition a measurement: improve robustness, fit detector bandwidth, or increase dynamic range. But when the goal is true pulse characterization (especially phase), the added dispersion becomes part of the “instrument response” and must be modeled and validated—often pushing teams toward FROG/SPIDER or well-calibrated interferometric methods.

Sanity checks (units, limits, interpretation)

Checks
  • Units: Dλ L Δλ → (ps/(km·nm))·(km)·(nm) = ps, consistent with a time broadening term.
  • Limiting case: if L→0, then Δt_out→Δt0 and Δτ_mod→√(Δt0^2+Δt0^2)=√2Δt0, matching part (a).
  • Interpretation: short pulses have large bandwidth, so even modest dispersion can stretch them; hence the surprisingly short (~12 cm) fiber length here.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpreting the formulas (what each term controls)

  • Δτ_AC = √2·Δt: the factor √2 comes from “two-pulse overlap” of identical Gaussians; it is a shape-dependent deconvolution factor.
  • Δt_out = √(Δt0² + (|Dλ|LΔλ)²): output width is the quadrature sum of the original duration and dispersive delay spread.
  • Δτ_mod = √(Δt1² + Δt2²): cross-correlation width reflects both pulse widths; the larger one dominates once it is much bigger.

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Increase bandwidth Δλ (shorter pulses) → dispersion stretching increases for a given fiber length.
  • Increase fiber length L or |Dλ| → broader stretched pulse and broader modified correlation trace.
  • Increase stretch factor S → modified correlation width grows like √(1+S²).

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of time-domain completion of the square, you can use the convolution theorem: G_I(τ) is the convolution of I(t) with I(−t). The Fourier transform of a Gaussian is a Gaussian, and multiplication in frequency corresponds to convolution in time—leading again to Gaussian G_I(τ) with variance sum.

Concept checks (self-test with answers)

  • Q: If the pulse were Lorentzian instead of Gaussian, would the √2 factor still hold?
    A: No. The deconvolution factor depends on pulse shape; √2 is specific to Gaussian intensity autocorrelation.
  • Q: Why doesn’t the sign of Dλ matter for pulse width?
    A: Width depends on magnitude of delay spread; the sign flips chirp direction but not duration.
  • Q: If you stretch to extremely large S, what sets the practical limit?
    A: Higher-order dispersion, loss, nonlinearities in fiber (SPM), bandwidth limits of nonlinear crystal/detector, and calibration stability.
  • Q: Can an intensity autocorrelator uniquely determine pulse shape?
    A: Not uniquely; it provides a width-related metric and some qualitative shape, but phase is lost and multiple pulse shapes can share similar autocorrelations.
