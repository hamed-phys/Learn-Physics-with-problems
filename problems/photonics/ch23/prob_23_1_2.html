Quick Summary

  • What this is about: A pulse with field envelope f(t) = sech(t/τ); find the time-domain intensity FWHM and the spectral intensity shape and FWHM.
  • Key physics idea: The Fourier transform converts temporal localization into spectral broadening (time–bandwidth trade-off).
  • Governing transform (appendix convention): F(ν) = ∫ f(t) e^{-j2πνt} dt, f(t) = ∫ F(ν) e^{+j2πνt} dν.
  • Time intensity: I(t) ∝ |f(t)|² = sech²(t/τ).
  • Time FWHM result: ΔtFWHM = 2τ arcosh(√2) ≈ 1.763τ.
  • Spectrum: F(ν) = πτ sech(π²τν) under the appendix’s e^{-j2πνt} convention, hence S(ν) ∝ |F(ν)|² ∝ sech²(π²τν).
  • Spectral FWHM (same convention): ΔνFWHM = 2 arcosh(√2)/(π²τ) ≈ 0.1786/τ (Hz).
  • Physical meaning: Shorter pulses (smaller τ) require larger bandwidth (larger Δν).
  • Comparison vs Gaussian: Gaussian has larger time–bandwidth product; for intensity-FWHM, sech²: ΔtΔν ≈ 0.315 vs Gaussian: ΔtΔν ≈ 0.441.
  • Important note: Published constants can differ if a book uses angular frequency ω or a different “τ” definition; the derivation below shows exactly where factors of 2π enter.

Key outcome usable constants

For f(t)=sech(t/τ) (field) and I(t)∝sech²(t/τ) (intensity):

  • ΔtFWHM ≈ 1.763τ
  • ΔνFWHM ≈ 0.1786/τ (with the appendix’s FT convention)
  • ΔtFWHMΔνFWHM ≈ 0.315

Convention alert 2π matters

If you instead use F(ω)=∫ f(t)e^{-jωt}dt, the spectrum becomes F(ω)=πτ sech(πτω/2). Converting ω↔ν via ω=2πν returns the same Δν.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • Time t (seconds, s).
  • Pulse time constant τ (seconds, s). In this problem it sets how quickly sech(t/τ) decays.
  • Field envelope f(t) (arbitrary field units; often √W for optical field amplitude).
  • Intensity I(t) ∝ |f(t)|² (e.g., W for power, or W/m² for irradiance).
  • Frequency ν (Hz = s⁻¹, cycles/s). Angular frequency ω (rad/s) relates by ω = 2πν.
  • Fourier transform pair (appendix style): F(ν) = ∫ f(t)e^{-j2πνt}dt, f(t)=∫ F(ν)e^{+j2πνt}dν.
  • Spectral intensity S(ν) ∝ |F(ν)|² (power per Hz, or arbitrary units).
  • FWHM (full width at half maximum): width between the two points where the quantity equals half its peak value.

0.2 Physical meaning (intuition)

A localized pulse in time necessarily contains a spread of frequencies. The narrower the pulse in time, the broader its spectrum. This is not “instrument limitation”—it is mathematics (Fourier analysis) and physics (uncertainty-like trade-off).

0.3 Key principles and validity conditions

Fourier theorem for pulses linear systems

If the medium/system is linear and time-invariant (LTI), the spectrum fully characterizes how filters, dispersion, and bandwidth limits affect the pulse.

  • Assumes linear superposition holds.
  • Assumes envelope is well-behaved (integrable, or tempered distributions in practice).

What is “sech” pulse? laser physics

Hyperbolic-secant pulses commonly arise as soliton solutions of the nonlinear Schrödinger equation in fibers and mode-locked lasers. The intensity shape is often sech².

0.4 Common models/approximations and why engineers use them

  • Envelope model: Use a slowly varying envelope f(t) instead of the optical carrier. This isolates the “information-bearing” pulse shape.
  • Intensity from field: Use I(t) ∝ |f(t)|² when detector response is much slower than the carrier oscillation.
  • FWHM metrics: FWHM is a practical spec that connects directly to measurement equipment readouts and datasheets (oscilloscope/OSA).

0.5 Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • Shorter pulse → broader spectrum: If τ halves, the pulse decays twice as fast; its spectrum spreads roughly twice as wide (Δν ∝ 1/τ).
  • Shape matters: Two pulses can have the same FWHM time width but different spectral widths (e.g., Gaussian vs sech²).
  • Engineering consequence: A bandwidth-limited modulator or photodiode can distort a pulse even if average power is fine.

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

Pitfall: mixing conventions 2π & τ

  • Using e^{-jωt} vs e^{-j2πνt} changes constants unless you convert variables correctly.
  • Some texts define the sech pulse as sech(t/T0) but report widths in terms of T0 or intensity parameters.

Pitfall: FWHM of field vs intensity square matters

Here, intensity is sech², not sech. FWHM must be computed on the quantity requested.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this shows up in industry (concrete areas)

  • Fiber-optic communications: Pulse bandwidth sets dispersion penalty, filter passband requirements, and inter-symbol interference risk.
  • Ultrafast lasers (manufacturing, micromachining): Pulse duration and spectrum affect peak intensity, ablation threshold, heat-affected zone, and process repeatability.
  • Metrology & spectroscopy: Short pulses provide broad spectra for frequency-comb and pump–probe systems; spectral width determines resolution and accessible transitions.
  • High-speed electronics/EO modulators: Driver + modulator bandwidth must support the required Δν to avoid pulse broadening and ringing.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: bandwidth-limited components reshape pulses, reducing eye opening and increasing BER in telecom.
  • Reliability & safety: peak power scales inversely with pulse duration for fixed energy; short pulses can exceed damage thresholds.
  • Cost & manufacturability: wider bandwidth demands higher-grade RF packaging, lower parasitics, better dispersion management, and tighter tolerances.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Time domain: fast photodiode + sampling scope (limited by bandwidth), autocorrelator (for ultrafast), cross-correlation.
  • Frequency domain: optical spectrum analyzer (OSA), RF spectrum analyzer after detection, frequency-resolved optical gating (FROG) for full characterization.
  • Model validation: compare measured FWHM and spectral width to analytical constants (like 1.763 and 0.1786) to infer whether a pulse is close to sech² or Gaussian.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Non-idealities: chirp, gain narrowing, finite filter slopes, detector saturation, and dispersion alter the ideal sech spectrum.
  • Tolerances: manufacturing variations change τ (temporal) and therefore Δν; specs often include “typical” and “max/min.”
  • System constraints: dispersion compensation or pulse shaping may be required to meet both time and spectral masks.
From lab to product: how the ideal model is adapted
In practice, engineers treat the sech² result as a reference baseline. Real pulses may be chirped or truncated; systems add filters and dispersion. A common product workflow is: measure Δt and Δν, compute the time–bandwidth product, then decide whether pulse compression, dispersion compensation, or spectral shaping is needed to hit performance and compliance targets.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Rewrite the problem (clear statement)

A pulse has a complex field envelope f(t) = sech(t/τ), where sech(x)=1/cosh(x) and τ is a time constant. Using the Fourier-transform convention shown in Appendix A:

  • (a) Show that the intensity width (FWHM) satisfies ΔtFWHM = 1.76 τ (more precisely 1.763τ).
  • (b) Show that the spectral intensity has the form S(ν) ∝ sech²(π² τ ν) and find its FWHM spectral width Δν.
  • (c) Compare with a Gaussian pulse (Appendix A).

Given quantities

  • f(t)=sech(t/τ)
  • τ is a constant (s)
  • Fourier pair: F(ν)=∫ f(t)e^{-j2πνt}dt

Unknowns

  • Time FWHM of intensity I(t) ∝ |f(t)|²
  • Form of spectral intensity S(ν) ∝ |F(ν)|²
  • Frequency FWHM ΔνFWHM
  • Comparison constants vs Gaussian

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Definition of intensity: I(t) ∝ |f(t)|² applies because the pulse is described by a complex envelope.
  • Fourier transform: directly yields F(ν), hence spectrum and spectral intensity.
  • Even symmetry: sech(t/τ) is even, simplifying FWHM calculations and transform properties.

Assumptions explicit

  • The pulse envelope is ideal and extends to ±∞ (no truncation).
  • We use the appendix’s transform convention with e^{-j2πνt}.
  • We measure widths using intensity FWHM (not field FWHM).

Why other tools aren’t needed keep it clean

No differential equations or propagation models are needed here; the task is purely shape + Fourier transform + FWHM definitions.

Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

  1. Direct FWHM from definition: Solve I(t)=I(0)/2 for t. Fast, exact.
  2. Use known transform table: Recall ∫sech(at)e^{-iωt}dt = (π/a)sech(πω/2a). Fastest if known.
  3. Complex analysis / residues: Derive transform from scratch using poles of sech. Most general, but longer.

Best approach: Use direct FWHM definition for part (a), and the standard transform identity (with careful conversion between ω and ν) for part (b). This is both rigorous and efficient, and matches how engineers use transform pairs in practice.

  1. Goal: Write intensity. Tool: I(t)∝|f(t)|². Meaning: what detectors “see.”
  2. Goal: Find time FWHM. Tool: solve sech²(t/τ)=1/2. Meaning: pulse duration spec.
  3. Goal: Compute Fourier transform. Tool: known identity for ∫sech plus variable substitution. Meaning: spectrum amplitude.
  4. Goal: Convert to spectral intensity. Tool: S(ν)∝|F(ν)|². Meaning: power vs frequency.
  5. Goal: Spectral FWHM. Tool: solve sech²(…)=1/2. Meaning: bandwidth requirement.
  6. Goal: Compare to Gaussian. Tool: standard Gaussian FT + FWHM. Meaning: shape-dependent bandwidth penalty.
  7. Goal: Sanity checks. Tool: units + scaling (Δν∝1/τ). Meaning: catch missing 2π factors.

Common mistakes quick tips

  • Computing FWHM of sech when the problem asks for sech² (intensity).
  • Forgetting that half-maximum is 1/2 of the peak, not half the argument.
  • Mixing ν (Hz) and ω (rad/s) without ω=2πν.
  • Dropping the scaling from substitution u=t/τ in the integral.

Constant you’ll see repeatedly arcosh

Both FWHMs hinge on solving cosh(x)=√2, giving:

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before calculation)

Because sech(t/τ) becomes narrower when τ decreases, we expect: ΔtFWHM ∝ τ and ΔνFWHM ∝ 1/τ. Also, since sech has exponential-like tails, its spectrum should also have a smooth, non-oscillatory shape (no sharp edges that would cause spectral ringing).

(a) Time-domain intensity FWHM

The complex envelope is: f(t) = sech(t/τ). The intensity is proportional to the squared magnitude:

Intensity from field envelope
I(t) ∝ |f(t)|^2 = sech^2(t/τ)

The peak occurs at t=0 because sech(0)=1, so I(0) ∝ 1. The FWHM condition is:

Half-maximum condition
sech^2(t/τ) = 1/2

Take the square root (positive because intensity is positive and sech is positive for real arguments): sech(t/τ) = 1/√2. Invert sech using sech(x)=1/cosh(x):

Solve for the half-maximum points
sech(x)=1/√2  ⇒  cosh(x)=√2  ⇒  x = arcosh(√2) = ln(√2+1)

Here x = t/τ. Therefore the two half-maximum times are: t = ± τ arcosh(√2). The full width (difference between the two) is:

Time FWHM (intensity)
Δt_FWHM = 2 τ arcosh(√2)
        = 2τ ln(√2+1)
        ≈ 1.763 τ

(b) Spectral intensity shape and spectral FWHM

The appendix uses the Fourier transform:

Appendix A Fourier transform
F(ν) = ∫_{-∞}^{∞} f(t) e^{-j 2π ν t} dt

Substitute f(t)=sech(t/τ) and use the change of variables u=t/τ (so t=τu and dt=τ du):

Scale out τ
F(ν)= ∫ sech(t/τ) e^{-j2πνt} dt
    = τ ∫_{-∞}^{∞} sech(u) e^{-j 2π (ν τ) u} du

Now we use a standard transform identity (can be derived by complex analysis, but commonly listed in Fourier tables):

Known integral identity
∫_{-∞}^{∞} sech(u) e^{-j ω u} du = π sech( (π ω)/2 )

In our case, the angular variable in the exponential is ω = 2π(ντ). Plugging into the identity gives:

Fourier transform of sech(t/τ)
F(ν) = τ · π · sech( (π/2)·[2πντ] )
     = πτ sech( π^2 τ ν )

The spectral intensity is proportional to magnitude squared:

Spectral intensity shape
S(ν) ∝ |F(ν)|^2 ∝ sech^2(π^2 τ ν)

To find the FWHM in frequency, set S(ν)=S(0)/2. Since S(0) is at ν=0, we solve: sech²(π²τν)=1/2. This is the same equation as before, with argument x = π²τν.

Half-maximum frequencies
sech^2(π^2 τ ν)=1/2
⇒ sech(π^2 τ ν)=1/√2
⇒ cosh(π^2 τ ν)=√2
⇒ |ν| = arcosh(√2)/(π^2 τ)

Therefore the full spectral FWHM is twice that:

Spectral FWHM (Hz, appendix convention)
Δν_FWHM = 2 arcosh(√2)/(π^2 τ)
        = 2 ln(√2+1)/(π^2 τ)
        ≈ 0.1786 / τ
Why some sources report different numbers (e.g., 1.786/τ)
Differences almost always come from definitions:
  • Using angular frequency ω (rad/s) vs frequency ν (Hz).
  • Using a different pulse parameter (e.g., defining the field as sech(t/T0) but naming the intensity parameter as τ).
  • Defining width for the field rather than the intensity.
With the appendix’s explicit e^{-j2πνt} convention and the stated f(t)=sech(t/τ), the consistent result is S(ν)∝sech²(π²τν) and Δν≈0.1786/τ.

(c) Compare with Gaussian pulse (Appendix A)

A common Gaussian field envelope is f_G(t)=exp[-(t/τ_G)²], giving intensity I_G(t)∝exp[-2(t/τ_G)²]. Its Fourier transform is also Gaussian, and the intensity FWHM products are well-known.

Gaussian intensity FWHM (typical parameterization)

If I_G(t)∝exp[-2(t/τ_G)²], then:

Time FWHM (Gaussian intensity)
Δt_FWHM(Gaussian) = τ_G √(2 ln 2) ≈ 1.177 τ_G

With the appendix frequency variable (Hz), the Gaussian spectral intensity is also Gaussian and yields Δt_FWHM Δν_FWHM ≈ 0.441.

Sech² vs Gaussian (engineering takeaway)

  • Sech²: ΔtΔν ≈ 0.315 (more bandwidth-efficient for a given intensity FWHM).
  • Gaussian: ΔtΔν ≈ 0.441 (needs more bandwidth for same time width).
  • Interpretation: pulse shape affects required optical/electrical bandwidth and dispersion sensitivity.
Final Answer (copyable)
Final results (for f(t)=sech(t/τ), Appendix-A convention F(ν)=∫f(t)e^{-j2πνt}dt):
(a) I(t)∝sech²(t/τ) ⇒ Δt_FWHM = 2τ arcosh(√2) = 2τ ln(√2+1) ≈ 1.763τ.
(b) F(ν)=πτ sech(π²τν) ⇒ S(ν)∝sech²(π²τν) ⇒ Δν_FWHM = 2 arcosh(√2)/(π²τ) ≈ 0.1786/τ (Hz).
(c) Time–bandwidth: sech² gives ΔtΔν≈0.315, Gaussian gives ΔtΔν≈0.441 (for intensity FWHM).

Sanity checks

Units / dimensions must pass

  • Δt has units of seconds because it is proportional to τ (seconds).
  • Δν has units of Hz because it is proportional to 1/τ.

Limiting behavior physics intuition

  • If τ → 0, pulse becomes extremely short: Δt → 0 and Δν → ∞.
  • If τ → ∞, pulse becomes quasi-continuous: Δt → ∞ and Δν → 0.

Interpretation plain language

The constants (1.763 and 0.1786) are “shape fingerprints.” If your measured pulse deviates strongly, it is likely chirped, filtered, truncated, or not actually sech²-shaped.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpreting the final formulas

  • ΔtFWHM = 2τ arcosh(√2): τ is the fundamental time scale; the constant 2arcosh(√2) is purely due to the sech² shape.
  • S(ν) ∝ sech²(π²τν): the argument π²τν shows that scaling time by τ scales frequency by 1/τ, with additional π factors from the Fourier kernel.
  • ΔνFWHM = 2 arcosh(√2)/(π²τ): bandwidth requirement grows inversely with τ; shorter pulses demand wider passbands.

Parameter dependence (what happens if you change τ?)

  • If τ increases by 20%, then Δt increases by 20% and Δν decreases by about 16.7% (since Δν ∝ 1/τ).
  • Because ΔtΔν is constant for a given shape (here ≈0.315), improving time resolution forces bandwidth expense.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of using a transform table, you can derive the integral for ∫ sech(u)e^{-jωu}du via complex analysis: sech(z) has simple poles at z = jπ/2 + jπk. Summing residues yields the same closed-form π sech(πω/2). This is a useful method when you encounter transforms not present in tables.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: If you measure Δt and Δν and find ΔtΔν ≈ 0.31, what pulse shape is suggested?
    A: A transform-limited sech²-like pulse (close to the analytical constant 0.315).
  • Q: Why does the spectrum involve π²?
    A: One π comes from the sech transform identity, and another from using e^{-j2πνt} rather than e^{-jωt}.
  • Q: What would chirp do to these results?
    A: Chirp typically increases spectral width for the same time width (or increases time width after propagation), so ΔtΔν becomes larger than the transform-limited constant.
  • Q: Is intensity FWHM the same as field FWHM?
    A: No. Intensity is squared magnitude; its half-maximum occurs at a different point than the field’s half-maximum.

Axes and units
