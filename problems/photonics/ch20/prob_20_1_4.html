Combined Acousto‑Optic and Electro‑Optic Modulation

A guided, theory‑first solution to a classic wave‑interaction problem involving Bragg diffraction, piezoelectricity, and time‑dependent optical spectra.

Quick Summary

  • The problem studies light scattering from an acoustic wave inside a piezoelectric crystal.
  • A microwave field excites an acoustic wave via the piezoelectric effect.
  • The acoustic wave forms a moving diffraction grating (acousto‑optic effect).
  • The same microwave field also directly modulates the refractive index (electro‑optic effect).
  • The Bragg condition determines the reflection angle.
  • The reflected light spectrum contains frequency‑shifted sidebands.
  • Short microwave pulses lead to time‑dependent spectral evolution.

PART 0 — Concept Primer

Core Definitions

  • Acoustic wave: A propagating density and strain modulation with velocity vs.
  • Bragg diffraction: Coherent scattering from a periodic structure when momentum is conserved.
  • Electro‑optic effect: Change of refractive index due to an applied electric field.
  • Acousto‑optic effect: Light scattering from a sound‑induced refractive‑index grating.

Physical Meaning

The acoustic wave periodically compresses the crystal, creating a moving refractive‑index grating. Light reflecting from this grating experiences both a change in direction and a Doppler frequency shift.

Key Principles

  • Energy and momentum conservation for wave interactions.
  • Linear response: small refractive‑index modulation.
  • Plane‑wave approximation for light and sound.

Common Approximations

  • Single‑phonon scattering (first‑order Bragg diffraction).
  • Small Bragg angle.
  • Uniform acoustic wave.
What to watch for:
  • Confusing Raman‑Nath and Bragg regimes.
  • Forgetting the Doppler frequency shift.
  • Mixing optical and acoustic wavelengths.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

A lithium niobate crystal is placed in a microwave cavity. A 3 GHz electric field excites an acoustic wave via the piezoelectric effect. A He–Ne laser beam (633 nm) reflects from this acoustic wave.

Given

  • Refractive index: n = 2.3
  • Sound velocity: vs = 7.4 km/s
  • Microwave frequency: fmw = 3 GHz
  • Laser wavelength (vacuum): λ0 = 633 nm

Unknowns

  • Bragg angle
  • Optical spectrum of reflected light
  • Time‑dependent spectrum for pulsed excitation

Relevant Physics

  • Bragg diffraction from a moving grating
  • Doppler frequency shift
  • Electro‑optic phase modulation

Approach Options

  • Wave‑vector conservation: Most direct and physical.
  • Coupled‑mode theory: More general but heavier.
  • Time‑domain Fourier analysis: Useful for pulses.

Chosen approach: Wave‑vector and frequency conservation.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

  1. Compute acoustic wavelength from microwave frequency.
  2. Apply Bragg condition for reflection.
  3. Calculate Bragg angle.
  4. Determine frequency shift from moving grating.
  5. Add electro‑optic modulation sidebands.
  6. Analyze pulse‑duration effects.
Tip: Always work inside the crystal (use λ = λ0/n).

PART 3 — Full Solution

Physical Intuition

The acoustic wave acts like a mirror moving at the sound velocity. Light reflecting from it is deflected and frequency‑shifted.

Step 1: Acoustic Wavelength

The acoustic frequency equals the microwave frequency:

λs = vs / fmw

Numerically:

λs = (7.4×10³ m/s) / (3×10⁹ Hz) ≈ 2.47 μm

Step 2: Optical Wavelength in Crystal

λ = λ0 / n ≈ 275 nm

Step 3: Bragg Condition

For first‑order Bragg reflection:

2k sinθB = K

where k = 2π/λ and K = 2π/λs.

Thus:

sinθB = λ / (2λs)

Numerically:

θB ≈ 3.2°

Step 4: Frequency Spectrum

The moving grating shifts the optical frequency by ±fmw. Electro‑optic modulation adds symmetric sidebands at multiples of fmw.

freflected = f0 ± fmw

Step 5: Pulsed Excitation

A short microwave pulse produces:

  • Immediate electro‑optic sidebands.
  • Delayed acousto‑optic sidebands after sound propagation.

The spectrum evolves in time as the acoustic wave builds and decays.

Sanity checks:
  • Angle is small → consistent with Bragg regime.
  • Frequency shift equals acoustic frequency.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

The Bragg angle depends on the ratio of optical to acoustic wavelengths. Higher microwave frequency → smaller acoustic wavelength → larger angle.

Concept Checks

  • Q: Why is the reflected light frequency‑shifted?
    A: The grating is moving.
  • Q: Why do electro‑optic sidebands appear instantly?
    A: They depend directly on the electric field.
