Front Bragg Diffraction in a Uniaxial Crystal

A complete, theory-first derivation with physical intuition, polarization analysis, and interactive visualizations.

Quick Summary

  • We analyze front (backward) Bragg diffraction of light by an acoustic wave in a uniaxial crystal.
  • The acoustic wave forms a moving refractive-index grating with period Λ.
  • Bragg diffraction is governed by momentum conservation between optical and acoustic waves.
  • Anisotropy allows polarization conversion between extraordinary and ordinary waves.
  • The Bragg condition links the optical vacuum wavelength λ₀ to Λ and refractive indices.
  • Final result: a symbolic Bragg condition and a numerical acoustic wavelength.

PART 0 — Concept Primer

Core Definitions

  • Uniaxial crystal: one optic axis (z) with refractive indices no (ordinary) and ne (extraordinary).
  • Acoustic wave: elastic wave of wavelength Λ producing a periodic strain field.
  • Bragg diffraction: coherent scattering from a periodic structure when phase matching is satisfied.

Physical Meaning

The acoustic wave modulates the refractive index via the photoelastic effect, creating a moving diffraction grating. Light interacting with this grating can exchange momentum with the acoustic wave.

Key Principle

Momentum conservation (phase matching):
The difference between incident and diffracted optical wavevectors must equal the acoustic wavevector.

Models & Assumptions

  • Plane waves (no beam divergence).
  • First-order Bragg diffraction.
  • Weak acousto-optic interaction (single scattering).
  • Propagation along a principal crystal axis.

Mini Intuition

  • A shorter acoustic wavelength means a “tighter” grating → higher optical momentum change.
  • Backward diffraction requires roughly twice the optical wavevector.

What to Watch For

  • Using vacuum vs. in-crystal wavelength.
  • Forgetting polarization-dependent refractive indices.
  • Sign errors in backward scattering geometry.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

A transverse acoustic wave of wavelength Λ travels along the x-direction in a uniaxial crystal. The optic axis is along z. An optical wave of vacuum wavelength λ₀ propagates along x and is polarized along z.

Given

  • λ₀ = 633 nm
  • ne = 2.200
  • no = 2.286

Unknowns

  • Bragg condition for λ₀
  • Polarization of the reflected wave
  • Acoustic wavelength Λ

Relevant Physics

  • Wavevector matching (Bragg condition)
  • Anisotropic wave propagation
  • Photoelastic coupling between polarizations

Possible Approaches

  • Geometric Bragg planes: intuitive but less general.
  • Wavevector conservation: systematic and exact.
  • Coupled-mode theory: powerful but unnecessary here.

Chosen approach: wavevector conservation, because it naturally incorporates anisotropy and polarization.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

  1. Identify optical wavevectors inside the crystal.
  2. Determine acoustic wavevector magnitude.
  3. Apply backward Bragg phase-matching.
  4. Account for polarization-dependent refractive indices.
  5. Solve symbolically for λ₀.
  6. Insert numerical values.
Tip: For backward diffraction, the diffracted wavevector reverses direction.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Qualitative Expectation

Backward diffraction requires the acoustic grating to supply nearly twice the optical momentum. Because the crystal is anisotropic, the diffracted wave can emerge with a different polarization.

Wavevectors

Incident extraordinary wave (polarized along z):

ki = 2π ne / λ₀

Reflected ordinary wave (polarized along y):

kd = 2π no / λ₀

Acoustic wavevector:

K = 2π / Λ

Bragg Condition (Backward)

For front (backward) Bragg diffraction:

K = ki + kd

Substituting:

2π / Λ = 2π (ne + no) / λ₀

Final Symbolic Result

Bragg condition:
λ₀ = (ne + no) Λ

Numerical Evaluation

Λ = λ₀ / (ne + no) = 633 nm / 4.486 ≈ 141 nm

Polarization of Reflected Wave

The transverse acoustic wave couples extraordinary and ordinary modes. Thus, the front-reflected wave is ordinary polarized (along y).

Sanity Checks

  • Units: nm / dimensionless → nm ✔
  • Larger indices → smaller Λ ✔
  • Backward geometry → sum of wavevectors ✔

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

Interpreting the Formula

Both refractive indices contribute equally: the acoustic wave must bridge the momentum gap between two different optical modes.

Parameter Dependence

  • Increasing Λ shifts Bragg resonance to longer λ₀.
  • Stronger birefringence increases polarization selectivity.

Alternative View

One may derive the same condition using energy–momentum diagrams or coupled-wave equations.

Concept Checks

  • Q: Why not use 2nΛ? A: Because polarization changes.
  • Q: Would isotropic media allow this? A: No polarization conversion.
