PART 0 — Concept Primer

Core Definitions

  • Acousto‑optic effect: modulation of refractive index by an acoustic wave.
  • Bragg regime: diffraction from a thick, moving phase grating.
  • Acoustic intensity Is (W·cm⁻²): power carried by the sound wave.
  • Figure of merit ℳ: material efficiency parameter.

Key Equations

Δn = √(½ ℳ Iₛ)
ℳ = p² n⁶ / (ρ vₐ³)

Physical Meaning

A traveling sound wave periodically compresses the crystal, creating a moving refractive‑index grating that diffracts light. Stronger sound → larger Δn → higher diffraction efficiency.

Validity: Linear elasticity, small index modulation, Bragg diffraction (single order).

What to Watch For

  • Confusing Raman‑Nath and Bragg regimes
  • Forgetting wavelength inside the medium (λ = λ₀/n)
  • Ignoring material transparency limits

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

Restated Problem

Evaluate CaF₂, fused silica, TiO₂, LiNbO₃, Si, and Ge as Bragg‑cell materials by comparing transparency, acousto‑optic strength, Bragg angles, and diffraction efficiency.

Given

  • λ₀ = 2.5 μm
  • f = 100 MHz
  • Iₛ = 10–100 W·cm⁻²
  • Interaction length = 1 mm

Unknowns

  • Δn for each material
  • Internal and external Bragg angles
  • Reflectance ℜ

Applicable Physics

  • Elastic wave propagation
  • Bragg diffraction condition
  • Coupled‑wave theory

Chosen Approach

Use analytical Bragg‑regime formulas to compare materials on equal footing.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

  1. Identify transparency window for each material.
  2. Compute ℳ from material parameters.
  3. Calculate Δn from acoustic intensity.
  4. Determine acoustic wavelength Λ = vₐ / f.
  5. Apply Bragg condition to find θ.
  6. Compute diffraction efficiency.
Tip: Always convert λ₀ to wavelength inside the medium.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Physical Expectation

High‑index, slow‑sound materials should perform best, provided they are transparent at the operating wavelength.

Bragg Condition

sin θ_B = λ₀ f / (2 n vₐ)

Diffraction Efficiency

ℜ = sin²(π Δn L / (λ cos θ_B))

Final Result

Best materials:
• Visible (0.532 μm): LiNbO₃, TiO₂
• Near‑IR (1.06 μm): LiNbO₃, fused silica
• Mid‑IR (10.6 μm): Ge, ZnSe‑class semiconductors

Sanity Checks

  • ℳ has units s³·kg⁻¹
  • ℜ → 0 as Iₛ → 0
  • Higher n increases efficiency

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

  • ℳ strongly favors high refractive index.
  • Low acoustic velocity dramatically boosts efficiency.
  • Transparency ultimately limits usable wavelength.

Concept Checks

  • Q: Why not always use Ge?
    A: Opaque in visible.
  • Q: Why Bragg regime?
    A: High efficiency, single order.
