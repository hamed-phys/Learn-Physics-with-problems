Frequency Shifting with a Bragg Reflector

Designing an acousto‑optic system that converts a monochromatic optical wave into a cosine‑modulated wave using up‑ and down‑shifted Bragg reflections.

Quick Summary

  • The problem asks how to convert a single‑frequency optical wave into an amplitude‑modulated wave.
  • The key physics idea is Doppler frequency shifting by a traveling acoustic grating (Bragg diffraction).
  • An acousto‑optic cell can generate optical sidebands at frequencies ω ± Ω.
  • By coherently recombining equal up‑shifted and down‑shifted waves, a cosine modulation envelope appears.
  • The governing relation is the superposition of two frequency‑shifted complex exponentials.
  • The final result is a symbolic field expression showing amplitude modulation at frequency Ω.

PART 0 — Concept Primer

Core Definitions

  • Optical angular frequency (ω): carrier frequency of the light wave (rad/s).
  • Acoustic angular frequency (Ω): frequency of the sound wave driving the acousto‑optic cell (rad/s).
  • Acousto‑optic cell: a medium where a traveling acoustic wave creates a moving refractive‑index grating.
  • Bragg diffraction: coherent scattering of light from a periodic structure when the Bragg condition is satisfied.

Physical Meaning

The acoustic wave acts like a moving diffraction grating. When light is Bragg‑diffracted from this moving grating, it experiences a Doppler shift. Depending on geometry, the optical frequency is shifted up or down by the acoustic frequency Ω.

Key Principles

  • Energy and momentum conservation between photons and phonons.
  • Linear superposition of coherent optical fields.
  • Validity: weak diffraction (first order), steady acoustic wave, coherent optics.

Mini Intuition Example

If you add two pure tones at frequencies ω+Ω and ω−Ω with equal amplitudes, the result is a carrier at ω whose amplitude oscillates at Ω. This is exactly how amplitude modulation arises.

What to watch for:
  • Unequal amplitudes destroy pure cosine modulation.
  • Loss of phase coherence prevents clean interference.
  • Confusing intensity modulation with field modulation.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

We are given a monochromatic optical field

U(t) = A · exp(j ω t)

and an acoustic wave in an acousto‑optic cell:

s(x,t) = S₀ · cos(Ω t − q x)

Goal

Devise a system that outputs

U_out(t) = A · cos(Ω t) · exp(j ω t)

Relevant Physics

  • Bragg diffraction produces frequency‑shifted optical waves.
  • Superposition of coherent waves leads to modulation.

Possible Approaches

  • Single‑pass AOM: produces only one sideband (insufficient).
  • Two AOMs: flexible but hardware‑heavy.
  • Single AOM + Bragg reflector: elegant and minimal.

We choose the last approach because it naturally yields both up‑ and down‑shifted components from one device.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

  1. Split the input optical wave into two coherent paths.
  2. Send one path through the AOM to obtain an up‑shifted wave.
  3. Reflect the other path so it traverses the AOM in reverse, yielding a down‑shifted wave.
  4. Ensure equal amplitudes and phase coherence.
  5. Recombine the two waves interferometrically.
  6. Use superposition to obtain the final field.
Tip: Always track frequency shifts relative to the original carrier.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Qualitative Expectation

Combining equal optical fields at ω+Ω and ω−Ω should yield a carrier at ω whose amplitude oscillates at Ω.

Step‑by‑Step Derivation

After Bragg diffraction, the two fields are:

U₊(t) = (A/2) · exp[j(ω + Ω)t] U₋(t) = (A/2) · exp[j(ω − Ω)t]

The total field is their sum:

U_out(t) = (A/2)[exp(j(ω+Ω)t) + exp(j(ω−Ω)t)]

Factor out exp(jωt):

U_out(t) = A · exp(jωt) · cos(Ω t)
Final Result:
U_out(t) = A · cos(Ω t) · exp(j ω t)

Sanity Checks

  • Units: argument of cosine is dimensionless.
  • If Ω → 0, modulation disappears.
  • Amplitude oscillates between ±A as expected.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

The cosine term controls the envelope, while exp(jωt) carries the optical phase. Changing Ω changes how fast the envelope oscillates.

Concept Checks

  • Q: Why are two sidebands needed? A: One sideband alone cannot produce amplitude modulation.
  • Q: What if amplitudes differ? A: The modulation becomes imperfect.
