Quick Summary

  • What it’s about: A normally incident laser beam enters a uniaxial crystal whose optic axis is tilted relative to propagation, causing double refraction into ordinary (o) and extraordinary (e) waves.
  • Key physics idea: The ordinary wave sees constant index no, while the extraordinary wave sees an angle-dependent effective index ne,eff and its energy flow (ray) walks off from its wave-normal direction.
  • Governing equations: Effective extraordinary index and phase retardation:
    Key equation (copyable) 1/(n_e,eff^2) = (cos^2θ)/(n_e^2) + (sin^2θ)/(n_o^2) Δφ = (2π/λ0) (n_o - n_e,eff) L
  • Result type: Numeric (all parameters given). Physically: beam splitting produces a measurable lateral separation; birefringence produces large phase delay between orthogonal polarizations.
  • Main numeric results (part a): ne,eff ≈ 2.23; walk-off ρ ≈ 3.1°; lateral separation ≈ 0.54 mm; retardation ≈ 6.16×10³ rad (≈ 980 waves).
  • Electro-optic effect (part b): E ‖ optic axis changes ne (and thus ne,eff) linearly; for E = 30 V/m the extra phase shift is tiny (≈ 4.6×10⁻⁴ rad).
  • Applications: With practical fields (kV/mm range), the device becomes a controllable phase modulator, polarization modulator, or electro-optic switch; with beam separation it can act as a polarization beam splitter + EO phase control.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core idea
In a uniaxial crystal, the dielectric response depends on direction relative to the optic axis. A wave decomposes into two eigenpolarizations: ordinary (index no, isotropic in direction) and extraordinary (index depends on angle).
Why double refraction happens
Boundary conditions allow two refracted waves with different phase velocities and polarizations. Their phase fronts propagate differently, and the extraordinary wave’s energy flow (Poynting vector) generally is not collinear with its wave-normal.

Key laws / principles & validity conditions

  • Wave eigenmodes in anisotropic media: For a given propagation direction, the crystal supports two orthogonal polarization eigenstates with distinct refractive indices.
  • Extraordinary effective index (uniaxial): The extraordinary wave sees an angular-dependent index due to the index ellipsoid.
  • Ray (energy) walk-off: In anisotropic media, the Poynting vector is generally not parallel to the wavevector (except special directions).
  • Pockels effect: Applied E linearly perturbs the impermeability tensor (index ellipsoid), so Δ(1/n²) ∝ r·E. Valid for fields small enough to remain linear and below breakdown.

Common models/approximations (and why)

  • Plane-wave / geometric-optics view: Lets us compute phase delay and ray direction through a uniform slab without diffraction complications.
  • Normal incidence on the plate: Avoids Snell-angle complications at entry/exit; splitting comes from anisotropy, not refraction angle at the interface.
  • Small-angle walk-off: Here ρ is a few degrees, so d ≈ L tanρ is accurate and easy.
  • Small electro-optic perturbation: For modest E, compute Δn to first order (linearization).

Mini intuition examples (conceptual)

  • θ = 0° (propagate along optic axis): ordinary and extraordinary indices become equal in propagation behavior (no birefringent splitting).
  • θ = 90° (propagate perpendicular to optic axis): extraordinary wave reaches maximum distinction from ordinary; birefringence strongest (in many materials).
  • Increase thickness L: both lateral separation (∝ L) and phase delay (∝ L) increase linearly.
What to watch for (pitfalls)
  • Do not use ne directly for phase delay when the optic axis is tilted; use ne,eff(θ).
  • Walk-off concerns the ray direction (energy flow), not the phase velocity direction.
  • Use vacuum wavelength λ₀ in Δφ = (2π/λ₀) n L.
  • For EO effects, the perturbation is naturally expressed in Δ(1/n²), not Δn directly.
Optional deeper note: where ne,eff(θ) comes from

In a uniaxial crystal (optic axis = “extraordinary axis”), the refractive index surface for the extraordinary wave is an ellipsoid. Intersecting that surface with the plane containing the propagation direction yields the phase index for that direction:

Index-ellipsoid result (uniaxial, phase index) 1/(n_e,eff^2) = (cos^2θ)/(n_e^2) + (sin^2θ)/(n_o^2)

Here θ is the angle between the optic axis and the wave-normal (propagation direction). This is standard for uniaxial crystals.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Rewrite the problem (in plain words)

An unpolarized He–Ne beam (633 nm) passes normally through a 1-cm thick LiNbO₃ plate. The optic axis lies in the plane of incidence (here the plane containing the beam and optic axis) and is tilted by 45° relative to the beam direction. Because the crystal is birefringent, the transmitted light splits into an ordinary and an extraordinary beam. We must find: (a) how far apart the two beams emerge laterally, and the phase retardation between them; (b) how an electric field of 30 V/m applied parallel to the optic axis affects the transmitted beams and what devices this enables.

Given quantities

QuantityValueNotes
Vacuum wavelengthλ₀ = 633 nmHe–Ne
ThicknessL = 1 cm = 0.01 mUniform slab
Ordinary indexno = 2.29At 633 nm (given)
Extraordinary principal indexne = 2.17At 633 nm (given)
Electro-optic coefficientsr33 = 30.9 pm/V, r13 = 8.6 pm/VLiNbO₃, relevant for E ‖ optic axis
GeometryBeam ⟂ plate; optic axis 45° to beamθ = 45° internal
Applied fieldE = 30 V/mParallel to optic axis

Unknowns

  • Lateral displacement between emerging o- and e-beams (distance on exit face / far field just after exit).
  • Phase retardation Δφ between o and e after traversing L.
  • How E modifies indices / phase and thus the transmitted beams.

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Uniaxial birefringence: LiNbO₃ supports o and e modes for this geometry; unpolarized input excites both, so two transmitted components exist.
  • Effective extraordinary index: optic axis is not perpendicular/parallel; therefore extraordinary phase index depends on θ = 45°.
  • Walk-off: extraordinary ray direction differs from wave-normal, creating lateral separation across thickness L.
  • Electro-optic effect: E ‖ optic axis changes the index ellipsoid (Pockels), modifying n values and hence Δφ (and slightly the walk-off).

What does not dominate here

  • Interface refraction (Snell): beam is normally incident on the plate, so entry/exit refraction does not create angular splitting in isotropic sense.
  • Diffraction: not needed for mm-scale lateral shifts in a 1-cm slab (geometric optics is enough).
  • Nonlinear optics: fields are tiny; only linear EO perturbation is relevant.
Assumptions
  • Uniform crystal properties, no spatial gradients in index except anisotropy.
  • Normal incidence; the wave-normal inside is along the slab normal.
  • Neglect absorption/scattering; treat as lossless.
  • Linear EO regime; small Δn.

Possible approaches (compare & choose)

Approach A — Index ellipsoid + walk-off formula (fast, standard)
  • Compute ne,eff(θ) from uniaxial relation.
  • Compute walk-off ρ via known uniaxial expression.
  • Compute Δφ from Δn·L.
Pros: direct, minimal algebra, best for slab geometry. Cons: requires knowing the walk-off expression.
Approach B — Wavevector surface + group velocity direction (more fundamental)
  • Use dispersion surface and compute group velocity direction from gradient.
  • Extract walk-off and phase delay.
Pros: conceptually deep. Cons: heavier math than needed here.
Chosen approach
We use Approach A because the problem supplies no, ne, and EO coefficients and asks for slab-level outputs (separation and retardation). It’s the cleanest route.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Set geometry: identify θ = 45° between optic axis and propagation direction.
  2. Compute ne,eff: use uniaxial effective-index relation for extraordinary phase index.
  3. Compute birefringence: Δn = no − ne,eff; interpret as phase-velocity difference.
  4. Compute walk-off ρ: use uniaxial walk-off formula; interpret as energy-flow tilt for e-ray.
  5. Lateral separation: d = L tanρ (ordinary travels straight; extraordinary drifts sideways).
  6. Phase retardation: Δφ = (2π/λ₀)(Δn)L; interpret as relative phase between orthogonal polarizations at exit.
  7. Electro-optic perturbation: compute Δ(1/n²) = r·E, convert to Δn, propagate to Δφ and (optionally) ρ.
  8. Conclude applications: phase modulator, polarization modulator, EO switch; note required field scales.
Common mistakes
  • Using Δn = no − ne instead of no − ne,eff(θ).
  • Confusing walk-off angle ρ with θ (optic-axis angle).
  • Forgetting that phase retardation uses vacuum wavelength λ₀.
  • Applying Pockels effect directly as Δn = rE (incorrect); the primary relation is in Δ(1/n²).

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before numbers)

Since no ≠ ne, the two polarization eigenmodes accumulate different phase. Because the optic axis is tilted, the extraordinary wave is “mixed” between ne and no and will have an effective index between them. The extraordinary ray should walk off by a few degrees, producing a lateral shift on the order of L × (few degrees) ≈ 0.01 m × 0.05 ≈ 5×10⁻⁴ m (sub-mm), while the phase retardation could be very large because (2π/λ₀) is huge.

(a) Effective extraordinary index ne,eff(θ)

For a uniaxial crystal, the extraordinary phase index for a wave whose wave-normal makes angle θ to the optic axis is:

Effective extraordinary index 1/(n_e,eff^2) = (cos^2θ)/(n_e^2) + (sin^2θ)/(n_o^2)

Here θ = 45°, so cos²θ = sin²θ = 1/2:

θ = 45° specialization 1/(n_e,eff^2) = (1/2)(1/n_e^2 + 1/n_o^2)

Insert ne = 2.17, no = 2.29:

  • ne² = 2.17² = 4.7089 ⇒ 1/ne² ≈ 0.2124
  • no² = 2.29² = 5.2441 ⇒ 1/no² ≈ 0.1908
  • Average: (0.2124 + 0.1908)/2 = 0.2016
  • Thus ne,eff² = 1/0.2016 ≈ 4.960 ⇒ ne,eff ≈ 2.228
Result
ne,eff(45°) ≈ 2.23 (between ne = 2.17 and no = 2.29, as expected).

(a) Birefringence relevant for retardation

The ordinary wave accumulates phase with no while the extraordinary accumulates phase with ne,eff. The effective birefringence for this geometry is therefore:

Geometry-specific birefringence Δn = n_o - n_e,eff(θ)

Numerically:

  • Δn = 2.29 − 2.228 = 0.062

(a) Extraordinary ray walk-off angle ρ and lateral displacement

In a uniaxial crystal, the extraordinary ray (energy flow) is tilted relative to the wave-normal by the walk-off angle ρ. A standard uniaxial expression is:

Walk-off angle (uniaxial, extraordinary ray) tanρ = ((n_o^2 - n_e^2) sinθ cosθ) / (n_e^2 cos^2θ + n_o^2 sin^2θ)

This has the right qualitative behavior: ρ = 0 if θ = 0° or 90°, and grows with (no² − ne²). For θ = 45°, sinθ cosθ = 1/2 and sin²θ = cos²θ = 1/2:

θ = 45° simplification tanρ = ( (n_o^2 - n_e^2)/2 ) / ( (n_e^2 + n_o^2)/2 ) = (n_o^2 - n_e^2)/(n_e^2 + n_o^2)

Compute:

  • no² − ne² = 5.2441 − 4.7089 = 0.5352
  • no² + ne² = 5.2441 + 4.7089 = 9.9530
  • tanρ = 0.5352 / 9.9530 = 0.0538
  • ρ ≈ arctan(0.0538) ≈ 3.08° (0.0538 rad to good accuracy)

The ordinary ray travels along the slab normal (no walk-off), while the extraordinary ray drifts sideways. The lateral displacement on exiting a thickness L is:

Lateral beam separation d = L tanρ

Insert L = 0.01 m and tanρ = 0.0538:

  • d = 0.01 × 0.0538 = 5.38×10⁻⁴ m = 0.538 mm
Beam separation (part a)
d ≈ 5.4×10⁻⁴ m ≈ 0.54 mm (extraordinary beam laterally displaced relative to ordinary).

(a) Phase retardation between ordinary and extraordinary beams

Phase accumulated over length L in a medium is (2π/λ₀) n L. The relative phase (retardation) is:

Retardation Δφ = (2π/λ0) (n_o - n_e,eff) L = (2π/λ0) Δn L

Compute Δn L:

  • Δn L = 0.062 × 0.01 = 6.20×10⁻⁴ m (optical path difference in meters of vacuum wavelength scaling)

Convert to phase using λ₀ = 633 nm = 6.33×10⁻⁷ m:

  • (Δn L)/λ₀ = (6.20×10⁻⁴)/(6.33×10⁻⁷) ≈ 979.5 waves
  • Δφ = 2π × 979.5 ≈ 6.16×10³ rad
Retardation (part a)
Δφ ≈ 6.16×10³ rad (equivalently ≈ 980 wavelengths of phase delay between o and e).

Sanity checks

  • Units: Δφ is dimensionless because (2π/λ₀) has units 1/m and Δn L has units m.
  • Magnitude: phase delay ≫ 2π is expected for cm-thick crystals at visible wavelengths even for modest birefringence.
  • Limiting cases: If θ→0°, ne,eff→ne and ρ→0 (no lateral splitting). If L→0, both d and Δφ→0.
  • Direction: Ordinary beam exits centered; extraordinary exits shifted in the plane defined by beam and optic axis (the plane of incidence in the crystal).

(b) Effect of an electric field E parallel to the optic axis

With E applied along the optic axis (the “3” axis in standard LiNbO₃ notation), the index ellipsoid changes via the Pockels effect. The natural first-order relation is:

Pockels effect expressed in impermeability change Δ(1/n_i^2) = r_i3 E (for E along optic axis)

For LiNbO₃, the dominant changes for indices are commonly summarized (for E along optic axis) as:

Approximate index changes (small-signal) Δn_e ≈ -(1/2) n_e^3 r_33 E Δn_o ≈ -(1/2) n_o^3 r_13 E

Numerical estimate for E = 30 V/m:

  • r33 = 30.9 pm/V = 30.9×10⁻¹² m/V
  • r13 = 8.6 pm/V = 8.6×10⁻¹² m/V

Extraordinary principal index change

  • ne³ = 2.17³ ≈ 10.22
  • Δne ≈ −(1/2)(10.22)(30.9×10⁻¹²)(30)
  • Δne ≈ −4.74×10⁻⁹

Ordinary index change

  • no³ = 2.29³ ≈ 12.01
  • Δno ≈ −(1/2)(12.01)(8.6×10⁻¹²)(30)
  • Δno ≈ −1.55×10⁻⁹

These are extremely small changes. The effective extraordinary index ne,eff also changes slightly because it depends on both ne and no. A good first-order estimate is that the birefringence Δn changes by a few ×10⁻⁹, so the extra phase shift is:

Extra phase shift estimate from EO perturbation Δφ_EO ≈ (2π/λ0) (Δn_EO) L

Using a representative ΔnEO ≈ (Δno − Δne) ≈ (+3.2×10⁻⁹):

  • (ΔnEO L)/λ₀ ≈ (3.2×10⁻⁹×0.01)/(6.33×10⁻⁷) ≈ 5.1×10⁻⁵ waves
  • ΔφEO ≈ 2π×5.1×10⁻⁵ ≈ 3.2×10⁻⁴ rad (same order as 10⁻⁴–10⁻³ rad)
Effect at E = 30 V/m
The applied field changes the indices by only a few parts in 10⁹, producing an extra retardation of order 10⁻⁴ rad. Practically, the transmitted beams are essentially unchanged at this field level.
Optional deeper note: what would happen at realistic modulator fields?

Electro-optic modulators typically use much larger fields (often 10⁵–10⁷ V/m) created by electrodes across sub-mm gaps. Since Δn ∝ E, increasing E by 10⁶ multiplies the phase shift by 10⁶, turning a tiny 10⁻⁴ rad effect into ~10² rad, which is more than enough for a controllable half-wave (π) phase shift when designed appropriately.

Applications of this device

  • Phase modulator: Use EO-induced Δφ to modulate the relative phase between polarization components (requires larger E in practice).
  • Polarization modulator / waveplate: With suitable thickness and voltage, acts as a tunable quarter-wave or half-wave plate.
  • Electro-optic switch: Combine with polarizers/analyzers to convert phase modulation into intensity modulation.
  • Beam steering / splitting optics: Double refraction provides spatial separation of polarization eigenmodes, useful for polarization beam splitting and diagnostics.
  • Interferometric sensors: Phase sensitivity enables electric-field sensing (with higher fields) and precision metrology.
Final answer (copyable)
Final numeric results (parts a & b) Given: λ0 = 633 nm, L = 1 cm, n_o = 2.29, n_e = 2.17, θ = 45°. (a) Effective extraordinary index: n_e,eff ≈ 2.23. Walk-off: tanρ = (n_o^2 - n_e^2)/(n_o^2 + n_e^2) = 0.0538 ⇒ ρ ≈ 3.08°. Lateral displacement: d = L tanρ ≈ 0.01 × 0.0538 = 5.4×10^-4 m ≈ 0.54 mm. Retardation: Δn = n_o − n_e,eff ≈ 0.062 Δφ = (2π/λ0) Δn L ≈ 2π × 979.5 ≈ 6.16×10^3 rad (≈ 980 waves). (b) Electro-optic effect for E = 30 V/m parallel to optic axis: Δn_e ≈ −(1/2) n_e^3 r_33 E ≈ −4.7×10^-9 Δn_o ≈ −(1/2) n_o^3 r_13 E ≈ −1.6×10^-9 So the additional retardation is only ~10^-4 to 10^-3 rad (negligible here). With larger practical fields, the device functions as a voltage-controlled phase/polarization modulator or EO switch.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the formulas: what controls what?

  • ne,eff(θ): smoothly interpolates between ne (θ→0) and no (θ→90). It controls the extraordinary phase velocity.
  • Δφ ∝ (no − ne,eff)L/λ₀: phase retardation grows linearly with thickness and inversely with wavelength.
  • ρ depends on (no² − ne²): stronger anisotropy → stronger walk-off → larger beam separation.
  • EO effect: Δn ∝ rE; bigger r and bigger E give bigger modulation. Geometry (electrode spacing) matters more than plate thickness for achievable E.

Parameter dependence (qualitative + useful scaling)

  • Thickness L: d scales as ~L, Δφ scales as L.
  • Wavelength λ₀: Δφ scales as 1/λ₀; shorter wavelength gives larger phase delay for same crystal.
  • Angle θ: both ne,eff and ρ depend on θ; maximum walk-off tends to occur around intermediate θ (not 0 or 90).
  • Electric field E: EO phase shift scales linearly with E; doubling E doubles ΔφEO.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

One can derive walk-off by computing the group velocity direction from the extraordinary dispersion surface (index ellipsoid in k-space). The ray direction is normal to the wave-normal surface; taking gradients yields the same tanρ. This method generalizes to biaxial crystals and arbitrary orientations.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why does unpolarized light split into two beams? A: The crystal supports two orthogonal eigenpolarizations with different propagation properties; unpolarized light contains both.
  • Q: Why is the extraordinary index angle dependent? A: Because the dielectric tensor is anisotropic; the index ellipsoid gives direction-dependent phase velocity.
  • Q: Why can you get large Δφ but only modest lateral separation? A: Phase accumulates over ~10⁴ wavelengths in 1 cm, while walk-off is only a few degrees.
  • Q: If L were doubled, what happens? A: d doubles and Δφ doubles.
  • Q: What changes under applied E? A: The index ellipsoid (thus n’s), giving controllable phase retardation (and slightly modifying walk-off).
