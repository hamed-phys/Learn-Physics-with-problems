A) Damping type from the displacement‚Äìtime plot

classification

Physical intuition

An underdamped oscillator continues to oscillate while its amplitude decays. A critically damped or overdamped oscillator returns to equilibrium without oscillating (no repeated zero crossings).

What the graph shows

  • The displacement crosses the equilibrium line multiple times.
  • Peaks and troughs shrink in magnitude over time (decaying envelope).

That behavior is the hallmark of underdamped motion.

‚úÖ Boxed result

\(\boxed{\text{A) The oscillator is underdamped.}}\)

Sanity checks: Underdamped ‚Üí oscillations with decay ‚úÖ; Over/critical ‚Üí no oscillations ‚ùå.

B) Beat period of \(y_1(t)+y_2(t)\)

superposition

Physical intuition

Two close frequencies superpose to produce an amplitude envelope that grows and shrinks: beats. The beat rate is governed by the difference in frequencies.

Step-by-step derivation

\[ y_1(t)=A\cos(3\pi t),\qquad y_2(t)=A\cos(4\pi t) \] Identify angular frequencies: \[ \omega_1=3\pi\ \text{rad/s},\qquad \omega_2=4\pi\ \text{rad/s} \] Beat angular frequency: \[ \omega_{\text{beat}}=|\omega_2-\omega_1|=|4\pi-3\pi|=\pi\ \text{rad/s} \] Beat period: \[ T_{\text{beat}}=\frac{2\pi}{\omega_{\text{beat}}}=\frac{2\pi}{\pi}=2\ \text{s} \]
‚úÖ Boxed result

\(\boxed{\text{B) }T_{\text{beat}}=2~\text{s}.}\)

Sanity checks: Units: seconds ‚úÖ. If \(\omega_2\to\omega_1\), then \(T_{\text{beat}}\to\infty\) ‚úÖ (beats slow down).

C) Underwater pendulum frequency vs out of water

fluids

Physical intuition

  • Drag (damping) is much larger in water ‚Üí reduces the observed underdamped frequency.
  • Buoyancy reduces the effective restoring force (effective \(g\) smaller) ‚Üí lowers frequency.
  • Added mass (entrained fluid) increases effective inertia ‚Üí lowers frequency.

Removing the system from water reduces all these effects, so the oscillation frequency should increase.

More formal argument

For an underdamped oscillator: \[ \omega_d=\sqrt{\omega_0^2-\gamma^2} \] In water, \(\gamma\) (damping rate) is larger, hence \(\omega_d\) is smaller. Also for a small-angle pendulum: \[ \omega_0 \approx \sqrt{\frac{g_{\text{eff}}}{L}} \] Water reduces \(g_{\text{eff}}\) via buoyancy and can increase effective inertia via added mass, both decreasing \(\omega_0\).
‚úÖ Boxed result

\(\boxed{\text{C) The oscillation frequency increases when taken out of water.}}\)

Limiting cases: If \(\gamma\to 0\), \(\omega_d\to\omega_0\) ‚úÖ. More damping ‚Üí smaller \(\omega_d\) ‚úÖ.
Physical interpretation: less drag + stronger effective restoring + less added inertia ‚Üí faster oscillations ‚úÖ.

D) Symmetry matrix \(S\) for the 3-mass system

linear algebra

Physical intuition

A symmetry operation is a linear transformation of the displacement vector \(\mathbf{x}=(x_1,x_2,x_3)^\mathsf{T}\). If \(\mathbf{x}'=S\mathbf{x}\), then each new component \(x_i'\) is a linear combination of \(x_1,x_2,x_3\).

Derivation

Given mapping: \[ x_1'=-x_3,\quad x_2'=-x_2,\quad x_3'=-x_1 \] Write each in coefficient form: \[ x_1' = 0\cdot x_1 + 0\cdot x_2 - 1\cdot x_3 \] \[ x_2' = 0\cdot x_1 - 1\cdot x_2 + 0\cdot x_3 \] \[ x_3' = -1\cdot x_1 + 0\cdot x_2 + 0\cdot x_3 \] Therefore: \[ S= \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \]
‚úÖ Boxed result

\(\boxed{\text{D) }S=\begin{pmatrix}0&0&-1\\0&-1&0\\-1&0&0\end{pmatrix}.}\)

Check: \(S(x_1,x_2,x_3)^\mathsf{T}=(-x_3,-x_2,-x_1)^\mathsf{T}\) ‚úÖ matches the mapping.

E) Why does a driven underdamped oscillator look large/erratic at turn-on?

transients

Physical intuition

When a drive is switched on, the system does not instantly jump to steady-state. The motion initially contains both the oscillator‚Äôs natural response and the forced response. Their superposition can create temporary amplitude modulation (beats), making motion look ‚Äúerratic.‚Äù

Rigorous explanation using solution structure

The driven damped solution can be written as \[ x(t)=x_{\text{hom}}(t)+x_{\text{part}}(t). \] Here, \[ x_{\text{hom}}(t)\propto e^{-\gamma t}\cos(\omega_d t+\phi) \] is a transient at the system‚Äôs (damped) natural frequency \(\omega_d\), while \(x_{\text{part}}(t)\) oscillates at the drive frequency \(\omega\). Right after turn-on, \(x_{\text{hom}}\) is generally nonzero due to mismatched initial conditions, so interference between \(\omega_d\) and \(\omega\) can produce large envelopes. As \(t\) increases, \(e^{-\gamma t}\) decays, leaving only the steady-state.
‚úÖ Boxed result

\(\boxed{\text{E) The early ‚Äúerratic‚Äù motion is transient (natural) + forced response interference; the transient decays exponentially, leaving steady-state.}}\)

Limiting cases: Larger \(\gamma\) ‚Üí faster decay ‚Üí shorter ‚Äúerratic‚Äù phase ‚úÖ.
Physical interpretation: not chaos‚Äîjust short-lived interference/beating ‚úÖ.

F) Electrical equivalents of \(m\) and \(k\) in a series \(LC\)

analogy

Physical intuition

Mass‚Äìspring oscillations swap kinetic and potential energy. An LC circuit swaps magnetic energy in the inductor and electric energy in the capacitor. Since both are governed by the same type of second-order ODE, parameters map by matching natural frequencies.

Derivation by matching \(\omega_0\)

Mechanical: \[ \omega_0=\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \] Series LC: \[ \omega_0=\frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} \] Matching the forms implies: \[ m \leftrightarrow L,\qquad k \leftrightarrow \frac{1}{C}. \]
‚úÖ Boxed result

\(\boxed{\text{F) } m \leftrightarrow L,\quad k \leftrightarrow \dfrac{1}{C}\ \ (\text{so } C \leftrightarrow 1/k).}\)

Check via limits: Larger \(m\) lowers \(\omega_0\); larger \(L\) lowers \(\omega_0\) ‚úÖ. Larger \(k\) raises \(\omega_0\); larger \(C\) lowers \(\omega_0\), so \(k\) must map to \(1/C\) ‚úÖ.
