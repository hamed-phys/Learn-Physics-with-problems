Standing Electromagnetic Wave Between Two Perfectly Conducting Plates

Two perfectly conducting planes at z = 0 and z = L form a 1D cavity. The electric field is along ŷ and the magnetic field along x̂. We derive the allowed normal modes, obtain Bx(z,t) using Maxwell’s equations, compute Poynting vector and energy densities, and sketch the n=2 profiles at t=0.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Conceptual + Setup

Restatement (in plain words)

We have a 1D electromagnetic cavity between two perfect conductors. The wave is standing and polarized such that: E points along ŷ and B points along x̂. The fields depend only on z and t. We must find the allowed field patterns (normal modes), then compute energy flow and storage.

Given

  • Conducting planes at z=0 and z=L.
  • E(z,t)=ŷEy(z,t), B(z,t)=x̂Bx(z,t).
  • Region is vacuum with constants ε₀, μ₀, wave speed c.

Unknowns / Targets

  • Find Ey(n)(z,t) obeying conductor boundary conditions.
  • Find corresponding Bx(n)(z,t) and mode frequency ωn.
  • Compute Poynting vector S and energy densities uE, uB + time averages.
  • Sketch shapes for n=2 at t=0.

Why These Laws Apply

Perfect conductor: tangential electric field must vanish at the surface ⇒ here Ey(0,t)=Ey(L,t)=0.

Maxwell–Faraday: spatial variation of E drives time variation of B.
Ampère–Maxwell: spatial variation of B drives time variation of E (in vacuum).

Energy relations: energy density and flux are given by u = (ε₀E²)/2 + (B²)/(2μ₀) and S = (1/μ₀)E×B.

Candidate approaches
  • Wave-equation + boundary conditions: derive allowed eigenmodes (most direct).
  • Standing wave = 2 traveling waves: enforce nodes at conductors; get k-quantization quickly.
  • Curl-equation method: start from an assumed modal E and integrate for B using Maxwell–Faraday.
Best choice

Use boundary conditions to fix the spatial form of Ey, then apply Maxwell curl equations to compute Bx and enforce the dispersion relation ω = ck. This is rigorous, clean, and minimizes guesswork.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Plan + Pitfalls

Enforce conductor boundary conditions

Make the tangential electric field vanish at both plates.

Tool: E∥ = 0 ⇒ Ey(0,t)=Ey(L,t)=0

Choose a standing-wave modal form

Write E as a product of spatial eigenfunction and time oscillation.

Tool: separation / normal modes

Quantize the wavenumbers

Use the boundary nodes to determine kn.

Tool: sin(kL)=0 ⇒ kn=nπ/L

Compute B from Maxwell–Faraday

Relate ∂E/∂z to ∂B/∂t, ensuring correct component/sign.

Tool: (∇×E)x=−∂Ey/∂z=−∂Bx/∂t

Lock in ω via Ampère–Maxwell

Ensure the fields satisfy both curl equations → ω = ck.

Tool: (∇×B)y=∂Bx/∂z=μ₀ε₀ ∂Ey/∂t

Compute S, uE, uB and time averages

Use standard EM energy expressions, average over a period.

Tool: S=(1/μ₀)E×B, uE=ε₀E²/2, uB=B²/(2μ₀), ⟨sin²⟩=⟨cos²⟩=1/2

Common mistakes

1) Forgetting that Ey is tangential to the plates ⇒ must be zero at z=0 and z=L.
2) Mixing curl components: with E=ŷEy(z,t), we have (∇×E)x=−∂Ey/∂z.
3) Standing-wave phase: E and B are typically 90° out of time phase (cos vs sin).
4) Dropping c factors: in vacuum, amplitude relation gives B₀=E₀/c.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Derivation + Results

Physical intuition

A perfect conductor forces the tangential electric field to vanish at its surface, so the cavity walls are electric-field nodes. Only wavelengths that fit an integer number of half-wavelengths between z=0 and z=L can survive. The magnetic field is tied to how E varies (in space and time), so it peaks where E’s spatial slope is large and oscillates with a quarter-period time shift. Energy sloshes between electric and magnetic storage; the time-averaged net energy flow is zero (standing wave).

(a) Electric field \(E_y(z,t)\) in the n-th normal mode

For a perfect conductor, the tangential component of \(\mathbf E\) must vanish at the surface. Here \(\mathbf E = \hat{\mathbf y} E_y(z,t)\) is tangential to both plates, so:

E_y(0,t) = 0, E_y(L,t) = 0 for all t.

Choose a separated standing-wave form that naturally creates nodes at the boundaries:

E_y(z,t) = E_{0n} sin(k z) cos(ω t).

Apply the boundary at \(z=L\): \(E_y(L,t)=0\) implies \(\sin(kL)=0\), so \(kL=n\pi\) with \(n=1,2,3,\dots\). Therefore \(k_n = n\pi/L\).

Answer (a)
Ey(n)(z,t) = E0n sin(nπ z / L) cos(ωn t),    kn = nπ/L.

(b) Magnetic field \(B_x(z,t)\) for the n-th mode

Use Maxwell–Faraday: \(\nabla \times \mathbf E = -\partial \mathbf B/\partial t\). With \(\mathbf E = \hat{\mathbf y}E_y(z,t)\) depending only on z, the x-component of the curl is:

(∇×E)_x = -∂E_y/∂z.

Since \(\mathbf B = \hat{\mathbf x}B_x(z,t)\), the x-component of the RHS is \(-∂B_x/∂t\). Therefore:

-∂E_y/∂z = -∂B_x/∂t ⇒ ∂B_x/∂t = ∂E_y/∂z.

Differentiate the mode from (a):

E_y^{(n)}(z,t) = E_{0n} sin(k_n z) cos(ω_n t) ⇒ ∂E_y/∂z = k_n E_{0n} cos(k_n z) cos(ω_n t).

Integrate in time (choosing zero static field):

∂B_x/∂t = k_n E_{0n} cos(k_n z) cos(ω_n t) ⇒ B_x^{(n)}(z,t) = (k_n/ω_n) E_{0n} cos(k_n z) sin(ω_n t).

Now enforce Ampère–Maxwell in vacuum: \(\nabla \times \mathbf B = μ_0 ε_0 \, ∂\mathbf E/∂t\). This yields the vacuum dispersion relation:

ω_n = c k_n, with c = 1/√(μ0 ε0).

Thus \(k_n/ω_n = 1/c\), giving:

Answer (b)
Bx(n)(z,t) = (E0n/c) cos(nπ z / L) sin(ωn t),
ωn = c kn = nπ c / L.

(c) Poynting vector, energy densities, and time averages

Poynting vector \(\mathbf S\)

By definition: \(\mathbf S = (1/μ_0)\mathbf E \times \mathbf B\). Here \(\mathbf E = \hat{\mathbf y}E_y\) and \(\mathbf B = \hat{\mathbf x}B_x\), so: \(\hat{\mathbf y}\times \hat{\mathbf x} = -\hat{\mathbf z}\).

S(z,t) = -(ẑ/μ0) E_y(z,t) B_x(z,t).

Substitute the mode expressions:

E_y = E0 sin(kz) cos(ωt), B_x = (E0/c) cos(kz) sin(ωt) ⇒ S = -(ẑ)(E0^2/(μ0 c)) sin(kz)cos(kz) cos(ωt)sin(ωt).

Using identities \(\sin(kz)\cos(kz)=\tfrac12\sin(2kz)\) and \(\cos(ωt)\sin(ωt)=\tfrac12\sin(2ωt)\):

Poynting vector
S(n)(z,t) = −ẑ [E0n2/(4 μ0 c)] sin(2kn z) sin(2ωn t).
⟨S⟩ = 0 (time-average over one period).

Energy densities \(u_E\) and \(u_B\)

Electric energy density: \(u_E = (ε_0/2)E^2 = (ε_0/2)E_y^2\).

u_E^{(n)}(z,t) = (ε0/2) E0n^2 sin^2(k_n z) cos^2(ω_n t).

Magnetic energy density: \(u_B = B^2/(2μ_0) = B_x^2/(2μ_0)\). With \(B_x=(E0n/c)\cos(k_n z)\sin(ω_n t)\) and \(1/(μ_0 c^2)=ε_0\), we get:

u_B^{(n)}(z,t) = (ε0/2) E0n^2 cos^2(k_n z) sin^2(ω_n t).

Time averages over a period: \(\langle \cos^2\rangle = \langle \sin^2\rangle = 1/2\).

Energy + averages
⟨uE(n)(z)⟩ = (ε0/4) E0n2 sin2(kn z)
⟨uB(n)(z)⟩ = (ε0/4) E0n2 cos2(kn z)
⟨utot⟩ = ⟨uE⟩ + ⟨uB⟩ = (ε0/4) E0n2 (uniform in z).

(d) n = 2 mode — spatial variation at t = 0

For n=2: \(k_2 = 2π/L\), \(ω_2 = ck_2 = 2πc/L\). Evaluate fields at t=0:

E_y(z,0) = E02 sin(2π z/L) cos(0) = E02 sin(2π z/L) B_x(z,0) = (E02/c) cos(2π z/L) sin(0) = 0

So at \(t=0\), magnetic field and magnetic energy are zero everywhere; all energy is electric:

u_E(z,0) = (ε0/2) E02^2 sin^2(2π z/L), u_B(z,0)=0.
n = 2 profiles at t = 0 (qualitative)
Ey(z,0) uE(z,0) ∝ E² Bx(z,0) = 0

n = 2 key features at t = 0

Ey(z,0) is a sine with two half-waves across the cavity: nodes at z=0, L/2, L; antinodes at L/4 and 3L/4.

Bx(z,0)=0 everywhere because it is proportional to sin(ωt).

Therefore uB(z,0)=0, and uE(z,0) ∝ sin²(2πz/L) has two identical energy “humps.”

Sanity Checks

Units • Limits • Meaning

Units

B amplitude: \(E/c\) ⇒ (V/m)/(m/s) = V·s/m² = Tesla ✓
S: \((1/μ_0)EB\) ⇒ W/m² ✓
u: \(ε_0E^2\) ⇒ J/m³ ✓

Limiting behavior

Larger cavity \(L\) ⇒ smaller \(k_n=nπ/L\) ⇒ lower \(\omega_n\) ✓
Higher mode \(n\) ⇒ more nodes/shorter wavelength ⇒ higher \(\omega_n\) ✓

Physical interpretation

Electric field is forced to zero at the conducting walls (tangential condition), so the cavity supports only discrete standing-wave modes. E and B are time-quadrature (cos vs sin), so energy oscillates between electric and magnetic storage. The instantaneous Poynting vector oscillates in sign, but the time-average is zero: no net power transport in a standing wave.
