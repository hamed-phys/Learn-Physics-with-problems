Problem 1 • 30 pts

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Restated Problem

A mass \(M\) hangs at rest from a vertical spring (constant \(k\)). At \(t=0\), a piece falls off, leaving \(m=\alpha M\) attached. With gravity \(g\) and motion along the \(y\)-axis (upward positive), and \(y(0)=0\), find: (a) new equilibrium position, (b) period, (c) \(y(t)\), (d) amplitude and phase, (e) energies vs time.

Given / Unknowns

  • Given: \(M,\,\alpha,\,k,\,g\); initial rest; \(y(0)=0\).
  • Unknowns: \(y_{\mathrm{eq}},\,T,\,y(t),\,A,\phi,\,K(t),\,U(t)\).
  • Need: rigorous, beginner-friendly derivation + sanity checks.

The key physics is: (i) static force balance sets equilibrium; (ii) Newton’s 2nd law gives a linear ODE; (iii) constant gravity shifts equilibrium but does not change the SHM form about equilibrium.

Relevant principles
  • Equilibrium: net force \(=0 \Rightarrow kx = mg\).
  • Dynamics: \(m\ddot{x}=mg-kx\) (linear ODE).
  • SHM about equilibrium: shift variable to remove constant \(mg\) term.
  • Energy: \(K=\tfrac12 m\dot{y}^2\); \(U\) from spring + gravity or effective SHM potential.

Approach options (and best pick)

  • Force + shift to equilibrium (best): fastest, clean SHM form.
  • Energy method: elegant but still needs \(\omega=\sqrt{k/m}\).
  • Work in extension \(x\): equivalent; just be consistent with signs.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Roadmap (5–10 steps)
  1. Old equilibrium: compute \(x_{\text{old}}\) from \(kx=Mg\).
  2. New mass: set \(m=\alpha M\).
  3. New equilibrium: compute \(x_{\text{new}}\) from \(kx=mg\).
  4. Relate \(y\) to extension: with \(y\) upward, use \(x=x_{\text{old}}-y\).
  5. Equation of motion: write \(m\ddot{x}=mg-kx\).
  6. Shift variable: let \(u=x-x_{\text{new}}\) to get \(\ddot u+\omega^2 u=0\).
  7. Initial conditions: at \(t=0\), \(x(0)=x_{\text{old}}\), \(\dot x(0)=0\).
  8. Solve: \(u(t)=u(0)\cos(\omega t)\) and convert to \(y(t)\).
  9. Amplitude/phase + energies: rewrite \(y(t)\) about equilibrium; compute \(K(t)\), \(U(t)\).

Common mistakes

  • Using \(M\) instead of \(m=\alpha M\) in \(\omega=\sqrt{k/m}\).
  • Sign errors: upward \(y\) means extension decreases with \(y\).
  • Confusing equilibrium shift with amplitude: they can be equal here but conceptually differ.
  • For potential energy, forgetting you can add an arbitrary constant (only changes reference).

PART 3 — Full Solution

Physical intuition

Initially, the spring is stretched so that its upward force balances \(Mg\). When the mass drops to \(\alpha M\), the spring is now “too stretched” for the smaller weight. The remaining mass is pulled upward and oscillates about a new, higher equilibrium.

Step 1 — Use extension \(x\) (downward positive)

Let \(x\) be the spring extension measured downward from the natural length. Then the forces are: weight \(mg\) downward and spring force \(kx\) upward. Newton’s 2nd law (downward positive) is:

Equation of motion
$$m\ddot{x}=mg-kx.$$

(a) New equilibrium position

At equilibrium, \(\ddot{x}=0\), so \(mg-kx_{\mathrm{eq}}=0\Rightarrow x_{\mathrm{eq}}=mg/k\). Before breaking:

Old vs new equilibrium (in extension)
$$x_{\text{old}}=\frac{Mg}{k},\qquad x_{\text{new}}=\frac{\alpha Mg}{k}.$$

The problem’s coordinate \(y\) is upward, with \(y(0)=0\) at the instant the system is still located at the old equilibrium. Since moving upward reduces extension, $$x(t)=x_{\text{old}}-y(t).$$ Therefore the new equilibrium position in \(y\) is

New equilibrium in y
$$y_{\mathrm{eq}}=x_{\text{old}}-x_{\text{new}}=\frac{Mg}{k}-\frac{\alpha Mg}{k} =\frac{(1-\alpha)Mg}{k}.$$

(b) Period of oscillations

Shift the coordinate to remove the constant gravity term: $$u(t)=x(t)-x_{\text{new}}.$$ Substitute \(x=u+x_{\text{new}}\) into \(m\ddot{x}=mg-kx\):

Shift to SHM
\[ m\ddot{u}=mg-k(u+x_{\text{new}}) =mg-ku-k\left(\frac{mg}{k}\right) =-ku. \] Hence \[ \ddot{u}+\frac{k}{m}u=0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \omega=\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}=\sqrt{\frac{k}{\alpha M}}. \]

Therefore $$T=\frac{2\pi}{\omega}=2\pi\sqrt{\frac{\alpha M}{k}}.$$

(c) Time dependence \(y(t)\)

At \(t=0\), position is unchanged by the break, so \(x(0)=x_{\text{old}}\), and the mass was at rest so \(\dot{x}(0)=0\). Thus

Initial conditions in u
\[ u(0)=x(0)-x_{\text{new}}=\frac{Mg}{k}-\frac{\alpha Mg}{k}=\frac{(1-\alpha)Mg}{k},\qquad \dot{u}(0)=0. \]

The SHM solution is \(u(t)=u(0)\cos(\omega t)\). Then \[ x(t)=x_{\text{new}}+u(0)\cos(\omega t) =\frac{\alpha Mg}{k}+\frac{(1-\alpha)Mg}{k}\cos(\omega t). \] Convert to \(y\) using \(y=x_{\text{old}}-x\):

Position vs time
\[ y(t)=\frac{Mg}{k}-\left[\frac{\alpha Mg}{k}+\frac{(1-\alpha)Mg}{k}\cos(\omega t)\right] =\frac{(1-\alpha)Mg}{k}\Bigl(1-\cos(\omega t)\Bigr), \quad \omega=\sqrt{\frac{k}{\alpha M}}. \]

(d) Amplitude and phase

Write \(y(t)\) as equilibrium + oscillation: \[ y(t)=y_{\mathrm{eq}}-\underbrace{\frac{(1-\alpha)Mg}{k}}_{A}\cos(\omega t). \] So the amplitude about the new equilibrium is \(A=(1-\alpha)Mg/k\). If you want the standard form \(y(t)=y_{\mathrm{eq}}+A\cos(\omega t+\phi)\), note \(-\cos(\omega t)=\cos(\omega t+\pi)\), hence one valid choice is \(\phi=\pi\).

(e) Energies \(K(t)\) and \(U(t)\)

Differentiate \(y(t)\): \[ \dot{y}(t)=\frac{(1-\alpha)Mg}{k}\,\omega\,\sin(\omega t). \] Kinetic energy:

Kinetic energy
\[ K(t)=\frac12 m\dot{y}^2 =\frac12 (\alpha M)\left(\frac{(1-\alpha)Mg}{k}\right)^2\omega^2\sin^2(\omega t). \] Using \(\omega^2=\frac{k}{\alpha M}\), \[ K(t)=\frac{(1-\alpha)^2 M^2 g^2}{2k}\,\sin^2(\omega t). \]

For potential energy, the clean SHM form uses displacement from equilibrium: \(y(t)-y_{\mathrm{eq}}=-A\cos(\omega t)\). The oscillation potential is:

Oscillation potential energy about equilibrium
\[ U_{\text{osc}}(t)=\frac12 k\bigl(y(t)-y_{\mathrm{eq}}\bigr)^2 =\frac12 kA^2\cos^2(\omega t) =\frac{(1-\alpha)^2 M^2 g^2}{2k}\,\cos^2(\omega t). \]

Energy conservation (no damping)

\(K(t)+U_{\text{osc}}(t)=\dfrac{(1-\alpha)^2 M^2 g^2}{2k}\), a constant. (Any additional constant from choosing a different zero of gravitational/spring potential does not change the time dependence.)

Sanity checks

Units

  • \(y_{\mathrm{eq}}=\frac{Mg}{k}\Rightarrow \text{N}/(\text{N/m})=\text{m}\)
  • \(T=2\pi\sqrt{\frac{\alpha M}{k}}\Rightarrow \sqrt{\text{s}^2}=\text{s}\)
  • \(\frac{M^2g^2}{k}\Rightarrow \text{J}\)

Limiting cases

  • \(\alpha\to 1:\; y_{\mathrm{eq}}\to 0,\;A\to 0\) ⇒ no motion (correct).
  • \(\alpha\to 0^+:\; \omega\to\infty,\;T\to 0\) ⇒ tiny mass oscillates rapidly.
  • \(\alpha<1:\; y_{\mathrm{eq}}>0\) ⇒ equilibrium shifts upward (spring was too stretched).

Final Boxed Results

Answer set

(a) New equilibrium (in \(y\)): $$\boxed{y_{\mathrm{eq}}=\dfrac{(1-\alpha)Mg}{k}}$$

(b) Frequency and period: $$\boxed{\omega=\sqrt{\dfrac{k}{\alpha M}},\qquad T=2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{\alpha M}{k}}}$$

(c) Position vs time: $$\boxed{y(t)=\dfrac{(1-\alpha)Mg}{k}\Bigl(1-\cos(\omega t)\Bigr)}$$

(d) Amplitude and phase (one valid choice): $$\boxed{A=\dfrac{(1-\alpha)Mg}{k},\qquad \phi=\pi}$$

(e) Energies vs time: $$\boxed{K(t)=\dfrac{(1-\alpha)^2 M^2 g^2}{2k}\sin^2(\omega t),\quad U_{\text{osc}}(t)=\dfrac{(1-\alpha)^2 M^2 g^2}{2k}\cos^2(\omega t)}$$
