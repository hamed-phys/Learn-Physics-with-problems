Rewritten Problem (1.1–1.5)

You must answer five short physics questions: (1.1) small-amplitude period in a cosine potential, (1.2) infer driving vs natural frequency and damping from a time trace, (1.3) estimate bandwidth for 1 ns square pulses, (1.4) decide when electron interference appears for high/low emission rates, (1.5) compute lowest normal-mode angular frequency of a square membrane.

Core Ideas to Use

  • Small oscillations: quadratic approximation near stable equilibrium.
  • Driven damped motion: transient + steady-state; steady-state at driving frequency.
  • Fourier/time–frequency: short pulses need high-frequency components.
  • Quantum interference: coherence & no which-path info matter, not emission rate.
  • Membrane modes: quantized wave numbers in 2D; \(\omega = v\sqrt{k_x^2+k_y^2}\).
Given quantities, unknowns, and what must be found

(1.1) Cosine potential

  • Given: \(U(x)=A(1-\cos(\alpha x))\), \(A>0\), \(\alpha>0\), mass \(m\).
  • Unknowns: \(\omega_0\), \(T\).
  • Find: small-amplitude period \(T\) about equilibrium.

(1.2) Driven oscillator trace

  • Given: displacement vs time trace; sinusoidal driving of fixed amplitude.
  • Unknowns: relation \(\omega_d\) vs \(\omega_0\); damping regime.
  • Find: which statements (a–f) are true.

(1.3) Square pulses

  • Given: pulse width \(\tau = 1\,\mathrm{ns}\).
  • Unknown: bandwidth \(\Delta f\).
  • Find: approximate frequency range needed.

(1.4) Electron experiment

  • Given: electron source → two-slit-like setup → detector; rate can be high or single-electron.
  • Unknown: interference pattern presence.
  • Find: which statements (a–d) are true.

(1.5) Square membrane

  • Given: side \(L\), wave speed \(v\).
  • Unknown: \(\omega_{\min}\).
  • Find: lowest excitable normal-mode angular frequency.
Approaches (2–3) and the best choice

(1.1) Two equivalent routes

  • Taylor expansion of \(U\) near the minimum → effective \(k\).
  • Force linearization: \(F=-\frac{dU}{dx}\) near equilibrium → SHM equation.

Best: Taylor expansion is fastest and most direct for \(\omega_0\).

(1.2) Read transient vs steady state

  • Late-time oscillation is at \(\omega_d\).
  • Early-time decaying transient reveals damping and relates to natural dynamics.

Best: Compare early vs late-time behavior from the trace.

(1.3) Bandwidth estimate

  • Rule-of-thumb: \(f_{\max}\sim 1/\tau\).
  • Sinc spectrum: zeros near \(f = 1/\tau\); sharper edges need multiple harmonics.

Best: Use \(1/\tau\) as the main scale, “few ×” for fidelity.

(1.4) Coherence beats “rate”

  • Interference depends on coherence + no which-path info.
  • One-by-one electrons can still self-interfere; pattern builds statistically.

Best: Conceptual quantum reasoning (coherence/measurement).

(1.5) Normal modes on a square

  • Quantized \(k_x, k_y\) from boundary conditions.
  • \(\omega = v\sqrt{k_x^2+k_y^2}\).

Best: Standard rectangular membrane mode formula.

Minimal Step-by-Step Plan (5–10 steps)

  2. (1.1) Quadratic approximation

  3. (1.1) Extract \(\omega_0\) and \(T\)
    Goal: compute \(T\). Tool: \(\omega_0=\sqrt{k/m}\), \(T=2\pi/\omega_0\).
  4. (1.2) Read long-time period
    Goal: identify \(\omega_d\). Tool: steady-state motion is at the driving frequency.
  5. (1.2) Diagnose damping from transient
    Goal: classify damping. Tool: decaying oscillations ⇒ underdamped; no oscillations ⇒ over/critical.
  6. (1.3) Estimate bandwidth
    Goal: frequency scale. Tool: rectangular pulse spectrum \(\sim\text{sinc}(f\tau)\) and rule \(1/\tau\).
  7. (1.4) Decide interference

  8. (1.5) Lowest membrane mode
    Goal: compute \(\omega_{\min}\). Tool: \(k_x=m\pi/L\), \(k_y=n\pi/L\), \(\omega=v\sqrt{k_x^2+k_y^2}\).

Common Mistakes

  • (1.1) Mis-identifying \(k\): remember \(U\approx \tfrac12 k\xi^2\).
  • (1.2) Saying “no damping” despite a decaying transient.
  • (1.3) Treating a square pulse as a single frequency (it needs many harmonics).
  • (1.4) Thinking “one electron at a time” removes interference (it doesn’t, if coherent).
  • (1.5) Using \((m,n)=(1,0)\) for fixed edges (not allowed); lowest is \((1,1)\).

Quick Tips

  • Driven systems: late-time motion always matches the driving frequency.
  • Bandwidth: \(1\,\mathrm{ns}\Rightarrow 1\,\mathrm{GHz}\) as the key scale.
  • Interference depends on coherence/measurement, not “crowd size.”
  • Membrane modes: think of a 2D \(k\)-vector with components quantized.

PART 3 — Full Solution Intuition → Derivation → Final

1.1 — Small-amplitude period in \(U(x)=A(1-\cos(\alpha x))\)

Physical intuition: Near any stable equilibrium, the potential looks like a parabola. Parabolic potentials produce simple harmonic motion, so we only need the curvature (effective “spring constant”).
Step-by-step derivation

1) Find equilibrium points

\[ U(x)=A\bigl(1-\cos(\alpha x)\bigr),\quad \frac{dU}{dx}=A\alpha\sin(\alpha x). \] \[ \frac{dU}{dx}=0\;\Rightarrow\;\sin(\alpha x)=0\;\Rightarrow\;\alpha x_0=n\pi. \]

2) Determine which equilibria are stable (minima)

\[ \frac{d^2U}{dx^2}=A\alpha^2\cos(\alpha x). \] Stable equilibrium requires \(U''(x_0)>0\Rightarrow \cos(\alpha x_0)>0\). The minima occur at \(\cos(\alpha x_0)=1\Rightarrow \alpha x_0=2\pi n\), so \[ x_0=\frac{2\pi n}{\alpha}. \]

3) Expand \(U(x)\) near a minimum

Let \(\xi=x-x_0\) be small. Since \(\alpha x_0=2\pi n\), we have \(\cos(\alpha(x_0+\xi))=\cos(\alpha\xi)\).

\[ \cos(\alpha\xi)\approx 1-\frac{(\alpha\xi)^2}{2}\quad(\text{for small }\xi). \] \[ U(x)\approx A\left(1-\left[1-\frac{\alpha^2\xi^2}{2}\right]\right) =\frac{A\alpha^2}{2}\,\xi^2. \]

4) Identify the effective spring constant \(k\)

\[ U\approx \frac12 k\xi^2 \;\Rightarrow\; k=A\alpha^2. \]

5) Compute \(\omega_0\) and the period \(T\)

\[ \omega_0=\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}=\sqrt{\frac{A\alpha^2}{m}}=\alpha\sqrt{\frac{A}{m}}, \qquad T=\frac{2\pi}{\omega_0}=\frac{2\pi}{\alpha}\sqrt{\frac{m}{A}}. \]
Final Result
\[ \boxed{\,T=\frac{2\pi}{\alpha}\sqrt{\frac{m}{A}}\,} \]
Sanity checks
  • Units: \(A\) is energy, \(\alpha\) is \(1/\text{length}\). The combination \(\frac{2\pi}{\alpha}\sqrt{\frac{m}{A}}\) has units of time.
  • Limits: larger \(A\) ⇒ stiffer well ⇒ smaller \(T\). larger \(m\) ⇒ more inertia ⇒ larger \(T\).
  • Physical meaning: the oscillation frequency is set by the curvature of the potential at the minimum.

1.2 — Driven oscillator trace: which statements are true?

Physical intuition: A driven, damped oscillator typically shows (i) a decaying transient (system’s natural response) and (ii) a steady-state sinusoid at the driving frequency. If the transient decays while oscillating, the system is underdamped.
Reasoning from the trace
  • The plot shows strong early-time oscillations that decay in amplitude → damping is present (so “no damping” is false).
  • Oscillations persist (the motion is not purely monotonic) → it is not overdamped and not critically damped, but underdamped.
  • The early-time oscillations appear faster than the late-time steady oscillation. Late-time oscillation is at \(\omega_d\), so \(\omega_d\) is smaller than the natural frequency scale → \(\omega_d < \omega_0\).
Final Selection

True: (b) driving frequency is smaller than natural resonant frequency, (f) system is underdamped.
False: (a), (c), (d), (e).

\[ \boxed{\text{True: (b), (f). \;\; False: (a), (c), (d), (e).}} \]
Sanity checks
  • No damping? Would show no decay of the transient; the trace clearly decays.
  • Over/critical damping? Would not oscillate; the trace oscillates.
  • Driving frequency? Steady-state frequency equals \(\omega_d\); early-time “faster wiggles” imply \(\omega_0\) is larger.

1.3 — Bandwidth required for 1 ns square pulses

Physical intuition: Sharper time features demand higher frequencies. A square pulse is built from many harmonics; the shorter the pulse width \(\tau\), the larger the characteristic frequency scale \(1/\tau\).
Derivation-level estimate

A rectangular pulse of width \(\tau\) has a spectrum shaped like a sinc: the first spectral null occurs at approximately \(f \approx 1/\tau\). That sets the main bandwidth scale.

\[ \tau = 1\,\text{ns} = 10^{-9}\,\text{s} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{1}{\tau} = 10^{9}\,\text{Hz} = 1\,\text{GHz}. \]

If you want a “more square” pulse (sharper edges, less rounding), you typically need several harmonics above \(1/\tau\), i.e. a few GHz.

Final Result
\[ \boxed{\Delta f \sim 1\,\text{GHz}\ \text{(order of magnitude)},\quad \text{and up to a few GHz for good square fidelity.}} \]
Sanity checks
  • Units: \(1/\tau\) has units of Hz.
  • Limit: smaller \(\tau\) → larger bandwidth (correct physical trend).
  • Interpretation: “Bandwidth” depends on acceptable distortion; “few × \(1/\tau\)” is typical for squarer edges.

1.4 — Electron interference: high rate vs single-electron emission

Physical intuition: Interference depends on coherence and the absence of which-path information. Even when electrons are emitted one at a time, each electron’s wavefunction can interfere with itself; the pattern emerges after many detections.
Evaluate statements (a)–(d)
  • (a) High emission rate ⇒ interference pattern: True (assuming coherence and no which-path detection).
  • (b) High emission rate ⇒ no interference: False.
  • (c) One electron each time ⇒ no interference: False (classic single-electron double-slit result).
  • (d) One electron each time ⇒ interference: True (pattern builds statistically over time).
Final Selection
\[ \boxed{\text{True: (a), (d). \;\; False: (b), (c).}} \]
Sanity checks
  • Key condition: interference disappears if which-path information is obtained (measurement or decoherence), not merely from changing emission rate.
  • Physical interpretation: the detector records discrete hits; the interference is in the probability distribution.

1.5 — Lowest normal mode of a square membrane (side \(L\), wave speed \(v\))

Physical intuition: A membrane has standing waves in two directions. Fixed edges force nodes at boundaries, quantizing \(k_x\) and \(k_y\). The lowest mode is the smallest nonzero \(k=\sqrt{k_x^2+k_y^2}\).
Step-by-step derivation

1) Quantize wave numbers (fixed boundaries on a square \(L\times L\)):

\[ k_x=\frac{m\pi}{L},\qquad k_y=\frac{n\pi}{L},\qquad m,n=1,2,3,\dots \]

2) Combine into total wave number:

\[ k=\sqrt{k_x^2+k_y^2} =\frac{\pi}{L}\sqrt{m^2+n^2}. \]

3) Convert to angular frequency using \(\omega = v k\):

\[ \omega_{mn}=v\,\frac{\pi}{L}\sqrt{m^2+n^2}. \]

4) Choose the lowest allowed mode: smallest \(\sqrt{m^2+n^2}\) with \(m,n\ge 1\) is \((1,1)\Rightarrow \sqrt{2}\).

\[ \omega_{\min}=\omega_{11}=v\,\frac{\pi}{L}\sqrt{2}. \]
Final Result
\[ \boxed{\,\omega_{\min}=v\,\frac{\pi}{L}\sqrt{2}\,} \]
Sanity checks
  • Units: \(v/L\) is \(1/\text{s}\), so \(\omega\) is \(1/\text{s}\) (rad/s).
  • Limits: larger \(L\) ⇒ smaller \(\omega\). larger \(v\) ⇒ larger \(\omega\).
  • Physical interpretation: the fundamental membrane mode has one half-wave in each direction.
