Radiating Charge with a Sudden Bend (Problem 4) — Complete Structured Solution

A point charge moves at constant speed, briefly experiences a perpendicular force that changes its direction by a small angle \( \Delta\alpha \) over a short time \( \Delta t \), then continues straight again. We analyze the average acceleration, the far-field radiation direction, angular intensity maxima/minima, distance scaling from \(P_1\) to \(P_2\), and the total radiated energy.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Clarify givens/unknowns • pick the right physics • compare approaches

1) Clean restatement

A point charge \(+q\) moves with constant speed \(v\) along a straight line until time \(t=t_0\). During the short interval \([t_0,\, t_0+\Delta t]\), a force acts perpendicular to the motion, so the speed stays constant but the velocity direction changes by a small angle \(\Delta\alpha\). After \(t_0+\Delta t\), the charge again moves uniformly (straight line) at the same speed \(v\). Radiation is observed far away at points \(P_1\) and \(P_2\) in the plane of the motion.

2) Given quantities

  • Charge: \(q\)
  • Speed magnitude (constant): \(v\) (problem’s \(w\))
  • Short bending time: \(\Delta t\)
  • Small deflection angle: \(\Delta\alpha\)
  • Observers \(P_1, P_2\) are very far and in the trajectory plane
  • Distance relation (part e): \(R_2 = 2R_1\)

3) Unknowns / tasks

  • (a) \(\vec a_{\text{avg}}\) during the bend
  • (b) direction of \(\vec E_{\text{rad}}(P_1)\) (acceleration field component)
  • (c) direction of maximum radiation intensity
  • (d) direction of minimum radiation intensity
  • (e) amplitude scaling from \(P_1\) to \(P_2\)
  • (f) total radiated energy \(U_{\text{rad}}\)

4) Physics principles (why they apply)

  • Kinematics: direction change at constant speed implies nonzero \(\Delta\vec v\), hence acceleration \(\vec a = \Delta\vec v/\Delta t\).
  • Radiation from acceleration (far zone): an accelerated charge produces radiation fields scaling as \(1/R\) and transverse to the propagation direction.
  • Nonrelativistic angular pattern: for \(v\ll c\), \( \frac{dP}{d\Omega}\propto \sin^2\theta\), where \(\theta\) is between \(\vec a\) and the line of sight \(\hat n\).

Key idea: during the short bend, the charge experiences a sideways acceleration. In the far field, the radiation electric field direction is the projection of \(\vec a\) perpendicular to the line of sight: \(\ \hat n\times(\hat n\times \vec a)=\vec a-(\vec a\cdot \hat n)\hat n\).

5) Approaches (compare & pick)

  • Approach A (best): nonrelativistic radiation using \( \vec E_{\text{rad}}\propto \hat n\times(\hat n\times \vec a)/R\), intensity \(\propto \sin^2\theta\), and Larmor power. Ideal since no relativistic cues are given and the task emphasizes geometry & scaling.
  • Approach B: full Liénard–Wiechert fields (relativistic beaming). More general, but unnecessary unless \(v\sim c\) is stated.
  • Approach C: integrate Poynting vector from the full fields. Equivalent to Larmor/Liénard but longer algebra.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Intuition → math derivation → boxed results → checks

Physical intuition

Before and after the bend, motion is uniform straight-line: no acceleration → no far-field radiation. During the short bend, the speed stays constant but direction changes, so there is a sideways acceleration. Radiation from an accelerated charge is emitted into the far zone with electric field transverse to the propagation direction and proportional to the acceleration’s transverse component.

\[ \vec E_{\text{rad}}(P)=\frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 c^2}\,\frac{\hat n\times(\hat n\times \vec a)}{R} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \hat n\times(\hat n\times \vec a)=\vec a-(\vec a\cdot \hat n)\hat n. \]

Hence \(\vec E_{\text{rad}}\) points along the part of \(\vec a\) perpendicular to \(\hat n\).

(a) Average acceleration during the bend

Choose axes consistent with the figure: initial motion along \(+\hat x\), deflection clockwise by \(\Delta\alpha\).

\[ \vec v_i = v\,\hat x, \qquad \vec v_f = v(\cos\Delta\alpha\,\hat x - \sin\Delta\alpha\,\hat y). \]

Compute the change in velocity \(\Delta\vec v=\vec v_f-\vec v_i\):

\[ \Delta \vec v = v(\cos\Delta\alpha-1)\hat x - v\sin\Delta\alpha\,\hat y. \]

Average acceleration over the short interval \(\Delta t\):

\[ \vec a_{\text{avg}}=\frac{\Delta\vec v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v(\cos\Delta\alpha-1)}{\Delta t}\hat x -\frac{v\sin\Delta\alpha}{\Delta t}\hat y. \]

For small \(\Delta\alpha\): \(\cos\Delta\alpha\simeq 1-\frac{(\Delta\alpha)^2}{2}\), \(\sin\Delta\alpha\simeq \Delta\alpha\). Then the dominant term is downward:

\[ \boxed{ \vec a_{\text{avg}} \approx -\frac{v\,\Delta\alpha}{\Delta t}\,\hat y } \qquad \boxed{ |\vec a_{\text{avg}}|\approx \frac{v\,\Delta\alpha}{\Delta t} } \]

(b) Direction of the radiation electric field at \(P_1\)

In the far field, \[ \vec E_{\text{rad}} \propto \hat n\times(\hat n\times \vec a) = \vec a-(\vec a\cdot \hat n)\hat n. \] So \(\vec E_{\text{rad}}\) is the projection of \(\vec a\) perpendicular to \(\hat n\).

From part (a), \(\vec a\) is approximately downward (\(-\hat y\)). The point \(P_1\) lies up-left of the bend, so \(\hat n\) points up-left. Removing the component of \(\vec a\) parallel to \(\hat n\) leaves a vector in the plane that is perpendicular to \(\hat n\), generally pointing down-left.

Direction statement: \(\boxed{\ \vec E_{\text{rad}}(P_1)\ \perp \hat n\ \text{and points along the transverse component of}\ \vec a\ \text{(here: down-left in the plane).}\ }\)

Sketch rule (fast): draw \(\hat n\) from the bend to \(P_1\); draw \(\vec a\) (down). Then \(\vec E_{\text{rad}}\) is \(\vec a\) “shadowed” onto the line perpendicular to \(\hat n\).

(c) Direction of maximum radiation intensity

For nonrelativistic radiation, the angular distribution is \[ \frac{dP}{d\Omega}\propto \sin^2\theta, \] where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\vec a\) and \(\hat n\).

Maximum occurs when \(\sin^2\theta=1\Rightarrow \theta=90^\circ\), i.e. observers located perpendicular to \(\vec a\). With \(\vec a\approx -\hat y\), the perpendicular directions are \(\pm \hat x\).

\[ \boxed{\ \text{Maximum intensity for } \hat n \perp \vec a\ \text{ (here: along } \pm \hat x\text{).}\ } \]

(d) Direction of minimum radiation intensity

Minimum occurs when \(\sin^2\theta=0\Rightarrow \theta=0^\circ\) or \(180^\circ\), i.e. along the acceleration axis.

\[ \boxed{\ \text{Minimum (zero) intensity for } \hat n \parallel \vec a\ \text{ or } \hat n \parallel (-\vec a)\ \text{ (here: along } \pm \hat y\text{).}\ } \]

(e) Amplitude decrease from \(P_1\) to \(P_2\) (distance doubles)

Radiation fields in the far zone scale as \(|\vec E_{\text{rad}}|\propto 1/R\). With \(R_2=2R_1\),

\[ \frac{E_2}{E_1}=\frac{R_1}{R_2}=\frac{1}{2}, \qquad \frac{E_1-E_2}{E_1}=1-\frac12=\frac12. \]
\[ \boxed{\ \frac{E_2}{E_1}=\frac12\ \Rightarrow\ \text{amplitude decreases by }50\%.\ } \]

(f) Total radiated energy

Use Larmor power (nonrelativistic): \[ P=\frac{q^2 a^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3}. \] If the acceleration magnitude is approximately constant over \(\Delta t\), then \(U_{\text{rad}}=P\Delta t\).

With \(a\approx \dfrac{v\Delta\alpha}{\Delta t}\),

\[ U_{\text{rad}} = \left(\frac{q^2 a^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3}\right)\Delta t = \frac{q^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3}\left(\frac{v^2(\Delta\alpha)^2}{(\Delta t)^2}\right)\Delta t = \frac{q^2 v^2(\Delta\alpha)^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3\,\Delta t}. \]
\[ \boxed{\ U_{\text{rad}}=\frac{q^2 v^2(\Delta\alpha)^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3\,\Delta t}\ } \]

Interpretation: in this problem’s nonrelativistic regime, intensity is strongest in directions perpendicular to \(\vec a\) and vanishes along \(\pm \vec a\). The field direction at a given observer is the transverse projection of \(\vec a\) relative to that observer’s line of sight.

Sanity checks

Units • limiting cases • physical meaning

Units

Larmor power \(P=\frac{q^2 a^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3}\) has units of watts. Multiplying by \(\Delta t\) gives joules, so \(U_{\text{rad}}\) has correct energy units.

Limiting cases

  • \(\Delta\alpha\to 0\): no direction change → \(a\to 0\) → \(U_{\text{rad}}\to 0\).
  • \(\Delta t\to \infty\) at fixed \(\Delta\alpha\): gentle bend → \(a\to 0\) → \(U_{\text{rad}}\to 0\).
  • \(\Delta t\) small: sharp bend → larger \(a\) → larger radiated energy \(\propto 1/\Delta t\).

Physical interpretation

Radiation is produced only during the acceleration “kick” (the short bend). The far field is transverse and decays as \(1/R\), so doubling distance halves the field amplitude. The angular intensity pattern follows \(\sin^2\theta\), maximizing sideways to \(\vec a\) and vanishing along \(\vec a\).

Final results (summary)

  • \(\vec a_{\text{avg}}\approx -\dfrac{v\Delta\alpha}{\Delta t}\hat y\)
  • \(\vec E_{\text{rad}}(P_1)\parallel \vec a-(\vec a\cdot \hat n)\hat n\) (transverse projection)
  • Max intensity: \(\hat n\perp \vec a\) (here \(\pm\hat x\))
  • Min intensity: \(\hat n\parallel \pm\vec a\) (here \(\pm\hat y\))
  • \(E_2/E_1 = 1/2\) (50% drop when distance doubles)
  • \(U_{\text{rad}}=\dfrac{q^2 v^2(\Delta\alpha)^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3\,\Delta t}\)

Note on assumptions: This solution uses the standard nonrelativistic radiation framework (Larmor + far-field projection). If \(v\sim c\), the full Liénard–Wiechert expressions introduce forward beaming via factors of \((1-\hat n\cdot\beta)^{-3}\).
