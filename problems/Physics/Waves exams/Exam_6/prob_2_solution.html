PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

1) Restate the problem (in my own words)

Two identical beads (\(m_1=m_2=m\)) lie on a taut, massless string of total length \(5L\) fixed at both ends. The beads divide the string into three straight segments of lengths \(2L\), \(L\), and \(2L\). With large constant tension \(T\), we neglect gravity and study small transverse oscillations: the bead displacements are \(y_1(t)\) and \(y_2(t)\).

Tasks: (a) derive the linear equations of motion and identify \(M^{-1}K\); (b) find normal mode shapes and angular frequencies; and (c) with \(y_1(0)=0\), \(y_2(0)=A\), \(\dot y_1(0)=\dot y_2(0)=0\), find \(y_1(t)\).

2) Given quantities

  • Masses: \(m_1=m_2=m\)
  • Tension: \(T\) (constant)
  • Segment lengths: left \(2L\), middle \(L\), right \(2L\)
  • Small oscillations in \(y\); gravity ignored

3) Unknowns

  • Displacements \(y_1(t)\), \(y_2(t)\)
  • Normal mode shapes (eigenvectors)
  • Normal mode frequencies \(\omega_1,\omega_2\)
  • Requested: explicit \(y_1(t)\) for the stated initial condition

4) Relevant physics principles (and why they apply)

LinearizationTension forceEigenmodes
  • Small-angle approximation: each string segment is almost horizontal, so \(\sin\theta \approx \tan\theta \approx \Delta y/\Delta x\). This makes forces linear in \(y_1,y_2\).
  • Restoring force from tension: vertical components of tension from adjacent segments pull each bead back toward equilibrium.
  • Newton’s 2nd law: \(m\ddot y_i = \sum F_{y,i}\) for each bead.
  • Normal modes: a linear coupled system \(M\ddot{\mathbf y}+K\mathbf y=0\) is solved via \((K-\omega^2 M)\mathbf a=0\).

5) Candidate approaches

  1. Direct forces from slopes (Newton): compute slopes of segments and vertical tension components → quickest to \(M^{-1}K\).
  2. Lagrangian/energy method: write kinetic energy of beads and effective potential from tension → systematic, great for complex variants.
  3. Symmetry-first mode guessing: use mirror symmetry to guess eigenvectors \((1,1)\), \((1,-1)\) → fast for mode shapes.

6) Best approach

We’ll use Newton + small-slope geometry to get the equations and matrices cleanly, then use symmetry to identify eigenvectors quickly and compute \(\omega\).

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Define variables: set bead displacements \(y_1(t)\), \(y_2(t)\).
    Tool: small oscillations, linear regime.
  2. Write segment slopes: express each segment’s slope using \(\Delta y/\Delta x\) with lengths \(2L, L, 2L\).
    Tool: geometry.
  3. Compute vertical tension forces: for each bead, sum vertical components from its two adjacent segments.
    Tool: \(F_y \approx T\,(\text{slope})\) with correct sign.
  4. Apply Newton’s law: \(m\ddot y_i = \sum F_{y,i}\) to get two coupled linear ODEs.
    Tool: dynamics.
  5. Matrix form: rewrite as \(M\ddot{\mathbf y}+K\mathbf y=0\) and compute \(M^{-1}K\).
    Tool: linear algebra.
  6. Normal modes: solve eigenproblem of \(M^{-1}K\) to get \(\omega^2\) and eigenvectors.
    Tool: eigenvalues/eigenvectors.
  7. Initial conditions: expand \(\mathbf y(t)\) as a sum of mode vectors with cosine time factors (since initial velocities are zero).
    Tool: superposition.
  8. Extract \(y_1(t)\): read off the first component from the modal sum.
Common mistakes (click to expand) ⌄
  • Sign errors: tension pulls along the segment toward the neighboring point; track the direction carefully.
  • Wrong segment length in slopes: left/right use \(2L\), middle uses \(L\).
  • Dropping \(m\): remember \(\omega^2\) comes from eigenvalues of \(M^{-1}K\), not \(K\) alone.
  • Mode confusion: symmetric \((1,1)\) vs antisymmetric \((1,-1)\) is determined by mirror symmetry.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Physical intuition

Each bead is a junction of two taut segments. A vertical displacement tilts the segments, and the tension’s vertical components pull the bead back. Because the geometry is symmetric about the center:

  • Symmetric mode \(y_1=y_2\): the middle segment has little slope → weaker restoring force → lower \(\omega\).
  • Antisymmetric mode \(y_1=-y_2\): the middle segment slope is large → stronger restoring force → higher \(\omega\).

(a) Equations of motion and matrix \(M^{-1}K\)

Let the fixed ends have transverse displacement \(0\). In the small-slope limit, each segment is straight and \(\tan\theta \approx \Delta y/\Delta x\). Compute the slopes:

\[ s_L = \frac{y_1-0}{2L}=\frac{y_1}{2L},\qquad s_M = \frac{y_2-y_1}{L},\qquad s_R = \frac{0-y_2}{2L}=-\frac{y_2}{2L}. \]

The vertical force contribution from a segment on a bead is \(F_y \approx T(\text{slope})\), with sign set by the pull direction.

Force bookkeeping (signs) — expand ⌄
  • Bead 1: left segment pulls toward the left wall → vertical component \(-T\,\frac{y_1}{2L}\). Middle segment pulls toward bead 2 → \(+T\,\frac{y_2-y_1}{L}\).
  • Bead 2: middle segment pulls toward bead 1 → \(+T\,\frac{y_1-y_2}{L}\). Right segment pulls toward the right wall → \(-T\,\frac{y_2}{2L}\).

Newton’s 2nd law for each bead gives:

\[ m\ddot y_1 = T\frac{y_2-y_1}{L} - T\frac{y_1}{2L} = \frac{T}{L}\left(y_2-\frac{3}{2}y_1\right), \] \[ m\ddot y_2 = T\frac{y_1-y_2}{L} - T\frac{y_2}{2L} = \frac{T}{L}\left(y_1-\frac{3}{2}y_2\right). \]

Rearranging into the standard coupled-oscillator form \(M\ddot{\mathbf y}+K\mathbf y=0\) with \(\mathbf y=(y_1,y_2)^T\):

\[ m\ddot y_1+\frac{T}{L}\left(\frac{3}{2}y_1-y_2\right)=0,\qquad m\ddot y_2+\frac{T}{L}\left(\frac{3}{2}y_2-y_1\right)=0. \]
\[ M=m\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix},\qquad K=\frac{T}{L}\begin{pmatrix}\frac{3}{2}&-1\\-1&\frac{3}{2}\end{pmatrix}. \]
Boxed result
\[ M^{-1}K=\frac{T}{mL}\begin{pmatrix}\frac{3}{2}&-1\\-1&\frac{3}{2}\end{pmatrix}. \]

(b) Normal mode shapes and angular frequencies

Normal modes satisfy \((M^{-1}K)\mathbf a=\omega^2\mathbf a\). The matrix is symmetric and invariant under swapping beads 1 and 2, so the eigenvectors are the symmetric and antisymmetric combinations:

SymmetryEigenvectors

\(\mathbf a_1=(1,1)\) (symmetric) and \(\mathbf a_2=(1,-1)\) (antisymmetric).

Symmetric mode \(\mathbf a_1=(1,1)\):

\[ \begin{pmatrix}\frac{3}{2}&-1\\-1&\frac{3}{2}\end{pmatrix}\!\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix} =\begin{pmatrix}\frac{1}{2}\\\frac{1}{2}\end{pmatrix} =\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}. \] \[ \Rightarrow\quad \omega_1^2=\frac{T}{mL}\cdot\frac{1}{2}=\frac{T}{2mL} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \omega_1=\sqrt{\frac{T}{2mL}}. \]

Antisymmetric mode \(\mathbf a_2=(1,-1)\):

\[ \begin{pmatrix}\frac{3}{2}&-1\\-1&\frac{3}{2}\end{pmatrix}\!\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix} =\begin{pmatrix}\frac{5}{2}\\-\frac{5}{2}\end{pmatrix} =\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}. \] \[ \Rightarrow\quad \omega_2^2=\frac{T}{mL}\cdot\frac{5}{2}=\frac{5T}{2mL} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \omega_2=\sqrt{\frac{5T}{2mL}}. \]
Boxed results

Mode shapes: \(y_1=y_2\) (low) and \(y_1=-y_2\) (high).

\[ \omega_1=\sqrt{\frac{T}{2mL}},\qquad \omega_2=\sqrt{\frac{5T}{2mL}}. \]

(c) Initial conditions and the displacement \(y_1(t)\)

With zero initial velocities, each normal coordinate oscillates as a cosine. Write the superposition:

\[ \mathbf y(t)=c_1(1,1)\cos(\omega_1 t)+c_2(1,-1)\cos(\omega_2 t). \]

Apply \(\mathbf y(0)=(y_1(0),y_2(0))^T=(0,A)^T\):

\[ c_1(1,1)+c_2(1,-1)=(c_1+c_2,\;c_1-c_2)=(0,\;A). \] \[ \Rightarrow\quad c_1+c_2=0,\quad c_1-c_2=A \Rightarrow\quad c_1=\frac{A}{2},\;\;c_2=-\frac{A}{2}. \]

Therefore, the bead that started at equilibrium (\(y_1(0)=0\)) moves as:

Final boxed answer
\[ y_1(t)=\frac{A}{2}\left[\cos\!\Big(\sqrt{\tfrac{T}{2mL}}\,t\Big)-\cos\!\Big(\sqrt{\tfrac{5T}{2mL}}\,t\Big)\right]. \]
(Optional) Also \(y_2(t)\) — expand ⌄
\[ y_2(t)=\frac{A}{2}\left[\cos(\omega_1 t)+\cos(\omega_2 t)\right]. \]

Sanity checks

UnitsLimitsMeaning

Units: \(\omega^2 \sim T/(mL)\). Since \(T\) has units \(N = kg\cdot m/s^2\), then \(\frac{T}{mL} = \frac{kg\cdot m/s^2}{kg\cdot m}=1/s^2\), so \(\omega\) is \(1/s\). ✅

Limiting cases:

  • \(T\uparrow \Rightarrow \omega_{1,2}\uparrow\): stiffer string → faster oscillations. ✅
  • \(m\uparrow \Rightarrow \omega_{1,2}\downarrow\): heavier beads → slower. ✅
  • \(\omega_2/\omega_1=\sqrt{5}>1\): antisymmetric mode is higher frequency because the middle segment tilts more. ✅

Physical interpretation: The initial condition excites both modes, producing a difference of cosines in \(y_1(t)\), i.e., an interference/“beating-like” exchange of motion between beads. ✅

Quick Summary

\[ M^{-1}K=\frac{T}{mL}\begin{pmatrix}\frac{3}{2}&-1\\-1&\frac{3}{2}\end{pmatrix},\quad \omega_1=\sqrt{\frac{T}{2mL}},\ \omega_2=\sqrt{\frac{5T}{2mL}}, \] \[ y_1(t)=\frac{A}{2}\Big[\cos(\omega_1 t)-\cos(\omega_2 t)\Big]. \]
