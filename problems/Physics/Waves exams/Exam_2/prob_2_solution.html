Problem 2 — Plasma Charge-Density Fluctuations (Full Structured Solution)

We analyze the 1D wave equation \(\;a^{2}\rho_{xx}-\Omega_p^{2}\rho=\rho_{tt}\;\), then derive the traveling-wave form, the dispersion relation \(\omega(k)\), allowed frequencies, group velocity, and limiting phase velocity — with sanity checks.

Format: Analysis → Strategy → Full Solution Tools: Plane-wave ansatz, dispersion, group/phase velocity Focus: rigorous + beginner-friendly

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Conceptual setup

1) Restate the problem (in my own words)

A plasma supports small charge-density deviations \(\rho(x,t)\) governed by \[ a^{2}\frac{\partial^{2}\rho}{\partial x^{2}}-\Omega_p^{2}\rho=\frac{\partial^{2}\rho}{\partial t^{2}}. \] We must: (a) write a harmonic traveling wave moving toward \(-x\) satisfying \(\rho(0,0)=0\); (b) derive \(\omega(k)\) and describe \(\omega\) vs \(k\) and which \(\omega\) can propagate; (c) compute the group velocity and decide if the medium is dispersive; (d) find the large-\(k\) limiting phase velocity.

2) Given quantities

  • PDE: \(\;a^{2}\rho_{xx}-\Omega_p^{2}\rho=\rho_{tt}\).
  • Constants: \(\;a\) (speed-like), \(\Omega_p\) (plasma frequency).
  • Wave parameters: wavenumber \(\;k\), angular frequency \(\;\omega\), amplitude \(\;A\).
  • Condition: \(\;\rho(0,0)=0\).
  • Direction: wave travels toward the \(-x\) direction.

3) Unknowns

  • Harmonic traveling-wave form \(\rho(x,t)\) consistent with direction and \(\rho(0,0)=0\).
  • Dispersion relation \(\omega(k)\).
  • Allowed propagating frequency range (what \(\omega\) can exist as traveling waves).
  • Group velocity \(v_g=\frac{d\omega}{dk}\) and dispersiveness.
  • Large-\(k\) limit of phase velocity \(v_\phi=\frac{\omega}{k}\).

4) Physical principles & why they apply

  • Plane-wave/Fourier modes: The PDE is linear with constant coefficients, so exponential/sinusoidal modes are natural eigen-solutions.
  • Dispersion relation: Substituting \(\rho\propto e^{i(kx\pm \omega t)}\) converts the PDE into an algebraic relationship between \(k\) and \(\omega\).
  • Wave kinematics: \(v_\phi=\omega/k\) for phase fronts; \(v_g=d\omega/dk\) for wave packets.

5) Candidate approaches (compare)

  1. Complex exponential ansatz: Fastest for \(\omega(k)\), then enforce direction/initial condition via phase.
  2. Real sine/cosine form: Directly uses \(\rho(0,0)=0\), slightly slower for dispersion derivation.
  3. Separation of variables: Works, but longer and less direct for traveling-wave interpretation.

Chosen approach: Plane-wave (complex exponential) for efficiency + clarity.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

No algebra yet

Roadmap (5–10 steps)

  1. Pick a harmonic traveling-wave form
    Goal: encode motion toward \(-x\).
    Tool: constant-phase condition; choose sign in \(kx\pm \omega t\).
  2. Enforce \(\rho(0,0)=0\)
    Goal: fix phase \(\phi\).
    Tool: evaluate the wave at \((x,t)=(0,0)\).
  3. Substitute plane wave into PDE
    Goal: obtain an algebraic condition linking \(\omega\) and \(k\).
    Tool: \(\rho_{xx}\rightarrow -k^2\rho\), \(\rho_{tt}\rightarrow -\omega^2\rho\).
  4. Solve for \(\omega(k)\)
    Goal: explicit dispersion relation.
    Tool: algebraic rearrangement.
  5. Characterize the graph \(\omega\) vs \(k\)
    Goal: intercept, asymptotes, symmetry.
    Tool: evaluate at \(k=0\) and \(|k|\to\infty\).
  6. Determine allowed propagating frequencies
    Goal: find cutoff/propagation condition.
    Tool: require real \(k\) for traveling waves.
  7. Compute group velocity
    Goal: \(v_g=d\omega/dk\).
    Tool: differentiate \(\omega(k)\).
  8. Compute phase velocity & large-\(k\) limit
    Goal: \(v_\phi=\omega/k\), then \(\lim_{k\to\infty} v_\phi\).
    Tool: asymptotics.
⚑Direction sign

For motion toward \(-x\), use phase \(kx+\omega t\) (for \(k>0\)) so constant phase shifts to decreasing \(x\).

✕Common pitfall

Dropping the \(-\Omega_p^2\rho\) term incorrectly gives \(\omega=ak\). That term creates a cutoff \(\Omega_p\).

✓Quick tip

Allowed propagating waves typically mean real \(k\). If \(k\) is imaginary, the mode is evanescent.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Derivation + checks

Physical intuition

The PDE resembles a wave equation with an extra restoring term \(-\Omega_p^2\rho\). That term makes the plasma oscillate even when the disturbance is spatially uniform (\(k=0\)), creating a cutoff frequency \(\Omega_p\). For very short wavelengths (large \(k\)), the \(\Omega_p\) term becomes less important and the wave approaches a “standard” wave with speed scale \(a\).

(a) Harmonic traveling wave toward \(-x\) with \(\rho(0,0)=0\)

A real harmonic traveling wave may be written as \[ \rho(x,t)=A\sin(kx+\omega t+\phi). \] To confirm the direction, set the phase \(\theta=kx+\omega t+\phi\). Constant phase requires \[ kx+\omega t+\phi=\text{const}\quad\Rightarrow\quad x=-\frac{\omega}{k}t+\text{const}. \] As \(t\) increases, \(x\) decreases, so the wave travels toward \(-x\).

Now impose \(\rho(0,0)=0\): \[ \rho(0,0)=A\sin(\phi)=0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \phi=n\pi. \] Choosing the simplest \(\phi=0\) gives

\[ \boxed{\rho(x,t)=A\sin(kx+\omega t).} \]

(b) Dispersion relation \(\omega(k)\), description of \(\omega(k)\), and allowed \(\omega\)

Use the plane-wave (complex) trial solution \[ \rho(x,t)=\rho_0 e^{i(kx+\omega t)}. \] Then the second derivatives become \[ \rho_{xx}=(ik)^2\rho=-k^2\rho,\qquad \rho_{tt}=(i\omega)^2\rho=-\omega^2\rho. \] Substitute into the PDE: \[ a^2(-k^2)\rho-\Omega_p^2\rho=(-\omega^2)\rho. \] Cancel \(\rho\neq 0\) to obtain \[ -a^2k^2-\Omega_p^2=-\omega^2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \omega^2=\Omega_p^2+a^2k^2. \]

\[ \boxed{\omega(k)=\sqrt{\Omega_p^2+a^2k^2}}\qquad (\omega\ge 0). \]

Shape / “draw” description:

  • \(\omega(0)=\Omega_p\) (starts at a positive intercept).
  • Even in \(k\) (depends on \(k^2\)), symmetric about \(k=0\).
  • For large \(|k|\): \(\omega\approx a|k|\) (approaches a straight line).

Allowed propagating frequencies: Solve for \(k^2\):

\[ a^2k^2=\omega^2-\Omega_p^2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad k^2=\frac{\omega^2-\Omega_p^2}{a^2}. \]

For a traveling harmonic wave we require real \(k\), hence \(\omega^2-\Omega_p^2\ge 0\). Therefore:

\[ \boxed{\text{Propagating harmonic waves require }\omega\ge \Omega_p.} \]

If \(0\le \omega<\Omega_p\), then \(k\) is imaginary and the spatial dependence is evanescent rather than traveling.

(c) Group velocity and dispersiveness

The group velocity is \(v_g=\dfrac{d\omega}{dk}\). With \(\omega(k)=\left(\Omega_p^2+a^2k^2\right)^{1/2}\),

\[ \frac{d\omega}{dk} =\frac{1}{2}\left(\Omega_p^2+a^2k^2\right)^{-1/2}(2a^2k) =\frac{a^2k}{\sqrt{\Omega_p^2+a^2k^2}}. \]
\[ \boxed{v_g(k)=\frac{a^2k}{\sqrt{\Omega_p^2+a^2k^2}}.} \]

To check dispersion, compute the phase velocity: \[ v_\phi=\frac{\omega}{k} =\frac{\sqrt{\Omega_p^2+a^2k^2}}{k} =\sqrt{a^2+\frac{\Omega_p^2}{k^2}}. \] Since \(v_\phi\) depends on \(k\), different wavelengths travel with different phase speeds.

\[ \boxed{\text{Yes — the medium is dispersive.}} \]
Extra intuition (optional) ⌄

As \(k\to 0\), \(\omega\to \Omega_p\) so the medium oscillates in time without much spatial propagation; the group velocity tends to zero. As \(k\) grows, the wave behaves increasingly like an ordinary wave with speed \(a\), so both \(v_\phi\) and \(v_g\) approach \(a\).

(d) Limiting phase velocity at large \(k\)

Using \(v_\phi=\omega/k\), \[ v_\phi(k)=\frac{\sqrt{\Omega_p^2+a^2k^2}}{k}=\sqrt{a^2+\frac{\Omega_p^2}{k^2}}. \] Therefore, as \(k\to\infty\),

\[ \boxed{\lim_{k\to\infty}v_\phi(k)=a.} \]

Final boxed results

(a) Traveling wave (\(-x\) direction)
\[ \boxed{\rho(x,t)=A\sin(kx+\omega t).} \]
(b) Dispersion relation
\[ \boxed{\omega(k)=\sqrt{\Omega_p^2+a^2k^2}.} \]
(b) Allowed propagating frequencies
\[ \boxed{\omega\ge\Omega_p.} \]
(c) Group velocity
\[ \boxed{v_g(k)=\frac{a^2k}{\sqrt{\Omega_p^2+a^2k^2}}.} \]
(c) Dispersive?
\[ \boxed{\text{Dispersive medium (}v_\phi\text{ depends on }k\text{).}} \]
(d) Large-\(k\) limit
\[ \boxed{\lim_{k\to\infty}v_\phi=a.} \]

Sanity checks

1) Units

  • \([\omega]=[\Omega_p]=\mathrm{s^{-1}}\), \([k]=\mathrm{m^{-1}}\).
  • From \(\omega^2=\Omega_p^2+a^2k^2\): \([a^2k^2]=(\mathrm{m^2/s^2})(1/\mathrm{m^2})=1/\mathrm{s^2}\), consistent.
  • \(v_g\) and \(v_\phi\) both have units of \(\mathrm{m/s}\).

2) Limiting cases

  • \(k\to 0\): \(\omega\to\Omega_p\) (uniform plasma oscillations), and \(v_g\to 0\) (little propagation).
  • \(k\to\infty\): \(\omega\approx a|k|\), \(v_\phi\to a\), \(v_g\to a\) (approaches a standard wave).

3) Physical interpretation

  • \(\Omega_p\) creates a cutoff: for \(\omega<\Omega_p\), the spatial dependence is evanescent (no traveling harmonic wave).
  • Because \(\omega(k)\) is not linear in \(k\), the medium is dispersive.
