PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Conceptual Setup

Restatement

We are given a monochromatic plane-wave electric field in vacuum with a phase \(\omega t-4x+3y\) and a polarization vector proportional to \(3\hat x+a\hat y\). We must infer the wavevector \(\vec k\), the propagation direction \(\hat k\), compute \(\omega\) and \(\lambda\) using the vacuum dispersion relation, enforce the transverse condition to determine \(a\), find the magnetic field \(\vec B\), and compute the energy flux (Poynting vector) \(\vec S\).

Given & Unknowns

  • Given: \(\vec E(\vec r,t)=E_0(3\hat x+a\hat y)\cos(\omega t-4x+3y)\), vacuum, speed \(c\).
  • Unknown: \(\hat k\), \(\omega\), \(\lambda\), \(a\), \(\vec B(\vec r,t)\), \(\vec S(\vec r,t)\) (and \(\langle \vec S\rangle\)).

Why these laws apply (vacuum plane wave)

  • Phase matching: A plane wave has \(\cos(\omega t-\vec k\cdot \vec r)\), so the given phase directly encodes \(\vec k\).
  • Transversality: In vacuum, \(\nabla\cdot\vec E=0\Rightarrow \vec k\cdot\vec E_0=0\) (no free charge), forcing \(\vec E \perp \vec k\).
  • Dispersion: In vacuum, \(\omega=c|\vec k|\).
  • Field coupling: For a plane wave, \(\vec B=\frac{1}{\omega}\vec k\times\vec E\) (same phase), and energy flow is \(\vec S=\frac{1}{\mu_0}\vec E\times\vec B\).

Candidate approaches

  • Direct plane-wave identities: read \(\vec k\) from phase; use \(\vec k\cdot\vec E_0=0\); then \(\omega=c|\vec k|\), \(\lambda=2\pi/|\vec k|\), \(\vec B=(1/\omega)\vec k\times\vec E\), \(\vec S=(1/\mu_0)\vec E\times\vec B\).
  • Full Maxwell differential form: compute divergences/curls explicitly; correct but longer.
  • Phasor method: complex exponentials; elegant and systematic, similar complexity to direct identities.

Best choice: the direct plane-wave approach is shortest and uses identities that are exactly valid in vacuum.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Derivation

Physical intuition

The planes of constant phase \(\omega t-4x+3y=\text{const}\) are the wavefronts. Their normal vector is the wavevector \(\vec k\). In vacuum, Maxwell’s equations enforce a transverse wave: \(\vec E \perp \vec k\) and \(\vec B \perp \vec k\). The magnetic field is set by \(\vec B \propto \vec k\times\vec E\), and the energy flow is along \(\vec S \propto \vec E\times\vec B\), i.e., along the propagation direction.

(a) Propagation direction

A standard plane wave is \(\vec E(\vec r,t)=\vec E_0\cos(\omega t-\vec k\cdot\vec r)\). Here, \[ \omega t-\vec k\cdot\vec r \equiv \omega t-4x+3y = \omega t-(4x-3y). \] Therefore \[ \vec k\cdot\vec r = 4x-3y+0z \quad\Rightarrow\quad \boxed{\vec k=4\hat x-3\hat y}. \] The magnitude is \[ |\vec k|=\sqrt{4^2+(-3)^2}=5, \] so the propagation direction is \[ \boxed{\hat k=\frac{\vec k}{|\vec k|}=\frac{4\hat x-3\hat y}{5}}. \]

(b) Angular frequency and wavelength

In vacuum, the dispersion relation is \(\omega=c|\vec k|\). With \(|\vec k|=5\), \[ \boxed{\omega=5c}. \] Also, \(|\vec k|=\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\), hence \[ \boxed{\lambda=\frac{2\pi}{|\vec k|}=\frac{2\pi}{5}}. \]

(c) Determine \(a\)

The field amplitude (polarization) is \(\vec E_0=E_0(3\hat x+a\hat y)\). In vacuum, \(\nabla\cdot\vec E=0\) implies \(\vec k\cdot\vec E_0=0\). Using \(\vec k=4\hat x-3\hat y\): \[ \vec k\cdot\vec E_0 =(4\hat x-3\hat y)\cdot E_0(3\hat x+a\hat y) =E_0(12-3a). \] Setting this to zero gives \[ 12-3a=0 \Rightarrow \boxed{a=4}. \]

(d) Magnetic field \(\vec B(\vec r,t)\)

For a plane wave in vacuum, \[ \vec B=\frac{1}{\omega}\,\vec k\times \vec E \quad \text{(same phase)}. \] With \(a=4\), \(\vec E=E_0(3\hat x+4\hat y)\cos\phi\) and \(\phi=\omega t-4x+3y\). Compute the amplitude cross product: \[ \vec k\times \vec E_0 =(4\hat x-3\hat y)\times E_0(3\hat x+4\hat y) =25E_0\hat z. \] Then \[ \vec B_0=\frac{1}{\omega}(25E_0\hat z)=\frac{25E_0}{5c}\hat z=\frac{5E_0}{c}\hat z. \] Therefore, \[ \boxed{\vec B(\vec r,t)=\frac{5E_0}{c}\,\hat z\,\cos(\omega t-4x+3y)},\qquad \boxed{\omega=5c}. \]

(e) Directional energy flux density (Poynting vector)

The instantaneous energy flux density is \[ \vec S(\vec r,t)=\frac{1}{\mu_0}\vec E\times\vec B. \] Using \(\vec E=E_0(3\hat x+4\hat y)\cos\phi\) and \(\vec B=\frac{5E_0}{c}\hat z\cos\phi\): \[ \vec E\times\vec B =\frac{5E_0^2}{c}\cos^2\phi\,(3\hat x+4\hat y)\times \hat z. \] Since \(\hat x\times\hat z=-\hat y\) and \(\hat y\times\hat z=\hat x\), \[ (3\hat x+4\hat y)\times \hat z = 4\hat x-3\hat y. \] Hence \[ \boxed{\vec S(\vec r,t)=\frac{1}{\mu_0}\frac{5E_0^2}{c}\,(4\hat x-3\hat y)\,\cos^2(\omega t-4x+3y)}. \] The time-averaged energy flux uses \(\langle \cos^2\phi\rangle=\tfrac12\): \[ \boxed{\langle \vec S\rangle=\frac{1}{2\mu_0}\frac{5E_0^2}{c}\,(4\hat x-3\hat y)} =\left(\frac{25E_0^2}{2\mu_0 c}\right)\hat k. \]

Final Results (boxed)

\(\;\vec k=4\hat x-3\hat y\;\) \(\;|\vec k|=5\;\) \(\;\hat k=\dfrac{4\hat x-3\hat y}{5}\;\) \(\;\omega=5c\;\) \(\;\lambda=\dfrac{2\pi}{5}\;\) \(\;a=4\;\) \(\;\vec B=\dfrac{5E_0}{c}\hat z\cos(\omega t-4x+3y)\;\) \(\;\vec S=\dfrac{1}{\mu_0}\dfrac{5E_0^2}{c}(4\hat x-3\hat y)\cos^2(\cdot)\;\) \(\;\langle \vec S\rangle=\dfrac{1}{2\mu_0}\dfrac{5E_0^2}{c}(4\hat x-3\hat y)\;\)

Sanity checks

Units: \(|\vec k|\) has units rad/m (phase must be dimensionless); \(\omega=c|\vec k|\Rightarrow \text{s}^{-1}\). \(B_0\sim E_0/c\) has Tesla units. \(\vec S=(1/\mu_0)\vec E\times\vec B\) gives W/m\(^2\).
Limiting/consistency: With \(a=4\), \(\vec k\cdot\vec E_0=0\) (transverse). \(\vec B\propto \vec k\times\vec E\) ensures \(\vec B\perp \vec E\) and \(\vec B\perp\vec k\). \(\vec S\) points along \(4\hat x-3\hat y\), the propagation direction.
Physical interpretation: \(\vec E\) oscillates in the \(xy\)-plane; \(\vec B\) oscillates along \(+\hat z\). Energy flows (on average) along \(\hat k\), as required for a plane wave in vacuum.
