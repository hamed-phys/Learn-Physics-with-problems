Radiation from an Oscillating Charge on a Spring

A point charge \(+Q\) (mass \(M\)) attached to a spring (constant \(k\)) oscillates along the \(x\)-axis. We derive the far-zone radiated electric field in the \(x\text{–}z\) plane, compute the total time-averaged power radiated, and estimate the amplitude decay time to \(A/e\) using both a rough and refined model.

Motion: \(x(t)=A\cos(\omega t)\) \(\omega=\sqrt{k/M}\) Radiation zone: \(R\gg A\) Retarded time: \(t_{\rm ret}=t-\frac{R}{c}\)

PART 1 Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Setup

Rewrite the problem

A charge \(+Q\) of mass \(M\) is attached to a spring along the \(x\)-axis. The equilibrium point is the origin. The charge is displaced to \(x=A\) and released from rest at \(t=0\). Assume the charge is point-like and damping is very small. In the far field (\(R\gg A\)), determine:

  • (6.a) the radiated electric field in an arbitrary direction in the \(x\text{–}z\) plane,
  • (6.b) the total time-averaged radiated power,
  • (6.c) a rough time for amplitude to drop to \(A/e\) assuming radiated power is approximately constant, and whether that is realistic,
  • (6.d) a refined time using \(dA/dt=(dA/dE)(dE/dt)\) with cycle-averaged power.

Given / Unknowns / What to find

Given: \(Q,\,M,\,k,\,A\), far-field distance \(R\gg A\), motion along \(x\), weak damping.

Unknowns: \(\mathbf E_{\rm rad}(R,\theta,t)\), \(\langle P\rangle\), and decay time(s) \(t_{1/e}\).

Goal: derive explicit formulas, then check units/limits and interpret physically.

\[ \omega=\sqrt{\frac{k}{M}},\qquad t_{\rm ret}=t-\frac{R}{c}. \]

Key principles (and why)

  • SHO dynamics: spring force \(-kx\) → sinusoidal motion to leading order.
  • Radiation from acceleration: accelerated charges emit EM radiation; far field depends on \(\mathbf a(t_{\rm ret})\).
  • Radiation-zone fields: keep only \(1/R\) terms; near-field terms fall faster with \(R\).
  • Larmor formula: total radiated power for nonrelativistic motion scales as \(a^2\).
  • Energy–amplitude relation: \(E=\frac12kA^2\) connects radiated loss to amplitude decay.

Approaches & best choice

  • A: Use \(\mathbf E_{\rm rad}=\frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0c^2}\frac{\mathbf n\times(\mathbf n\times\mathbf a)}{R}\) + Larmor power.
  • B: Dipole method: \(p(t)=Qx(t)\), \(\mathbf E\propto \ddot{\mathbf p}\), \(P\propto \ddot p^2\).
  • C: Full Liénard–Wiechert (relativistic) — unnecessary unless \(v\sim c\).
Best: Approach A is the most direct: it yields \(\mathbf E_{\rm rad}\) immediately and feeds power + decay estimates cleanly.

PART 2 Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Plan

  1. Write the leading-order trajectory \(x(t)\)

    Goal: model motion with weak damping. Tool: \(\omega=\sqrt{k/M}\), initial conditions.

  2. Compute acceleration \(\mathbf a(t)\)

    Goal: radiation source term. Tool: \(\mathbf a=\ddot x\,\hat{\mathbf x}\).

  3. Define observer direction in \(x\text{–}z\) plane

    Goal: get angular dependence. Tool: \(\mathbf n(\theta)=\cos\theta\,\hat{\mathbf x}+\sin\theta\,\hat{\mathbf z}\).

  4. Apply far-zone radiation field formula

    Goal: \(\mathbf E_{\rm rad}(R,\theta,t)\). Tool: \(\mathbf n\times(\mathbf n\times\mathbf a)\), \(t_{\rm ret}=t-R/c\).

  5. Compute \(\langle P\rangle\) via Larmor and cycle average

    Goal: total time-averaged power. Tool: \(P=\frac{Q^2a^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0c^3}\), \(\langle\cos^2\rangle=\frac12\).

  6. Rough decay time using constant \(\langle P\rangle\)

    Goal: quick \(t_{1/e}\). Tool: \(E=\frac12kA^2\), \(t\approx \Delta E/\langle P\rangle\).

  7. Refined decay time using \(dE/dt=-\langle P(A)\rangle\)

    Goal: solve for \(A(t)\). Tool: \(dA/dt=(dA/dE)(dE/dt)\), exponential decay.

Common mistakes Avoid
  • Forgetting the retarded time \(t_{\rm ret}=t-R/c\).
  • Mixing near-field (\(\propto 1/R^2,1/R^3\)) with radiation field (\(\propto 1/R\)).
  • Using the wrong angle: dipole radiation is zero on-axis and maximal broadside.
  • Assuming constant power in (6.c) even though \(\langle P\rangle\propto A^2\).
Speed tips Hints
  • Use \(\mathbf n\times(\mathbf n\times\mathbf a)=\mathbf a-(\mathbf n\cdot\mathbf a)\mathbf n\).
  • For SHM: \(\langle\cos^2\rangle=\frac12\).
  • Remember scalings: \(a\sim\omega^2A\), so \(\langle P\rangle\sim A^2\omega^4\).

PART 3 Full Solution

Final

Physical intuition

An accelerating charge emits electromagnetic radiation. In the far field the radiation is transverse, falls like \(1/R\), and forms a dipole-like angular pattern. Since the charge oscillates along \(x\), the radiation vanishes along the \(x\)-axis and is strongest for observers perpendicular to \(x\).

Step 0: Motion and acceleration

With weak damping, take the leading-order SHM solution with \(x(0)=A\), \(\dot x(0)=0\).

\[ \omega=\sqrt{\frac{k}{M}},\qquad x(t)=A\cos(\omega t). \] \[ \ddot x(t)=-A\omega^2\cos(\omega t). \] \[ \mathbf a(t)=\ddot x(t)\,\hat{\mathbf x}=-A\omega^2\cos(\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf x}. \]

Step 1: Observation direction in \(x\text{–}z\) plane

Let \(\theta\) be the angle from \(+x\) toward \(+z\). Then:

\[ \mathbf n=\cos\theta\,\hat{\mathbf x}+\sin\theta\,\hat{\mathbf z}, \qquad t_{\rm ret}=t-\frac{R}{c}. \]

(6.a) Radiated electric field in the far zone

Nonrelativistic radiation-zone electric field:

\[ \mathbf E_{\rm rad}(\mathbf r,t)= \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0c^2}\frac{\mathbf n\times(\mathbf n\times\mathbf a(t_{\rm ret}))}{R}. \]

Use \(\mathbf n\times(\mathbf n\times\mathbf a)=\mathbf a-(\mathbf n\cdot\mathbf a)\mathbf n\). Here \(\mathbf a(t_{\rm ret})=a_x(t_{\rm ret})\hat{\mathbf x}\) with \(a_x(t_{\rm ret})=-A\omega^2\cos(\omega t_{\rm ret})\).

\[ \mathbf n\cdot\mathbf a=a_x\cos\theta \quad\Rightarrow\quad \mathbf n\times(\mathbf n\times\mathbf a)= a_x\Big(\sin^2\theta\,\hat{\mathbf x}-\sin\theta\cos\theta\,\hat{\mathbf z}\Big). \]
\[ \boxed{ \mathbf E_{\rm rad}(R,\theta,t)= \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0c^2}\frac{a_x(t_{\rm ret})}{R} \Big(\sin^2\theta\,\hat{\mathbf x}-\sin\theta\cos\theta\,\hat{\mathbf z}\Big) } \] \[ \boxed{ \mathbf E_{\rm rad}(R,\theta,t)= -\frac{Q A\omega^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0c^2}\frac{\cos\!\big(\omega(t-R/c)\big)}{R} \Big(\sin^2\theta\,\hat{\mathbf x}-\sin\theta\cos\theta\,\hat{\mathbf z}\Big) } \]
Angular behavior: The radiated field is transverse and has a dipole pattern: zero at \(\theta=0\) (along \(x\)) and maximal at \(\theta=\pi/2\) (along \(z\)).

(6.b) Total time-averaged power radiated

Use Larmor’s formula for nonrelativistic radiation and average over one cycle.

\[ P(t)=\frac{Q^2a^2(t)}{6\pi\varepsilon_0c^3}, \qquad a(t)=A\omega^2\cos(\omega t). \] \[ \langle a^2\rangle=A^2\omega^4\langle\cos^2\rangle=\frac12A^2\omega^4. \]
\[ \boxed{ \langle P\rangle=\frac{Q^2A^2\omega^4}{12\pi\varepsilon_0c^3} } \qquad \boxed{ \langle P\rangle= \frac{Q^2A^2}{12\pi\varepsilon_0c^3}\frac{k^2}{M^2} \ \ (\omega^2=k/M) } \]

(6.c) Rough time to reduce amplitude to \(A/e\) (constant power)

For a SHO, the energy at amplitude \(A\) is \(E=\tfrac12kA^2\). If the time-averaged radiated power does not change appreciably, estimate \(t\approx\Delta E/\langle P\rangle_0\).

\[ E_i=\frac12kA^2,\quad E_f=\frac12k\left(\frac{A}{e}\right)^2=\frac{E_i}{e^2},\quad \Delta E=E_i\left(1-e^{-2}\right). \]
\[ t_{\rm rough}\approx\frac{\Delta E}{\langle P\rangle_0} = \frac{\frac12kA^2(1-e^{-2})}{\frac{Q^2A^2\omega^4}{12\pi\varepsilon_0c^3}}. \]
\[ \boxed{ t_{\rm rough}\approx(1-e^{-2})\frac{6\pi\varepsilon_0c^3\,k}{Q^2\omega^4} } \qquad \boxed{ t_{\rm rough}\approx(1-e^{-2})\frac{6\pi\varepsilon_0c^3\,M^2}{Q^2k} } \]
Is the assumption realistic?
Typically no. Because \(\langle P\rangle\propto A^2\), the radiated power decreases as the amplitude shrinks. The constant-power estimate therefore underestimates the true decay time.

(6.d) Refined estimate using \(dA/dt=(dA/dE)(dE/dt)\)

Use the amplitude dependence of the cycle-averaged radiated power and convert the energy-loss equation into a differential equation for \(A(t)\).

\[ E(A)=\frac12kA^2 \Rightarrow \frac{dA}{dE}=\frac{1}{kA}. \] \[ \langle P(A)\rangle=\frac{Q^2\omega^4}{12\pi\varepsilon_0c^3}A^2 \Rightarrow \frac{dE}{dt}=-\langle P(A)\rangle. \]
\[ \frac{dA}{dt}=\frac{dA}{dE}\frac{dE}{dt} = \frac{1}{kA}\left(-\frac{Q^2\omega^4}{12\pi\varepsilon_0c^3}A^2\right) =-\gamma A, \] \[ \gamma=\frac{Q^2\omega^4}{12\pi\varepsilon_0c^3\,k}. \] \[ A(t)=A_0e^{-\gamma t}. \]
\[ A(t_{1/e})=\frac{A_0}{e}\Rightarrow t_{1/e}=\frac{1}{\gamma}. \] \[ \boxed{ t_{1/e}=\frac{12\pi\varepsilon_0c^3\,k}{Q^2\omega^4} } \qquad \boxed{ t_{1/e}=\frac{12\pi\varepsilon_0c^3\,M^2}{Q^2k} } \]
Comparison:
\(t_{\rm rough}\approx 0.432\,t_{1/e}\). The refined time is longer because power decreases as \(A^2\) during damping.

Sanity Checks

Units • Limits • Meaning

Units

\(\mathbf E_{\rm rad}\sim \dfrac{Q a}{\varepsilon_0c^2R}\Rightarrow\) V/m. Larmor \(P\Rightarrow\) W. \(t_{1/e}\sim \dfrac{\varepsilon_0c^3M^2}{Q^2k}\Rightarrow\) s.

Limiting cases

\(Q\to0\Rightarrow\) no radiation, \(t_{1/e}\to\infty\). \(A\to0\Rightarrow\) no motion, \(\langle P\rangle\to0\). \(\theta=0\Rightarrow \mathbf E_{\rm rad}=0\) (dipole null).

Physical interpretation

Dipole-like radiation about the \(x\)-axis: maximum broadside, zero on-axis. Because \(\langle P\rangle\propto A^2\), the refined model gives exponential amplitude decay \(A(t)=A_0e^{-\gamma t}\).

Optional: radiation-zone magnetic field Extra
In the radiation zone, \[ \mathbf B_{\rm rad}=\frac{1}{c}\,\mathbf n\times \mathbf E_{\rm rad}, \] and \(\mathbf E_{\rm rad}\perp \mathbf B_{\rm rad}\perp \mathbf n\).
