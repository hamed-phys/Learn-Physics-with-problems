! Key Insight
Constant tension T produces an effective coupling stiffness k_c=\frac{T}{L}=2k because the horizontal component is proportional to x_2-x_1.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Interpretation

Restated problem

Two masses move only along x. The lower mass is 2m and is attached to the left wall with a spring constant 2k. The upper mass is m and is attached to the right wall with spring constant k. A vertical band of length L couples the masses and stays at constant tension T=2kL. At equilibrium, both masses share the same x-position and both springs are un-stretched. For small displacements x_1,x_2\ll L, the tension remains constant. Find: (a) coupled ODEs, (b) normal mode frequencies, (c) normal mode shapes.

Given / unknowns / targets

  • Given: masses 2m, m; springs 2k, k; band length L; tension T=2kL; 1D motion; no friction, no gravity.
  • Unknown: x_1(t), x_2(t), normal frequencies \omega, mode ratios.
  • Find: equations of motion, \omega_1,\omega_2, and eigenvectors.

Principles (why they apply)

  • Newton’s 2nd law: motion constrained to x so \sum F_x = m\ddot{x}.
  • Hooke’s law: springs give linear restoring forces about equilibrium.
  • Small-angle band coupling: band force magnitude is T along the band, hence its horizontal component is linear in (x_2-x_1)/L for small displacements.

Approaches (compare)

  • Force + geometry: clearest for handling constant tension and signs.
  • Lagrangian: elegant, but needs careful linearized potential for band.
  • Matrix: fastest after building M and K.

Choice: force method for maximum transparency.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

5–10 steps
Define coordinates Goal: set \(x_1,x_2\) from equilibrium. Tool: sign convention (+ right).
Wall spring forces Goal: write restoring forces. Tool: Hooke’s law.
Band coupling Goal: linearize horizontal component. Tool: \(F_x \approx T(x_2-x_1)/L\).
Newton’s law Goal: obtain coupled ODEs. Tool: \(\sum F_x=m\ddot{x}\).
Normal-mode ansatz Goal: turn ODEs into algebra. Tool: \(x_i=A_i e^{i\omega t}\).
Determinant condition Goal: find \(\omega\). Tool: nontrivial solution ⇒ det = 0.
Mode shapes Goal: compute \(B/A\). Tool: substitute each \(\omega\) back.
Sketch Goal: show relative motion. Tool: sign/magnitude of \(B/A\).
× Common mistakes
  • Sign errors in coupling: forces must be equal and opposite.
  • Forgetting one mass is \(2m\) (changes inertia).
  • Missing \(k_c=T/L\) (the band acts like a coupling spring).

PART 3 — Full Solution

Derivation
✓ Physical intuition
If \(x_1=x_2\), the band stays nearly vertical, giving almost no horizontal restoring force: that produces a lower-frequency in-phase mode. If the masses move oppositely, the band tilts and its horizontal component becomes large, making the restoring effect stronger and the frequency higher.

(2.a) Coupled differential equations

Wall spring forces

F_{\text{wall on }1}=-2k\,x_1,\qquad F_{\text{wall on }2}=-k\,x_2.

Band coupling (small-angle)

Relative horizontal displacement is \(\Delta x=x_2-x_1\). For \(|\Delta x|\ll L\), the band’s horizontal component is linear.

k_c=\frac{T}{L}=\frac{2kL}{L}=2k,\quad F_{\text{band on }1,x}=-k_c(x_1-x_2),\quad F_{\text{band on }2,x}=-k_c(x_2-x_1).

Newton’s 2nd law for each mass

2m\,\ddot{x}_1 = -2k x_1 -2k(x_1-x_2) \Rightarrow 2m\,\ddot{x}_1 +4k x_1 -2k x_2=0.
m\,\ddot{x}_2 = -k x_2 -2k(x_2-x_1) \Rightarrow m\,\ddot{x}_2 +3k x_2 -2k x_1=0.

Equations of motion

\[ \boxed{ \begin{aligned} 2m\,\ddot x_1 +4k\,x_1 -2k\,x_2 &= 0,\\ m\,\ddot x_2 +3k\,x_2 -2k\,x_1 &= 0. \end{aligned}} \]

(2.b) Normal mode frequencies

Normal-mode ansatz and eigenvalue condition

Assume \(x_1=Ae^{i\omega t}\), \(x_2=Be^{i\omega t}\) so \(\ddot{x}=-\omega^2 x\).

(4k-2m\omega^2)A-2kB=0,\qquad -2kA+(3k-m\omega^2)B=0.

For nontrivial \((A,B)\), determinant must vanish:

(4k-2m\omega^2)(3k-m\omega^2)-4k^2=0.

Let \(\lambda=m\omega^2\):

(4k-2\lambda)(3k-\lambda)-4k^2=0 \Rightarrow \lambda^2-5k\lambda+4k^2=0.
\lambda=\frac{5k\pm\sqrt{25k^2-16k^2}}{2} =\frac{5k\pm 3k}{2} \Rightarrow \lambda_1=4k,\;\lambda_2=k.

Normal mode frequencies

\[ \boxed{ \omega_1^2=\frac{4k}{m},\ \omega_2^2=\frac{k}{m} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \omega_1=2\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}},\ \omega_2=\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} } \]

(2.c) Normal modes (mode shapes)

Amplitude ratios

Use \((4k-2m\omega^2)A-2kB=0\) to find \(B/A\).

Low-frequency mode \(\omega_2^2=k/m\)

m\omega^2=k \Rightarrow (4k-2k)A-2kB=0 \Rightarrow B=A.

Shape: \((x_1,x_2)\propto(1,1)\) (in phase, equal amplitudes).

High-frequency mode \(\omega_1^2=4k/m\)

m\omega^2=4k \Rightarrow (4k-8k)A-2kB=0 \Rightarrow B=-2A.

Shape: \((x_1,x_2)\propto(1,-2)\) (out of phase; top moves 2×).

Mode vectors

\[ \boxed{ \text{Mode at }\omega_2:\ (x_1,x_2)\propto(1,1),\qquad \text{Mode at }\omega_1:\ (x_1,x_2)\propto(1,-2). } \]

Sanity Checks

Units • limits • meaning

Units

\(k/m\) has units \(\text{s}^{-2}\), so \(\sqrt{k/m}\) has units \(\text{s}^{-1}\). Correct for angular frequency.

Limiting / interpretation

  • Coupling stiffness \(k_c=T/L=2k\) grows with tension → stronger coupling → higher frequency mode.
  • In-phase mode \(x_1=x_2\) keeps band vertical → coupling force ~ 0 → lower frequency.
  • Out-of-phase mode tilts band → large horizontal component → higher frequency.
