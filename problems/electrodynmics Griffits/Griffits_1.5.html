Quick Summary

  • What this problem is about: identities involving the cross product × and nested (“triple”) cross products.
  • Key physics idea: cross products encode moments (torque, angular momentum) and axis–perpendicular structure; triple products often simplify force/torque chains.
  • Governing algebraic tool: the vector triple-product (BAC–CAB) identity: X × (Y × Z) = Y (X·Z) − Z (X·Y).
  • Result type: purely symbolic (no numbers). The cyclic sum identity is an algebraic “conservation/cancellation” statement.
  • Main proof outcome: the cyclic sum A × (B × C) + B × (C × A) + C × (A × B) equals 0 by expansion and term-by-term cancellation.
  • Associativity question: generally × is not associative; equality holds only under special geometric conditions.
  • Condition for equality: (A·B) C = (B·C) A, which is satisfied (nontrivially) if A ∥ C or if B ⟂ A and B ⟂ C (plus trivial zero-vector cases).

Math Insight Algebraic structure

The identity A × (B × C) + B × (C × A) + C × (A × B) = 0 is the Jacobi identity for the cross product. In mathematical physics, it is a signature property of a Lie algebra bracket, explaining why cross products fit naturally into rotations and rigid-body dynamics.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Mathematical model (what objects we are manipulating)

We work in 3D Euclidean space with vectors A, B, C ∈ ℝ³. Choose any right-handed orthonormal Cartesian basis {e₁, e₂, e₃}. Each vector is represented by components: A = (A₁, A₂, A₃), etc. This is a purely algebraic problem (no boundary/initial conditions), but we still state the “domain”:

  • Domain: ℝ³, standard Euclidean metric.
  • Operations: dot product (·) and cross product (×).
  • Assumptions: standard vector identities in ℝ³; bilinearity; distributivity; right-handed orientation.

0.2 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • Dot product: A·B = |A||B| cosθ. Units: if A has units [a] and B has [b], then A·B has [a·b].
  • Cross product: A×B is a vector with magnitude |A||B| sinθ and direction by the right-hand rule. Units: [a·b] (same multiplicative unit as dot, but vector-valued).
  • Levi-Civita symbol: εᵢⱼₖ (totally antisymmetric, ε₁₂₃ = +1). In components: (A×B)ᵢ = εᵢⱼₖ Aⱼ Bₖ.

Physical meaning (why this matters in physics)

  • Torque: τ = r×F (N·m). Nested cross products appear in constrained forces and rigid-body equations.
  • Magnetic force: F = q v×B (N). Combinations like v×(B×n) appear in drift and boundary conditions.
  • Angular momentum transport: L = r×p; manipulating r×(ω×r) is standard in rotating machinery.

0.3 Key law/identity used (BAC–CAB)

The essential identity is: X × (Y × Z) = Y (X·Z) − Z (X·Y). It is valid in ℝ³ because the cross product corresponds to the antisymmetric tensor εᵢⱼₖ and the dot product to δᵢⱼ, enabling index contraction and simplification.

Copied!
X × (Y × Z) = Y (X·Z) − Z (X·Y)    (vector triple product / BAC–CAB)

0.4 Common models/approximations & why they appear

No approximation is needed here (this is exact algebra), but in applications you often see:

  • Small-angle approximations in rotations (sinθ≈θ, cosθ≈1−θ²/2), where cross products linearize angular kinematics.
  • Rigid-body idealization (no deformation), where identities simplify ω×(ω×r) into radial + tangential parts.
  • Linear material response in electromagnetics (B, H linear), making cross-product manipulations purely algebraic.

0.5 Mini intuition examples (quick)

  • If B is parallel to C, then B×C = 0, hence A×(B×C)=0.
  • If B ⟂ A, then A·B = 0, and BAC–CAB reduces to A×(B×C)=B(A·C), i.e., the result is along B.
  • Nested cross products often “project” vectors: A×(B×C) is a linear combination of B and C (never along A unless special geometry forces it).

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Non-associativity: (A×B)×C ≠ A×(B×C) in general.
  • Sign errors: swapping the order of a cross product introduces a minus sign.
  • Confusing scalar triple product A·(B×C) (a scalar) with vector triple products A×(B×C).
  • Dimension check: dot and cross both multiply units; a sum is only valid if units match.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this shows up (concrete application areas)

  • Robotics & automation: Jacobians, moments, and wrench transformations use cross products; nested × terms appear in rigid-body kinematics and dynamics.
  • Rotating machinery (industrial engineering / maintenance): centripetal and Coriolis-type terms involve ω×(ω×r) and 2ω×v; simplifying these correctly matters for vibration prediction and safety margins.
  • Electromagnetic actuators & motors: Lorentz forces and torque density use J×B and r×(J×B); vector identities reduce computational cost and help interpret force directions.
  • Computer-aided engineering (CAE): finite element post-processing often reports forces and moments; identities prevent double-counting and reduce numerical sensitivity.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: correct vector simplifications reduce computation and reveal dominant directions (useful for control and optimization).
  • Safety & reliability: sign/direction mistakes in torque chains can flip predicted loads, leading to under-designed supports or bearings.
  • Cost & manufacturability: understanding which terms cancel (like the cyclic identity) helps simplify models without losing physics.

Typical measurement / validation methods

  • Torque and force sensors (6-axis load cells) validate predicted moments r×F.
  • IMU + motion capture validates ω, α and cross-product kinematics for robotics.
  • Magnetic field mapping + current probes validate J×B force distributions in electromechanical systems.

Design trade-offs and constraints (real-world)

  • Non-ideal geometry: vectors are estimated from noisy measurements → robust algebraic forms reduce sensitivity.
  • Tolerances and misalignment: small angular errors change cross products linearly; triple products can amplify or cancel these effects.
  • Coordinate conventions: right-hand vs left-hand frames; a wrong convention breaks sign consistency.

From lab to product Applied note

In product-grade models, you rarely “compute nested cross products blindly.” You rewrite them using identities (like BAC–CAB and Jacobi) to expose projections and cancellations. This improves numerical stability, simplifies parameter identification, and makes direction-of-load reasoning auditable in design reviews.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

1.1 Restate the problem

We must do two things:

  1. Prove the identity (cyclic sum): A × (B × C) + B × (C × A) + C × (A × B) = 0.
  2. Find the conditions on vectors A, B, C such that: A × (B × C) = (A × B) × C.

1.2 Given quantities, unknowns, and targets

  • Given: arbitrary vectors A, B, C in ℝ³.
  • Unknown: none numerically; we seek algebraic relationships/conditions.
  • To prove: the cyclic identity equals the zero vector.
  • To determine: geometric/algebraic constraints for associativity-like equality.

1.3 Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Bilinearity & distributivity of × and ·: lets us expand sums and factor scalars.
  • Vector triple product identity (BAC–CAB): converts nested × into dot products and linear combinations of vectors.
  • Symmetry / cyclic structure: the expression is a cyclic sum, strongly hinting at cancellation after expansion.

Tools like ∇, ∇·, ∇× are not needed because there are no fields here; this is pure vector algebra. (In field theory, the same identity mirrors Lie-bracket structure behind rotations.)

Assumptions Exact algebra

  • Standard ℝ³ cross product (right-handed orientation).
  • No approximations (no “small-angle” etc.).
  • All vectors can be zero unless stated otherwise; we will separately note trivial cases.

1.4 Possible approaches (pros/cons)

Approach A: Use BAC–CAB directly

  • Pros: shortest; cancellations become obvious; very physics-friendly.
  • Cons: you must trust/know BAC–CAB or prove it quickly.

Approach B: Component proof with εᵢⱼₖ

  • Pros: fully rigorous; shows exactly why it works in 3D.
  • Cons: heavier notation; more index steps.

Approach C: Geometric projection interpretation

  • Pros: strong intuition; reveals “projection onto plane” behavior.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Goal: turn each nested cross product into a linear combination of vectors.
    Tool: BAC–CAB identity.
    Physical meaning: nested cross products behave like projections along the inner vectors.
  2. Goal: expand A×(B×C), B×(C×A), C×(A×B) explicitly.
    Tool: substitute X,Y,Z appropriately into BAC–CAB.
  3. Goal: add the three expressions and group like vector terms.
    Tool: commutativity of dot product: A·B = B·A.
  4. Goal: show each grouped coefficient cancels exactly → sum is 0.
    Meaning: cyclic symmetry forces cancellation (Jacobi identity).
  5. Goal: for associativity condition, set A×(B×C) − (A×B)×C = 0.
    Tool: BAC–CAB twice.
  6. Goal: reduce to a vector equation of the form (A·B)C − (B·C)A = 0.
    Meaning: a collinearity/projection constraint among A and C relative to B.
  7. Goal: interpret solutions geometrically (parallel/perpendicular cases) and include trivial zeros.

Common mistakes Quick tips

  • Do not assume associativity: A×(B×C) ≠ (A×B)×C generally.
  • When using BAC–CAB, keep the order: X × (Y × Z) = Y(X·Z) − Z(X·Y) (middle minus last).
  • Remember: dot products are scalars and commute: A·B = B·A (this is crucial for cancellations).

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

The expression is a cyclic sum. In physics, cyclic sums often encode conservation-like cancellations. Since each term is “a vector crossed with another cross product,” we expect that expanding them into dot products will produce repeated scalar factors that cancel when summed cyclically.

3.2 Step-by-step proof of the cyclic identity

Define symbols: A, B, C are arbitrary vectors in ℝ³. “·” is the dot product; “×” is the cross product.

Copied!
X × (Y × Z) = Y (X·Z) − Z (X·Y)

Apply this identity three times:

Step 1: Expand A × (B × C)

Set X=A, Y=B, Z=C:

Copied!
A × (B × C) = B (A·C) − C (A·B)

Why valid: BAC–CAB follows from bilinearity of × and contraction identities of εᵢⱼₖ with δᵢⱼ (see optional note below).

Step 2: Expand B × (C × A)

Set X=B, Y=C, Z=A:

Copied!
B × (C × A) = C (B·A) − A (B·C)

Step 3: Expand C × (A × B)

Set X=C, Y=A, Z=B:

Copied!
C × (A × B) = A (C·B) − B (C·A)

Step 4: Add them and cancel terms

Sum the three expansions:

Copied!
A×(B×C)+B×(C×A)+C×(A×B)
= [B(A·C)−C(A·B)] + [C(B·A)−A(B·C)] + [A(C·B)−B(C·A)]

Now group by vector directions A, B, C (this is legal because vectors form a linear space, and we are taking linear combinations):

  • B terms: B(A·C) − B(C·A) = B[(A·C) − (C·A)] = 0 because A·C = C·A.
  • C terms: −C(A·B) + C(B·A) = C[−(A·B) + (B·A)] = 0 because A·B = B·A.
  • A terms: −A(B·C) + A(C·B) = A[−(B·C) + (C·B)] = 0 because B·C = C·B.

Conclusion (Identity proved) Jacobi

Every grouped coefficient cancels, so the sum is exactly the zero vector: A × (B × C) + B × (C × A) + C × (A × B) = 0.

Optional deeper note (mathematical physics): BAC–CAB via εᵢⱼₖ contraction

In components, (A×(B×C))ᵢ = εᵢⱼₖ Aⱼ (B×C)ₖ = εᵢⱼₖ Aⱼ εₖₗₘ Bₗ Cₘ. Use the contraction identity: εᵢⱼₖ εₖₗₘ = δᵢₗ δⱼₘ − δᵢₘ δⱼₗ. Then

Copied!
(A×(B×C))i
= εi j k Aj εk ℓ m Bℓ Cm
= (δiℓ δjm − δim δjℓ) Aj Bℓ Cm
= B i (A·C) − C i (A·B)

This proves BAC–CAB rigorously and also shows why the identity is intrinsically 3D (it relies on εᵢⱼₖ).

3.3 When does A × (B × C) = (A × B) × C ?

Start from the two BAC–CAB expansions:

Copied!
A × (B × C) = B (A·C) − C (A·B)
(A × B) × C = B (A·C) − A (B·C)

Why the second line is valid: apply the variant identity (X×Y)×Z = Y(Z·X) − X(Z·Y), which follows from BAC–CAB by using (X×Y)×Z = − Z×(X×Y). Setting X=A, Y=B, Z=C gives: (A×B)×C = B(C·A) − A(C·B) = B(A·C) − A(B·C).

Now enforce equality:

Copied!
A×(B×C) = (A×B)×C
⇔  B(A·C) − C(A·B) = B(A·C) − A(B·C)
⇔  −C(A·B) = −A(B·C)
⇔  (A·B) C = (B·C) A

Condition for “associativity” Final condition

A × (B × C) = (A × B) × C holds if and only if: (A·B) C = (B·C) A.

3.4 Geometric interpretation of the condition

The equation (A·B) C = (B·C) A says that vectors A and C must be aligned in a way set by their projections onto B. There are a few clean nontrivial cases:

  • Case 1: A ∥ C (collinear): If A = λC (including λ=0), then A·B = λ(C·B) and the condition holds automatically. So if A and C are parallel, the identity holds.
  • Case 2: B ⟂ A and B ⟂ C: Then A·B = 0 and B·C = 0, so the condition becomes 0 = 0 (always true). Thus if B is perpendicular to both A and C, the identity holds.
  • Trivial zeros: If A=0 or B=0 or C=0, both sides are the zero vector.

Sanity checks

  • Units: A×(B×C) has units [A][B][C]; the condition (A·B)C = (B·C)A has the same units on both sides: ([A][B])[C] = ([B][C])[A].
  • Limiting cases: If B×C=0 (B ∥ C), then A×(B×C)=0; the right side becomes (A×B)×C which also becomes 0 if C ∥ B (since (A×B) is ⟂ B, and crossing with C parallel to B yields a vector in the plane, but by the condition it reduces consistently). The derived condition correctly predicts when equality holds.
  • Direction reasoning: In general, A×(B×C) lies in span{B,C} and (A×B)×C lies in span{A,B}. Equality forces a strong geometric constraint so both vectors lie in the intersection of these spans—exactly what (A·B)C=(B·C)A encodes.

3.5 Final answer (copyable)

Copied!
1) A×(B×C) + B×(C×A) + C×(A×B) = 0.

2) A×(B×C) = (A×B)×C  ⇔  (A·B)C = (B·C)A.
Nontrivial sufficient cases:
  (i) A ∥ C, or
  (ii) B ⟂ A and B ⟂ C
(plus trivial zero-vector cases).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpret the cyclic identity

Using BAC–CAB, each term becomes a difference of projections: A×(B×C) = B(A·C) − C(A·B). The cyclic sum adds three such “projection differences.” Because dot products are symmetric (A·B = B·A), all projection coefficients cancel in pairs. This is why the identity is exact and general.

4.2 What each term “controls”

  • B(A·C): component along B scaled by how much A aligns with C.
  • −C(A·B): subtract component along C scaled by how much A aligns with B.
  • Cyclic permutations rotate this role among (A,B,C).

4.3 Parameter dependence (qualitative)

Think in angles: A·B = |A||B|cosθ_AB, etc. The equality condition (A·B)C = (B·C)A depends on the cosines of angles with B. If B is nearly orthogonal to both A and C, both dot products are small → the difference between the two nested cross products shrinks.

4.4 Alternative derivation idea (brief)

Another clean route is to use the ε–δ tensor algebra: write every cross product in components with εᵢⱼₖ, contract indices, and use εᵢⱼₖ εₖₗₘ = δᵢₗ δⱼₘ − δᵢₘ δⱼₗ. The cyclic identity then becomes a direct symmetry cancellation. This is the mathematical physics “operator-level” explanation.

4.5 Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Is A×(B×C) always perpendicular to A? A: Not necessarily; it lies in span{B,C}. It is perpendicular to A only in special geometries.
  • Q: When is A×(B×C)=0? A: If B×C=0 (B ∥ C) or if A is parallel to B×C (i.e., A ⟂ span{B,C}).
  • Q: Why does the cyclic sum vanish? A: Because after BAC–CAB expansion, every term is paired with its negative using dot-product symmetry.
  • Q: Give a nontrivial case where A×(B×C)=(A×B)×C. A: A ∥ C (collinear), or B ⟂ A and B ⟂ C.
