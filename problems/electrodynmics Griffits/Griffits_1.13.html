Problem 1.13 — Gradients of the Separation Vector r

We prove three core identities used everywhere in electromagnetics and potential theory: ∇(r²), ∇(1/r), and the general formula ∇(rⁿ). These are the “atomic moves” behind fields like E = -∇V for point charges and many engineering models.

Quick Summary What you will learn

What the problem is about

  • Define the separation vector from a fixed source point (x′,y′,z′) to a field point (x,y,z).
  • Compute gradients with respect to the field coordinates (x,y,z).
  • Show three identities used in electrostatics, gravitation, and diffusion models.

Key physics idea (and math object)

  • For radially symmetric scalars f(r), the gradient points in the radial direction: ∇f = (df/dr) r̂.
  • Math Insight: this is the chain rule in ℝ³ for the composite map (x,y,z) ↦ r(x,y,z) ↦ f(r).

Governing equations (operator form)

  • ∇ = (∂/∂x) x̂ + (∂/∂y) ŷ + (∂/∂z) ẑ
  • r⃗ = (x − x′)x̂ + (y − y′)ŷ + (z − z′)ẑ
  • r = |r⃗| = √[(x−x′)²+(y−y′)²+(z−z′)²]

Results (symbolic) & physical meaning

  • ∇(r²) = 2 r⃗ (gradient points away from the source point)
  • ∇(1/r) = − r̂ / r² = − r⃗ / r³ (key to inverse-square fields)
  • ∇(rⁿ) = n rⁿ⁻² r⃗ = n rⁿ⁻¹ r̂ (general power-law rule)

PART 0 Concept Primer (theory before solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • x,y,z: field-point coordinates (m).
  • x′,y′,z′: fixed source-point coordinates (m); treated as constants when differentiating.
  • r⃗: separation vector from source to field point (m).
  • r = |r⃗|: separation distance (m).
  • r̂ = r⃗ / r: radial unit vector (dimensionless), defined only for r > 0.
  • ∇: gradient operator with respect to (x,y,z) (units: 1/m).

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • r⃗ tells you “which way” and “how far” the field point is from the source point.
  • Gradients of functions of r appear when converting potentials into fields, e.g. E = -∇V.
  • Because r depends on (x,y,z) only through distance, ∇f(r) must point radially (symmetry).

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

  • Multivariable chain rule: if f = f(r) and r = r(x,y,z), then ∂f/∂x = (df/dr)(∂r/∂x), similarly for y,z.
  • Differentiability: all identities are pointwise valid for r > 0; the point r=0 is a singular/undefined point for 1/r and r̂.
  • Linearity of ∇: ∇(a f + b g) = a ∇f + b ∇g for constants a,b.

Common models/approximations (and why)

  • Point-source model: replace a small source by a point at (x′,y′,z′); fields depend on r.
  • Far-field / large-r scaling: many engineering estimates rely on the asymptotic behavior as r → ∞ (e.g., 1/r, 1/r² decay).
  • Ignoring singular core: in practice, sources have finite size; the singularity at r=0 is resolved by geometry or material physics.

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • If you move the field point a small distance dℓ directly away from the source, distance r increases most rapidly. So ∇r points along r̂.
  • A potential V(r) ∝ 1/r produces a field E = -∇V that scales like 1/r² and points radially—this is the inverse-square law geometry.
  • r² behaves like a “quadratic bowl” centered at the source point; its gradient is linear in displacement, like a spring force direction (but note: force would be minus gradient of energy).

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Which variables are differentiated? Here ∇ acts on (x,y,z); the primed coordinates are constants.
  • Unit vector vs vector: r⃗ has units of meters; r̂ is dimensionless.
  • Singularity: expressions with 1/r, 1/r², 1/r³ require r>0.
  • Sign errors: ∇(1/r) is negative; the function decreases as you move outward.

PART 0B Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where it appears in industry (concrete areas)

  • Electrostatic design: ESD risk assessment, sensor electrodes, capacitance modeling, insulation coordination.
  • RF/microwave & EMC: near-field coupling models and potential-based approximations in quasi-static regimes.
  • Gravimetry & geophysics: inverse-square fields for mass distributions; gradient formulas are building blocks.
  • Heat & diffusion analogies: Green’s functions for Poisson/Laplace problems often contain 1/r-type kernels.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: field intensity controls breakdown, leakage, or sensor sensitivity.
  • Safety & reliability: knowing how fields scale with distance supports clearance/creepage and ESD mitigation.
  • Cost & manufacturability: analytic gradients enable fast estimations before expensive simulation iterations.
  • Model-based design: many reduced-order models start from potentials and use gradients to obtain fields/forces.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Field probes / scanning: measure E or V and compare to -∇V predictions.
  • Capacitance/impedance tests: infer field distribution indirectly through lumped parameters.
  • Finite element benchmarking: validate analytic gradients against FEM results (COMSOL/ANSYS) in controlled geometries.

Design trade-offs & non-idealities

  • Finite electrode size removes the r=0 singularity.
  • Materials introduce ε, boundaries, and surface charges—so 1/r may be replaced by image methods or numerical solutions.
  • Tolerances shift the effective source location (x′,y′,z′), so gradients help estimate sensitivity.

From lab to product (how idealized models are adapted)

  • Start with a point-source or simple geometry to get scaling: V ∝ 1/r ⇒ |E| ∝ 1/r².
  • Replace the point with a finite distribution (line/surface/volume integrals). The same gradient identities still appear inside integrands.
  • Add boundaries/materials: solve Laplace/Poisson with boundary conditions; still, ∇(1/r) remains central in Green’s function formulations.

PART 1 Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Restatement: Let r⃗ be the vector from a fixed point (x′,y′,z′) to the point (x,y,z), and let r = |r⃗|. Show: (a) ∇(r²) = 2 r⃗, (b) ∇(1/r) = − r̂ / r², (c) find the general formula for ∇(rⁿ).

Given

  • Fixed source point: (x′,y′,z′) (constants).
  • Field point: (x,y,z) (variables).
  • Separation vector and distance: r⃗ = (x−x′)x̂ + (y−y′)ŷ + (z−z′)ẑ, r = √(r⃗·r⃗).

Unknowns / what must be proved

  • Compute three gradients with respect to (x,y,z).
  • Express answers cleanly using r⃗ and r̂.
  • State domain of validity (especially for 1/r).

Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Definition of gradient in Cartesian coordinates: direct partial differentiation works because r is an explicit function of x,y,z.
  • Chain rule for f(r): simplifies ∇f to (df/dr)∇r.
  • Symmetry: since f depends only on distance from (x′,y′,z′), the gradient must be radial (helps sanity-check direction and sign).

Assumptions (explicit)

  • Cartesian coordinates; Euclidean distance.
  • x′,y′,z′ constants w.r.t. ∂/∂x, ∂/∂y, ∂/∂z.
  • For parts involving 1/r, assume r > 0.

Possible approaches (pros/cons)

  • Component differentiation (best for rigor): write r² explicitly and differentiate term-by-term. Very transparent.
  • Chain rule with ∇r (fast and general): compute ∇r once, then reuse for any f(r).
  • Geometric/symmetry argument (insightful): shows direction must be r̂, then find magnitude by 1D derivative along radial motion.

Choice: We’ll use a hybrid: (i) component differentiation for part (a) (clean and exact), then (ii) chain rule for parts (b) and (c) because it generalizes immediately.

PART 2 Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Roadmap (minimal plan)

  1. Define the model: write r⃗, r, r² as functions of (x,y,z).
  2. Apply ∇ in Cartesian form: ∇ = x̂∂x + ŷ∂y + ẑ∂z.
  3. Compute ∇(r²) by differentiating (x−x′)²+(y−y′)²+(z−z′)² term-by-term.
  4. Compute ∇r using chain rule: r = (r²)^{1/2}.
  5. Use chain rule for any power: for f(r)=rⁿ, use ∇f=(df/dr)∇r.
  6. Specialize to f(r)=1/r and simplify into r̂ form.
  7. Sanity checks: dimensions, direction (radial), limiting cases (r→∞, r→0).

Common mistakes (quick tips)

  • Forgetting that x′,y′,z′ are constants.
  • Dropping the negative sign in ∇(1/r).
  • Mixing r⃗ and r̂: remember r̂ = r⃗/r.
  • Using results at r=0 where they’re undefined.

PART 3 Full Solution (detailed + teaching)

Qualitative expectation first: Any scalar that depends only on the distance r from the source point is spherically symmetric around that source. Therefore, its gradient must point directly outward (along r̂) or inward (along -r̂), depending on whether the scalar increases or decreases with r.

Mathematical model (variables, domain, operators)

  • Independent variables: x,y,z ∈ ℝ (field point).
  • Parameters: x′,y′,z′ fixed constants (source point).
  • Vector field: r⃗(x,y,z) = (x−x′, y−y′, z−z′) in Cartesian basis.
  • Scalar field: r(x,y,z) = √(r⃗·r⃗), defined for all points, but r̂ and 1/r require r>0.
  • Operator: ∇ = x̂ ∂/∂x + ŷ ∂/∂y + ẑ ∂/∂z acting on the unprimed coordinates.
  • Domain: parts (b,c) are pointwise valid on Ω = ℝ³ \ {(x′,y′,z′)}.

Part (a): Show ∇(r²) = 2 r⃗

Key setup Write r² explicitly 
r⃗ = (x−x′) x̂ + (y−y′) ŷ + (z−z′) ẑ
r² = r⃗·r⃗ = (x−x′)² + (y−y′)² + (z−z′)²

Apply the gradient component-by-component. Because x′,y′,z′ are constants:

Differentiate Use ∂/∂x, ∂/∂y, ∂/∂z 
∂(r²)/∂x = ∂/∂x[(x−x′)² + (y−y′)² + (z−z′)²]
         = 2(x−x′) + 0 + 0
         = 2(x−x′)

∂(r²)/∂y = 2(y−y′)
∂(r²)/∂z = 2(z−z′)

Assemble into the vector gradient:

Assemble Build ∇(r²) 
∇(r²) = x̂ ∂(r²)/∂x + ŷ ∂(r²)/∂y + ẑ ∂(r²)/∂z
      = 2(x−x′)x̂ + 2(y−y′)ŷ + 2(z−z′)ẑ
      = 2 r⃗

Result (a)

FinalPart (a)
∇(r²) = 2 r⃗

Why this makes sense: r² increases quadratically away from the source; its steepest ascent is directly away from the source, and the slope grows linearly with displacement.

Part (b): Show ∇(1/r) = − r̂ / r²

A clean way is to first compute ∇r using the chain rule from the already-known ∇(r²). Start from r = (r²)^{1/2} for r>0.

Chain rule Compute ∇r 
r = (r²)^(1/2)
∇r = ∇[(r²)^(1/2)]
   = (1/2)(r²)^(-1/2) ∇(r²)        (by chain rule; scalar multiple)
   = (1/2)(1/r) (2 r⃗)             (use part (a) and (r²)^(1/2)=r)
   = r⃗ / r
   = r̂

Now apply the chain rule again to f(r)=1/r = r^{-1}:

Chain rule Differentiate 1/r 
f(r) = r^(-1)
df/dr = -1 * r^(-2) = -1/r²

∇(1/r) = ∇f(r) = (df/dr) ∇r
       = (-1/r²) (r̂)
       = - r̂ / r²

You’ll often see the equivalent form obtained by replacing r̂ = r⃗/r:

Equivalent Vector form 
∇(1/r) = - r̂ / r² = - (r⃗/r) / r² = - r⃗ / r³

Result (b)

FinalPart (b)
∇(1/r) = − r̂ / r² = − r⃗ / r³   (valid for r > 0)

Physical link: If V(r) ∝ 1/r (point-source potential), then E = -∇V points outward and scales as 1/r².

Part (c): General formula for ∇(rⁿ)

Let n be any real number for which the expressions make sense on the domain of interest (for negative n, require r>0). Define f(r)=rⁿ. Then:

General rule Apply chain rule 
f(r) = r^n
df/dr = n r^(n−1)

∇(r^n) = (df/dr) ∇r
       = (n r^(n−1)) (r̂)
       = n r^(n−1) r̂

Using r̂ = r⃗/r, we can rewrite it in a form that is often more convenient in Cartesian work:

Equivalent In terms of r⃗ 
∇(r^n) = n r^(n−1) (r⃗/r)
       = n r^(n−2) r⃗

Result (c)

FinalPart (c)
∇(r^n) = n r^(n−1) r̂ = n r^(n−2) r⃗
(valid for r>0 when n is not a nonnegative integer)
Sanity checks (dimensions, limits, direction)

1) Dimensions

  • ∇ has units 1/m.
  • r² has units m² ⇒ ∇(r²) has units m, matching 2 r⃗.
  • 1/r has units 1/m ⇒ ∇(1/r) has units 1/m², matching r̂/r².
  • rⁿ has units mⁿ ⇒ ∇(rⁿ) has units mⁿ⁻¹, matching n rⁿ⁻¹ r̂.

2) Limiting cases

  • As r→∞, ∇(1/r) ~ -r̂/r² → 0 (decays as inverse-square).
  • As r→0, ∇(1/r) diverges (reflecting the point-source singularity).
  • For n=2, the general formula gives ∇(r²)=2r⃗, consistent with part (a).
  • For n=-1, it gives ∇(r^{-1}) = -r^{-2} r̂, consistent with part (b).

3) Direction and sign

  • r² increases outward ⇒ gradient outward ⇒ along +r̂.
  • 1/r decreases outward ⇒ gradient inward ⇒ along -r̂.
Final Answer All parts together (plain text) 
(a) ∇(r²) = 2 r⃗

(b) ∇(1/r) = − r̂ / r² = − r⃗ / r³   (valid for r>0)

(c) ∇(rⁿ) = n rⁿ⁻¹ r̂ = n rⁿ⁻² r⃗     (domain: r>0 when needed)

PART 4 Deeper Understanding (theory around the result)

Re-interpretation: what controls the gradient

  • Direction: always radial for f(r) because level sets are spheres centered at (x′,y′,z′).
  • Magnitude: equals the 1D derivative df/dr (how fast the scalar changes with distance).
  • Compact statement: ∇f(r) = (df/dr) r̂, valid wherever r̂ exists.

Parameter dependence & scaling

  • Changing the source location (x′,y′,z′) shifts the “center” of symmetry—important in tolerance/sensitivity analysis.
  • For power laws: |∇(rⁿ)| = |n| rⁿ⁻¹. So:
    • n>1: gradient grows with distance.
    • 0<n<1: gradient decays but more slowly than inverse-square.
    • n<0: gradient diverges near the source (singular).
  • Dimensional analysis check: r is the only length scale here, so any gradient must scale as a power of r consistent with units.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Use a directional derivative along the outward radial direction. If you move the field point by dℓ r̂, then dr = dℓ. By definition, the directional derivative is D_{r̂} f = ∇f · r̂. But also D_{r̂} f = df/dr because along that path only r changes. Hence ∇f · r̂ = df/dr. By symmetry ∇f must be parallel to r̂, so ∇f = (df/dr) r̂.

Math Insight (core equation as a mathematical object)

The identity ∇f(r) = (df/dr) r̂ is the ℝ³ chain rule written in geometric form. It formalizes the idea that for radially symmetric scalars, the gradient is a normal vector to spherical level sets. This is the same structure that underlies: E = -∇V in electrostatics, and (more broadly) Green’s-function kernels for Poisson/Laplace equations where 1/r appears.

Concept checks (self-test, with answers)
  • Q: Why must ∇f(r) be radial?
    A: Because f is constant on spheres centered at the source, and gradients are normal to level sets.
  • Q: What is ∇r for r>0?
    A: ∇r = r̂.
  • Q: Does ∇(1/r) point outward or inward?
    A: Inward (negative radial direction) because 1/r decreases as r increases.
  • Q: Plug n=0 into the general rule. What happens?
    A: r⁰=1 is constant, so ∇(r⁰)=0. The formula gives n r^(n−2) r⃗ = 0, consistent.
  • Q: Why is r̂ undefined at r=0?
    A: The direction from source to itself is not unique, and division by r occurs in r̂ = r⃗/r.
