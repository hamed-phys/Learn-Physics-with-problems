Quick Summary

  • What this problem is about: proving the vector triple product identity (BAC–CAB rule).
  • Key physics idea: a “double rotation” A × (B × C) collapses into projections onto B and C.
  • Mathematical tool: component/index notation with the Levi–Civita symbol ε and Kronecker delta δ.
  • Governing relations: (X×Y)_i = ε_{ijk} X_j Y_k and ε_{ijk} ε_{klm} = δ_{il}δ_{jm} − δ_{im}δ_{jl}.
  • Result type: symbolic identity valid for all vectors in ℝ³.
  • Final result (meaning): the output lies in the plane spanned by B and C, not perpendicular to it.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Mathematical model (variables, domain, coordinates)

Model

We work in 3D Euclidean space, ℝ³, with an orthonormal Cartesian basis {e₁, e₂, e₃}. Vectors A, B, C ∈ ℝ³ have components A_i, B_i, C_i for i ∈ {1,2,3}.

No boundary/initial conditions are needed: this is a purely algebraic identity in vector space.

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • Vectors: A, B, C (units depend on context, e.g., N, m, T, A/m, etc.).
  • Dot product: A·B (units multiply: unit(A)×unit(B)).
  • Cross product: A×B (units multiply: unit(A)×unit(B)).
  • Levi–Civita symbol: ε_{ijk} (dimensionless, antisymmetric).
  • Kronecker delta: δ_{ij} (dimensionless; acts like identity matrix).

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Dot product measures projection/alignment: A·B = |A||B|cosθ.
  • Cross product measures oriented area/rotation: |A×B| = |A||B|sinθ, direction by right-hand rule.
  • Triple cross product A×(B×C) is not “3D volume”; it is a vector built from two successive perpendicular operations.

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

Assumptions / validity
• Standard 3D cross product (exists specifically in ℝ³ with right-hand orientation).
• Orthonormal Cartesian coordinates used for component expansion.
• Einstein summation convention over repeated indices.

Common models/approximations in this topic

None are required here. This is an exact identity. In physics/engineering it is often used as a simplification step when modeling torques, Lorentz forces, rigid-body kinematics, fluid vorticity, or electromagnetic field identities.

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • If A ⟂ B and A ⟂ C: then A·B = A·C = 0, so BAC–CAB predicts A×(B×C)=0. That matches the idea: A has no component along B or C to “pull out” via projection.
  • If B = C: then B×C=0, so LHS is zero. RHS gives B(A·B) − B(A·B)=0.
  • Geometry: RHS is a combination of B and C, so the result must lie in the plane spanned by B and C.

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Cross product is not associative: (A×B)×C ≠ A×(B×C).
  • Keep index positions consistent and sum only repeated indices.
  • Use the correct contraction identity: εε → δδ − δδ, with signs in the right order.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (concrete application areas)

  • Robotics & automation: torque and moment transformations, simplifying expressions like r × (ω × r) (centripetal/centrifugal terms in rigid-body motion).
  • Electromechanical systems: magnetic forces/torques where nested cross products appear in actuator modeling and motor design.
  • Fluid & process engineering: vector identities in vorticity and Navier–Stokes manipulations (e.g., rewriting v×(∇×v) forms).

Why engineers care

  • Performance: algebraic simplification reduces computation and clarifies dominant terms.
  • Reliability & safety: correct torque/force directions matter for fatigue, stability, and control.
  • Cost/efficiency: cleaner models enable faster simulation, easier controller tuning, and fewer design iterations.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Force/torque sensors (load cells, torque transducers) to validate mechanical models.
  • Motion capture/IMUs to validate rigid-body kinematic terms with cross products.
  • Field probes and current measurements for electromagnetic actuator validation.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Real systems have tolerances, misalignment, nonlinearity, saturation, and friction—vector identities still help, but parameters become uncertain.
  • Coordinate frames may rotate; careful frame definitions and sign conventions are critical.
From lab to product: adapting the idealized model

The BAC–CAB identity is exact, but in products you often estimate vectors from sensors with noise. Engineers combine this identity with filtering, calibration, and coordinate transformation pipelines (e.g., rotation matrices) so that the simplified expression remains numerically stable and physically meaningful.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Restated problem (in my words): Show that the vector triple product satisfies the BAC–CAB rule by expanding both sides into components and verifying equality for each component.

Given

A, B, C are arbitrary vectors in ℝ³.

Unknown

A proof (identity) rather than a numerical value.

Must prove

A × (B × C) = B(A·C) − C(A·B).

Relevant principles/laws (and why they apply)

  • Definition of cross product in components using ε_{ijk} — directly converts vectors to algebra.
  • Definition of dot product as a contraction — allows recognizing projections.
  • ε–ε contraction identity — the key algebraic bridge that eliminates the nested cross product.

No calculus operators (∇, ∇·, ∇×) are required here because this is not a field identity, just a vector algebra identity.

Assumptions

Assumptions box
• 3D Euclidean space, right-handed orthonormal basis.
• Standard definitions of dot and cross product.
• Linearity of sums (finite sums over indices) and distributive algebra.

Possible approaches (compare)

  • Component/index proof (best here): systematic, unambiguous, general. Requires ε and δ familiarity.
  • Geometric proof: interpret as projection onto plane spanned by B and C. Intuitive but can be less rigorous.
  • Determinant/coordinate expansion: expand cross products via determinant forms; works but is longer and error-prone.

Chosen approach: component/index notation, because the problem explicitly asks for component form and it yields a short, rigorous proof with clear algebraic justification.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Goal: Express B×C in components. Tool: (X×Y)_k = ε_{klm} X_l Y_m. Meaning: a vector perpendicular to B and C.
  2. Goal: Express A×(B×C) in components. Tool: cross product definition again. Meaning: another perpendicular operation applied to the result.
  3. Goal: Replace ε_{ijk}ε_{klm} with deltas. Tool: ε–ε contraction identity. Meaning: turns “rotations” into projection operators.
  4. Goal: Collapse deltas onto components. Tool: δ_{ij}X_j = X_i. Meaning: extracts the correct component.
  5. Goal: Recognize dot products. Tool: A·B = A_j B_j. Meaning: scalar projections appear naturally.
  6. Goal: Reassemble vector form and box the identity.
Common mistakes
• Treating × as associative: (A×B)×C is different from A×(B×C).
• Swapping indices in ε without changing sign.
• Losing track of dummy indices (renaming is allowed, but be consistent).
• Forgetting that repeated indices are summed.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before algebra)

B×C is perpendicular to the plane of B and C. Then A×(B×C) is perpendicular to B×C, so it must lie in the plane spanned by B and C. That already suggests the answer should be a linear combination of B and C, matching B(A·C) − C(A·B).

Step-by-step component derivation

Define symbols (again, explicitly):

  • ε_{ijk}: Levi–Civita symbol, antisymmetric, ε_{123}=+1.
  • δ_{ij}: Kronecker delta, δ_{ij}=1 if i=j else 0.
  • Einstein summation: repeated indices are summed from 1 to 3.
Key equation: cross product in components
(X × Y)_i = ε_{ijk} X_j Y_k

1) Write the inner cross product B×C in components.

Inner cross product
(B × C)_k = ε_{klm} B_l C_m

2) Now write the outer cross product A×(B×C) in components.

Using the definition again (linearity justifies substitution because we are manipulating finite sums):

Outer cross product
(A × (B × C))_i = ε_{ijk} A_j (B × C)_k

3) Substitute the expression for (B×C)_k.

Substitution
(A × (B × C))_i = ε_{ijk} A_j (ε_{klm} B_l C_m)
                  = ε_{ijk} ε_{klm} A_j B_l C_m

Why this step is valid: cross product is bilinear; in components it becomes a sum of products. We are simply regrouping scalars (commutative/associative multiplication of real numbers) inside a finite sum.

4) Use the ε–ε contraction identity.

Key tensor identity
ε_{ijk} ε_{klm} = δ_{il} δ_{jm} − δ_{im} δ_{jl}

Substitute it into the expression:

Apply contraction
(A × (B × C))_i
= (δ_{il} δ_{jm} − δ_{im} δ_{jl}) A_j B_l C_m

5) Distribute and simplify each term using δ properties.

Term 1:

First term simplification
δ_{il} δ_{jm} A_j B_l C_m
= δ_{il} B_l · (δ_{jm} A_j C_m)
= B_i · (A_m C_m)
= B_i (A·C)

Explanation: δ_{il}B_l = B_i picks out the i-th component of B, and δ_{jm}A_j = A_m then contracts with C_m to form the dot product.

Term 2:

Second term simplification
δ_{im} δ_{jl} A_j B_l C_m
= δ_{im} C_m · (δ_{jl} A_j B_l)
= C_i · (A_l B_l)
= C_i (A·B)

6) Combine terms (note the minus sign).

Component result
(A × (B × C))_i = B_i (A·C) − C_i (A·B)

Since this holds for every component i = 1,2,3, the vector identity follows:

Final BAC–CAB Rule
A × (B × C) = B (A·C) − C (A·B)

Sanity checks

Dimensional analysis

LHS has units unit(A)·unit(B)·unit(C) in the sense of two multiplications: B×C has unit(B)unit(C), then cross with A gives unit(A)unit(B)unit(C). RHS: A·C has unit(A)unit(C), multiplying by B gives the same; similarly for the other term. Units match.

Limiting cases

If B and C are parallel, B×C=0 so LHS = 0. RHS becomes B(A·C) − C(A·B). If C = λB, then RHS = B(A·(λB)) − (λB)(A·B) = 0. Works.

Direction reasoning

RHS is a linear combination of B and C, so it lies in span{B,C}. This matches the geometric expectation that A×(B×C) lies in the plane of B and C.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpretation: what each term controls

  • B(A·C): scales B by how much A aligns with C.
  • −C(A·B): subtracts a component along C scaled by how much A aligns with B.
  • So the operation is essentially a difference of projection-weighted directions.
Math Insight (interpreting the core equation as a mathematical object)
The BAC–CAB rule is a contraction identity:
a rank-3 antisymmetric tensor (ε) composed twice reduces to products of the identity tensor (δ),
turning “rotations” (cross products) into “projections” (dot products times vectors).

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • If A becomes more aligned with C, (A·C) increases, strengthening the B term.
  • If A becomes more aligned with B, (A·B) increases, strengthening the subtraction along C.
  • If A is orthogonal to both B and C, both dot products vanish and the result is zero.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Geometric/projection-based derivation sketch

One can show that for fixed B, C, the map A ↦ A×(B×C) is linear in A and always lies in span{B,C}. Therefore it must be of the form α(A)B + β(A)C, where α and β are linear functionals (dot products with something). By testing special choices of A (e.g., A=B, A=C, and A orthogonal to both), one uniquely determines α(A)=A·C and β(A)=-(A·B), recovering B(A·C) − C(A·B).

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Is A×(B×C) perpendicular to B? A: Not necessarily. The result can have a component along B (it literally contains a B term).
  • Q: Where does A×(B×C) live geometrically? A: In the plane spanned by B and C.
  • Q: What breaks if we move to dimensions other than 3? A: The standard cross product (as a binary vector → vector) is special to 3D; the ε-based proof assumes 3 indices.
  • Q: Why is the sign “−” and not “+”? A: It comes from the antisymmetry of ε and the specific ε–ε contraction identity.
