Divergence Transforms as a Scalar Under 2D Rotations

Problem 1.17: In two dimensions, show that the divergence is invariant under rotations: (∂v̄y/∂ȳ + ∂v̄z/∂ż) = (∂vy/∂y + ∂vz/∂z). We’ll do it explicitly using rotation relations and the chain rule, and then interpret the result as a tensor-trace invariance.

Quick Summary

  • What it’s about: Proving that the divergence of a 2D vector field is rotation-invariant (a scalar).
  • Key physics idea: Divergence measures local expansion/compression (net outflow per area). A rigid rotation of axes should not change a physical expansion rate.
  • Governing operator: ∇·v = ∂vy/∂y + ∂vz/∂z (here the plane is labeled by coordinates y and z).
  • Coordinate transform: A rotation by angle θ maps (y, z) ↔ (ȳ, ż) by an orthogonal matrix R(θ).
  • Core math tool: Chain rule for partial derivatives under coordinate changes.
  • Result type: Purely symbolic identity (no numbers needed).
  • Main result: ∂v̄y/∂ȳ + ∂v̄z/∂ż = ∂vy/∂y + ∂vz/∂z.
  • Physical meaning: The local volumetric (area) strain rate is objective (frame-invariant) under rigid rotations.

Key equation Divergence (2D)

∇·v = ∂v_y/∂y + ∂v_z/∂z

Units check: v has units m/s, derivative adds 1/m, so divergence has 1/s (a rate).

Math Insight Divergence as a trace

The divergence is the trace of the velocity gradient tensor: ∇·v = tr(∇v). Under a rotation, ∇v transforms by a similarity transform, which preserves the trace.

Why this matters mathematically

If Ā = R A Rᵀ with R orthogonal (RᵀR=I), then tr(Ā)=tr(R A Rᵀ)=tr(A RᵀR)=tr(A). That “trace invariance” is the clean conceptual reason divergence behaves like a scalar under rotations.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Mathematical model and definitions

We work in a two-dimensional region Ω ⊂ ℝ² with coordinates (y, z). A (sufficiently smooth) vector field is v(y,z) = vy(y,z) ey + vz(y,z) ez.

Symbols and typical SI units

  • y, z: spatial coordinates [m]
  • vy, vz: velocity components [m/s]
  • ∂/∂y, ∂/∂z: spatial derivatives [1/m]
  • ∇·v: divergence [1/s]
  • θ: rotation angle [rad] (dimensionless)
  • R(θ): 2×2 rotation matrix (dimensionless, orthogonal)

Physical meaning of divergence

Divergence at a point is the net outflow per unit area from an infinitesimal box. Positive ∇·v means local expansion (source-like behavior); negative means compression (sink-like behavior).

In fluid mechanics, mass conservation often gives (for incompressible flow) ∇·v = 0. This condition must not depend on how you rotate your axes.

0.2 Operator notation and validity conditions

In 2D (y,z), the divergence operator is ∇·v = ∂vy/∂y + ∂vz/∂z. This formula assumes:

Assumptions (what must be true)

  • Rigid rotation of coordinates (no stretching/shearing): orthogonal transform.
  • Field is at least C¹ (continuous first partial derivatives) so chain rule applies cleanly.
  • We interpret v as a geometric vector field: components change with basis, but the physical vector is the same.

0.3 Common models/approximations in this topic

This problem is purely geometric: no small-angle or linearization approximations are needed. The only “model” is the use of an orthogonal rotation matrix.

0.4 Mini intuition examples (no heavy algebra)

  • Uniform translation: v = constant ⇒ ∇·v = 0. Rotating axes won’t change “zero.”
  • Pure expansion: v = (a y) ey + (a z) ez ⇒ ∇·v = 2a. Rotating axes doesn’t change the expansion rate.
  • Pure rotation field: v = (−ω z) ey + (ω y) ez ⇒ ∇·v = 0 (rotation has no net expansion).

0.5 What to watch for (pitfalls)

What to watch for

  • Mixing active vs passive rotation: Are you rotating the vector, the axes, or both? Here: rotate axes (coordinate change).
  • Using the wrong inverse transform: Derivatives need y(ȳ,ż) and z(ȳ,ż), not the forward map.
  • Forgetting chain rule cross-terms: ∂/∂ȳ involves both ∂/∂y and ∂/∂z.
  • Sign convention errors: Rotation matrix form must be consistent for coordinates and components.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Rotation-invariance of divergence is not just “nice math” — it’s a consistency requirement for any physical law written in vector calculus form.

Where this appears in industry (3+ concrete areas)

  • CFD (computational fluid dynamics): Incompressibility (∇·v=0) and mass conservation must hold regardless of mesh orientation.
  • Continuum mechanics & manufacturing: Volumetric strain rate (linked to ∇·v) in forming, extrusion, rolling, and polymer processing.
  • Heat/mass transfer & process engineering: Conservation laws in rotating equipment, mixers, pumps; coordinate frames can change in rotating machinery.
  • Robotics & autonomy: Flow fields for navigation, divergence-based collision/expansion cues; invariance matters when sensor frames rotate.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: Correct conservation (no artificial sources/sinks due to coordinate choice).
  • Reliability: Frame-invariant diagnostics (expansion/compression metrics) prevent orientation-dependent bugs.
  • Cost & time: Debugging simulation/measurement pipelines is easier when invariances are enforced.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • PIV (Particle Image Velocimetry): Measures velocity fields; divergence estimates should not change under camera frame rotations (after proper component transforms).
  • Hot-wire / LDV: Local velocity components; reconstruction into different frames should preserve ∇·v.
  • Numerical verification: Manufactured solutions in CFD: rotate the coordinate system and confirm invariants (like divergence) remain unchanged.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Discrete derivatives: On a grid, numerical divergence can be sensitive to stencil choice; ensuring near-rotation-invariance improves accuracy.
  • Noise amplification: Differentiation amplifies measurement noise; engineers use filtering/regularization, but must preserve invariants as much as possible.
  • Non-idealities: Real systems have 3D effects, compressibility, and anisotropy; still, rigid rotation of axes should not alter the scalar divergence computed from the same physical field.
From lab to product: how the ideal model is adapted

In products (fans, pumps, reactors), you rarely measure a full continuous field. You sample velocities, transform components between coordinate frames, and estimate derivatives numerically. The “divergence is a scalar” principle becomes a quality check: if your pipeline yields different divergence values after a rigid rotation of the frame, something is wrong (calibration, coordinate transform, discretization, or sign conventions).

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

1.1 Restate the problem

In the (y,z) plane, consider a vector field v with components (vy, vz). Rotate the coordinate axes by an angle θ to new coordinates (ȳ, ż) and corresponding components (v̄y, v̄z). Prove that the divergence is unchanged: 

∂v̄_y/∂ȳ + ∂v̄_z/∂ż = ∂v_y/∂y + ∂v_z/∂z

1.2 Given, unknowns, and what must be proved

Given

  • A rotation by angle θ between frames (y,z) and (ȳ,ż).
  • A differentiable vector field v(y,z).
  • Transformation rules for coordinates and components (from the referenced Eq. 1.29 idea).

To prove

  • Divergence computed in the rotated frame equals divergence in the original frame.
  • Interpretation: divergence behaves like a scalar under rotations.

1.3 Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Rotation relations: coordinates and components are related by an orthogonal matrix R(θ).
  • Chain rule: converts ∂/∂ȳ and ∂/∂ż into combinations of ∂/∂y and ∂/∂z.
  • Linearity: partial derivatives distribute over sums and constants like sinθ, cosθ.

1.4 Explicit assumptions

Assumptions

  • θ is constant (a rigid rotation, not varying with position).
  • v has continuous first derivatives (so mixed partials exist and the chain rule is valid).
  • We do a passive rotation (change coordinates/basis), not changing the physical field.

1.5 Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

Approach A Direct chain-rule computation

Write v̄ components in terms of v, and ∂/∂ȳ, ∂/∂ż in terms of ∂/∂y, ∂/∂z. Expand and show cross-terms cancel using sin²+cos²=1.

Pros: explicit, matches the problem hint. Cons: algebra-heavy.

Approach B Tensor/linear algebra (trace invariance)

Use ∇·v = tr(∇v) and the rotation law ∇v → R(∇v)Rᵀ. Trace is invariant under similarity transforms.

Pros: short and conceptual. Cons: requires comfort with tensors/matrices.

We choose Approach A (direct chain rule) because it follows the hint and teaches the mechanics of coordinate differentiation. We will still add Approach B afterward as an elegant alternative interpretation.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Define the rotation: write (ȳ,ż) in terms of (y,z) and also the inverse map. Tool: rotation matrix; orthogonality.
  2. Transform vector components: express (v̄y, v̄z) in terms of (vy, vz). Tool: same rotation matrix for components.
  3. Convert derivative operators: compute ∂/∂ȳ and ∂/∂ż as combinations of ∂/∂y and ∂/∂z via chain rule. Meaning: how slopes change under coordinates.
  4. Compute ∂v̄y/∂ȳ: apply the operator to the expression for v̄y; keep every term. Tool: linearity of differentiation.
  5. Compute ∂v̄z/∂ż: similarly, and track signs carefully.
  6. Add them: show the mixed (cross) derivative terms cancel exactly; use sin²θ+cos²θ=1.
  7. Conclude invariance: identify the remaining terms as ∂vy/∂y + ∂vz/∂z.
  8. Sanity checks: units (1/s), special fields (pure rotation gives 0), and physical meaning (expansion rate is objective).

Common mistakes

  • Using the forward transform where the inverse is needed for derivatives.
  • Differentiating sinθ or cosθ as if θ depended on position (it does not here).
  • Dropping cross terms: ∂/∂ȳ is not the same as ∂/∂y.
  • Inconsistent sign convention between (ȳ,ż) rotation and (v̄y, v̄z) rotation.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

Divergence measures net outflow per area from an infinitesimal region. A rigid rotation of axes does not stretch space; it only changes how we label directions. So any physically meaningful scalar rate like “local expansion” should remain the same. We expect the divergence to be invariant under rotations.

3.2 Step 1 — Define the rotation (coordinates)

Let the new coordinates (ȳ, ż) be obtained from (y, z) by a rotation of axes by angle θ. A standard (right-handed) rotation mapping is: 

ȳ =  y cosθ + z sinθ
ż = -y sinθ + z cosθ

Why this is valid: this is an orthogonal transform with determinant +1 (a proper rotation). Orthogonality preserves lengths and areas.

We will need the inverse relations to apply the chain rule. Since rotations are orthogonal, the inverse is the transpose (θ → −θ): 

y =  ȳ cosθ - ż sinθ
z =  ȳ sinθ + ż cosθ
Matrix form (compact)

Define column vectors r = [y, z]ᵀ and r̄ = [ȳ, ż]ᵀ. Then r̄ = R r with R = [[cosθ, sinθ], [-sinθ, cosθ]]. Orthogonality: RᵀR = I.

3.3 Step 2 — Transform the vector components

A physical vector v is the same geometric object, but its components change with the basis. Under the same rotation of axes, the components transform as: 

v̄_y =  v_y cosθ + v_z sinθ
v̄_z = -v_y sinθ + v_z cosθ

Why this is valid: in an orthonormal basis, rotating the coordinate frame corresponds to an orthogonal change of basis, so component vectors transform by the rotation matrix.

3.4 Step 3 — Convert derivative operators via the chain rule

We want derivatives with respect to (ȳ, ż), but vy and vz are functions of (y, z). Use the chain rule for partial derivatives: 

∂/∂ȳ = (∂y/∂ȳ) ∂/∂y + (∂z/∂ȳ) ∂/∂z
∂/∂ż = (∂y/∂ż) ∂/∂y + (∂z/∂ż) ∂/∂z

From the inverse coordinate map: y = ȳ cosθ − ż sinθ and z = ȳ sinθ + ż cosθ, we read off the needed partial derivatives: 

∂y/∂ȳ = cosθ     ∂z/∂ȳ = sinθ
∂y/∂ż = -sinθ    ∂z/∂ż = cosθ

Therefore the derivative operators become: 

∂/∂ȳ = cosθ ∂/∂y + sinθ ∂/∂z
∂/∂ż = -sinθ ∂/∂y + cosθ ∂/∂z

Why each step is valid: θ is constant, so sinθ and cosθ are constants with respect to y,z,ȳ,ż; differentiation is linear; and the chain rule applies since the mapping is smooth and invertible.

3.5 Step 4 — Compute ∂v̄y/∂ȳ explicitly

Start from v̄y = vy cosθ + vz sinθ, and apply ∂/∂ȳ: 

∂v̄_y/∂ȳ = (cosθ ∂/∂y + sinθ ∂/∂z)( v_y cosθ + v_z sinθ )

Expand step-by-step using linearity:

∂v̄_y/∂ȳ
= cosθ ∂(v_y cosθ + v_z sinθ)/∂y  +  sinθ ∂(v_y cosθ + v_z sinθ)/∂z

Because cosθ and sinθ are constants:

∂(v_y cosθ + v_z sinθ)/∂y = (cosθ) ∂v_y/∂y + (sinθ) ∂v_z/∂y
∂(v_y cosθ + v_z sinθ)/∂z = (cosθ) ∂v_y/∂z + (sinθ) ∂v_z/∂z

Substitute back and distribute:

∂v̄_y/∂ȳ
= cosθ[ cosθ ∂v_y/∂y + sinθ ∂v_z/∂y ] + sinθ[ cosθ ∂v_y/∂z + sinθ ∂v_z/∂z ]
= cos²θ ∂v_y/∂y + (sinθ cosθ) ∂v_z/∂y + (sinθ cosθ) ∂v_y/∂z + sin²θ ∂v_z/∂z

3.6 Step 5 — Compute ∂v̄z/∂ż explicitly

Now v̄z = −vy sinθ + vz cosθ, and apply ∂/∂ż = −sinθ ∂/∂y + cosθ ∂/∂z: 

∂v̄_z/∂ż = (-sinθ ∂/∂y + cosθ ∂/∂z)( -v_y sinθ + v_z cosθ )

Expand carefully:

∂v̄_z/∂ż
= -sinθ ∂(-v_y sinθ + v_z cosθ)/∂y + cosθ ∂(-v_y sinθ + v_z cosθ)/∂z

Differentiate inside (sinθ, cosθ constants):

∂(-v_y sinθ + v_z cosθ)/∂y = -(sinθ) ∂v_y/∂y + (cosθ) ∂v_z/∂y
∂(-v_y sinθ + v_z cosθ)/∂z = -(sinθ) ∂v_y/∂z + (cosθ) ∂v_z/∂z

Substitute and distribute:

∂v̄_z/∂ż
= -sinθ[ -(sinθ) ∂v_y/∂y + (cosθ) ∂v_z/∂y ] + cosθ[ -(sinθ) ∂v_y/∂z + (cosθ) ∂v_z/∂z ]
= sin²θ ∂v_y/∂y - (sinθ cosθ) ∂v_z/∂y - (sinθ cosθ) ∂v_y/∂z + cos²θ ∂v_z/∂z

3.7 Step 6 — Add them: cross terms cancel

Add the two expressions: 

(∂v̄_y/∂ȳ) + (∂v̄_z/∂ż)
= [cos²θ ∂v_y/∂y + (sinθ cosθ) ∂v_z/∂y + (sinθ cosθ) ∂v_y/∂z + sin²θ ∂v_z/∂z]
+ [sin²θ ∂v_y/∂y - (sinθ cosθ) ∂v_z/∂y - (sinθ cosθ) ∂v_y/∂z + cos²θ ∂v_z/∂z]

The mixed terms cancel exactly: 

+(sinθ cosθ) ∂v_z/∂y  and  -(sinθ cosθ) ∂v_z/∂y  cancel
+(sinθ cosθ) ∂v_y/∂z  and  -(sinθ cosθ) ∂v_y/∂z  cancel

The remaining terms combine using sin²θ + cos²θ = 1: 

∂v̄_y/∂ȳ + ∂v̄_z/∂ż
= (cos²θ + sin²θ) ∂v_y/∂y + (sin²θ + cos²θ) ∂v_z/∂z
= ∂v_y/∂y + ∂v_z/∂z

✅ Final Answer (boxed)

∂v̄_y/∂ȳ + ∂v̄_z/∂ż = ∂v_y/∂y + ∂v_z/∂z

Therefore, in two dimensions, divergence transforms as a scalar under rotations.

3.8 Sanity checks

Dimensional analysis

Both sides are sums of spatial derivatives of velocity components: [v]/[x] = (m/s)/m = 1/s. Rotation is dimensionless. Units match.

Limiting cases

  • θ = 0: barred and unbarred frames coincide ⇒ identity holds trivially.
  • θ = π/2: swap axes (with signs) ⇒ still gives the same divergence.

Physical interpretation

The quantity ∇·v is the local area expansion rate (in 2D). Rotating your coordinate frame cannot change a physical expansion/compression rate, so invariance is exactly what “objectivity” demands.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpret the result: what each term controls

In 2D, the velocity gradient (Jacobian) matrix is 

∇v = [ ∂v_y/∂y   ∂v_y/∂z
       ∂v_z/∂y   ∂v_z/∂z ]

The divergence is the trace of this matrix: 

∇·v = tr(∇v) = ∂v_y/∂y + ∂v_z/∂z

The diagonal terms represent expansion/compression along the coordinate directions; the trace sums them into a coordinate-independent scalar for rigid rotations.

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative)

There is no “material parameter” here; the only parameter is the rotation angle θ. The result says the divergence does not depend on θ at all for rigid rotations.

4.3 Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Approach B: linear algebra proof (trace invariance)

Let R be a 2×2 rotation matrix (orthogonal: RᵀR=I). Under a change of orthonormal basis, the velocity gradient transforms as: 

∇v  →  ∇̄v̄ = R (∇v) Rᵀ

Divergence is the trace: 

∇̄·v̄ = tr(∇̄v̄) = tr( R (∇v) Rᵀ )
= tr( (∇v) RᵀR )   (cyclic property of trace)
= tr(∇v)
= ∇·v

This proof highlights the deep reason: divergence is an invariant (trace) of a linear map under orthogonal similarity transforms.

4.4 Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: What physical quantity does divergence represent in a velocity field? A: Local net outflow per area (area expansion/compression rate in 2D).
  • Q: Why can we treat sinθ and cosθ as constants in the derivatives? A: Because θ is a fixed rotation angle (independent of position).
  • Q: What mathematical identity drives the cancellation? A: Orthogonality of rotation (sin²θ+cos²θ=1) and equal/opposite cross terms.
  • Q: If the coordinate transform were not orthogonal (e.g., a shear), would divergence remain the same? A: Not in general; the invariance shown here is specifically for rigid rotations (orthogonal transforms).
  • Q: What is the “math object” viewpoint of divergence? A: The trace of the Jacobian (velocity gradient) tensor.
