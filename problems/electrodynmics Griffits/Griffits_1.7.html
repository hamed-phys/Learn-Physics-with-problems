Problem 1.8 — Why Rotation Matrices Preserve Dot Products (2D) and Lengths (3D)

We prove (a) a 2D rotation leaves the dot product invariant, and (b) derive the exact algebraic constraints on a 3D rotation matrix so that every vector keeps its length. This is the mathematical backbone of “rigid motion” used across physics and engineering.

Quick Summary

  • What this is about: Rotations in 2D/3D and the algebraic conditions that make them “length-preserving.”
  • Key physics idea: A rotation represents a rigid change of coordinates (or rigid motion), so it must keep angles and lengths unchanged.
  • Governing equations: Linear map A′ = R A with dot product invariance A′·B′ = A·B.
  • 2D result: The standard rotation matrix R(φ) = [[cosφ, sinφ],[-sinφ, cosφ]] satisfies R(φ)ᵀ R(φ) = I, hence preserves dot products.
  • 3D condition: To preserve length for all vectors, the 3×3 matrix must satisfy RᵀR = I (orthogonal columns/rows). For a proper rotation: det R = +1.
  • Physical meaning: Orthogonality encodes “no stretching/shearing”; determinant +1 selects orientation-preserving rotations (not reflections).
  • Sanity check: If RᵀR = I then |RA|² = (RA)·(RA) = A·A = |A|² automatically.

Math Insight

“Preserving dot products” means the transformation is an isometry of Euclidean space. In linear algebra, the set of all matrices that preserve the Euclidean inner product forms the orthogonal group O(n) = { R ∈ ℝ^{n×n} : RᵀR = I }. The subset with det R = +1 is the special orthogonal group SO(n), i.e., proper rotations.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Mathematical model (explicit)

We model vectors as elements of Euclidean space: 2D vectors A,B ∈ ℝ² and 3D vectors A ∈ ℝ³. A rotation is a linear operator R acting by A′ = R A.

  • Domain: vectors in ℝ² or ℝ³.
  • Coordinate system: Cartesian basis (unit vectors along axes). Components are A = (A_y, A_z) in 2D (as given) and A = (A_x, A_y, A_z) in 3D.
  • Boundary/initial conditions: Not applicable (this is linear algebra / geometry, not a PDE/ODE evolution problem).

0.2 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

Dot product (inner product)

A·B = Σ_i A_i B_i (units: product of units of A and B)

In 2D: A·B = A_y B_y + A_z B_z.

In 3D: A·B = A_x B_x + A_y B_y + A_z B_z.

Length (Euclidean norm)

|A| = sqrt(A·A) (same SI units as A)

Squared length: |A|² = A·A.

0.3 Physical meaning

  • Dot product encodes angles: A·B = |A||B| cosθ. If dot products are preserved for all pairs, then angles and lengths are preserved.
  • Rotation is a rigid transformation: it changes orientation but not shape, size, or angles.

0.4 Key principle (and when it’s valid)

A true rotation in Euclidean space is an orthogonal linear transformation. Algebraically, orthogonality is expressed by: RᵀR = I. This is valid when we use the standard Euclidean metric (ordinary Cartesian dot product).

Assumptions (hidden in the word “rotation”)

  • The space is Euclidean (standard dot product, no curved metrics).
  • The transformation is linear and acts the same everywhere (no translations, no nonlinear warping).
  • We mean a rigid rotation (no scaling, shearing, or reflection unless stated).

0.5 Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • Compass needle: Rotating the coordinate axes should not change the measured length of the needle; only its components change.
  • Robot arm pose: The orientation of a tool changes with joint angles, but the tool’s physical size does not. Rotation matrices model these orientation changes.
  • RF / EM polarization: If you rotate your reference axes, the measured field components change, but the field magnitude (power) must remain the same in lossless coordinate change.

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Mixing up active rotation (rotating the vector) vs passive rotation (rotating the coordinate axes). The same matrix forms appear, but interpretation matters.
  • For 2D matrices, not every matrix with determinant 1 is a rotation; you still need RᵀR=I.
  • In 3D, RᵀR=I includes both rotations and reflections; det R separates them.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where rotations show up in industry (concrete examples)

  • Robotics & automation: Forward/inverse kinematics use rotation matrices to convert between link frames and tool frames.
  • Manufacturing metrology & quality: Coordinate transformations in CMM (coordinate measuring machines), alignment of parts, and best-fit rigid-body registration rely on orthogonal transforms.
  • Aerospace / automotive: Attitude estimation (IMU fusion), rigid body dynamics, and frame transformations (body ↔ inertial frames) use rotation matrices.
  • Computer vision & AR: Camera pose estimation and 3D reconstruction involve rotations; preserving lengths is essential for consistent geometry.
  • EM/RF test and calibration: Rotating polarization bases or reference frames should not change power norms; orthogonality ensures “energy-preserving” coordinate changes.

Why engineers care (performance, safety, cost)

  • Safety & reliability: A non-orthogonal “rotation” introduces artificial stretching, causing accumulated errors in control loops and state estimators.
  • Accuracy: Sensor fusion and robot calibration depend on keeping rotation matrices orthonormal; drift can destabilize computations.
  • Efficiency & manufacturability: Enforcing orthogonality constraints avoids expensive compensation downstream and improves numerical conditioning.

How it’s measured/validated in practice

  • Orthonormality checks: compute RᵀR and verify it is close to I within tolerance.
  • Determinant check: verify det R ≈ +1 to ensure it’s a proper rotation.
  • Calibration experiments: rigid-body motions with known geometry (calibration fixtures, gauge blocks, turntables) to verify that lengths/angles are preserved.

Design trade-offs & non-idealities

  • Noise and drift: IMU integration yields matrices that slowly lose orthogonality; engineers re-orthonormalize (e.g., via Gram–Schmidt or SVD projection onto SO(3)).
  • Tolerances: Small misalignments are modeled as small-angle rotations; using correct orthogonal structure avoids biasing error budgets.
  • Numerical stability: Repeated matrix multiplications can accumulate floating-point error; constraints like RᵀR=I act like “physical plausibility” guards.
From lab to product: how ideal rotations become real engineering tools

In textbooks, R is exactly orthogonal. In products, measurements produce an approximate R̃. A common industrial step is to project R̃ onto the nearest valid rotation: find R minimizing ||R − R̃|| subject to RᵀR=I and det R=+1. This turns “messy sensor reality” into a physically consistent rigid rotation used for control and design decisions.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Restating the problem in clear words

You are given the 2D rotation matrix (acting on components y,z) and asked to:

  1. (a) Prove that applying the 2D rotation to vectors A and B leaves their dot product unchanged: A′·B′ = A·B.
  2. (b) For a general 3D rotation matrix with elements R_ij, determine the constraints those elements must satisfy so that the length of any vector A is preserved: |A′| = |A| for all A.

Given quantities

  • 2D rotation matrix (as provided):
    [A′_y; A′_z] = [[cosφ, sinφ],[-sinφ, cosφ]] [A_y; A_z]
  • General 3D linear transformation labeled as rotation:
    [A′_x; A′_y; A′_z] = R [A_x; A_y; A_z] where R has entries R_ij.

Unknowns / what must be found

  • (a) A proof of dot-product invariance under the given 2D rotation.
  • (b) Algebraic conditions on R_ij that guarantee length preservation for all vectors.

Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Linearity: Rotations are linear maps in Euclidean space: A′ = R A.
  • Euclidean inner product invariance: A rigid rotation preserves the dot product, which implies preservation of both lengths and angles.
  • Matrix transpose identity: (RA)·(RB) = Aᵀ(RᵀR)B. This converts geometry into algebra.

Assumptions for this problem

  • We use the standard Euclidean dot product in Cartesian coordinates.
  • “Preserve the length of A for all vectors A” means the condition must hold for every choice of components, not just special cases.
  • In (b), we interpret “rotation matrix” in physics as a proper rotation, typically implying det R = +1, though the length-preservation condition itself gives RᵀR=I.

Possible approaches (compare)

Approach 1: Direct component expansion

Expand A′ and B′ in components using trig identities (e.g., cos²φ + sin²φ = 1).

Pros: Very explicit; matches many introductory physics texts.

Cons: Longer; can obscure the general pattern.

Approach 2: Matrix/inner-product form

Use A′·B′ = (RA)·(RB) = Aᵀ(RᵀR)B, then show RᵀR = I.

Pros: Short, general, scales to any dimension.

Cons: Requires comfort with transpose and matrix multiplication.

Best choice: Use the matrix/inner-product form for conceptual clarity and generalization, while still showing the key intermediate steps in full detail.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Define the objects. A,B as vectors; R as the rotation matrix; A′=RA, B′=RB. Meaning: components change by rotation.
  2. Rewrite dot products in matrix notation. Use A·B = AᵀB. Meaning: dot product becomes an algebraic bilinear form.
  3. Compute A′·B′. A′·B′ = (RA)ᵀ(RB) = Aᵀ(RᵀR)B. Meaning: invariance depends on RᵀR.
  4. (a) Show R(φ)ᵀR(φ)=I in 2D. Multiply explicitly and use trig identity sin²φ+cos²φ=1. Meaning: the map is orthogonal, so it preserves dot products.
  5. (b) Translate length preservation into an algebraic condition. Length preservation means |RA|² = |A|² for all A, i.e., Aᵀ(RᵀR)A = AᵀA for all A. Meaning: forces RᵀR=I.
  6. Convert RᵀR=I into component constraints. This yields orthonormality of columns/rows: Σ_k R_{k i} R_{k j} = δ_{ij}. Meaning: columns are unit vectors and mutually perpendicular.
  7. Add the “proper rotation” condition. Require det R = +1. Meaning: excludes reflections (orientation reversal).

Common mistakes

  • Forgetting the transpose: (RA)·(RB) becomes Aᵀ(RᵀR)B, not Aᵀ(RR)B.
  • Assuming “length preserved” implies det R = +1. Actually, length preservation gives |det R|=1; reflections have det R = −1.
  • Only checking length for one vector. The condition must hold for all vectors, which is much stronger.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative intuition before calculating

A rotation is “rigid”: it turns arrows without stretching them or changing the angle between them. Since dot products encode both length and angle, we expect a true rotation to preserve dot products. Algebraically, that rigidity becomes the orthogonality condition RᵀR = I.

(a) Prove the 2D rotation matrix preserves dot products

Setup

Let A = (A_y, A_z)ᵀ and B = (B_y, B_z)ᵀ. Define the rotated vectors by A′ = R(φ)A and B′ = R(φ)B, where R(φ) = [[cosφ, sinφ],[-sinφ, cosφ]].

Key equation to prove 
A′·B′ = A·B

Step 1: Write the dot product in matrix form

In Euclidean space, the dot product equals the transpose product: A·B = AᵀB. This is valid because the Euclidean metric in Cartesian coordinates is the identity matrix.

Step 2: Express the rotated dot product and simplify using transpose rules

Start from the left-hand side:

Algebra (no jumps) 
A′·B′
= (A′)ᵀ B′
= (RA)ᵀ (RB)
= Aᵀ Rᵀ R B

Each step is justified:

  • (RA)ᵀ = AᵀRᵀ (transpose of a product reverses order).
  • Matrix multiplication is associative, so we group as Aᵀ (RᵀR) B.

Step 3: Compute RᵀR explicitly in 2D

First write the transpose: Rᵀ = [[cosφ, -sinφ],[sinφ, cosφ]]. Now multiply:

Compute RᵀR 
RᵀR
= [[cosφ, -sinφ],[sinφ, cosφ]] [[cosφ, sinφ],[-sinφ, cosφ]]

= [[cosφ·cosφ + (-sinφ)(-sinφ), cosφ·sinφ + (-sinφ)·cosφ],
   [sinφ·cosφ + cosφ·(-sinφ),   sinφ·sinφ + cosφ·cosφ]]

= [[cos²φ + sin²φ,  cosφ sinφ - sinφ cosφ],
   [sinφ cosφ - cosφ sinφ,     sin²φ + cos²φ]]

= [[1, 0],
   [0, 1]]
= I.

The only trigonometric identity used is sin²φ + cos²φ = 1, and the cross-terms cancel exactly.

Step 4: Conclude dot product invariance

Since RᵀR = I, we return to Step 2: A′·B′ = Aᵀ(RᵀR)B = Aᵀ I B = AᵀB = A·B. Therefore the given 2D rotation matrix preserves dot products.

Result (a)

Dot products are preserved: A′·B′ = A·B for all A,B ∈ ℝ².

Underlying reason: R(φ) is orthogonal: R(φ)ᵀR(φ)=I.

Sanity checks for (a)

  • Units/dimensions: dot product units remain the same because we are just linearly recombining components.
  • Limiting case: if φ=0, then R=I and invariance is trivial.
  • Right angle: if φ=π/2, the matrix swaps axes (up to sign), which clearly should not change dot products.

(b) Constraints on a 3D rotation matrix to preserve the length of A (for all A)

Step 1: State the requirement in mathematical form

Let A ∈ ℝ³ be arbitrary and define A′ = R A where R ∈ ℝ^{3×3} has entries R_ij. “Preserve length for all vectors” means:

Length preservation condition 
|A′| = |A|  for all A ∈ ℝ³
equivalently: |A′|² = |A|² for all A

We use squared lengths because they avoid square roots and are algebraically equivalent for nonnegative norms.

Step 2: Rewrite squared length using dot products and matrix transpose

Compute:

Algebra (explicit) 
|A′|²
= A′·A′
= (RA)·(RA)
= (RA)ᵀ (RA)
= Aᵀ Rᵀ R A.

Also, |A|² = A·A = AᵀA. Therefore the requirement becomes:

Condition for all A 
Aᵀ Rᵀ R A = Aᵀ A  for all A ∈ ℝ³.

Step 3: Deduce the matrix constraint RᵀR = I

Let M = RᵀR. The condition is: Aᵀ M A = Aᵀ I A for all A, i.e., Aᵀ (M - I) A = 0 for all A.

A standard linear-algebra fact: if a symmetric matrix S satisfies Aᵀ S A = 0 for all vectors A, then S = 0. Here, M - I is symmetric because M = RᵀR is symmetric: (RᵀR)ᵀ = Rᵀ(Rᵀ)ᵀ = RᵀR. Thus M - I = 0, so:

Core constraint 
Rᵀ R = I.
Optional deeper note: why “for all A” forces RᵀR = I (sketch)

Write S = M - I and let e_i be standard basis vectors. From e_iᵀ S e_i = S_{ii} = 0, all diagonal entries vanish. Then using A = e_i + e_j gives (e_i+e_j)ᵀ S (e_i+e_j) = S_{ii}+S_{jj}+2S_{ij} = 0, so S_{ij}=0. Hence S=0 and M=I.

Step 4: Convert RᵀR=I into conditions on the elements R_ij

The matrix product RᵀR has elements: (RᵀR)_{ij} = Σ_{k=1..3} (Rᵀ)_{ik} R_{kj} = Σ_{k=1..3} R_{k i} R_{k j}. Setting this equal to I means:

Element-wise orthonormality 
Σ_{k=1}^{3} R_{k i} R_{k j} = δ_{ij}  for i,j ∈ {1,2,3}.

Interpretation:

  • If i=j, then Σ_k R_{k i}² = 1: each column has unit length.
  • If i≠j, then Σ_k R_{k i} R_{k j} = 0: different columns are perpendicular.

Geometric meaning

The columns of R are the images of the basis vectors under the rotation. The conditions above say those images form an orthonormal triad: three mutually perpendicular unit vectors. That is exactly “a rotated coordinate frame.”

Step 5: Proper rotations vs reflections

From RᵀR=I, taking determinants gives: det(RᵀR)=det(I)=1 ⇒ (det R)²=1 ⇒ det R = ±1. Both values preserve length, but:

  • det R = +1 → orientation-preserving: a proper rotation.
  • det R = −1 → includes a reflection (improper rotation).

Final result (b)

Length preservation for all vectors: RᵀR = I (orthogonality).

Element constraints: Σ_{k=1}^{3} R_{k i} R_{k j} = δ_{ij}.

For a true rotation (no reflection): additionally require det R = +1.

Sanity checks for (b)

  • Dimensional analysis: R is dimensionless; it mixes components without changing units, so |A| has the same units as before.
  • Limiting case: If R=I, all constraints hold and length is preserved.
  • Physical interpretation: Orthogonality blocks stretching/shear; determinant +1 ensures handedness (right-handed stays right-handed).
Final Answer (copy plain text) 
(a) For A′=R(φ)A and B′=R(φ)B with R(φ)=[[cosφ, sinφ],[-sinφ, cosφ]]:
A′·B′ = (RA)ᵀ(RB) = Aᵀ(RᵀR)B.  A direct multiplication gives RᵀR=I, hence A′·B′=AᵀB=A·B.

(b) In 3D, requiring |A′|=|A| for all A with A′=RA implies
|A′|² = AᵀRᵀRA = AᵀA for all A, so RᵀR=I.
Element-wise: Σ_{k=1}^{3} R_{k i}R_{k j}=δ_{ij} (columns/rows orthonormal).
For a proper rotation (no reflection) also require det R = +1 (since orthogonality gives det R = ±1).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpreting the final formulas

  • RᵀR=I says the transformation preserves the Euclidean metric (inner product). In physics terms: it preserves “geometric measurements.”
  • Element form Σ_k R_{k i}R_{k j}=δ_{ij} says the columns are an orthonormal basis—exactly what you get by rotating coordinate axes.
  • det R = +1 distinguishes rotations from reflections; it preserves handedness (right-hand rule), crucial for cross products and angular momentum.

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

In 2D, the single parameter is the rotation angle φ. As φ varies, components mix sinusoidally, but invariants like dot products and lengths do not change. In 3D, a rotation has (effectively) three degrees of freedom (e.g., axis + angle), yet the constraint RᵀR=I always holds.

Small-angle approximation (useful in engineering)

For small φ, sinφ ≈ φ, cosφ ≈ 1 − φ²/2. Then in 2D: R(φ) ≈ [[1, φ],[-φ, 1]] up to first order. This is widely used for alignment tolerances and error propagation. Mathematically, you are neglecting terms of order O(φ²) (and higher) when linearizing.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of computing RᵀR, you can argue geometrically: if a transformation preserves dot products, it preserves lengths and angles, so it maps an orthonormal basis to another orthonormal basis. The matrix formed by those new basis vectors must have orthonormal columns—again giving RᵀR=I. In applications, this viewpoint directly connects to “columns of R are the new axes.”

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: If RᵀR=I, does that automatically imply dot product preservation for all A,B? A: Yes, because (RA)·(RB)=Aᵀ(RᵀR)B=AᵀB.
  • Q: Can a matrix preserve lengths but flip orientation? A: Yes; reflections preserve length and have RᵀR=I but det R = −1.
  • Q: Why is “for all vectors A” crucial in (b)? A: It forces the quadratic form Aᵀ(RᵀR−I)A to vanish for every direction and magnitude, which can only happen if RᵀR−I=0.
  • Q: What do the off-diagonal conditions Σ_k R_{k i}R_{k j}=0 mean physically? A: Different axes remain perpendicular after rotation.

Operator notation note (why no ∇ here)

This problem concerns a finite-dimensional linear operator R acting on vectors, not a field operator on functions. Therefore differential operators like ∇, ∇·, ∇×, or the Laplacian do not naturally appear. The relevant “operator” is the matrix (linear map) itself and its adjoint (transpose) Rᵀ.

Tip: In numerical pipelines, if you ever get a matrix that is “almost” a rotation, verify RᵀR≈I and det R≈+1.
