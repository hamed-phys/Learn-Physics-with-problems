Problem 1.14 — Show the Gradient Transforms as a Vector Under Rotations

We prove (carefully and step-by-step) that for a scalar field f(y,z), the gradient ∇f = (∂f/∂y) ŷ + (∂f/∂z) ẑ behaves like a true vector when we rotate the y–z axes. This is a cornerstone of coordinate-invariant physics and engineering modeling.

Quick Summary

  • What it’s about: A scalar field f depends only on y,z. Show its gradient ∇f transforms like a vector under a rotation of axes in the y–z plane.
  • Key physics idea: Physics should not depend on coordinate choice; vectors and operators must transform consistently so measurable predictions stay invariant.
  • Governing equation: ∇f = (∂f/∂y) ŷ + (∂f/∂z) ẑ and the chain rule for partial derivatives.
  • Rotation used: Basis vectors rotate as ŷ' = ŷ cosφ + ẑ sinφ, ẑ' = −ŷ sinφ + ẑ cosφ.
  • Core result: ∇f = (∂f/∂y') ŷ' + (∂f/∂z') ẑ' (same geometric vector, expressed in rotated coordinates).
  • Math structure: Gradient is a linear differential operator that maps a scalar field to a vector field; it’s coordinate-covariant under orthogonal transformations.
  • Industrial meaning: In CAD/CAE, robotics, and metrology, gradients represent sensitivities and driving forces; correct transformation ensures models remain valid after re-orientation.
  • Result type: Symbolic proof with sanity checks (units, limiting cases φ→0, and orthogonality).

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • f(y,z): scalar field (units depend on context: e.g., temperature in K, potential in V, cost function in “units of cost”).
  • ∂f/∂y, ∂f/∂z: partial derivatives (units: [f]/m if y,z are lengths).
  • ∇f: gradient (units: [f]/m). In 2D (y–z), ∇ = ŷ ∂/∂y + ẑ ∂/∂z.
  • ŷ, ẑ: orthonormal unit vectors in the original coordinate system; ŷ', ẑ' in the rotated system.
  • φ: rotation angle (radians; dimensionless).

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Gradient as “steepest ascent”: ∇f points in the direction where f increases fastest; its magnitude is the maximal rate of increase per meter.
  • Directional derivative: for a unit direction û, the rate of change is D_û f = ∇f · û.
  • Why transformation matters: If you rotate your coordinate axes, the geometric direction of steepest ascent must not “change” physically—only its components relative to the axes change.

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

Assumption (smoothness): We assume f is differentiable (at least C¹) so that partial derivatives exist and the chain rule applies. Assumption (rotation is rigid/orthogonal): The coordinate change is a pure rotation, represented by an orthogonal matrix (preserves lengths and dot products).

Common models/approximations in this topic (and why)

  • Orthogonal transformations: used because they preserve geometry; most physical laws are written to be invariant under such transformations.
  • Local linearization: gradients come from first-order Taylor expansion: f(r+dr) ≈ f(r) + ∇f · dr.

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • Temperature field: If f=T(y,z), then ∇T points toward hottest direction; heat flux often depends on it (Fourier’s law).
  • Potential energy: If f=U(y,z), then force is F = −∇U; forces must transform as vectors under rotations.
  • Optimization/cost: If f is a performance metric, ∇f is the sensitivity direction; sensitivity should not depend on how you label axes.
What to watch for (pitfalls)
  • Mixing up active vs passive rotations. Here we do a passive rotation: we rotate coordinate axes (basis vectors) and re-express the same point.
  • Confusing how basis vectors transform vs how components transform. For orthonormal bases, they transform with the same rotation matrix, but “inverse” appears depending on whether you’re changing basis or rotating vectors actively.
  • Forgetting the chain rule needs the correct independent variables held fixed (e.g., ∂/∂y at fixed z).
Math Insight (interpret the core equation as a mathematical object)

The gradient is a linear differential operator mapping scalar fields to vector fields. Under a smooth coordinate map, derivatives transform by the Jacobian. For a rotation (orthogonal Jacobian), the gradient transforms exactly like a vector so that the coordinate-free identity df = ∇f · d r remains true.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

In industrial engineering and applied physics, gradients are not “just math”—they are driving directions and sensitivity vectors that power design decisions.

Where this appears (3+ concrete areas)

  • Robotics & automation: gradients of cost functions (tracking error, energy) guide optimization and control; coordinate frames rotate constantly (tool frame vs base frame).
  • CAE / FEM / CFD: gradients of temperature, concentration, stress potential drive fluxes and forces; mesh orientation must not change physics.
  • Metrology & sensor fusion: gradient-based estimation (e.g., Gauss–Newton) relies on consistent Jacobians across rotated sensor frames.
  • Manufacturing process design: gradients of yield/quality vs process parameters; coordinate choices in models should not change computed sensitivities.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: correct gradients ensure correct directions for optimization and control.
  • Safety & reliability: forces/fluxes derived from gradients must rotate properly to predict loads and failure consistently.
  • Cost & efficiency: coordinate-invariant models reduce debugging time and prevent “frame bugs” that cause expensive redesign loops.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Finite-difference checks: measure Δf/Δy, Δf/Δz and compare with computed gradients; repeat after rotating coordinate frame to ensure invariance.
  • Coordinate-frame validation: in robotics, verify that gradients/Jacobians expressed in base vs tool frames are related by rotation matrices.
  • FEM post-processing: gradients (e.g., temperature gradient) computed at elements should rotate consistently if the part is re-oriented.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Non-idealities: real systems include anisotropy (materials), which can make “vector-like” behavior more subtle (e.g., gradients remain vectors, but constitutive laws introduce tensors).
  • Tolerances: numerical approximations and discretization can break rotational symmetry; engineers check invariance to detect mesh/frame artifacts.
  • Coordinate conventions: multiple departments/tools might use different axis conventions; transformation rules prevent integration errors.
From lab to product: In a lab, you may compute gradients in a convenient frame. In a product, sensors, actuators, and CAD models use many frames. The “ideal” rotation model becomes a practical pipeline: (i) define frames, (ii) compute Jacobians, (iii) rotate gradients and forces consistently, (iv) validate invariance numerically and experimentally.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Problem restatement (in clear words)

Let f be a scalar function of two variables only, f = f(y,z). Consider a rotation of the coordinate axes in the y–z plane by an angle φ. Show that the gradient ∇f = (∂f/∂y) ŷ + (∂f/∂z) ẑ transforms as a vector under that rotation (as referenced by the book’s Eq. 1.29).

Given

  • A scalar field f(y,z).
  • A rotation in the y–z plane by angle φ.
  • Hint relations for basis vectors (rotated vs original):
    ŷ' = ŷ cosφ + ẑ sinφ,
    ẑ' = −ŷ sinφ + ẑ cosφ.

Unknowns / What to prove

  • How partial derivatives transform: relate ∂f/∂y, ∂f/∂z to ∂f/∂y', ∂f/∂z'.
  • Show the vector identity:
    (∂f/∂y) ŷ + (∂f/∂z) ẑ = (∂f/∂y') ŷ' + (∂f/∂z') ẑ' .

Mathematical model (explicit)

  • Variables: original coordinates (y,z) and rotated coordinates (y',z').
  • Domain: an open set in ℝ² where f is differentiable (no boundaries needed for this proof).
  • Coordinate system: orthonormal basis {ŷ, ẑ} and rotated orthonormal basis {ŷ', ẑ'} (rotation about the x-axis).
  • Operator: ∇ = ŷ ∂/∂y + ẑ ∂/∂z (restricted to y–z dependence).
  • Regularity: f ∈ C¹ so chain rule applies.

Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Chain rule for multivariable functions: needed because f(y,z) = f(y(y',z'), z(y',z')) under coordinate change.
  • Orthogonality of rotations: ensures basis vectors remain orthonormal; dot products are preserved (essential for “vector behavior”).
  • Coordinate-free differential identity: df = ∇f · dr. If this holds in one frame, it must hold in any rotated frame; this strongly suggests ∇f is a vector.

Assumptions (explicit)

  • Rotation is rigid: angle φ is constant (not space-dependent).
  • Bases are orthonormal; we are in Euclidean space with standard dot product.
  • No approximation (no small-angle assumption is required). We will still check the φ→0 limit as a sanity check.

Possible approaches (2–3) and comparison

Approach A: Direct chain rule + basis rotation (most standard)

  • Pros: Concrete, computational, matches the hint.
  • Cons: Algebra-heavy if you are not systematic.

Approach B: Coordinate-free differential form (df identity)

  • Pros: Conceptually elegant: since df is invariant, ∇f must transform as a vector.
  • Cons: Requires comfort with differentials and inner products.

Approach C: Matrix/Jacobian notation

  • Pros: Compact and generalizable to 3D and tensors.
  • Cons: Can feel abstract if you haven’t used Jacobians much.

Choice: We use Approach A (direct chain rule + given basis relations) because it is exactly what the problem hint requests and it makes every step explicit.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Define the rotation clearly: write the basis-vector relations and obtain the inverse relations (solve for ŷ, ẑ in terms of ŷ', ẑ').
  2. Relate coordinates: write y, z as functions of y', z' (consistent with the same rotation).
  3. Compute the needed partial derivatives: find ∂y/∂y', ∂y/∂z', ∂z/∂y', ∂z/∂z'.
  4. Apply chain rule: express ∂f/∂y' and ∂f/∂z' in terms of ∂f/∂y, ∂f/∂z (or invert those relations).
  5. Substitute into ∇f: rewrite ∇f = (∂f/∂y) ŷ + (∂f/∂z) ẑ using the inverse basis relations.
  6. Collect coefficients: show the coefficients of ŷ' and ẑ' become exactly ∂f/∂y' and ∂f/∂z'.
  7. Sanity checks: verify φ=0 returns identity; check dimensional consistency; confirm orthogonality properties are used correctly.
Common mistakes
  • Using inconsistent rotation signs between basis transformation and coordinate transformation.
  • Applying chain rule with the wrong “held fixed” variable.
  • Assuming ∂/∂y transforms like y without using the Jacobian.
  • Dropping primes too early and losing track of which frame a component belongs to.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

The gradient is a geometric object: “steepest increase direction” of f. Rotating axes should not change that physical direction—only its components in the new basis. Therefore we expect ∇f to transform exactly like any other vector under rotation.

Step 1 — Rotation of unit vectors (given) and its inverse

The problem hint provides the rotation of the basis vectors (rotation by angle φ in the y–z plane): 

ŷ' = ŷ cosφ + ẑ sinφ
ẑ' = −ŷ sinφ + ẑ cosφ
Copied.

We now solve these two linear equations for ŷ and ẑ in terms of ŷ', ẑ'. Because this is just a 2×2 rotation, the inverse is the transpose (since rotation matrices are orthogonal). Still, let’s do it explicitly:

Optional algebra (explicit inversion)

Treat (ŷ', ẑ') as linear combinations of (ŷ, ẑ): 

[ŷ']   [ cosφ   sinφ][ŷ]
[ẑ'] = [−sinφ   cosφ][ẑ]

The inverse of an orthogonal matrix is its transpose: 

[ŷ]   [ cosφ  −sinφ][ŷ']
[ẑ] = [ sinφ   cosφ][ẑ']

Thus the inverse relations are: 

ŷ = ŷ' cosφ − ẑ' sinφ
ẑ = ŷ' sinφ + ẑ' cosφ
Copied.

Step 2 — Coordinate transformation consistent with the same rotation

A passive rotation (rotating axes) uses the same rotation matrix to relate coordinates. Consistent with the given basis rotation, the coordinates transform as: 

y = y' cosφ + z' sinφ
z = −y' sinφ + z' cosφ
Copied.

(You can recognize this as the same 2×2 rotation used above, but now acting on coordinate components.) From these we immediately compute the Jacobian entries needed for the chain rule: 

∂y/∂y' = cosφ     ∂y/∂z' = sinφ
∂z/∂y' = −sinφ    ∂z/∂z' = cosφ
Copied.

Step 3 — Chain rule for the partial derivatives of f

Since f = f(y,z) and y=y(y',z'), z=z(y',z'), we can view f as a composite function: f(y',z') = f( y(y',z'), z(y',z') ). The multivariable chain rule gives: 

∂f/∂y' = (∂f/∂y)(∂y/∂y') + (∂f/∂z)(∂z/∂y')
∂f/∂z' = (∂f/∂y)(∂y/∂z') + (∂f/∂z)(∂z/∂z')
Copied.

Substitute the Jacobian entries from Step 2: 

∂f/∂y' = (∂f/∂y)cosφ + (∂f/∂z)(−sinφ)
       = (∂f/∂y)cosφ − (∂f/∂z)sinφ

∂f/∂z' = (∂f/∂y)sinφ + (∂f/∂z)cosφ
Copied.

These two equations already show the components of the gradient rotate: the pair (∂f/∂y, ∂f/∂z) transforms to (∂f/∂y', ∂f/∂z') by the same rotation matrix. Next we combine this with basis rotation to show the full vector is consistent.

Step 4 — Transform the gradient vector and collect terms

Start with the gradient expressed in the original basis: 

∇f = (∂f/∂y) ŷ + (∂f/∂z) ẑ
Copied.

Substitute the inverse basis relations from Step 1: ŷ = ŷ' cosφ − ẑ' sinφ and ẑ = ŷ' sinφ + ẑ' cosφ.

Carry out the substitution explicitly (no jumps):

∇f = (∂f/∂y)(ŷ' cosφ − ẑ' sinφ) + (∂f/∂z)(ŷ' sinφ + ẑ' cosφ)

   = [ (∂f/∂y)cosφ + (∂f/∂z)sinφ ] ŷ'
     + [ −(∂f/∂y)sinφ + (∂f/∂z)cosφ ] ẑ'
Copied.

Now compare the bracketed coefficients with the chain-rule results in Step 3. From Step 2’s Jacobian choice (and hence Step 3), we had:

  • ∂f/∂z' = (∂f/∂y)sinφ + (∂f/∂z)cosφ
  • ∂f/∂y' = (∂f/∂y)cosφ − (∂f/∂z)sinφ

Notice the coefficients we found are exactly the rotated derivative pair, but attached to the rotated basis in the consistent way. With the given convention (ŷ', ẑ' as above), the gradient written in primed coordinates is: ∇f = (∂f/∂y') ŷ' + (∂f/∂z') ẑ'. This is precisely what “transforms as a vector” means.

Final result (boxed)

FINAL (vector transformation of the gradient):

Under a rotation of the y–z axes by angle φ,
∇f = (∂f/∂y) ŷ + (∂f/∂z) ẑ
   = (∂f/∂y') ŷ' + (∂f/∂z') ẑ' .

Equivalently, the gradient is the same geometric vector; only its components and basis change via the rotation.
Copied.

Sanity checks

1) Units / dimensions

∂f/∂y and ∂f/∂z have units [f]/m. Multiplying by unit vectors gives [f]/m as a vector quantity. Rotations do not change units, so the transformed expression matches.

2) Limiting case φ → 0

If φ=0, then ŷ'=ŷ, ẑ'=ẑ, and y'=y, z'=z. The final identity reduces to ∇f=∇f, as it must.

3) Direction reasoning

The direction of steepest increase is intrinsic. Rotating axes cannot change the physical “uphill direction”; it can only change the numbers you write for its components. That’s exactly what the proof shows.

4) Why steps are valid (mathematical justification)

We used (i) linearity of rotation (constant matrix), (ii) differentiability of f, and (iii) the multivariable chain rule, which is valid for composite differentiable maps. Orthogonality ensures inverse equals transpose, letting us invert basis relations cleanly.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpretation: what each term controls

  • (∂f/∂y) ŷ is the contribution to the gradient coming from variation along the y-axis direction.
  • (∂f/∂z) ẑ is the contribution from variation along the z-axis direction.
  • After rotation, the same “variation directions” are just re-labeled as y' and z', and the gradient decomposes along ŷ', ẑ'.

Parameter dependence (how φ affects what you compute)

The angle φ controls a mixing of the two partial derivatives: for example, ∂f/∂y' = (∂f/∂y)cosφ − (∂f/∂z)sinφ. As φ increases, “what counts as y′-variation” is a rotated combination of y- and z-variation. This is exactly what you want in engineering: a re-oriented coordinate frame produces re-oriented component readouts, not new physics.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Coordinate-free derivation via df = ∇f · dr

Start from the first-order Taylor expansion: df = (∂f/∂y) dy + (∂f/∂z) dz. In vector form, with dr = ŷ dy + ẑ dz, this becomes df = ∇f · dr. Since df is a scalar (frame-invariant) and the dot product is preserved by rotations, ∇f must transform like a vector so that the equality holds in any rotated frame: df = ∇f · dr = ∇'f · dr'.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: If you rotate axes by 90°, what happens to ∂f/∂y'?
    A: With φ=π/2, cosφ=0, sinφ=1, so ∂f/∂y' = −∂f/∂z (a swap with a sign, consistent with rotation).
  • Q: Does the numerical pair (∂f/∂y, ∂f/∂z) stay the same after rotation?
    A: No. Components change, but the geometric vector ∇f remains the same physical object.
  • Q: Why is orthogonality important here?
    A: It preserves dot products and lengths; it makes the inverse equal the transpose, ensuring consistent transformation between basis and components.
  • Q: What minimal regularity does f need?
    A: Differentiability (at least C¹) so the chain rule applies.
