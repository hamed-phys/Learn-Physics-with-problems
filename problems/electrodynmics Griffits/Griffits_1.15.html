PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

Let v(x,y,z) be a vector field in 3D Cartesian space: v = vx(x,y,z) x̂ + vy(x,y,z) ŷ + vz(x,y,z) ẑ.

Typical SI units depend on context: velocity field v → m/s; electric flux density D → C/m²; heat flux q → W/m², etc.

Key operator (Cartesian divergence):
∇·v = (∂v_x/∂x) + (∂v_y/∂y) + (∂v_z/∂z)
Physical meaning (intuition)

Divergence is the net outward flow from an infinitesimal volume, per unit volume. If v is a velocity field, ∇·v is the volumetric expansion rate (1/s).

  • ∇·v > 0: local source (outflow dominates)
  • ∇·v < 0: local sink (inflow dominates)
  • ∇·v = 0: solenoidal/incompressible behavior (no net volumetric creation)
Key laws / principles + validity conditions

Divergence appears in conservation laws. For a generic conserved quantity with density ρ and flux v: 

Conservation form (local):
∂ρ/∂t + ∇·v = s

Here s is a source term. This statement is valid when fields are sufficiently smooth (differentiable) so partial derivatives exist (classical continuum model).

Optional deeper note: Divergence Theorem connection

The operator definition matches the integral statement: ∫∫∫_V (∇·v) dV = ∫∫_(∂V) v·n̂ dA. It says “total source inside” equals “net flux through boundary.” In this problem we use the differential (pointwise) form directly because components are polynomials (smooth everywhere).

Common models/approximations in this topic
  • Cartesian coordinates: assumes orthonormal basis x̂, ŷ, ẑ; divergence is a simple sum of partial derivatives.
  • Continuum assumption: fields vary smoothly; pointwise derivatives represent local behavior.
  • Linearity of ∇·: ∇·(a v + b w) = a ∇·v + b ∇·w for constants a,b.
What to watch for (pitfalls)
  • Different variables: when taking ∂/∂x, treat y and z as constants (and similarly for others).
  • Component-by-component: divergence uses vx with ∂/∂x, vy with ∂/∂y, vz with ∂/∂z (no cross-terms).
  • Units: divergence has units of (units of v)/(length). For velocity, 1/s.
  • Coordinate system: the simple formula here is for Cartesian coordinates only.
Mini intuition examples (no heavy algebra)
  • Uniform flow v = V0 x̂ → ∇·v = 0 (same amount enters and leaves any small cube).
  • Radially expanding “balloon” v = k x x̂ + k y ŷ + k z ẑ → ∇·v = 3k (uniform expansion everywhere).
  • Shear flow v = γ y x̂ → ∇·v = 0 (shear rearranges flow but doesn’t create volume).
Math Insight

The divergence operator ∇· is a first-order linear differential operator mapping vector fields to scalar fields. It is the “source density” operator in a conservation law: it measures how much a vector field behaves like a local generator of flux (source) versus a pure circulation/shear (often divergence-free).

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where divergence shows up in industry (concrete areas)
  • CFD & process engineering: incompressible flow design (pumps, pipes, mixing tanks). Constraint ∇·u = 0 is central.
  • Thermal management: heat flux q relates to sources via energy conservation; ∇·q ties to volumetric heat generation.
  • Electromagnetics & high-speed electronics: Gauss’s law uses divergence: ∇·D = ρfree (charge density), critical in capacitors, PCB fields, insulation design.
Why engineers care
  • Performance: divergence constraints affect pressure drop, mixing efficiency, heat removal.
  • Safety & reliability: localized sources/sinks in EM fields relate to charge accumulation → dielectric stress and breakdown risk.
  • Cost & manufacturability: design margins depend on field intensities predicted by conservation laws.
Measurement / validation in practice
  • Flow: PIV (particle image velocimetry) reconstructs velocity fields; divergence checks enforce incompressibility.
  • Heat: IR thermography + inverse heat conduction to infer heat sources (linked to ∇·q).
  • EM: field probes, capacitance measurements, and charge/space-charge diagnostics validate Gauss-law-based models.
Design trade-offs & non-idealities
  • Real materials introduce heterogeneity → coefficients vary in space; divergence couples to gradients of material properties.
  • Measurements are discrete and noisy; computing divergence numerically amplifies noise (derivatives are sensitive).
  • Boundary conditions matter: “no-flux” or “specified-flux” constraints decide whether sources are physical or artifacts.

From lab to product: the idealized smooth-field model becomes a discretized (finite-volume/finite-element) model, where divergence is enforced in an integral sense over small control volumes to maintain conservation.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement

We are given three vector functions in Cartesian coordinates. For each one, compute its divergence ∇·v.

Mathematical model
  • Variables: (x, y, z) ∈ ℝ³
  • Domain: all space (polynomials are smooth everywhere), unless otherwise restricted
  • Coordinate system: Cartesian basis {x̂, ŷ, ẑ}
  • Unknowns: scalar fields ∇·va, ∇·vb, ∇·vc
  • Boundary/initial conditions: none needed (local differential operator only)
Given vector fields (as provided)
(a) v_a = x^2 x̂ + 3 x z^2 ŷ − 2 x z ẑ
(b) v_b = x y x̂ + 2 y z ŷ + 3 z x ẑ
(c) v_c = y^2 x̂ + (2 x y + z^2) ŷ + 2 y z ẑ

Relevant principles (and why they apply)
  • Definition of divergence: applies because fields are expressed directly in Cartesian components.
  • Partial differentiation rules: polynomials are smooth; derivatives exist and are straightforward.
  • Linearity: we can differentiate each component separately and sum.

Principles like curl, Laplacian, or boundary-integral methods are not required here because the task is purely local and symbolic.

Assumptions (explicit)
  • Cartesian coordinates (not cylindrical/spherical).
  • Classical differentiability (no singularities).
  • x, y, z treated as independent variables.
Possible approaches (2–3) and comparison
  • Direct component method (best here): use ∇·v = ∂vx/∂x + ∂vy/∂y + ∂vz/∂z. Fast and exact.
  • Integral method (Divergence Theorem): choose a small box volume, compute flux out, divide by volume, take limit. Great for intuition, slower algebraically.
  • Symbolic operator expansion: treat ∇· as an operator and apply linearity term-by-term. Essentially the same as direct method, but highlights structure.

Chosen approach: direct component method, because the fields are polynomials in Cartesian coordinates, making partial derivatives immediate.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

Minimal plan (roadmap)
  1. Identify components vx, vy, vz for each vector field (goal: separate scalar functions).
  2. Apply divergence definition ∇·v = ∂vx/∂x + ∂vy/∂y + ∂vz/∂z (tool: operator notation).
  3. Differentiate carefully holding the other variables constant (tool: partial derivative rules).
  4. Sum the three terms to obtain ∇·v (meaning: net source density of the field).
  5. Sanity checks:
    • Dimensional check: divergence units = field units / length.
    • Limiting check: see what happens on axes (e.g., z=0) to interpret sources/sinks.
Common mistakes
  • Taking ∂/∂x of vy by accident (divergence pairs each component with its matching coordinate derivative).
  • Forgetting that ∂/∂y of any expression with no y is zero.
  • Dropping constants like x when differentiating with respect to z (x is constant under ∂/∂z).
  • Confusing divergence (scalar) with curl (vector).

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

Because these are polynomial fields, ∇·v will also be a polynomial (or constant). If the x-component depends only on x, it contributes via ∂/∂x. If a component has no dependence on its own coordinate, its contribution to divergence is zero. This can create cancellations (as in part a).

Key equation (used for all parts)

In Cartesian coordinates:
If v = v_x x̂ + v_y ŷ + v_z ẑ, then
∇·v = ∂v_x/∂x + ∂v_y/∂y + ∂v_z/∂z

(a) Compute ∇·va

Given:

v_a = x^2 x̂ + 3 x z^2 ŷ − 2 x z ẑ

Identify components (define every symbol before using it):

  • vx(x,y,z) = x²
  • vy(x,y,z) = 3 x z²
  • vz(x,y,z) = −2 x z

Apply divergence definition term-by-term:

∇·v_a = ∂(x^2)/∂x + ∂(3 x z^2)/∂y + ∂(−2 x z)/∂z

1) ∂(x^2)/∂x = 2x
   (power rule; y,z treated as constants)

2) ∂(3 x z^2)/∂y = 0
   (no y-dependence ⇒ partial derivative w.r.t. y is zero)

3) ∂(−2 x z)/∂z = −2x
   (x is constant under ∂/∂z; derivative of z is 1)

Sum: ∇·v_a = 2x + 0 + (−2x) = 0
Final answer (a)
∇·v_a = 0
Sanity checks for (a)
  • Units: if v were velocity, x has meters, so v_x ~ m² (not a physical velocity), but mathematically divergence still consistent as (field units)/m. No contradiction within pure math.
  • Interpretation: divergence-free means no net local source/sink; the +2x outflow from v_x is exactly canceled by −2x from v_z.
  • Limiting cases: holds for all x,y,z since result is identically zero.

(b) Compute ∇·vb

Given:

v_b = x y x̂ + 2 y z ŷ + 3 z x ẑ

Components:

  • vx = x y
  • vy = 2 y z
  • vz = 3 z x

Apply ∇·:

∇·v_b = ∂(x y)/∂x + ∂(2 y z)/∂y + ∂(3 z x)/∂z

1) ∂(x y)/∂x = y
   (y is constant under ∂/∂x)

2) ∂(2 y z)/∂y = 2z
   (z is constant under ∂/∂y)

3) ∂(3 z x)/∂z = 3x
   (x is constant under ∂/∂z)

Sum: ∇·v_b = y + 2z + 3x
Final answer (b)
∇·v_b = 3x + y + 2z
Sanity checks for (b)
  • Linearity check: Each component contributes independently; no mixed partials appear by definition.
  • Sign reasoning: ∇·v_b is positive in regions where 3x + y + 2z > 0 → acts like a source there, and negative where it is < 0 → sink.
  • Limiting axes: On x-axis (y=z=0), ∇·v_b = 3x; source for x>0, sink for x<0.

(c) Compute ∇·vc

Given:

v_c = y^2 x̂ + (2 x y + z^2) ŷ + 2 y z ẑ

Components:

  • vx = y²
  • vy = 2 x y + z²
  • vz = 2 y z

Apply ∇·:

∇·v_c = ∂(y^2)/∂x + ∂(2 x y + z^2)/∂y + ∂(2 y z)/∂z

1) ∂(y^2)/∂x = 0
   (no x-dependence)

2) ∂(2 x y + z^2)/∂y = 2x
   (z^2 is constant under ∂/∂y; derivative of 2xy w.r.t. y is 2x)

3) ∂(2 y z)/∂z = 2y
   (y is constant under ∂/∂z)

Sum: ∇·v_c = 0 + 2x + 2y = 2x + 2y = 2(x + y)
Final answer (c)
∇·v_c = 2x + 2y = 2(x + y)
Sanity checks for (c)
  • Dimension check: divergence scales as field/length; polynomial form is consistent for symbolic math.
  • Limiting cases: If x = −y, then ∇·v_c = 0 along the plane x + y = 0 → locally divergence-free there.
  • Interpretation: sources in regions where x+y>0, sinks where x+y<0.
All final answers (boxed)
(a) ∇·v_a = 0
(b) ∇·v_b = 3x + y + 2z
(c) ∇·v_c = 2x + 2y = 2(x + y)

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the formulas
  • (a) ∇·v = 0: perfect local cancellation of “expansion” in x with “contraction” in z. This is a hallmark of many incompressible/shear-like fields.
  • (b) ∇·v = 3x + y + 2z: the source/sink strength depends on position; the field is source-like in regions where this is positive and sink-like where negative.
  • (c) ∇·v = 2(x+y): divergence depends only on x+y; z does not influence net outflow even though z appears in vy and vz.
Parameter dependence (qualitative)

These are polynomial fields, so divergence is controlled by how each component changes along its own axis:

  • Increasing x boosts ∇·vb by 3 per unit x and boosts ∇·vc by 2 per unit x.
  • Increasing y boosts ∇·vb by 1 per unit y and boosts ∇·vc by 2 per unit y.
  • Increasing z boosts ∇·vb by 2 per unit z, but does nothing to ∇·vc.
Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Use the Divergence Theorem on a small rectangular box centered at (x,y,z): compute net outward flux through the six faces using Taylor expansion of components, then divide by volume and take the limit as box size → 0. This reproduces ∂vx/∂x + ∂vy/∂y + ∂vz/∂z and gives strong physical intuition.

Concept check (self-test with answers)
  • Q: If vy has no y in it, does it contribute to divergence? A: No; ∂vy/∂y = 0.
  • Q: Can a field have z-dependence but still yield no z-term in divergence? A: Yes (e.g., part c), if the z-dependence is not in vz (or cancels).
  • Q: What does ∇·v = 0 typically mean in fluid mechanics? A: Incompressible flow (constant density) under standard assumptions.
  • Q: Why is the method “differentiate + add” valid here? A: Because divergence is defined as that sum in Cartesian coordinates and the fields are differentiable.
