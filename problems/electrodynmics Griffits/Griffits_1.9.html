Problem 1.10 — Vector Components Under Translation & Inversion; Pseudovectors and Pseudoscalars

A rigorous (but physics-friendly) guide to how vectors and vector products transform under coordinate translations and inversions, with a practical engineering lens.

Quick Summary

  • This problem asks how vector components and vector products change under (i) translation of coordinates and (ii) inversion (parity) of coordinates.
  • Key physics idea: a polar vector (ordinary vector like displacement, velocity, force) is a geometric object independent of coordinates; only its components change with the coordinate map.
  • Governing mathematical model: linear coordinate transform x' = A x + a with Jacobian A; for inversion, A = -I and det(A) = -1.
  • Under pure translation, the basis does not rotate: vector components are unchanged.
  • Under inversion (parity), polar vectors flip sign, but axial vectors (pseudovectors) do not.
  • The cross product uses the Levi-Civita symbol; it behaves like an axial vector: A × B is invariant under inversion if A, B are polar vectors.
  • The scalar triple product A · (B × C) changes sign under inversion: it is a pseudoscalar.
  • Results are fully symbolic and interpreted physically (no numbers needed).

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitionswhat transforms, what doesn’t

A vector is a coordinate-free geometric object. In a given Cartesian basis e_i, it is represented by components V_i:

Vector representation
V = V_i e_i   (Einstein sum over i = 1..3)

Units depend on what the vector represents: displacement (m), velocity (m/s), force (N), torque (N·m), etc.

Two “kinds”polar vs axial

A polar vector (true vector) changes sign under inversion of coordinates: displacement r, velocity v, force F.

An axial vector (pseudovector) does not change sign under inversion: angular velocity ω, torque τ, magnetic field B.

Why do axial vectors exist?
Many axial vectors are built from a cross product of two polar vectors (e.g., τ = r × F). Because inversion flips each polar factor, the two sign flips cancel. The “extra” sign behavior traces to the Levi-Civita symbol’s dependence on orientation (handedness).

Mathematical model (variables, domain, coordinates)

  • Domain: 3D Euclidean space ℝ³ with Cartesian coordinates (x, y, z).
  • Coordinate change: an affine map x' = A x + a, where A is a constant 3×3 matrix (Jacobian), and a is a constant translation vector.
  • Translation: A = I, x' = x - a (or equivalent sign convention).
  • Inversion (parity): A = -I, hence (x',y',z') = (-x,-y,-z), with det(A) = -1.
  • Boundary/initial conditions: not applicable (this is a kinematics/symmetry transformation question, not a PDE/ODE evolution problem).

Key operators and tensors you should recognize

  • Jacobian / linear map: A = ∂x'/∂x.
  • Levi-Civita symbol: ε_ijk, used for cross products in components.
  • Dot product: A·B = A_i B_i (a scalar).
  • Cross product: (A×B)_i = ε_ijk A_j B_k.
  • Orientation: if det(A) = +1, orientation is preserved (proper rotations); if det(A) = -1, it is reversed (includes inversion, reflections).

Mini intuition examples (quick, minimal algebra)

Translation: If you shift the origin, the arrow you drew in space doesn’t change—only the labels on axes do. So the same physical vector has the same components in a translated, non-rotated frame.
Inversion: If you invert axes, what was “+x” becomes “−x”. A displacement that previously pointed +x now has negative components. But a rotation axis (like a screw direction) can stay the same because it depends on handedness.

What to watch for (pitfalls)

Pitfallscommon confusion
  • Active vs passive: Are you moving the vector in space (active) or relabeling coordinates (passive)? This problem is about coordinate changes (passive).
  • Translation vs rotation: Translation changes the origin, not the basis directions. Rotation changes the basis directions.
  • Cross product and handedness: The cross product depends on orientation. Under inversion, the right-hand rule flips.
  • Pseudo-objects: Scalars are invariant under inversion; pseudoscalars pick up a minus sign.
Math Insightsymmetry + representation

The heart of this problem is representation theory of the orthogonal group: under a change of coordinates with matrix A, vectors transform with A, while the Levi-Civita symbol effectively contributes a factor of det(A). That single determinant is what creates the “pseudo-” behavior.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

These transformation rules are not just abstract math—industry uses them whenever orientation, sensing, and mechanical actions depend on coordinate frames and handedness.

Where it shows up (concrete application areas)

  • Robotics & automation: coordinate frames for robot arms, tool frames, and sensor frames. Translation changes the origin; inversion/reflection mistakes can silently flip sign conventions (dangerous in calibration).
  • CAD/CAE & manufacturing metrology: transforming measured point clouds and vectors between machine coordinates and part coordinates; incorrect handling of orientation can create mirrored parts (left-handed vs right-handed coordinate systems).
  • Mechanical systems & drivetrains: torque and angular velocity are axial vectors. Correct sign behavior matters for shaft dynamics, control loops, and safety interlocks.
  • Electromechanics & sensing: magnetic field B (axial) vs electric field E (polar). Coordinate reflections affect how you interpret sensor outputs.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: control algorithms rely on consistent vector transformations (e.g., torque commands).
  • Safety: sign errors can reverse corrective actions in feedback systems.
  • Reliability: multi-sensor fusion (IMU + vision + encoders) depends on correct polar/axial handling.
  • Cost & time: debugging coordinate-handness bugs is expensive; robust conventions save weeks.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Frame calibration using reference artifacts (metrology) and known motions (robot/IMU).
  • Consistency checks: verify det(A)=+1 for intended rotations; flag unexpected det(A)=-1 (mirror).
  • Physical tests: apply a known torque and check measured angular acceleration direction.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Real sensors have bias and noise; frame conventions must be stable and documented.
  • Mechanical tolerances cause small misalignments; transformations are often estimated from data (least squares), so orientation errors can creep in.
  • Software pipelines sometimes mix left-handed and right-handed conventions (graphics vs physics engines)—a classic source of inversion bugs.

From lab to product

The ideal model assumes perfect Cartesian frames and exact matrices. In real systems, you often estimate A from calibration data, then enforce physical constraints: for a “pure” rotation you project the estimate onto SO(3) (orthogonal with determinant +1) to avoid accidental reflections that would break cross-product/torque logic.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement (in plain words)

You are given two coordinate transformations in 3D:

  • (a) Translation of coordinates (same axes directions, shifted origin): x' = x, y' = y − a, z' = z.
  • (b) Inversion of coordinates (parity): x' = −x, y' = −y, z' = −z.

You must determine how:

  • (a) the components of a vector transform under translation;
  • (b) the components of a vector transform under inversion;
  • (c) the cross product transforms under inversion; classify it (vector vs pseudovector) and give two classical pseudovector examples;
  • (d) the scalar triple product transforms under inversion and classify it as a pseudoscalar.

Given / unknowns

Given
  • Affine coordinate map (translation) with no rotation.
  • Inversion map with matrix A = −I.
  • Standard definitions of dot and cross products.
Unknowns (to find)
  • Component transformation rules for a vector under translation and inversion.
  • Transformation of A×B under inversion and its classification.
  • Transformation of A·(B×C) under inversion and its classification.

Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Linearity of component maps: For a linear change of basis (matrix A), vector components transform linearly.
  • Translation does not change basis: the axes directions stay fixed, so basis vectors are unchanged → components of a free vector are unchanged.
  • Orientation matters for cross products: the cross product is built using ε_ijk, which is sensitive to handedness (determinant).

We do not need dynamics (Newton’s laws) or field PDEs (like ∇·E), because the question is purely about geometry and symmetry.

Assumptionsexplicit idealizations
  • Euclidean 3D space; Cartesian frames; standard dot/cross product definitions.
  • The transformation is purely a change of coordinates (passive), not a physical deformation of space.
  • Vectors are “free vectors” (not tied to a specific point). If you discuss position vectors, you must transform the point as well.

Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

Approach A — Direct component reasoning
  • Use V = V_i e_i and how e_i change.
  • Pros: intuitive, short.
  • Cons: can get confusing for cross products unless you introduce ε-symbol.
Approach B — Matrix/Jacobian + determinant
  • Use x' = A x + a, track vectors and pseudovectors with det(A).
  • Pros: systematic, scales to engineering transforms.
  • Cons: slightly more abstract.
Chosen approach: Approach B. It cleanly separates translation (no change to basis) from inversion (orientation reversal) and naturally explains why cross products are “pseudo”.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Model the coordinate transform: write x' = A x + a. Identify A and det(A).
  2. Translation case: show the basis vectors are unchanged; conclude vector components are unchanged.
  3. Inversion case: use A = −I. For polar vectors, components transform with A → sign flip.
  4. Cross product: use component formula (A×B)_i = ε_ijk A_j B_k and how ε behaves under orientation reversal; infer pseudovector behavior.
  5. Triple scalar product: use A·(B×C) and the previous step; show it changes sign → pseudoscalar.
  6. Sanity checks: determinant sign, sign-cancellation logic, and physical examples (torque, angular momentum).
Common mistakesquick fixes
  • Mistake: thinking translation changes vector components. Fix: translation changes coordinates of points, not the basis directions.
  • Mistake: treating cross product as always a polar vector. Fix: it’s axial; it depends on handedness.
  • Mistake: forgetting det(−I)=−1. Fix: determinant controls orientation and pseudo-sign.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before equations)

  • Translation: shifting the origin should not change the “arrow” representing a free vector, so its components in a non-rotated frame should stay the same.
  • Inversion: flipping all axes should flip the sign of ordinary vectors (polar), but quantities like torque (built from cross products) should remain consistent with physical right-hand/left-hand convention changes—hinting at pseudovectors.

Step 1 — Set up the affine transformation

Consider a general coordinate change (passive transformation) in ℝ³:

Affine coordinate map
x' = A x + a

Here A is the constant Jacobian matrix A = ∂x'/∂x, and a is a constant translation. Linearity is valid because we are in Cartesian coordinates and the transformation is rigid (no deformation).

Step 2 — How a (polar) vector’s components transform

A polar vector V is geometric. Its components change according to the linear part A. For a pure translation, A=I, so nothing changes. For inversion, A=-I, so components flip sign.

Optional deeper note: passive vs active and why translation doesn’t affect free-vector components
A translation changes the coordinates of a point: if the origin moves, the numerical coordinates of the same point change. But the basis directions of the axes do not rotate, so a free vector (difference of two points) has the same components: V = r2 − r1 and both points shift by the same amount, canceling in the difference.

(a) Translation of coordinates

The problem gives (one axis shifted by a distance a): x' = x, y' = y − a, z' = z. This is a pure translation: the Jacobian is the identity, because ∂x'/∂x = 1, ∂y'/∂y = 1, ∂z'/∂z = 1, and mixed partials are zero.

Jacobian for translation
A = I
⇒ V'_i = V_i

Conclusion (a): Under translation (no rotation), the components of a vector do not change: V' = V component-wise.

(b) Inversion of coordinates

Inversion (parity) is: x' = −x, y' = −y, z' = −z. Thus A = −I and det(A) = det(−I) = (−1)^3 = −1, meaning orientation reverses.

Polar vector under inversion
A = -I
⇒ V'_i = (-δ_ij) V_j = -V_i

Conclusion (b): For a polar vector, inversion flips the sign of every component: (V_x', V_y', V_z') = (−V_x, −V_y, −V_z).

(c) Cross product of two vectors under inversion: why it’s a pseudovector

Let A and B be polar vectors. The cross product components are:

Cross product (component form)
(A×B)_i = ε_ijk A_j B_k

Under inversion, polar vectors transform as A' = −A and B' = −B. If we compute the cross product using the same algebraic definition:

Compute transformed cross product
A'×B' = (-A)×(-B)
      = (+1)(A×B)
      = A×B

So the cross product remains unchanged under inversion, even though each input vector flipped sign. This is the defining behavior of an axial vector (a pseudovector).

Deeper mathematical justification: the determinant factor
More generally, for an orthogonal transformation with matrix R, the cross product transforms as:
General rule (orthogonal R)
(R A)×(R B) = det(R) R (A×B)
For inversion R=-I, det(R)=-1. Then det(R)R = (-1)(-I)=I, so the cross product is invariant. The appearance of det(R) is the “pseudo” signature tied to orientation reversal.
Classificationwhat is A×B?

The cross product of two polar vectors is a pseudovector (axial vector). It is “pseudo” because it depends on the choice of orientation (right-handed vs left-handed) through the Levi-Civita symbol.

Two classical-mechanics pseudovectors: L = r × p (angular momentum), τ = r × F (torque). Also ω (angular velocity) is axial.

(d) Scalar triple product under inversion: pseudoscalar

The scalar triple product of three (polar) vectors is: S = A · (B × C). Under inversion: A' = −A, and we already found (B×C)' = B×C. Therefore:

Transform the triple product
S' = A'·(B'×C')
   = (-A)·(B×C)
   = - A·(B×C)
   = -S

Conclusion (d): The scalar triple product changes sign under inversion, so it is a pseudoscalar. Physically, A·(B×C) measures an oriented volume; inversion flips orientation, so the sign flips.

Sanity checks

Dimensional analysis
  • Translation/inversion are dimensionless coordinate maps → only signs/values of components change, not units.
  • A×B units = (units of A)(units of B).
  • A·(B×C) units = (units of A)(units of B)(units of C).
Limiting / consistency cases
  • If any of A,B,C is zero, all products are zero; sign rules remain consistent.
  • Inversion twice returns identity: applying sign flips twice returns original values (polar vectors: (-1)^2=1).
  • Oriented volume flips under mirror/inversion: matches S'=-S.

Final results (boxed)

FINAL ANSWER (plain text)
(a) Translation (no rotation): V'_i = V_i.

(b) Inversion (x'=-x, y'=-y, z'=-z): for a polar vector, V'_i = -V_i.

(c) Cross product of two polar vectors: (A×B)' = A×B, so A×B is a pseudovector (axial vector).
    Examples (classical mechanics): torque τ = r×F, angular momentum L = r×p (also angular velocity ω).

(d) Scalar triple product: [A·(B×C)]' = -A·(B×C), so it is a pseudoscalar.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpretation: what each transformation is “doing”

  • Translation changes only the origin: it does not change lengths, angles, or handedness. Therefore it cannot change components of a free vector in a fixed-direction basis.
  • Inversion reverses every axis and flips handedness (determinant −1). Polar vectors flip; axial vectors stay.
  • Cross product encodes orientation (right-hand rule). Under orientation reversal, it picks up an extra sign that cancels the polar-vector sign flips, creating an axial quantity.
  • Triple scalar product is an oriented volume: inversion mirrors the coordinate system, so the sign changes.

Parameter dependence and sensitivity

There are no continuous “parameters” to vary here besides the nature of the transformation (proper vs improper). The key switch is the determinant:

Determinant controls pseudo-sign
Proper rotation: det(R)=+1  → axial behaves like polar under R.
Improper (reflection/inversion): det(R)=-1 → axial picks up an extra minus relative to polar.

In engineering practice: if your fitted “rotation matrix” has det≈−1, you accidentally estimated a reflection—expect cross-product/torque inconsistencies.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

You can derive everything using oriented volume and wedge products: the cross product is the Hodge dual of the wedge product in 3D. Orientation reversal changes the Hodge dual’s sign, which exactly produces the pseudo-behavior. This is the geometric reason the determinant appears.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: If r is a position vector, does translation leave r unchanged? A: No. A translation changes the coordinates of a point. But a free vector like Δr is unchanged.
  • Q: Under inversion, does velocity v flip sign? A: Yes, it’s a polar vector: v' = −v.
  • Q: Under inversion, does angular momentum L flip? A: No, L = r×p is axial, so it is invariant.
  • Q: Under inversion, does the dot product A·B change? A: For polar vectors, no: (-A)·(-B)=A·B (true scalar).
  • Q: What does A·(B×C) represent geometrically? A: Oriented volume; it changes sign under inversion.
