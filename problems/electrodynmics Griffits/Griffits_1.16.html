Divergence of the Radial Inverse-Square Field: v = r̂ / r²

You compute ∇·v and get 0 (almost everywhere), yet the field has a nonzero outward flux through any sphere around the origin. The “surprise” is resolved by treating the origin correctly: the divergence contains a delta-function source.

Quick Summary
  • Problem: Sketch the vector field v(r) = r̂ / r² and compute its divergence.
  • Key physics idea: This is the canonical inverse-square radial field (like a point source in 3D).
  • Governing operator: Divergence ∇·v measures local “source/sink density” (net outflow per unit volume).
  • Result (classical): ∇·(r̂/r²) = 0 for r ≠ 0.
  • Surprise: The flux through any sphere around the origin is ∮ v·dA = 4π, not zero.
  • Resolution: The origin is a singular point; in the distribution sense ∇·(r̂/r²) = 4π δ³(r).
  • Physical meaning: “No sources in empty space” + “a point source at the origin.”
  • Industrial relevance: Models point sources/sinks (electrostatics, gravitation, fluid injection, heat, diffusion) and highlights why numerical methods need special handling of singularities.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • r: radial distance from the origin (SI: m).
  • r̂: radial unit vector (dimensionless).
  • v(r): vector field (units depend on context; here it scales as 1/m²).
  • ∇·v: divergence (units of v divided by length; here ~ 1/m³ in the classical region).
  • dA: surface area element (m²), dV: volume element (m³).
  • δ³(r): 3D Dirac delta (units 1/m³), representing a point-concentrated source.

0.2 Physical meaning: what divergence “represents”

Divergence is a local balance: it measures how much a field “flows out” of an infinitesimal volume compared to how much flows in. Formally, 

∇·v(r0) = lim_{V→0} (1/V) ∬_{∂V} v·n dA
Copied ✓

If ∇·v > 0, the point behaves like a local source. If ∇·v < 0, it behaves like a local sink. If ∇·v = 0, the flow is locally incompressible / source-free.

0.3 Key law: Divergence theorem (Gauss’s theorem)

For a sufficiently smooth field on a region V with boundary ∂V: 

∬_{∂V} v·n dA = ∭_{V} (∇·v) dV
Copied ✓

Validity condition: the theorem assumes the field is well-behaved (no singularities) inside the volume. If there is a singularity (like at r=0 here), you must exclude it or use distribution theory.

0.4 A common model in this topic: radial symmetry

For any purely radial field of the form v = f(r) r̂, symmetry implies the divergence depends only on r. In spherical coordinates, a standard identity (derived from the conservation-form of flux through spheres) is: 

If v = f(r) r̂, then  ∇·v = (1/r²) d/dr [ r² f(r) ]   (for r ≠ 0)
Copied ✓

0.5 Mini intuition examples (quick)

  • Uniform field (e.g., v = constant): divergence is zero—what flows in equals what flows out locally.
  • Expanding flow (e.g., v = α r): divergence is constant positive—everywhere acts like a distributed source.
  • Inverse-square radial flow (this problem): same total flux through any sphere—suggests a point source at the origin and no sources elsewhere.

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Singularities: formulas like (1/r²) d/dr (r² f) apply only for r ≠ 0.
  • “Zero divergence” does not automatically mean “zero flux” if the region includes a point where the field is not defined.
  • Distribution vs pointwise derivatives: the “missing source” often appears as a delta function.
  • Dimensional check: if v ~ 1/r², then ∇·v scales like 1/r³ away from singularities, while δ³(r) has units 1/volume.
Math Insight (core equation as a mathematical object)

The identity ∇·(f(r) r̂) = (1/r²) d/dr [r² f(r)] is a conservation-law form: it encodes that flux through a sphere of radius r is proportional to r² f(r). If that quantity is constant, the divergence is zero (no distributed sources) — except where the field is singular.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Why this matters in real engineering

Inverse-square radial fields are not just math exercises—they are a prototype for “point-source physics”. Engineers meet the same structure whenever something emits/absorbs “stuff” isotropically in 3D.

Applications Where it appears
  • Electrostatics & EMC: electric field of a point charge (and Green’s functions used in field solvers).
  • Gravitation & geophysics: gravity from a point mass; inverse-square kernels in potential-field imaging.
  • Fluid/thermal systems: idealized injection/withdrawal wells, leakage points, point heat sources in conduction models.
  • Diffusion & transport: fundamental solutions and source terms in PDEs used in reliability and contamination modeling.
Engineering stakes Why engineers care
  • Performance: field magnitude blows up near the source; design must handle peaks (breakdown, erosion, overheating).
  • Safety & reliability: singular behavior drives worst-case stresses; ignoring it can underpredict failure risk.
  • Cost & manufacturability: singular models guide where shielding, spacing, heat spreading, or tolerances matter most.
  • Simulation accuracy: FEM/FVM need special treatments (mesh refinement, singular elements, or source terms).

How it’s validated in practice (brief)

  • Field mapping: probes, E-field sensors, gravimeters, or PIV/flow measurements to infer flux patterns.
  • Integral checks: engineers often validate with global conservation (flux balance over surfaces), not only pointwise values.
  • Numerical verification: compare computed flux through control surfaces against known analytic constants (like 4π here).

Design trade-offs and non-idealities

  • Real sources have finite size: the 1/r² law holds away from the source region, but is regularized near it.
  • Materials introduce boundaries and anisotropy: flux may not remain isotropic; divergence can include distributed source terms.
  • Measurement bandwidth and sensor size effectively smear the singularity—physically similar to replacing the delta function by a narrow peak.
From lab to product: how the ideal model is adapted

In product-level models, a “point” source is replaced by one of: (i) a small but finite-radius source region, (ii) a boundary condition prescribing total flux through a small surface, or (iii) an explicit source term (often a delta function or a narrow Gaussian). This keeps global conservation correct while avoiding numerical instability at r=0.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

1.1 Restate the problem

Consider the vector field in 3D space v(r) = r̂ / r², where r = |r| and r̂ = r / |r|. Sketch its direction and magnitude, and compute its divergence ∇·v. Explain why the result seems surprising.

1.2 Given quantities, unknowns, and targets

  • Given: v(r) = r̂ / r² (defined for r ≠ 0).
  • Unknown: divergence ∇·v.
  • Also required: interpret the “surprise” using correct mathematics/physics.

1.3 Mathematical model (explicit)

Model Variables • domain • coordinates
  • Space: 3D Euclidean space ℝ³.
  • Coordinates: spherical coordinates (r, θ, φ) centered at the origin, because the field is radial and spherically symmetric.
  • Domain: ℝ³ \ {0} for pointwise calculus; potentially all ℝ³ in the distribution sense.
  • Field: v(r,θ,φ) = (1/r²) r̂.
  • Boundary/initial conditions: none (static field; no time variable). The key “condition” is handling the singularity at r=0.

1.4 Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Radial divergence identity: applies because v is purely radial and depends only on r.
  • Divergence theorem: connects flux through a sphere to volume integral of divergence; crucial for diagnosing the “surprise.”
  • Distribution theory idea: needed because v is singular at the origin, so “classical” divergence is incomplete if the region includes r=0.

1.5 Assumptions (explicit)

Assumptions What we idealize
  • Field is static (no time dependence).
  • Space is continuous and isotropic.
  • We treat the origin as an ideal point singularity (like a point charge or point source).
  • We will compute divergence in two senses:
    • Pointwise/classical for r ≠ 0.
    • Distributional on all space, revealing a delta function at r=0.

1.6 Possible approaches (compare)

  • Approach A: Direct computation in spherical coordinates
    Pros: fast, uses symmetry; Cons: only valid for r ≠ 0.
  • Approach B: Flux through a sphere + divergence theorem
    Pros: physically transparent, exposes the paradox; Cons: requires careful handling of the singular point.
  • Approach C: Distribution/Green’s function reasoning
    Pros: gives the correct “global” answer 4π δ³(r); Cons: conceptually heavier if you’re new to distributions.

Best plan: combine A and B, then use C briefly to formalize the delta-function source. This gives both a clean calculation and the correct explanation of the surprise.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Sketch the field qualitatively: identify direction (radial) and magnitude (~1/r²).
  2. State the coordinate choice: spherical coordinates centered at origin due to symmetry.
  3. Compute pointwise divergence for r ≠ 0: use ∇·(f(r) r̂) = (1/r²) d/dr (r² f).
  4. Compute flux through a sphere: evaluate ∮ v·dA.
  5. Compare with divergence theorem: if divergence were zero everywhere inside, flux should be zero—diagnose what fails.
  6. Fix the issue: exclude a tiny ball around the origin and take a limit, or state the distribution identity.
  7. Write the final divergence: zero for r≠0, plus 4π δ³(r) at the origin.
  8. Sanity checks: units, limiting cases, and physical interpretation (point source strength 4π).
Common mistakes Quick tips
  • Using the divergence theorem on a volume that includes the singularity without modification.
  • Concluding “flux must be zero” from “divergence is zero” while ignoring that the field is not defined at r=0.
  • Forgetting that δ³(r) has units 1/m³ and integrates to 1.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Physical intuition before calculating

The field v = r̂ / r² points directly outward from the origin everywhere (except at the origin), and its magnitude decreases as 1/r². That is exactly the pattern you’d expect from a point source in 3D: the same “amount” spreads over spheres of area 4πr², so the intensity must scale like 1/r².

This intuition already predicts something important: the total outward flux through a sphere might be constant (independent of radius), which would mean there is a net “source strength” at the origin.

3.2 Sketch (verbal)

At each point, draw an arrow pointing radially outward. Near the origin the arrows are very long (blow up like 1/r²), and far away they become tiny. The pattern is spherically symmetric: rotating the field around the origin changes nothing.

3.3 Pointwise divergence for r ≠ 0

Write the field as v = f(r) r̂ with f(r) = 1/r². For a radial field in 3D, the divergence (away from the origin) is: 

∇·(f(r) r̂) = (1/r²) d/dr [ r² f(r) ]    (valid for r ≠ 0)
Copied ✓

Substitute f(r)=1/r². First compute the bracket: 

r² f(r) = r² (1/r²) = 1
Copied ✓

Differentiate: 

d/dr [ r² f(r) ] = d/dr [1] = 0
Copied ✓

Therefore, for every point with r ≠ 0: 

∇·(r̂/r²) = 0   (for r ≠ 0)
Copied ✓

3.4 Flux through a sphere (the “surprise” appears)

Consider a sphere of radius R centered at the origin. On the sphere, r = R and the outward normal is n = r̂. Also, the area element is dA and the total area is 4πR².

Compute the flux: 

Φ(R) = ∬_{sphere} v·n dA
     = ∬_{sphere} (r̂/R²) · r̂ dA
     = ∬_{sphere} (1/R²) dA
     = (1/R²) (4πR²)
     = 4π
Copied ✓

The outward flux is a constant 4π, independent of R. That is exactly the signature of a point source: no matter how big the enclosing surface is, the same total amount “leaves” it.

3.5 Why this seems contradictory (and why it isn’t)

If we naively combine “∇·v = 0” with the divergence theorem, we might conclude: Φ(R) = ∭ (∇·v) dV = 0. But we just found Φ(R)=4π.

The key point: the divergence theorem requires the field to be well-behaved inside the volume. Our field is not defined (and not integrably smooth) at r=0. So the theorem cannot be applied directly to a volume that includes the origin without special care.

3.6 Correct resolution using an “excluded ball” (no magic jumps)

Let V be the ball of radius R. Remove a tiny inner ball of radius ε around the origin, creating an annular region: V_ε = { r : ε < r < R }. In this region, the field is smooth (no singularity), so Gauss’s theorem applies on V_ε.

Apply the divergence theorem: 

∭_{V_ε} (∇·v) dV = ∬_{r=R} v·n dA  +  ∬_{r=ε} v·n dA
Copied ✓

Why the plus sign on both surface integrals? Because ∂V_ε has two components: the outer sphere and the inner sphere. The normal n is defined as outward from the region V_ε. On the inner boundary (at r=ε), “outward from the annulus” points toward the origin, i.e. n = -r̂.

Now compute each flux term:

  • Outer boundary (r=R): as before, ∬ v·n dA = 4π.
  • Inner boundary (r=ε): here v = r̂/ε² and n=-r̂, so v·n = -1/ε². The inner sphere area is 4πε², hence ∬ v·n dA = (-1/ε²)(4πε²) = -4π.

Therefore: 

∭_{V_ε} (∇·v) dV = 4π + (-4π) = 0
Copied ✓

This is perfectly consistent with the pointwise result ∇·v = 0 for r≠0: the annulus contains no sources, so its net flux is zero.

3.7 The distributional divergence: the missing source is a delta function

Although ∇·v = 0 for r≠0, the full-space statement that matches the observed flux is: 

∇·(r̂/r²) = 4π δ³(r)
Copied ✓

Meaning: for any volume V that contains the origin, ∭_V ∇·(r̂/r²) dV = 4π. If V does not contain the origin, the integral is 0.

(Optional) Why the delta function coefficient must be 4π

A 3D delta function satisfies ∭ δ³(r) dV = 1 over any volume containing the origin. We already computed that the flux through a sphere is 4π. If Gauss’s theorem is to hold in the generalized sense, we need ∭ ∇·v dV = 4π for such volumes. That forces ∇·v = 4π δ³(r).

3.8 Final answer (boxed) + copy button

Field sketch: v points radially outward; |v| = 1/r² (singular at r=0).

Divergence (pointwise):
∇·(r̂/r²) = 0  for r ≠ 0.

Divergence (distributional / full-space):
∇·(r̂/r²) = 4π δ³(r).
Copied ✓

3.9 Sanity checks

  • Units: v ~ 1/m². A divergence in the distribution sense is ~ δ³(r) which has units 1/m³, so 4π δ³(r) has the correct dimensional “per volume” scaling for a point source term.
  • Limiting cases: as r → ∞, v → 0 rapidly; far away the field is negligible, consistent with inverse-square decay.
  • Flux consistency: integrating the distributional divergence over a ball yields 4π, matching the surface flux calculation.
  • Sign/direction: field points outward → positive source at origin (delta function coefficient positive).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpret the final formula

The statement ∇·(r̂/r²) = 4π δ³(r) is a compact way of saying:

  • Everywhere except the origin: no creation/destruction (source-free), so the field is locally “incompressible.”
  • At the origin: there is an ideal point source whose total strength is 4π.

In physics, the same structure appears as: ∇·E = ρ/ε₀ in electrostatics, where a point charge has ρ = q δ³(r). This problem is the pure geometric kernel behind that idea.

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative)

Here the field is fixed, but it’s useful to generalize to v = A r̂ / r² where A is a constant: 

If v = A r̂/r², then
∇·v = 0 (r≠0),  and  ∇·v = 4π A δ³(r) (distributional).
Copied ✓
  • Increasing A scales the flux through any enclosing sphere linearly.
  • The field remains singular at the origin; only the “strength” of the point source changes.

4.3 Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

A standard route uses potentials and Green’s functions: ∇(1/r) = - r̂/r² for r≠0. Then ∇·(r̂/r²) relates to ∇²(1/r). In distribution theory, ∇²(1/r) = -4π δ³(r), which implies the divergence identity immediately (up to a sign). This is the mathematical backbone of point-source solutions of Poisson’s equation.

4.4 Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: If ∇·v = 0 everywhere in a simply connected region, must the flux through every closed surface be zero?
    A: Yes—if the field is well-defined and sufficiently smooth everywhere inside that surface. Singularities break the assumption.
  • Q: Why does the coefficient become 4π?
    A: Because the surface area of a unit sphere is 4π, and the field is precisely tuned so that v·n = 1/r² gives constant total flux.
  • Q: Does “divergence zero for r≠0” mean the field is curl-free too?
    A: Not automatically. (In fact, this field is curl-free for r≠0, since it is the gradient of -1/r there, but divergence and curl are different operators.)
  • Q: In engineering simulation, what’s the practical fix for the singularity?
    A: Replace the point by a small finite source region or enforce the correct total flux as a boundary condition; don’t rely on pointwise values at r=0.

PART 5 — Optional Plot Guide
