Problem 1.2 — Is the Cross Product Associative?

We test whether (A × B) × C equals A × (B × C) . This looks like a purely algebraic question, but it also matters in applied physics (torque chains, rigid-body dynamics, coordinate transforms).

Quick Summary

  • What it’s about: whether the vector cross product “groups” like ordinary multiplication.
  • Key physics idea: cross products encode oriented area and rotation/torque directions in 3D; nesting them changes meaning.
  • Governing identities: vector triple product (BAC–CAB) and bilinearity.
  • Main result: cross product is not associative in general: (A×B)×C ≠ A×(B×C).
  • How we show it: derive both sides using triple-product identities, then give a counterexample.
  • Interpretation: the two nestings correspond to different linear maps built from dot products.
  • Result type: symbolic proof + explicit numeric counterexample (no plot needed).
Final: The cross product is NOT associative.
(A×B)×C ≠ A×(B×C) in general.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (THEORY BEFORE SOLVING)

Vectors in ℝ³ Bilinearity Right-hand rule Triple product identity Applied mechanics & EM

Mathematical model (variables, domain, coordinates)

We work in 3D Euclidean space ℝ³ with the standard Cartesian basis {êx, êy, êz}. Vectors A, B, C ∈ ℝ³ are free vectors.

No boundary/initial conditions are needed because this is an algebraic identity problem (not a PDE/ODE). Still, we keep the viewpoint of operators: the mapping X ↦ A×X is a linear operator on ℝ³.

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • A, B, C: vectors (units depend on context; e.g., m for position, N for force, T for magnetic field).
  • Dot product A·B: scalar; units multiply (e.g., m·N = N·m).
  • Cross product A×B: vector perpendicular to the plane of A and B; units multiply (e.g., m×N → N·m).

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • |A×B| equals the area of the parallelogram spanned by A and B: |A||B|sinθ.
  • Direction follows the right-hand rule, encoding orientation (handedness) in 3D.
  • Nesting cross products changes the geometric object: a “perpendicular to a perpendicular” depends on which plane you built first.

Key laws/principles & validity conditions

  • Bilinearity: linear in each slot: A×(B+C)=A×B+A×C and (A+B)×C=A×C+B×C.
  • Anticommutativity: A×B = −(B×A).
  • Triple product identities: derived from component/tensor algebra and valid in ℝ³.
  • Limitations: cross product as a 3D vector operation is special to 3D (and 7D in a different algebraic sense). In general dimensions we use wedge products / exterior algebra instead.

Common models/approximations in this topic

  • No approximations needed: this is exact algebra in ℝ³.
  • However, in engineering we often approximate rotations for small angles; those approximations live in the same “cross-product as generator” world. Misusing associativity can break those approximations.

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  1. Torque: τ = r×F is perpendicular to the plane formed by position and force. If you then cross τ with something else, the “plane of action” changes.
  2. Magnetic force: F=q v×B. If you form (v×B)×C, you are taking a new perpendicular relative to a plane defined by A×(B×C) with (A×B)×C — they are different maps.
  3. Dropping parentheses: with cross products, parentheses matter.
  4. Sign errors from right-hand rule and anticommutativity.
    5. Deeper note: operator notation viewpoint

    Define linear operators on ℝ³: L_A(X)=A×X. Then (A×B)×C = L_{(A×B)}(C), while A×(B×C)=L_A(L_B(C)). Associativity would mean L_{A×B}=L_A∘L_B, which is false in general.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (concrete areas)

Robotics & automation

Spatial kinematics uses cross products for angular velocity, moment arms, and Jacobians. Incorrect grouping can corrupt end-effector force/torque estimation.

Mechanical design & fatigue

Torque chains and bending moments (often cross products of position vectors and forces) must be computed in the correct order to avoid under/over-design.

Electromagnetics & power electronics

Lorentz force q v×B, Poynting vector S=E×H, and stress tensors involve nested vector products in derivations and code.

IMU/AR/VR sensor fusion

Small-angle rotation updates often use cross-product matrices. Assuming wrong algebra can destabilize filters (EKF/UKF).

Why engineers care (performance, safety, reliability, cost)

  • Performance: correct torque/force direction impacts control accuracy and energy efficiency.
  • Safety: wrong moment predictions can lead to structural failure or unsafe robot behavior.
  • Reliability: simulation drift and integration errors increase if algebra is mishandled.
  • Cost: design margins inflate if loads are miscomputed; field failures become expensive.

Measurement/validation methods (brief)

  • Torque sensors, strain gauges, multi-axis force plates (validate vector moment computations).
  • Motion capture + IMUs (validate kinematic cross-product-based angular updates).
  • Hall sensors / B-field mapping (validate Lorentz-force-related predictions in EM setups).

Design trade-offs & constraints

  • Coordinate misalignment and tolerances: a small axis error changes cross-product results directionally.
  • Numerical stability: repeated cross products can amplify floating-point noise if vectors become nearly parallel.
  • Non-idealities: flexible bodies and non-rigid joints mean “ideal” vector models are approximations; correct algebra is still essential.

From lab to product

In prototypes, you may compute moments directly from CAD geometry and measured forces. In products, this becomes embedded code: you implement cross products and rotation updates as matrix operations. The parentheses determine which intermediate vectors are computed and thus the physical meaning of each stage.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Problem restatement

Determine whether the vector cross product is associative, i.e., whether for all vectors A, B, C ∈ ℝ³ we have (A×B)×C = A×(B×C). If not, provide a counterexample.

Given quantities

  • Vectors A, B, C in ℝ³.

Unknowns / what must be found

  • Whether the identity holds for all vectors.
  • If false, a specific set of vectors that violates it.

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Triple product identities let us rewrite nested cross products using dot products, making equality checks transparent.
  • Bilinearity justifies expansions and substitutions line-by-line (no “magic steps”).
  • Tensor/Levi-Civita method provides a mathematically rigorous derivation and explains why the identities are valid in ℝ³.
Assumptions:
1) We work in ℝ³ with standard Euclidean dot and cross products.
2) Vectors are ordinary (no exotic non-Euclidean metric).
3) Exact algebra (no approximations).

Two–three approaches (pros/cons)

Approach A: Component expansion
  • Pros: direct, no identities needed.
  • Cons: long and error-prone.
Approach B: Triple product identity
  • Pros: shortest symbolic path; reveals structure.
  • Cons: must know/derive identity.
Approach C: Counterexample first
  • Pros: fastest to disprove.
  • Cons: does not teach the general formula unless supplemented.

Choice: Use Approach B (identity) for a clean proof, then confirm with a counterexample (Approach C).

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Goal: express each nested cross product as a linear combination of vectors.
    Tool: vector triple product identity (BAC–CAB).
  2. Goal: compute A×(B×C).
    Tool: identity A×(B×C)=B(A·C)-C(A·B).
    Meaning: result lies in the span of B and C.
  3. Goal: compute (A×B)×C.
    Tool: identity (A×B)×C = B(A·C) - A(B·C) (derived carefully below).
    Meaning: result lies in the span of A and B.
  4. Goal: compare formulas.
    Tool: linear independence / choose vectors to show mismatch.
  5. Goal: provide a concrete counterexample.
    Tool: pick easy basis vectors and compute quickly.
Common mistakes:
• Dropping parentheses: A×B×C is ambiguous; always parenthesize.
• Using the wrong triple-product formula: A×(B×C) ≠ (A×B)×C.
• Sign errors from anticommutativity: A×B = −B×A.
• Assuming associativity because dot product feels “nice”.

PART 3 — Full Solution (DETAILED + TEACHING)

Physical intuition first (qualitative expectation)

A single cross product A×B makes a vector perpendicular to the plane (A,B). If we then cross again, the second cross product is perpendicular to a different plane, and which plane that is depends on parentheses. So we should not expect associativity to hold.

Step 1: State the key identity and why it is valid

In ℝ³, with Levi-Civita symbol ε_ijk and Kronecker delta δ_ij, the cross product components are (A×B)_i = ε_ijk A_j B_k.

Using the contraction identity ε_ijk ε_klm = δ_il δ_jm − δ_im δ_jl, one derives the vector triple product (BAC–CAB): 

Vector triple product identity:
A×(B×C) = B(A·C) − C(A·B)

Why each step is valid: we are using index notation with Einstein summation; the contraction identity is a standard algebraic fact in ℝ³; dot products arise from repeated indices A·B = A_j B_j. Linearity of sums guarantees no hidden jumps.

Step 2: Compute the right-hand side A×(B×C)

Apply the identity directly: 

Right side:
A×(B×C) = B(A·C) − C(A·B)

Interpretation: the result is a linear combination of B and C, so it lies in the plane spanned by B and C.

Step 3: Compute the left-hand side (A×B)×C

We want an identity for (A×B)×C. Start by using anticommutativity to put it into the form X×(Y×Z): 

(A×B)×C
= − C×(A×B)     (since U×V = −V×U)

Now apply the triple identity with X=C, Y=A, Z=B: 

C×(A×B) = A(C·B) − B(C·A)

Use symmetry of dot product C·B = B·C, C·A = A·C, then apply the minus sign: 

Left side:
(A×B)×C
= −[A(B·C) − B(A·C)]
= B(A·C) − A(B·C)

Interpretation: the left side lies in the span of A and B, not in the span of B and C (in general).

Step 4: Compare both sides

We have derived the two exact formulas: 

A×(B×C) = B(A·C) − C(A·B)
(A×B)×C = B(A·C) − A(B·C)

They are equal for all vectors only if −C(A·B) = −A(B·C) always, which is false in general. Therefore, the cross product is not associative. 

FINAL RESULT (boxed):
The cross product is NOT associative in ℝ³.
In general: (A×B)×C ≠ A×(B×C).

Step 5: Concrete counterexample (quick check)

Choose simple vectors in Cartesian coordinates: 

Let A = (1,0,0),  B = (0,1,0),  C = (1,1,0).

Compute (A×B)×C:

A×B = (0,0,1)
(A×B)×C = (0,0,1)×(1,1,0)
        = (-1, 1, 0)

Compute A×(B×C):

B×C = (0,1,0)×(1,1,0) = (0,0,-1)
A×(B×C) = (1,0,0)×(0,0,-1) = (0,1,0)

Since (-1,1,0) ≠ (0,1,0), associativity fails.

Sanity checks

Dimensional analysis

Each cross product multiplies units: if A has units [a], B has [b], then A×B has [a][b]. Both sides are “two cross products,” so units match: [(A×B)×C] = [a][b][c] and [A×(B×C)] = [a][b][c]. So failure is not about units; it is about direction/structure.

Limiting cases

If vectors are special, equality can accidentally hold:
• If A ⟂ B and C is parallel to A, certain dot terms vanish.
• If B·C = 0 and A·B = 0, formulas may coincide.
But these are special cases, not general associativity.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (THEORY AROUND THE RESULT)

Re-interpret the final formulas (term-by-term meaning)

The two nestings can be read as different “projections and subtractions”: 

A×(B×C) =  B(A·C)  −  C(A·B)
            (along B)     (along C)

(A×B)×C =  B(A·C)  −  A(B·C)
          (along B)     (along A)

Notice the first expression lives in span{B,C}, while the second lives in span{A,B}. Unless A, B, C have special alignment, these subspaces differ — so the results differ.

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • If A·C increases, both sides gain a component along B of magnitude A·C.
  • If A·B increases, only A×(B×C) gains a negative component along C.
  • If B·C increases, only (A×B)×C gains a negative component along A.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Alternative: component proof with ε-δ contraction

Write (A×(B×C))_i = ε_ijk A_j (B×C)_k = ε_ijk A_j ε_klm B_l C_m. Contract using ε_ijk ε_klm = δ_il δ_jm − δ_im δ_jl to get (A×(B×C))_i = B_i (A·C) − C_i (A·B). Do the same for (A×B)×C and compare.

Math Insight callout

Math Insight: The cross product is best viewed as a bilinear map tied to the Lie algebra of rotations. It satisfies the Jacobi identity (a key Lie algebra property), not associativity. Associativity would imply a different algebraic structure than the one rotations naturally use. 

Jacobi identity (always true):
A×(B×C) + B×(C×A) + C×(A×B) = 0

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: If A·B=0, does that make the cross product associative?
    A: Not in general. It only removes the −C(A·B) term on the right, but the left still has −A(B·C).
  • Q: Why do both expressions share the term B(A·C)?
    A: Because both identities involve the projection of A onto C (via A·C) multiplying B; it’s a structural consequence of BAC–CAB.
  • Q: In physics code, what should you do to avoid mistakes?
    A: Always parenthesize, and prefer triple-product identities to reduce nested crosses.
  • Q: Does non-associativity mean cross product is “bad”?
    A: No. It means it encodes geometry/rotation in a way that isn’t scalar-like; it’s the right tool, used correctly.
