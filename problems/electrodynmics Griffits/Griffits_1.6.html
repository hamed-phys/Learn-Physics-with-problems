Problem 1.7 — Separation Vector, Magnitude, and Unit Vector in 3D

Given a source point and a field point in Cartesian coordinates, we construct the displacement (separation) vector from source to field, compute its length, and normalize it to get a unit direction. This is the backbone of many field models (electrostatics, gravitation, acoustics, heat flow direction, robotics positioning).

At a glance

Topic: vectors in 3D Output: numeric Tools: norm, normalization
Coordinate system: Cartesian (x,y,z), Euclidean metric.

Quick Summary

  • What this problem is about: Build the separation vector λ from a source point to a field point in 3D.
  • Key physics idea: Many point-source fields depend on the relative position (difference vector) and its length (distance).
  • Governing equations: λ = r − r', |λ| = sqrt(λ·λ), λ̂ = λ/|λ|.
  • Result type: numeric vector, numeric magnitude, numeric unit vector.
  • Physical meaning: λ̂ points from the source toward where you measure the field; |λ| is the source-to-field distance.
  • Math object insight: This is Euclidean geometry in R^3 with the dot product defining distance and angles.
Math Insight

The operation “field point minus source point” is a translation-invariant map R^3 × R^3 → R^3. Its norm is induced by the inner product a·b, making this a clean example of a metric space: the distance is d(r,r') = ||r − r'||.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • r = (x, y, z): position vector of the field point (units: meters, m).
  • r' = (x', y', z'): position vector of the source point (m).
  • λ = r − r': separation (displacement) vector from source to field (m).
  • λ = |λ|: magnitude (distance) (m).
  • λ̂ = λ/|λ|: unit vector in the separation direction (dimensionless).
  • Dot product: a·b = ax bx + ay by + az bz.

Physical meaning & intuition

  • Separation vector tells you “how to go from the source to the field point.”
  • Magnitude is the straight-line distance that controls many inverse-distance laws (e.g., 1/λ, 1/λ^2).
  • Unit vector isolates direction, useful for turning a scalar radial dependence into a vector field direction.

Example: In electrostatics, a point charge’s field at r points along λ̂ (radially away/toward) and scales with 1/λ^2.

Key laws/principles & validity conditions

  • Euclidean geometry assumption: We assume Cartesian coordinates with the standard Euclidean metric (flat space).
  • Translation invariance: Only relative position matters for separation: shifting both points by the same vector leaves λ unchanged.
  • Linearity: Vector subtraction and dot product are linear operations; norm is derived from the dot product.

Common models/approximations and why

  • Point-source model: Source treated as a point; field depends only on distance/direction from that point.
  • Far-field approximation (context): When λ is large compared to source size, you can approximate distributed sources as point sources.
  • Small-parameter expansions (context): If two points are close, you may use Taylor expansions of fields in λ; here we compute λ exactly.

Mini intuition examples (quick)

  • If field point equals source point, then λ = 0 and direction λ̂ is undefined (no unique direction).
  • If you move the field point +1 m in x while keeping the source fixed, the x-component of λ increases by +1 m.
  • If λ doubles, then distances double; in many physical laws, field magnitudes may drop by factors like 2 or 4 depending on the inverse-power dependence.
What to watch for (pitfalls)
  • Direction sign: “From source to field” means r − r', not the reverse.
  • Units: Components carry length units (e.g., meters). Unit vector must be dimensionless.
  • Zero separation: If |λ| = 0, you cannot normalize.
  • Notation: Many books use R for separation vector; here it’s λ.
Optional deeper note: where ∇ shows up (brief but meaningful)

In field theory, separation vectors enter kernels like G(r,r') = 1/(4π|r−r'|), the Green’s function of the Laplacian: ∇²G = −δ(r−r') (in 3D free space). Here ∇ is the gradient with respect to r, and the dependence is entirely through λ = r−r'. This problem is essentially building the geometric ingredient used inside those PDE solutions.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (at least 3 areas)

  • Robotics & automation: End-effector positioning uses displacement vectors between target and current pose for control and path planning.
  • Metrology & manufacturing: CMM (coordinate measuring machines) compute deviations as vectors between nominal and measured points; norms give tolerances.
  • Electromagnetics & RF engineering: Point-source/point-receiver models (antennas, near-field probes) require separation vectors for propagation direction and distance-dependent loss.
  • Acoustics & vibration: Microphone-array localization and source triangulation rely on relative position vectors and their magnitudes.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: Direction and distance drive control laws, attenuation, timing, and alignment accuracy.
  • Safety & reliability: Collision avoidance and safe-distance constraints depend on distances and direction vectors.
  • Cost & efficiency: Better geometric computation reduces calibration time, scrap, and rework in production.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • 3D coordinate measurement (laser trackers, photogrammetry, structured light) outputs points; software computes separation vectors and norms.
  • Sensor fusion (IMU + vision + encoders) estimates positions; residual vectors quantify model error.
  • RF/ultrasound time-of-flight uses distance; direction can be inferred via multi-sensor geometry.

Design trade-offs and constraints (real systems)

  • Tolerances & noise: Measured coordinates have uncertainty; thus λ and λ̂ inherit error.
  • Non-ideal geometry: Real surfaces aren’t points; engineers define representative points (centroids, fiducials).
  • Coordinate frames: Industrial systems often involve multiple frames; the “simple subtraction” happens only after transforming to a common frame.
From lab to product

The ideal model assumes exact coordinates in one Cartesian frame. In products, you add: (1) frame transformations (rotation/translation), (2) uncertainty propagation (error bars on distance), and (3) calibration offsets (systematic bias). The same core math remains: relative position is the fundamental primitive.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Problem restatement

We are given a source point at coordinates (2, 8, 7) and a field point at coordinates (4, 6, 8). We must compute: (1) the separation vector λ pointing from source to field, (2) its magnitude λ = |λ|, and (3) the unit vector λ̂.

Given

  • Source position: r' = (2, 8, 7)
  • Field position: r = (4, 6, 8)

Unknowns

  • Separation vector: λ
  • Magnitude: λ = |λ|
  • Unit vector: λ̂

Mathematical model (variables, domain, coordinate system, boundary/initial conditions)

  • Space: 3D Euclidean space R^3.
  • Coordinates: Cartesian basis (x,y,z) with orthonormal unit vectors (x̂, ŷ, ẑ).
  • Variables: r (field point), r' (source point), λ = r − r'.
  • Domain: All points in R^3; here we evaluate at two given points.
  • Boundary/initial conditions: Not applicable (this is an algebraic geometry task, not a time-evolution or boundary-value problem).

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Vector subtraction: The displacement from source to field is the difference of position vectors (r − r').
  • Euclidean norm: Distance is computed from the dot product: |λ| = sqrt(λ·λ).
  • Normalization: A direction unit vector is obtained by dividing by the magnitude (if nonzero).
Assumptions
  • Standard Cartesian coordinates with orthonormal axes (no scaling/shear).
  • Flat Euclidean geometry (no curvature effects).
  • Points are distinct so |λ| ≠ 0 (true here).

2–3 possible approaches (compare)

Approach A: Component-wise subtraction (best here)

  • How: Compute x, y, z differences directly.
  • Pros: Fast, transparent, minimal tools.
  • Cons: None for simple Cartesian data.

Approach B: Vector algebra with basis vectors

  • How: Write r = x x̂ + y ŷ + z ẑ, subtract formally.
  • Pros: Reinforces basis/linearity; scales to symbolic cases.
  • Cons: Slightly longer.

Chosen approach

We use Approach A (component-wise subtraction) because the points are given numerically in Cartesian coordinates, making the computation direct and least error-prone.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Define the position vectors r and r' in Cartesian form (what each coordinate means physically).
  2. Compute the separation vector using λ = r − r' (goal: direction from source to field).
  3. Compute the squared magnitude via dot product λ·λ (tool: Euclidean inner product).
  4. Take the square root to get |λ| (meaning: distance).
  5. Normalize to obtain λ̂ (meaning: direction only, unit length).
  6. Sanity checks: confirm unit length of λ̂ and correct direction signs.
Common mistakes
  • Swapping order: using r' − r instead of r − r' (flips direction).
  • Forgetting the square root when going from λ·λ to |λ|.
  • Normalizing with the wrong magnitude (must be |λ|, not |r| or |r'|).
  • Assuming unit vectors always exist: if |λ|=0, normalization is undefined.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

The field point (4,6,8) is to the right of the source in x (since 4>2), lower in y (since 6<8), and higher in z (since 8>7). So we expect the separation vector to have components: +x, −y, +z.

Step 1 — Define the position vectors

In Cartesian coordinates, the position vector of a point (x,y,z) is r = (x, y, z). Here: 

r' = (2, 8, 7)   (source point)
r  = (4, 6, 8)   (field point)

Step 2 — Construct the separation vector λ (source → field)

By definition, the separation vector from the source at r' to the field point at r is: 

λ = r − r'

This subtraction is valid because vector spaces are closed under addition and scalar multiplication; subtraction is addition with the additive inverse: r − r' = r + (−r'). Compute component-wise: 

λ = (4, 6, 8) − (2, 8, 7)
  = (4−2, 6−8, 8−7)
  = (2, −2, 1)
Interpretation

To go from the source to the field point, move +2 in x, −2 in y, and +1 in z (in the same length units as the coordinates).

Step 3 — Magnitude (distance) of the separation vector

The magnitude is the Euclidean norm induced by the dot product: |λ| = sqrt(λ·λ). Compute the dot product explicitly: 

λ·λ = (2, −2, 1)·(2, −2, 1)
    = 2^2 + (−2)^2 + 1^2
    = 4 + 4 + 1
    = 9

Therefore:

|λ| = sqrt(9) = 3
Dimensional analysis check

Each component of λ has units of length (e.g., meters), so λ·λ has units of length², and |λ| returns to length after the square root.

Step 4 — Unit vector in the separation direction

The unit vector is the normalized separation vector: λ̂ = λ/|λ|. Since |λ| = 3 ≠ 0, normalization is valid. 

λ̂ = (2, −2, 1) / 3
   = (2/3, −2/3, 1/3)
Sanity check: does λ̂ really have unit length?

Compute |λ̂|: 

|λ̂|^2 = (2/3)^2 + (−2/3)^2 + (1/3)^2
       = 4/9 + 4/9 + 1/9
       = 9/9
       = 1  ⇒  |λ̂| = 1
Final Answer 
λ = (2, −2, 1)
|λ| = 3
λ̂ = (2/3, −2/3, 1/3)

Sanity checks (summary)

  • Direction signs: (+, −, +) matches qualitative expectation.
  • Units: λ and |λ| have length units; λ̂ is dimensionless.
  • Normalization: |λ̂| = 1.
  • Physical meaning: The field point is 3 units away from the source along direction (2/3, −2/3, 1/3).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the final formula

The separation vector is always: λ = r − r'. Each component describes how much you must change x, y, z to move from the source to the field point. The magnitude |λ| is the distance, and λ̂ extracts direction only.

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • If the field point moves farther away, |λ| increases (nonlinearly via square root of squared components).
  • If only x changes, only the x-component of λ changes; the direction λ̂ can still change because normalization couples components.
  • If you scale all components of λ by a factor α, then |λ| scales by |α|, but λ̂ stays the same (for α>0).
Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Treat the points as vectors in an inner-product space. The displacement is the unique vector λ satisfying r' + λ = r. Existence/uniqueness follows from vector-space axioms. The magnitude is then the norm induced by the inner product: ||λ|| = sqrt(<λ,λ>), where in Cartesian coordinates <λ,λ> = Σ λ_i^2.

Concept check (with answers)
  • Q: If we swapped the order and used r' − r, what changes? A: The vector and unit vector flip sign (direction reverses), magnitude stays the same.
  • Q: Why is the unit vector dimensionless? A: It’s a ratio of a length vector to its length magnitude, so units cancel.
  • Q: What happens if the two points are identical? A: λ=0, magnitude 0, and the unit vector is undefined.
  • Q: How do you verify a unit vector quickly? A: Check that the sum of squares of components equals 1.
