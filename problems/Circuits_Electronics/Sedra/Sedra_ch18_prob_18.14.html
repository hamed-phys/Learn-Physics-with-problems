Wien-Bridge Oscillator Limiter Design

Amplitude Control Using Diode Limiter Network

Quick Summary

  • The problem concerns amplitude control in a Wien-bridge oscillator.
  • Oscillation frequency is determined by the RC network while amplitude is stabilized using diode limiters.
  • The limiter activates when the diode voltage reaches roughly 0.7 V.
  • The oscillator amplifier gain is approximately 3, so the relation between node voltage and output is v₁ ≈ v₀ / 3.
  • Changing R₃ and R₆ alters the voltage divider that determines when the diodes conduct.
  • The original circuit produced 21.36 V peak-to-peak.
  • To reduce amplitude to 8 V peak-to-peak, the limiter threshold must increase proportionally.
  • The required values are approximately R₃ = R₆ ≈ 8 kΩ.
  • If R₃ and R₆ are open-circuited, the limiter engages almost immediately and the oscillator amplitude collapses to about ≈ 2.1 V peak-to-peak.

PART 0 — Concept Primer

Wien Bridge Oscillator

A Wien bridge oscillator is a sinusoidal oscillator that uses a frequency-selective RC network. At the oscillation frequency:
ω₀ = 1 / (RC)
and the network attenuation is
β = 1/3
Therefore the amplifier must provide gain
A = 3
so that the Barkhausen criterion
Aβ = 1
is satisfied. ---

Amplitude Control

If gain is slightly greater than 3, oscillations grow. A limiter is used so that once the signal becomes large enough, the effective gain drops back to exactly 3. In this circuit the limiter consists of:
  • two diodes
  • a voltage divider (R₃,R₄,R₅,R₆)
When the diode voltage reaches about
V_D ≈ 0.7 V
the diode conducts and changes the feedback network. ---

Amplifier Relation

The op-amp gain is approximately
A ≈ 1 + R₂/R₁ ≈ 3
Thus
v₁ ≈ v₀ / 3
This relation is crucial for determining the limiter threshold.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where Wien Oscillators Are Used

Engineers use this circuit in:
  • audio signal generators
  • instrument calibration equipment
  • sensor excitation sources
  • low-distortion test oscillators

Why Amplitude Control Matters

In industry the oscillator must have
  • stable amplitude
  • low distortion
  • temperature stability
Poor amplitude control produces clipping or distortion.

Real-World Amplitude Control Methods

Practical systems may use
  • lamp stabilizers (classic HP oscillators)
  • diode limiters
  • automatic gain control loops

Design Trade-offs

Engineers balance
  • startup reliability
  • distortion level
  • component tolerance
  • temperature drift
The diode limiter used here is inexpensive and simple but slightly increases distortion.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

Given

From Exercise 18.5:
  • Oscillator amplitude: 21.36 V peak-to-peak
  • R₃ = R₆ = 3 kΩ
  • R₄ = R₅ = 1 kΩ
  • Diode drop = 0.7 V

Find

1. New values of R₃ and R₆ that give 8 V peak-to-peak output 2. What happens if R₃ and R₆ are open-circuited.

Key Principle

The limiter activates when
v₀ − v₁ ≈ 0.7 V
Since
v₁ ≈ v₀ / 3
the conduction condition becomes
(2/3)v₀ ≈ 0.7

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

Plan:
  1. Relate amplifier node voltage to output voltage.
  2. Determine diode conduction condition.
  3. Use scaling from the known amplitude (21.36 Vpp).
  4. Adjust R₃ and R₆ to shift the limiter threshold.
  5. Evaluate the open-circuit case.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting that oscillators use peak-to-peak amplitude.
  • Ignoring the amplifier gain relation v₁ ≈ v₀/3.
  • Confusing peak and RMS values.

PART 3 — Full Solution

1. Original Oscillator Amplitude

Given
Vpp₁ = 21.36 V
Peak value:
Vp₁ = 10.68 V
This corresponds to
R₃ = R₆ = 3 kΩ
---

2. Desired Output

Vpp₂ = 8 V
Peak:
Vp₂ = 4 V
---

3. Scaling the Divider

Limiter threshold scales approximately with the divider ratio. Therefore
R₃(new) ≈ R₃(old) × (21.36 / 8)
Thus
R₃(new) ≈ 3kΩ × 2.67 ≈ 8kΩ
Because of symmetry
R₆(new) ≈ 8kΩ
---
Required resistor values

R₃ ≈ 8 kΩ R₆ ≈ 8 kΩ
---

4. If R₃ and R₆ Are Open-Circuited

Then nodes a and b connect directly to the output through R₄ and R₅. The diode conducts when
v₀ − v₁ ≈ 0.7 V
Substitute
v₁ ≈ v₀ / 3
Thus
(2/3)v₀ ≈ 0.7
Solve:
v₀ ≈ 1.05 V (peak)
Therefore
Vpp ≈ 2.1 V

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

What Controls the Amplitude?

The amplitude depends on
  • diode threshold
  • feedback divider
  • amplifier gain
Increasing R₃ and R₆ raises the voltage needed to forward-bias the diode. Thus the oscillator stabilizes at a lower output level. ---

Concept Checks

Q1: Why is amplifier gain ≈3? A: Because the Wien bridge attenuates by 1/3. Q2: Why are two diodes used? A: To limit both positive and negative peaks. Q3: What happens if gain is less than 3? A: Oscillations decay to zero. Q4: Why does increasing R₃ reduce amplitude? A: It raises the limiter threshold.
