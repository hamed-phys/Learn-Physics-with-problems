Zener nonlinear circuits
Transfer characteristics

Transfer Characteristics vo–vi for the Circuits of Figure P18.10

We are asked to sketch and label the output–input characteristics of two ideal-op-amp circuits containing oppositely oriented zener diodes. The central idea is that the op-amp tries to enforce a virtual short at its inputs, while the zener pair creates piecewise-constant voltage constraints in the feedback path. The result is an asymmetric limiter: circuit (a) behaves like a two-level switching characteristic, and circuit (b) behaves like an inverting amplifier whose linear region is clipped at unequal positive and negative levels.

Quick Summary

  • This problem is about finding the static transfer characteristic vo(vi) of two op-amp circuits with zener-diode feedback.
  • The key physics idea is piecewise conduction: depending on current direction, one zener is forward biased (about 0.7 V) while the other is in reverse breakdown (its zener voltage).
  • For an ideal op-amp operating with feedback, the inverting input is held at virtual ground, so v- = v+ = 0.
  • The governing tools are KCL at the inverting node and the piecewise zener branch voltage relation.
  • In circuit (a), there is no resistor in the feedback path, so the output jumps between two constant levels depending on the sign of vi + VR.
  • In circuit (b), the resistor R2 gives an ordinary inverting-amplifier region vo = -(R2/R1)vi until the zeners conduct and clip the output.
  • The final result is symbolic: the clipping levels are set by VZ1 + 0.7 and VZ2 + 0.7.
  • Physically, the transfer curves are examples of analog limiting, thresholding, and protection behavior, which are widely used in instrumentation and signal conditioning.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions

  • vi: input voltage (V).
  • vo: output voltage (V).
  • v-, v+: op-amp input voltages (V).
  • R, R1, R2: resistances (Ω).
  • VR: DC reference source in circuit (a) (V).
  • VZ1, VZ2: zener breakdown voltages of zeners Z1 and Z2 (V).
  • 0.7 V: approximate forward drop of a forward-biased diode/zener.

Physical meaning

  • The op-amp provides very large gain, so with feedback it drives its output until the input difference is essentially zero.
  • The zener pair provides a direction-dependent fixed voltage drop.
  • Thus the feedback branch is not linear: it behaves differently for current flowing left-to-right versus right-to-left.
  • The transfer characteristic is therefore piecewise linear or piecewise constant.

Key laws, principles, and validity conditions

  • Ideal op-amp assumptions: infinite open-loop gain, infinite input resistance, zero input current, zero differential input voltage in closed-loop operation.
  • KCL: the algebraic sum of currents at a node is zero.
  • Zener approximation:
    • forward conduction drop ≈ 0.7 V,
    • reverse breakdown drop = zener voltage.
  • We assume the op-amp can supply whatever output voltage/current is needed; no saturation limits are given.

Mathematical model

This is a lumped, static circuit model, not a field-distribution problem. So we use algebraic equations rather than PDEs. The variables are node voltages and branch currents. The domain is the set of admissible input voltages vi ∈ ℝ. Since the question asks for the transfer characteristic, we are solving a piecewise algebraic boundary-value problem in the circuit sense.

KCL at the inverting node: i_1 + i_2 + i_f = 0 Ideal op-amp with negative feedback: v_- = v_+ = 0 Piecewise zener-pair branch law: v_left - v_right = +(V_Z1 + 0.7) for current left → right -(V_Z2 + 0.7) for current right → left The exact zener label appearing in each branch depends on the diode orientation shown in the figure; here the figure implies that left→right conduction uses Z1 in breakdown, while right→left conduction uses Z2 in breakdown.

Math Insight

The core equation here is a piecewise-linear constitutive relation. Mathematically, the circuit is a nonlinear algebraic system whose solution changes branch whenever a conduction threshold is crossed. This is a classic example of a switching manifold in nonlinear systems: one set of equations applies in one region of input space, and another set applies in a different region.

Common models and approximations

  • Constant-drop diode model: used because it gives closed-form transfer curves without solving exponential diode equations.
  • Ideal op-amp model: used to isolate the nonlinear feedback effect of the zeners.
  • Static transfer analysis: capacitive and dynamic effects are neglected because the question asks for the DC/slowly varying characteristic.

Mini intuition examples

  • If a normal inverting amplifier tries to produce too large a positive output, the zener branch may turn on and pin the output at a positive clamp level.
  • If the zener voltages are unequal, the positive and negative clip levels are unequal, so the transfer curve becomes asymmetric.
  • If no resistor feedback exists, the output tends to jump directly between clamp levels rather than varying linearly.

What to watch for

  • Do not assume both zeners break down at once. In each conduction direction, one is forward biased and the other is in reverse breakdown.
  • Do not forget the 0.7 V forward drop.
  • Keep the sign convention consistent when deciding whether the output is clamped positive or negative.
  • In circuit (b), the zeners are parallel to R2, not a replacement for R2. The linear region still exists.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry

Signal conditioning

Clippers and threshold circuits protect ADC inputs, shape waveforms, and prevent overload in measurement chains.

Instrumentation & sensors

Reference-shifted thresholding is used in comparators, alarm triggers, sensor-limit detection, and calibration interfaces.

Power & industrial electronics

Overvoltage limiting and asymmetric clamping are used in gate-drive protection, transient suppression, and control-board front ends.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: the clipping level determines usable signal range.
  • Safety and reliability: limiting protects later stages from destructive overvoltage.
  • Cost: simple zener-op-amp limiters are cheap and robust.
  • Manufacturability: piecewise designs can be easier to test than full nonlinear analog shaping networks.

Typical measurement and validation

  • Apply a slow input ramp and measure vo versus vi on an oscilloscope in X–Y mode.
  • Use a source-measure unit or function generator plus DAQ to map the transfer curve.
  • Check the clipping levels against zener tolerances and op-amp output swing limits.

Design trade-offs and non-idealities

  • Zener voltages have tolerances, so actual clip points vary.
  • The “0.7 V” forward drop changes with current and temperature.
  • Real op-amps have output current limits, finite gain, offset voltage, and slew rate.
  • The sharpness of the transfer transition depends on dynamic resistance and source resistance.

From lab to product

In a real product, the idealized piecewise model is refined by adding op-amp output-swing limits, zener dynamic resistance, temperature coefficients, and worst-case tolerance analysis. Industrial engineers care because those non-idealities affect pass/fail yield, calibration margins, and field reliability.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Restating the problem

We must determine and sketch the static transfer characteristics vo versus vi for the two circuits in Fig. P18.10. The op-amps are ideal. Each zener pair is oppositely oriented, and each conduction direction produces one forward drop of about 0.7 V plus one zener breakdown voltage.

Given quantities

  • Circuit (a): two equal resistors R, input vi, reference VR, and zeners Z1, Z2.
  • Circuit (b): R1, R2, input vi, and the same zener pair in parallel with R2.
  • Forward drop ≈ 0.7 V.
  • Zener breakdown voltages: VZ1, VZ2.

Unknowns

  • Piecewise formulas for vo(vi).
  • The breakpoints where the transfer characteristic changes branch.
  • A clear sketch interpretation: levels, slope, and transition points.

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • KCL applies because these are lumped node circuits.
  • Virtual ground applies whenever the op-amp has an active feedback path and is not otherwise constrained by saturation.
  • Zener piecewise conduction applies because the diode model is explicitly given as constant forward drop plus fixed breakdown voltage.

We do not need Maxwell’s equations in distributed form, wave equations, or transient ODEs, because the task is purely a static circuit transfer sketch.

Assumptions

  • Ideal op-amps.
  • Idealized zener model with sharp transitions.
  • No op-amp output saturation limits.
  • Steady-state/DC transfer only.
  • Node voltage at the noninverting input is grounded in both circuits.

Possible approaches

Approach Idea Pros Cons
Piecewise circuit-state analysis Assume one conduction state, solve, then check consistency. Direct, systematic, physically transparent. Requires careful sign handling.
Graphical load-line style reasoning Sketch the zener branch constraints and op-amp behavior qualitatively. Good intuition. Less precise for symbolic breakpoints.
Equivalent limiter viewpoint Treat zener pair as an asymmetric clamp in the feedback path. Fastest for final form. Can hide the derivation details.

The best method is piecewise circuit-state analysis, because it gives both the exact symbolic result and the correct switching points without guesswork.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

Step 1 — Identify the controlled node.
Goal: focus on the inverting-input node.
Tool: ideal op-amp virtual-short condition.
Meaning: if feedback is active, that node sits at approximately 0 V.
Step 2 — Write KCL at the inverting node.
Goal: determine the required feedback current.
Tool: algebraic current sum.
Meaning: the sign of this current tells us which zener conduction direction must occur.
Step 3 — Map conduction direction to branch voltage.
Goal: convert current direction into a fixed output clamp level.
Tool: zener + forward-diode model.
Meaning: this gives the actual output value during conduction.
Step 4 — For circuit (b), solve the “zener-off” region first.
Goal: get the central linear transfer law.
Tool: standard inverting amplifier relation.
Meaning: this is the part of the characteristic before clipping starts.
Step 5 — Find the input values where clipping begins.
Goal: compute breakpoints.
Tool: set the linear output equal to the zener clamp voltages.
Meaning: these are the corners of the piecewise graph.
Step 6 — Assemble the piecewise transfer law.
Goal: complete the sketch.
Tool: region matching and continuity check where appropriate.
Meaning: circuit (a) gives a jump; circuit (b) gives a clipped line.

Common mistakes

  • Using VZ1 and VZ2 with the wrong polarity branch.
  • Forgetting that circuit (a) switches at vi = -VR.
  • Forgetting that circuit (b) is linear in the middle region.
  • Missing the negative sign of the inverting amplifier slope.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition first

Before calculating, we expect circuit (a) to behave like a thresholded two-level output because there is no ordinary resistor feedback path: the zeners either conduct in one direction or the other, forcing the output to one of two nearly fixed voltages. In contrast, circuit (b) contains a normal feedback resistor R2, so it should behave like an inverting amplifier for moderate inputs and clip only when the output tries to exceed the zener-imposed limits.

Circuit (a)

Let the inverting-input node voltage be vx. Since the noninverting input is grounded and the op-amp uses feedback through the zener path, the ideal-op-amp condition gives

v_x = v_- = v_+ = 0

Now write KCL at the inverting node. Define if as the feedback current flowing from the node toward the output through the zener pair.

( v_x - v_i )/R + ( v_x - V_R )/R + i_f = 0

Since vx = 0,

(-v_i)/R + (-V_R)/R + i_f = 0 therefore i_f = (v_i + V_R)/R

This equation is crucial: it tells us the required direction of feedback current.

  • If vi + VR > 0, then if > 0, so current must flow from left to right through the zener pair.
  • If vi + VR < 0, then if < 0, so current must flow from right to left.

From the diode orientation shown, left-to-right conduction gives one forward drop and zener Z1 in reverse breakdown:

v_x - v_o = V_{Z1} + 0.7

Since vx = 0,

v_o = -(V_{Z1} + 0.7)

Similarly, for right-to-left conduction,

v_o - v_x = V_{Z2} + 0.7

and therefore

v_o = +(V_{Z2} + 0.7)

Combining these branches,

Circuit (a): v_o(v_i) = +(V_Z2 + 0.7), for v_i < -V_R discontinuous transition at v_i = -V_R -(V_Z1 + 0.7), for v_i > -V_R

At vi = -VR, the required feedback current is zero. In the idealized sharp-switch model, the characteristic is drawn as a vertical jump there.

Interpretation of circuit (a)

This is an asymmetric two-level threshold circuit. The threshold is shifted from zero to -VR by the reference source, while the two output levels are set by the two possible zener conduction combinations.

Circuit (b)

Here the zener pair is in parallel with the feedback resistor R2. So there are two natural regions:

  1. zener branch off, giving an ordinary inverting amplifier,
  2. zener branch on, giving output clipping.

Region 1: zeners off

With the zeners not conducting, the circuit is just an inverting amplifier. Let the inverting node again be vx = 0. KCL gives

(0 - v_i)/R_1 + (0 - v_o)/R_2 = 0

so

v_o = - (R_2 / R_1) v_i

This linear relation remains valid only as long as the zener branch voltage is below conduction threshold. Because the left zener terminal is at vx = 0, the zener pair sees the same voltage as the output, up to sign.

Positive-output clipping condition

If the linear law demands a sufficiently positive output, the current direction through the zener pair becomes right-to-left, and the output is clamped at

v_o = +(V_{Z2} + 0.7)

The transition occurs when the linear output first reaches this value:

-(R_2/R_1) v_i = V_{Z2} + 0.7

Solving for the input breakpoint,

v_i = - (R_1/R_2)(V_{Z2} + 0.7)

Negative-output clipping condition

If the linear law demands a sufficiently negative output, the zener current direction becomes left-to-right, and the output is clamped at

v_o = -(V_{Z1} + 0.7)

The transition occurs when

-(R_2/R_1) v_i = -(V_{Z1} + 0.7)

giving

v_i = + (R_1/R_2)(V_{Z1} + 0.7)

Therefore the full transfer characteristic is

Circuit (b): v_o(v_i) = +(V_Z2 + 0.7), for v_i ≤ -(R_1/R_2)(V_Z2 + 0.7) -(R_2/R_1) v_i, for -(R_1/R_2)(V_Z2 + 0.7) ≤ v_i ≤ +(R_1/R_2)(V_Z1 + 0.7) -(V_Z1 + 0.7), for v_i ≥ +(R_1/R_2)(V_Z1 + 0.7)

Final answer (boxed)

Circuit (a):

v_o = +(V_Z2 + 0.7), for v_i < -V_R -(V_Z1 + 0.7), for v_i > -V_R

The sketch is a vertical jump at vi = -VR, from the upper level +(VZ2+0.7) to the lower level -(VZ1+0.7).

Circuit (b):

v_o = +(V_Z2 + 0.7), for v_i ≤ -(R_1/R_2)(V_Z2 + 0.7) -(R_2/R_1) v_i, for -(R_1/R_2)(V_Z2 + 0.7) ≤ v_i ≤ +(R_1/R_2)(V_Z1 + 0.7) -(V_Z1 + 0.7), for v_i ≥ +(R_1/R_2)(V_Z1 + 0.7)

The sketch is an inverting straight line of slope -R2/R1 clipped at the asymmetric levels +(VZ2+0.7) and -(VZ1+0.7).

Sanity checks

Units/dimensions

Every term in the final expressions has units of volts. Ratios such as R2/R1 are dimensionless, so the linear middle branch in circuit (b) is dimensionally correct.

Limiting cases

  • If VZ1 = VZ2, the clip levels are symmetric about zero.
  • If R2/R1 increases, the central linear region in circuit (b) becomes steeper and narrower in input voltage.
  • If VR = 0 in circuit (a), the switching threshold moves to the origin.

Why each mathematical step is valid

  • KCL is valid because the inverting node is a lumped node with no charge accumulation in DC steady state.
  • v- = v+ is valid in the ideal-op-amp closed-loop limit.
  • The piecewise branches are valid because the zener model itself is piecewise.
  • No nondimensionalization is needed here because the governing equations are algebraic, not differential; still, the natural dimensionless gain parameter is R2/R1.
Deeper note: why there is no PDE, Laplacian, or time derivative here

The problem is purely a static nonlinear circuit problem, so the state is determined by algebraic current-balance equations. Operators such as ∇, ∇·, ∇×, the Laplacian, or time derivatives d/dt and ∂/∂t belong to spatially distributed or time-dependent models. Here the correct mathematical object is a piecewise algebraic map from input voltage to output voltage.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Reinterpreting the final formulas

  • VZ1 + 0.7 sets the magnitude of the negative clamp level in the orientation shown.
  • VZ2 + 0.7 sets the magnitude of the positive clamp level.
  • VR in circuit (a) shifts the switching threshold horizontally.
  • R2/R1 in circuit (b) sets the slope of the linear region and therefore the input width before clipping begins.

Parameter dependence

Parameter If increased Physical meaning
VZ1 More negative lower clip level Allows larger negative output before clamping
VZ2 Higher positive upper clip level Allows larger positive output before clamping
VR Switch point in (a) moves left to -VR Threshold biasing / offset control
R2/R1 Steeper middle slope; narrower input range before clipping Higher small-signal gain

Alternative derivation idea

Another way is to treat the zener pair as an asymmetric ideal limiter element between the inverting node and the output. Then you can first write the generic limiter law and combine it with the standard closed-loop op-amp equations. This is faster, but the KCL-based derivation used above is better pedagogically because it explicitly reveals why the switching condition depends on the sign of the required feedback current.

Concept checks

  • Q: Why is circuit (a) not linear?
    A: Because the feedback path contains only the piecewise zener network, not a resistor that gives continuous proportional feedback.
  • Q: Why does circuit (b) have a linear middle region?
    A: Because when the zeners are off, only R1 and R2 determine the feedback, which is the standard inverting-amplifier case.
  • Q: Why are the clip levels asymmetric?
    A: Because the two zeners can have different breakdown voltages, so positive and negative conduction use different values.
  • Q: What happens if the real op-amp cannot reach the calculated clamp voltage?
    A: Then the real transfer characteristic is limited by op-amp saturation rather than by the zeners.
