LC Oscillator Based on a BJT Positive-Gain Stage and an RLC Feedback Network

We analyze an oscillator built from a two-transistor small-signal amplifier and a frequency-selective feedback path. The main goals are to derive the small-signal model, identify the oscillation frequency, find the start-up condition, and estimate the steady output amplitude once nonlinear switching limits the collector waveform.

Quick Summary

  • This problem is about an oscillator: a circuit that creates a sinusoidal output without an external sinusoidal source.
  • The key physics idea is the Barkhausen viewpoint: oscillation starts when the loop phase is 0° and the loop gain magnitude reaches or exceeds 1 at some frequency.
  • The amplifier here is a positive-gain differential BJT stage; the feedback network is a series R feeding a parallel LC resonator.
  • The governing small-signal relation is A(ω)β(ω) with A = gmRC/2 and β(ω) = ZLC / (R + ZLC).
  • The parallel LC impedance is ZLC = 1 / (jωC + 1/(jωL)), which becomes ideally infinite at resonance.
  • Therefore the oscillation frequency is ω0 = 1/√(LC), or f0 = 1/(2π√(LC)).
  • The minimum start-up condition is gmRC/2 ≥ 1. Using gm = I/(2VT) gives (IRC)min = 4VT.
  • At room temperature, 4VT ≈ 0.10 V, so choosing IRC = 1 V easily ensures start-up.
  • If nonlinear action makes the collector signal a 1 V peak-to-peak square wave, the tuned LC extracts mainly the fundamental, giving approximately Vo,pp ≈ 4/π V ≈ 1.27 V.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Before solving, let us review what an LC oscillator is mathematically and physically, and why a feedback network plus an active gain stage can produce a sustained sinusoid.

Core Definitions

Symbol Meaning SI Unit
L Inductance of the inductor henry (H)
C Capacitance of the capacitor farad (F)
R Series feedback resistor between collector node and output node ohm (Ω)
RC Collector resistor of the transistor amplifier ohm (Ω)
I Tail current source value ampere (A)
gm BJT small-signal transconductance siemens (S)
VT Thermal voltage, approximately 25 mV at room temperature volt (V)
ω Angular frequency rad/s
ω0 Resonant angular frequency rad/s
Vo Output voltage at the LC node volt (V)

Physical Meaning of the Key Quantities

Inductor L

The inductor stores magnetic energy: WL = (1/2)Li². It resists rapid change in current.

Capacitor C

The capacitor stores electric energy: WC = (1/2)Cv². It resists rapid change in voltage.

Transconductance gm

This converts a small base-emitter voltage perturbation into collector current change. It is the gain-producing element of the active stage.

Key Laws and Principles

1) Small-signal linearization: around a DC operating point, a BJT behaves as a transconductance element: ic = gmvbe. This is valid for small incremental variations around bias.
2) Resonance: a parallel LC circuit has admittance YLC(ω) = jωC + 1/(jωL). At resonance, these two terms cancel, so the net admittance is zero and the impedance becomes ideally infinite.
3) Barkhausen viewpoint: a loop can start oscillating when, at some frequency, ∠Aβ = 0° (or 360°) and |Aβ| ≥ 1. This is a practical frequency-domain criterion for linear start-up analysis.

Mathematical Model

This is fundamentally a lumped-parameter dynamical system. Unlike field problems that require PDEs such as ∂u/∂t = D∇²u or Maxwell’s equations with ∇· and ∇×, this circuit reduces to ordinary differential equations because voltages and currents are assumed spatially uniform within each element.

Small-signal transistor relations: i_c1 = g_m (v_b1 - v_e) i_c2 = g_m (v_b2 - v_e) Emitter-node constraint (ideal tail current source ⇒ zero incremental current into source): i_c1 + i_c2 = 0 Parallel LC network: Y_LC(ω) = jωC + 1/(jωL) Z_LC(ω) = 1 / Y_LC(ω) Feedback factor: β(ω) = V_o / V_c = Z_LC / (R + Z_LC) Amplifier gain: A = V_c / V_o = g_m R_C / 2 Resonance condition: Y_LC(ω_0) = 0 ⇒ ω_0 = 1 / √(LC)

Validity Conditions and Approximations

  • Neglecting rπ: base current is ignored, so each transistor input is treated as infinite resistance in the small-signal sense.
  • Neglecting ro: Early effect is ignored, so the collector current source is ideal and independent of collector voltage.
  • Ideal tail current source: its small-signal current is zero, so it acts as an open circuit for AC.
  • Matched transistors: both BJTs are assumed to have the same gm.
  • Linear start-up analysis: valid only while amplitudes are small enough that the transistor relations stay approximately linear.

Mini Intuition Examples

Example 1: Why an LC network can select one frequency

At most frequencies, the inductor and capacitor currents do not cancel. At ω = 1/√(LC), their susceptances cancel exactly, making the parallel network look like a very large impedance. That makes the output node especially responsive at that frequency.

Example 2: Why gain is needed

Real resonators lose energy. The amplifier feeds energy back into the resonator. If the feedback just compensates the losses, oscillation is sustained. If it overcompensates, the amplitude grows until nonlinearities limit it.

What to Watch For

  • Do not confuse the collector node voltage with the output voltage. They are connected through R, not directly identical at all frequencies.
  • At resonance, the parallel LC is an open circuit ideally, not a short circuit.
  • The active stage here has positive small-signal gain from Vo to the collector of Q2.
  • The Barkhausen criterion predicts start-up frequency and gain condition, but not the exact nonlinear steady-state waveform shape.
Math Insight: the core object here is a linear second-order resonant system driven through feedback. In time-domain language, the LC tank behaves like a harmonic oscillator; in frequency-domain language, it produces a pole pair and a resonance. The oscillator starts when active feedback cancels effective damping.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

LC oscillators are not just textbook circuits. They sit at the heart of timing, communication, sensing, and control hardware across modern industry.

Where This Appears in Industry

  • RF communication systems: local oscillators in receivers, transmitters, frequency synthesizers, and mixers.
  • Instrumentation and test equipment: signal generators, spectrum analyzers, lock-in amplifiers, and tuned front-end stages.
  • Industrial sensing and automation: inductive proximity sensors, resonant converters, wireless telemetry links, and clock/reference generation in control electronics.
  • Power electronics and energy systems: resonant inverters, soft-switching converters, and impedance-based diagnostic circuits.

Why Engineers Care

Performance

Frequency accuracy, spectral purity, phase noise, start-up reliability, and amplitude stability strongly affect system performance.

Cost and Manufacturability

Component tolerances, transistor spread, temperature drift, and parasitic elements all shift resonance and change gain margin.

Reliability

Too little gain causes start-up failure; too much gain causes clipping, excess dissipation, and harmonic distortion.

Efficiency

Resonant operation can reduce losses, but only if the active stage and resonator are balanced carefully.

Measurement and Validation in Practice

  • Oscilloscope: to observe waveform amplitude, clipping, and start-up transient.
  • Spectrum analyzer: to measure fundamental frequency and harmonic content.
  • Network analyzer: to characterize resonator impedance and frequency response.
  • Temperature chamber and tolerance testing: to verify behavior across environment and manufacturing spread.

Design Trade-Offs and Constraints

Engineers never get the ideal LC tank drawn in textbooks. Real inductors have series resistance and finite Q; capacitors vary with temperature and voltage; transistor gm depends on bias; and layout parasitics add unintended capacitance and inductance. The design must tolerate all of these without failing to start or becoming too distorted.

From Lab to Product

In a real product, the ideal start-up condition |Aβ| ≥ 1 is not enough by itself. Designers add gain margin for process and temperature, then use amplitude-limiting mechanisms to keep the oscillator from clipping excessively. This problem’s part (c) is exactly that real-world transition: a linear analysis predicts start-up, while nonlinear switching determines the eventual waveform amplitude.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem Restated Clearly

We are given an oscillator made from two matched BJTs and a collector resistor RC, followed by a feedback path composed of a resistor R driving a parallel LC node. We must first replace the transistors with their simplified small-signal models. Then we must find the oscillation frequency and the minimum gain condition for start-up, expressed as the minimum value of IRC. Finally, for the specific choice IRC = 1 V, we must show start-up occurs and estimate the output sine-wave amplitude once nonlinear transistor switching produces a 1 V peak-to-peak square wave at the collector of Q2.

Given Quantities

  • Two BJTs Q1 and Q2, treated as matched.
  • A collector resistor RC.
  • A tail current source of value I.
  • A feedback resistor R.
  • A parallel resonator made of L and C.
  • Neglect rπ and ro.
  • In part (c), IRC = 1 V.

Unknowns / What Must Be Found

  • (a) The simplified small-signal circuit.
  • (b) The oscillation frequency ω0 or f0.
  • (b) The start-up condition, expressed as a minimum required IRC.
  • (c) Whether IRC = 1 V ensures start-up.
  • (c) The approximate peak-to-peak amplitude of the output sine wave Vo.

Relevant Principles and Why They Apply

Principle Why it applies Why alternatives are less central
Small-signal BJT model The problem explicitly asks for a small-signal replacement and tells us to neglect rπ and ro. Large-signal transistor equations are only needed later for the clipping interpretation, not for start-up derivation.
Resonance of parallel LC The feedback element is a frequency-selective resonator, so resonance determines the oscillation frequency. Pure time-domain transient solving is possible, but less direct here.
Barkhausen loop condition Oscillator start-up is most naturally handled via loop gain magnitude and phase. State-space eigenvalue analysis could also work, but it is more algebraic than needed.

Assumptions

Assumptions used throughout:
  • Transistors are matched, with equal gm.
  • rπ and ro are neglected.
  • The current source is ideal, so its incremental current is zero.
  • The start-up analysis is linear and frequency-domain based.
  • At large amplitude, the collector waveform becomes approximately a symmetric square wave because the transistors switch on and off.
  • The LC network has sufficiently high selectivity that the output is dominated by the fundamental component near resonance.

Possible Approaches

Approach 1: Direct small-signal loop gain

Pros: fastest, physically transparent, exactly aligned with the question.
Cons: needs careful sign handling.

Approach 2: Nodal analysis of the full small-signal circuit

Pros: systematic and rigorous.
Cons: more algebra than necessary for the resonance result.

Approach 3: Time-domain ODE/eigenvalue analysis

Pros: mathematically elegant.
Cons: overkill for this circuit and not the quickest route to the requested expressions.

Chosen Approach

We will use Approach 1: derive the amplifier gain by small-signal transistor relations, derive the feedback factor from the R–LC network, and combine them with the Barkhausen condition. This is the cleanest and most instructive route.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Build the small-signal transistor model.
    Tool: BJT transconductance relation ic = gmvbe.
    Physical meaning: converts small input voltage variations into collector current variations.
  2. Apply KCL at the common emitter node.
    Tool: ideal tail current source implies zero incremental current into that branch.
    Physical meaning: the two transistor incremental emitter/collector currents must balance.
  3. Find the voltage gain from output node to collector node.
    Tool: collector current through RC creates collector voltage change.
    Physical meaning: this is the active gain stage.
  4. Find the feedback factor of the R feeding the parallel LC node.
    Tool: voltage divider with frequency-dependent impedance.
    Physical meaning: selects the frequency that is fed back most strongly.
  5. Impose resonance and start-up conditions.
    Tool: YLC(ω0) = 0 and |Aβ| ≥ 1.
    Physical meaning: correct phase and enough gain to replace losses.
  6. Rewrite the start-up condition in terms of IRC.
    Tool: gm = IC/VT with IC = I/2 for each transistor at balance.
    Physical meaning: tail bias current controls how strong the active stage is.
  7. Estimate the steady sinusoidal output in part (c).
    Tool: Fourier fundamental of a square wave.
    Physical meaning: the LC tank suppresses harmonics and keeps mostly the sinusoidal fundamental.
Common mistakes
  • Using gm = I/VT instead of gm = (I/2)/VT for each transistor.
  • Forgetting that the parallel LC has infinite impedance at resonance in the ideal model.
  • Missing the factor of 1/2 in the amplifier gain.
  • Using the total square-wave peak-to-peak value directly as the sine-wave peak value.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical Intuition First

We expect the LC network to choose the oscillation frequency because it is the only strongly frequency-selective part of the loop. We also expect the transistor stage to provide positive voltage gain from the LC node back to the collector node. If the gain around the loop is large enough, any small noise component at the resonant frequency will be amplified repeatedly and grow. Eventually the transistors leave the strictly linear region, which limits amplitude and causes the collector waveform to look square-ish while the resonator keeps the output more sinusoidal.

(a) Replace the BJTs with Small-Signal Models

Because rπ and ro are neglected, each transistor is modeled only by its transconductance source:

i_c = g_m v_be

Let the common emitter node voltage be ve. Let the output node be vo. The base of Q1 is tied to vo, while the base of Q2 is grounded for AC. Thus:

v_b1 = v_o v_b2 = 0

Therefore the small-signal collector currents are

i_c1 = g_m (v_o - v_e) i_c2 = g_m (0 - v_e) = -g_m v_e

Since the tail current source is ideal, its incremental current is zero. Therefore KCL at the common emitter node requires that the incremental transistor currents sum to zero:

i_c1 + i_c2 = 0

Substitute the expressions above:

g_m (v_o - v_e) + (-g_m v_e) = 0

Factor out gm:

g_m (v_o - 2v_e) = 0

Since gm ≠ 0, we get

v_e = v_o / 2

Then the collector current of Q2 becomes

i_c2 = -g_m (v_o / 2)

The collector of Q2 is connected to RC to AC ground (through the supply), so the collector node voltage vc is

v_c = -i_c2 R_C

Substitute ic2:

v_c = -(-g_m v_o / 2) R_C = (g_m R_C / 2) v_o

So the active stage provides the small-signal gain

A = v_c / v_o = g_m R_C / 2
Result of part (a): the transistor pair plus RC behaves like a positive gain block with gain A = gmRC/2. The feedback network is a series R feeding a parallel LC to ground.
Optional deeper note: why this positive gain is not obvious at first glance

A common-emitter stage usually inverts. Here, however, the emitter node is not fixed; it is shared by both transistors and constrained by the ideal tail current source. That emitter-node motion introduces the extra sign behavior. The net result from vo to vc is positive.

(b) Find the Oscillation Frequency and Start-Up Condition

Step 1: Feedback network transfer function

The collector node vc drives the output node vo through resistor R. At the output node, the inductor and capacitor are in parallel to ground. Therefore the parallel impedance is

Z_L = jωL Z_C = 1 / (jωC)

For a parallel combination, it is cleaner to use admittance:

Y_LC(ω) = 1/Z_L + 1/Z_C = 1/(jωL) + jωC

Now simplify:

1/(jωL) = -j/(ωL)

Hence

Y_LC(ω) = jωC - j/(ωL) = j(ωC - 1/(ωL))

Therefore

Z_LC(ω) = 1 / Y_LC(ω) = 1 / { j(ωC - 1/(ωL)) }

The feedback factor from collector node to output node is the divider relation

β(ω) = v_o / v_c = Z_LC / (R + Z_LC)

Step 2: Resonance condition

The phase of the loop must be zero for oscillation. The active gain block A = gmRC/2 is purely real and positive in this simplified model, so the feedback factor must also be real and positive. That happens most naturally when the LC branch is at resonance.

Set the parallel LC admittance to zero:

Y_LC(ω_0) = j(ω_0 C - 1/(ω_0 L)) = 0

So the bracket must vanish:

ω_0 C - 1/(ω_0 L) = 0

Rearrange:

ω_0 C = 1/(ω_0 L)
ω_0^2 = 1/(LC)
ω_0 = 1 / √(LC)

Thus the oscillation frequency is

f_0 = ω_0 / (2π) = 1 / (2π√(LC))

Step 3: Start-up condition

At resonance, YLC(ω0) = 0, so the LC branch has ideally infinite impedance:

Z_LC(ω_0) → ∞

Therefore the divider becomes

β(ω_0) = Z_LC / (R + Z_LC) → 1

So the loop gain at resonance is simply

Aβ|_(ω0) = A = g_m R_C / 2

For oscillations to start, we require

g_m R_C / 2 ≥ 1

or equivalently

g_m R_C ≥ 2

Step 4: Express in terms of I RC

At balanced bias, the tail current I splits equally, so each transistor carries

I_C = I / 2

For a BJT,

g_m = I_C / V_T = (I/2)/V_T = I / (2V_T)

Substitute into the start-up condition:

(I / (2V_T)) R_C / 2 ≥ 1

Simplify carefully:

I R_C / (4V_T) ≥ 1
I R_C ≥ 4V_T

Therefore the minimum required value is

(I R_C)_min = 4V_T

At room temperature, using VT ≈ 25 mV,

(I R_C)_min ≈ 4(25 mV) = 100 mV = 0.10 V

(c) Show Oscillations Start for IRC = 1 V, and Estimate Vo,pp

Step 1: Start-up check

The criterion from part (b) is

I R_C ≥ 4V_T ≈ 0.10 V

Given I RC = 1 V, we have

1 V > 0.10 V

So the loop gain exceeds unity, and oscillations will start.

Step 2: Interpret the nonlinear steady state

As the oscillation grows, the transistors are driven hard enough that the collector of Q2 no longer remains a small-signal sine. The problem tells us it becomes a square wave of 1 V peak-to-peak.

A symmetric square wave with peak value A has Fourier series fundamental component

v_sq(t) = (4A/π) sin(ωt) + higher odd harmonics

Here the square wave has 1 V peak-to-peak, so its peak value is

A = (1 V_pp)/2 = 0.5 V

Therefore the fundamental sine amplitude at the collector is

V_1 = 4A/π = 4(0.5)/π = 2/π V ≈ 0.637 V (peak)

Hence the corresponding peak-to-peak value of that fundamental is

V_1,pp = 2V_1 = 4/π V ≈ 1.27 V_pp

At resonance the LC branch impedance is very large, so the output node follows the resonant-frequency component strongly, while higher harmonics are suppressed because they are away from resonance. Thus the output sine wave is approximately the fundamental component of the collector square wave:

V_o,pp ≈ 4/π V ≈ 1.27 V_pp

Highlighted Final Results

  • (a) Small-signal amplifier gain: A = gmRC/2.
  • (b) Oscillation frequency: ω0 = 1/√(LC), so f0 = 1/(2π√(LC)).
  • (b) Start-up condition: gmRC/2 ≥ 1.
  • (b) In terms of the given bias: (IRC)min = 4VT.
  • (c) With IRC = 1 V: start-up definitely occurs because 1 V > 4VT ≈ 0.10 V.
  • (c) Estimated output amplitude: Vo,pp ≈ 4/π V ≈ 1.27 V.

Sanity Checks

Units / Dimensions

  • ω0 = 1/√(LC) has units 1/√(H·F) = 1/s, correct for angular frequency.
  • gmRC is dimensionless since S·Ω = 1, correct for gain.
  • IRC has units of voltage, matching VT.

Limiting Cases

  • If RC is too small or I is too small, then gmRC/2 < 1 and oscillation cannot start.
  • If the LC were absent, there would be no clean frequency selection and no stable sinusoidal oscillation mechanism.
  • If L or C increases, ω0 decreases, which matches physical intuition for a slower resonator.

Sign / Direction Reasoning

The sign of the gain is positive because increasing vo raises the base voltage of Q1, which shifts the common emitter voltage upward, thereby reducing the current drawn by Q2. That reduced collector current causes the collector voltage of Q2 to increase across RC.

Physical Interpretation in Plain Language

The transistor pair acts like an energy-restoring block and the parallel LC acts like a frequency filter. Together they form a self-excited resonant system. Once the loop gain is above 1 at resonance, tiny random disturbances grow into oscillation. Nonlinear transistor switching prevents unbounded growth and fixes the steady amplitude.

Optional deeper note: a compact state-space viewpoint

One can interpret the resonator through the state variables iL(t) and vo(t). The passive tank alone gives a second-order oscillatory ODE. The transistor amplifier effectively injects negative damping when loop gain is above threshold. In eigenvalue language, start-up occurs when the real part of a complex pole pair crosses from negative to positive.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Reinterpreting the Final Formula

What controls the frequency?

Only L and C determine the ideal oscillation frequency: ω0 = 1/√(LC). The bias current and transistor gain mainly control whether oscillation starts, not where it occurs.

What controls start-up?

The product gmRC controls loop gain. Higher bias current raises gm; larger RC increases voltage gain of the active stage.

Parameter Dependence

Parameter increased Effect on frequency Effect on start-up Physical reason
L Decreases No direct first-order effect in ideal start-up criterion Larger inductance stores energy more slowly, lowering resonance.
C Decreases No direct first-order effect in ideal start-up criterion Larger capacitance slows voltage change, lowering resonance.
I No direct first-order effect Improves start-up Raises gm.
RC No direct first-order effect Improves start-up Raises voltage gain of active stage.
R No change in ideal resonance frequency In the ideal resonance model, no effect because β(ω0) → 1 In real circuits it matters because tank losses and finite Q make loading important.

Nondimensionalization / Scaling Insight

A useful dimensionless loop-gain parameter is

Λ = g_m R_C / 2 = I R_C / (4V_T)

Then start-up is simply

Λ ≥ 1

This is a nice scaling law: it compares the bias-generated gain voltage scale IRC to the transistor thermal voltage scale 4VT. When Λ is much larger than 1, start-up is strong and amplitude grows rapidly until nonlinear limiting takes over.

Alternative Derivation Idea

Instead of using loop gain, one could write nodal equations for the collector node and output node, eliminate internal variables, and demand a nontrivial homogeneous solution. The resulting characteristic equation would show resonance at ω0 and a threshold on the real part of the poles. This is mathematically equivalent to the Barkhausen result but less direct here.

Concept Check Q&A

  • Q: Why does the output become sine-like even if the collector waveform becomes square-like?
    A: Because the LC network strongly favors the resonant-frequency component and suppresses higher harmonics.
  • Q: Why is gm = I/(2VT) rather than I/VT?
    A: The total tail current I splits equally, so each transistor’s quiescent collector current is I/2.
  • Q: What does the resistor R do here?
    A: It couples the collector node to the resonator. In the ideal resonance analysis, β(ω0) → 1, but in real finite-Q circuits it influences loading and amplitude behavior.
  • Q: Why is the oscillation frequency not determined by transistor parameters?
    A: Because frequency selection comes mainly from the resonant condition of the passive LC network.
