Colpitts Oscillator with BJT Bias Network Included

We derive the governing small-signal equation for the transistor Colpitts oscillator shown, then extract the oscillation frequency and the start-up condition on gmRL. The derivation is done from first principles using a linear nodal model in the Laplace domain, with ro neglected and the final form simplified for large rπ.

Model2-node linear small-signal ODE / characteristic equation
Key physicsLC resonance + transistor negative-resistance effect
Main outputsω0, f0, and start-up inequality
Final simplificationLarge rπ → gmRL > C1/C2

Quick Summary

  • This is a Colpitts oscillator: an inductor L resonates with the series-equivalent of C1 and C2, while the transistor supplies energy to overcome losses.
  • The small-signal transistor is modeled by gm, rπ, and ro neglected; the base is AC-grounded.
  • The output coupling capacitor marked ∞ is treated as an AC short, so RL appears directly from the emitter node to ground.
  • The governing characteristic equation is
    L C1C2s3 + L C1G s2 + (C1+C2)s + (G+gm) = 0,   G = 1/RL + 1/rπ.
  • The oscillation frequency comes from setting s = jω and forcing the imaginary part to vanish:
    ω02 = (C1+C2)/(L C1C2) = (1/L)(1/C1 + 1/C2).
  • The start-up threshold is
    gm = G(C1/C2) = (1/RL + 1/rπ)(C1/C2).
    To ensure oscillations start, the practical requirement is strictly greater than this threshold.
  • For large rπ, the result simplifies to
    gmRL > C1/C2.

Physical picture of a Colpitts oscillator

A Colpitts oscillator is an energy-exchange system. The inductor and capacitors form a resonant tank. Left alone, a real tank loses energy because any conductance or resistance damps it. The active device (here the BJT) injects energy with the proper phase so that over one cycle the loss is compensated.

In mathematical language, the passive tank alone has poles in the left half-plane. The transistor shifts the effective damping toward zero; at the start-up threshold the system has purely imaginary roots ±jω0; beyond threshold, the linear model predicts growth, which in real hardware is later limited by nonlinearity.

Math Insight
The core equation here is a linear characteristic equation for a dynamical system. It is the circuit analogue of a damped harmonic oscillator with active feedback. The resonance frequency comes from the imaginary part; the gain condition comes from the real part, which controls damping versus antidamping.

Key laws and validity conditions

  • Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL): used at each node. Valid because the circuit is lumped and small enough that wave effects can be ignored.
  • Linear small-signal transistor model: valid around the DC bias point for sufficiently small AC amplitude during start-up analysis.
  • Laplace-domain admittances: YC = sC, YL = 1/(sL). Valid for linear time-invariant lumped elements.
  • Neglect of ro: means the collector-emitter output resistance is assumed large, so collector current is governed mainly by gmvπ.

Common approximations used here

Approximation 1: Large coupling capacitor

The capacitor marked ∞ is treated as an AC short. Mathematically, its impedance is approximated as zero at the oscillation frequency, so RL is directly connected from emitter node to ground in the AC model.

Approximation 2: Large rπ

Only the final expression is simplified using large rπ. Mathematically this means 1/rπ ≈ 0, so the emitter node loses less energy into the transistor input.

Mini intuition examples

  1. If gm=0, the network is purely passive, so any oscillation must decay.
  2. If RL becomes smaller, damping increases, so a larger gm is needed to start oscillation.
  3. If C1/C2 becomes larger, the transistor must provide more effective loop gain, which appears directly in the final threshold condition.
What to watch for
  • Do not forget that the base is AC ground, so vπ = vb - ve = -ve.
  • Do not place RL at the collector; in this problem it is effectively attached to the emitter node for AC.
  • Do not use the textbook resonant formula blindly until you verify the exact connection of the tank elements.
  • The condition from the characteristic equation is the threshold; for start-up you need a little margin above it.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this matters in real engineering

  • RF signal generation: local oscillators in radios, mixers, frequency synthesizers, and sensor front-ends.
  • Industrial instrumentation: resonant sensing circuits, inductive proximity systems, and analog excitation sources.
  • Embedded hardware: on-board clock generation, test-tone generation, and narrowband analog subsystems.
  • Communications hardware: voltage-controlled oscillator cores often build on the same resonance-and-negative-resistance idea.

Why engineers care

Performance

Frequency accuracy, phase noise, start-up margin, output swing, and power consumption all depend on the tank and active-device design.

Reliability and manufacturability

Component tolerances, transistor spread, temperature drift, parasitics, and PCB layout strongly affect whether oscillation starts reliably in every manufactured unit.

Measurement and validation in practice

  • Spectrum analyzer: checks oscillation frequency and harmonic content.
  • Oscilloscope: checks start-up, amplitude build-up, clipping, and waveform purity.
  • Network or impedance analysis: estimates tank resonance and Q.
  • Monte Carlo simulation and corner analysis: verifies start-up across tolerances and temperature.

Design trade-offs and real constraints

  • Higher gm helps start-up but increases current consumption and can worsen distortion.
  • Larger tank Q improves spectral purity but may make start-up slower.
  • Capacitor ratio C1/C2 sets feedback level, but extreme ratios can increase sensitivity to parasitics.
  • Real inductors have series resistance and parasitic capacitance, shifting the true frequency away from the ideal symbolic result.
From lab to product

The ideal derivation gives a clean start-up threshold and frequency. In a product, engineers add transistor output resistance, inductor loss, package capacitances, bias-network loading, temperature drift, and amplitude limiting. The symbolic result remains valuable: it tells which knobs matter first, and it gives the correct scaling law before higher-order details are added.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

Restating the problem

We are given a transistor Colpitts oscillator in which the base is AC grounded and the bias circuit is included. We must derive a mathematical equation governing small-signal operation, then determine:

  1. the oscillation frequency, and
  2. the condition that gmRL must satisfy for oscillations to start.

We are instructed to neglect ro and simplify the final result by assuming rπ is large.

Given

  • Inductor L
  • Capacitors C1 and C2
  • Load resistance RL
  • Transistor small-signal parameters gm, rπ
  • ro neglected
  • Base AC grounded
  • Large coupling capacitor → AC short

Unknowns

  • Governing equation / characteristic equation
  • Oscillation angular frequency ω0 or frequency f0
  • Start-up condition involving gmRL

Relevant principles

  • Small-signal transistor analysis applies because start-up is analyzed around the bias point.
  • KCL is ideal because the circuit is naturally described by two dynamic node voltages.
  • Laplace-domain algebra is preferred because energy storage elements become simple admittances.
  • Barkhausen language is conceptually useful, but the nodal determinant method is more direct and less error-prone here.
Assumptions
  • Linear small-signal regime.
  • Lumped-element model.
  • Base is AC grounded.
  • The bias current source has very large small-signal resistance, so it is open for AC.
  • The capacitor labeled ∞ is an AC short.
  • ro is neglected.

Possible approaches

ApproachIdeaProsCons
Nodal admittance matrix Write KCL at collector and emitter, then set determinant to zero Systematic, compact, gives governing equation directly Requires careful source-sign handling
Barkhausen loop gain Break loop and derive phase and magnitude conditions Good physical intuition Harder with this exact bias/loading configuration
Equivalent negative resistance Reflect transistor action into tank damping Useful design interpretation Less direct for exact symbolic derivation

Best choice: the nodal-admittance method. It gives the characteristic equation cleanly and separates the frequency condition from the gain condition with minimal ambiguity.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

Roadmap

  1. Define the AC nodes: collector voltage vc and emitter voltage ve.
  2. Write element admittances: YL=1/(sL), Y1=sC1, Y2=sC2, GL=1/RL, gπ=1/rπ.
  3. Use the transistor relation: because base is grounded, vπ=-ve, so collector current source contribution is gmvπ=-gmve.
  4. Write KCL at both nodes to obtain two linear algebraic equations.
  5. Set the determinant to zero for a nontrivial solution. This is the governing characteristic equation.
  6. Set s = jω and separate real and imaginary parts.
  7. From the imaginary part, obtain the resonance frequency.
  8. From the real part, obtain the threshold gain condition.
  9. Finally set large rπ to simplify the start-up inequality to the requested form.
Common mistakes
  • Using vπ = ve instead of vπ = -ve.
  • Forgetting the emitter node also sees the conductance 1/rπ.
  • Treating the current source bias branch as a short; for small signal it is effectively open.
  • Confusing the equality threshold with the actual start-up requirement; start-up needs a small excess above threshold.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Physical intuition before calculating

We expect two kinds of conditions:

  • a frequency condition set mainly by the passive LC network, and
  • a gain condition telling us when the transistor exactly cancels damping.

Because the Colpitts tank uses two capacitors, the effective tank capacitance should be the series combination Ceq = C1C2/(C1+C2), so we already expect ω02 = 1/(LCeq) = (C1+C2)/(LC1C2). The derivation below confirms this exactly.

Step 1 — Mathematical model

Let vc(t) be the collector-node voltage and ve(t) the emitter-node voltage, both measured relative to ground. The system is a lumped linear time-invariant network in the start-up regime. In the Laplace domain we write Vc(s) and Ve(s).

Define the admittances

YL = 1/(sL),   Y1 = sC1,   Y2 = sC2,   GL = 1/RL,   gπ = 1/rπ.

Since the base is AC grounded,

vπ = vb - ve = -ve.

Therefore the transistor collector current source is

ic = gmvπ = -gmve.

This sign is crucial: the controlled source links the emitter voltage to the collector current with opposite sign because the base is grounded.

Step 2 — KCL at the collector node

Sum currents leaving the collector node. There are three contributions:

  1. through the inductor to ground: YLVc,
  2. through C1 to the emitter node: Y1(Vc-Ve),
  3. through the controlled source from collector to emitter: -gmVe.

Hence

YLVc + Y1(Vc-Ve) - gmVe = 0.

Rearranging,

(YL+Y1)Vc - (Y1+gm)Ve = 0.

Step 3 — KCL at the emitter node

Sum currents leaving the emitter node. There are four contributions:

  1. through C2 to ground: Y2Ve,
  2. through RL to ground: GLVe,
  3. through rπ to ground: gπVe,
  4. through C1 to collector: Y1(Ve-Vc).

The controlled source also carries current from emitter to collector of amount +gmVe when counted as leaving the emitter node. Therefore,

(Y2+GL+gπ)Ve + Y1(Ve-Vc) + gmVe = 0.

Rearranging,

-Y1Vc + (Y1+Y2+GL+gπ+gm)Ve = 0.

Step 4 — Matrix form and nontrivial-solution condition

The two equations can be written as

[ [YL+Y1, -(Y1+gm)] ; [-Y1, Y1+Y2+GL+gπ+gm] ] [Vc, Ve]T = 0.

A nonzero solution exists only when the determinant vanishes:

(YL+Y1)(Y1+Y2+GL+gπ+gm) - Y1(Y1+gm) = 0.

Define G = GL + gπ = 1/RL + 1/rπ. Substituting the admittances gives

(1/(sL) + sC1)(sC1 + sC2 + G + gm) - sC1(sC1 + gm) = 0.

Now expand it carefully. First multiply out:

(1/(sL))(sC1 + sC2 + G + gm) + sC1(sC1 + sC2 + G + gm) - sC1(sC1 + gm) = 0.

The last two groups simplify because the s2C12 terms cancel and the sC1gm terms cancel:

(C1+C2)/L + (G+gm)/(sL) + s2C1C2 + sC1G = 0.

Multiply through by Ls to get a polynomial in s:

L C1C2s3 + L C1G s2 + (C1+C2)s + (G+gm) = 0.
Key equation

This is the requested governing equation for circuit operation in the linear small-signal regime.

Step 5 — Oscillation condition: set s = jω

For sustained sinusoidal oscillation at threshold, the roots lie on the imaginary axis. So set

s = jω.

Then s2 = -ω2 and s3 = -jω3. Substitute into the characteristic equation:

-jL C1C2ω3 - L C1G ω2 + j(C1+C2)ω + (G+gm) = 0.

Separate real and imaginary parts, because a complex number is zero only if both parts vanish.

Step 6 — Imaginary part gives the oscillation frequency

-L C1C2ω3 + (C1+C2)ω = 0.

Factor out ω:

ω [ (C1+C2) - L C1C2ω2 ] = 0.

The nontrivial oscillatory solution has ω ≠ 0, so

ω02 = (C1+C2)/(L C1C2).

Since 1/Ceq = 1/C1 + 1/C2, where Ceq = C1C2/(C1+C2), this may be written as

ω0 = 1/√(L Ceq) = √[(1/L)(1/C1 + 1/C2)].

Therefore the frequency in hertz is

f0 = (1/(2π)) √[(C1+C2)/(L C1C2)].

Step 7 — Real part gives the start-up threshold

-L C1Gω2 + (G+gm) = 0.

So

G + gm = L C1Gω2.

Insert the frequency expression found above:

G + gm = L C1G · (C1+C2)/(L C1C2).

Cancel L and C1:

G + gm = G(C1+C2)/C2.

Subtract G from both sides:

gm = G(C1/C2).

Recalling that G = 1/RL + 1/rπ, the threshold condition is

gm = (1/RL + 1/rπ)(C1/C2).

This equality corresponds to the borderline of sustained oscillation in the linear model. To ensure oscillations start from noise or a disturbance, we need slightly more gain:

gm > (1/RL + 1/rπ)(C1/C2).

Step 8 — Simplification for large rπ

If rπ is large, then 1/rπ ≈ 0. Thus G ≈ 1/RL, and the start-up condition becomes

gm > (1/RL)(C1/C2).

Multiply both sides by RL:

gmRL > C1/C2.

Dimensional check

  • Frequency: (C1+C2)/(L C1C2) has units F/(H F2) = 1/(H F) = s−2, so its square root is s−1. Correct.
  • Gain condition: gmRL is dimensionless, and C1/C2 is dimensionless. Correct.

Limiting-case checks

  • If C2 becomes small, ω0 increases and the threshold gmRL increases. This makes physical sense because the divider becomes harder to drive.
  • If RL decreases, damping increases, so the required gm increases. Again physically correct.
  • If gm=0, the real-part condition cannot be met for positive G; the circuit cannot self-oscillate. Correct.
Final Answer
Governing equation:
L C1C2s3 + L C1(1/RL + 1/rπ)s2 + (C1+C2)s + (gm + 1/RL + 1/rπ) = 0.
Oscillation frequency:
ω0 = √[(C1+C2)/(L C1C2)],    f0 = (1/(2π))√[(C1+C2)/(L C1C2)].
Start-up threshold:
gm = (1/RL + 1/rπ)(C1/C2).
Therefore, to ensure oscillations start:
gm > (1/RL + 1/rπ)(C1/C2).
For large rπ:
gmRL > C1/C2.
Optional deeper note: why the characteristic equation is cubic

The passive tank itself is second order because it contains one inductor and an effective capacitance. The cubic form appears because the emitter node is not a simple static divider node: the transistor and loading create an additional first-order dynamical coupling in the nodal description. At the oscillation threshold, one root pair is purely imaginary and the remaining root lies on the negative real axis for a physically reasonable design.

Optional deeper note: nondimensionalization idea

A useful scaling is to define the natural tank frequency ωT = 1/√(LCeq) with Ceq = C1C2/(C1+C2). Let s = ωTσ. Then the cubic can be written in dimensionless form, where the only control parameters are ratios such as C1/C2, gmRL, and RL/rπ. This reveals immediately that the start-up law must be a relation among dimensionless ratios, which is exactly what we found.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

What each term in the final formulas controls

  • L: increasing L lowers the oscillation frequency.
  • C1, C2: together they set the effective series capacitance and therefore the resonance frequency.
  • C1/C2: this ratio controls the feedback requirement; larger ratio means more required transconductance.
  • RL: a smaller load resistance means heavier damping and a more difficult start-up condition.
  • rπ: finite rπ adds extra loading; large rπ makes the transistor input less dissipative.
  • gm: supplies the active energy needed to compensate losses.

Alternative derivation idea

A meaningful alternative is a loop-gain derivation. One can treat the capacitive divider as a feedback network and the transistor as a gain block, then impose zero total phase shift and unity loop magnitude. That route gives the same resonance relation and the same threshold dependence on C1/C2, but the exact loading by RL and rπ is typically more cumbersome than with nodal analysis.

Concept checks

  • Q: Why does the frequency depend on both C1 and C2?
    A: Because the inductor resonates with their series-equivalent capacitance, not with either capacitor alone.
  • Q: Why does a larger RL help start-up?
    A: Because it reduces conductance to ground, which reduces damping.
  • Q: Why is the start-up inequality strict rather than equality?
    A: Equality is the neutral threshold. To grow from noise, the circuit needs net positive small-signal loop gain.
  • Q: Why is ro neglected?
    A: To simplify the model by removing collector-output loading. This is often a good first-order approximation if ro is large.
Industrial engineering lens

From a design-for-manufacture viewpoint, the most useful part of the result is the scaling law gmRL > C1/C2. It tells a designer which tolerances matter most. If capacitor matching drifts, the required transistor margin also drifts. That directly impacts production yield, startup robustness, and test escape risk.
