Finding the Required Amplifier Phase Shift and Minimum Gain for Oscillation

We are given a sinusoidal oscillator whose feedback network has a 12 dB loss and a 180° phase shift at the desired angular frequency ω0. The task is to determine the amplifier phase shift and the minimum amplifier gain needed for oscillation to start.

Quick Summary

  • This problem is about the start-up condition of a sinusoidal oscillator built from an amplifier and a frequency-selective feedback network.
  • The key physics idea is the Barkhausen condition: at the oscillation frequency, the loop must have net phase shift 0° (or 360°) and loop-gain magnitude at least 1 for start-up.
  • The governing equation is the loop gain L(jω)=A(jω)β(jω).
  • A loss of 12 dB in the feedback network means |β| = 10-12/20 ≈ 0.251.
  • Since the network phase shift is 180°, the amplifier must also contribute 180° so the total loop phase is 360°.
  • The minimum amplifier gain is |A|min = 1/|β| ≈ 3.98.
  • So the amplifier needs about 4 V/V, or equivalently +12 dB.
  • This is a numeric answer with a clear physical meaning: the amplifier must exactly cancel both the attenuation and the phase inversion of the feedback network.

PART 0 — Concept Primer

Core definitions

Symbol Meaning Units
ω Angular frequency rad/s
ω0 Oscillation angular frequency rad/s
A(jω) Amplifier transfer function dimensionless
β(jω) Feedback-network transfer factor dimensionless
L(jω)=A(jω)β(jω) Loop gain dimensionless
dB Logarithmic gain/loss measure: 20 log10(amplitude ratio) dimensionless logarithmic unit

Physical meaning of the key quantities

A sinusoidal oscillator is a self-sustaining feedback system. A tiny disturbance or noise signal goes around the loop. If, after one round trip, the signal returns with the right phase and enough amplitude, it reinforces itself and grows.

The amplifier A supplies energy from the DC power source. The frequency-selective network β selects the preferred oscillation frequency.

Key laws and validity conditions

Barkhausen criterion:
At the oscillation frequency, |A(jω0)β(jω0)| ≥ 1 for start-up, and ∠A(jω0) + ∠β(jω0) = 360°n.

This is a linear small-signal start-up model. It applies before nonlinear amplitude limiting becomes important.

Common models and approximations

  • Phasor model: represent sinusoidal signals by complex amplitudes.
  • Lumped-circuit approximation: no spatial fields are solved explicitly.
  • Linear start-up approximation: neglect saturation and amplitude control during the initial analysis.

Mini intuition examples

Too little loop gain

If |Aβ| < 1, the signal shrinks each round trip.

Wrong loop phase

If the total phase is not 0° modulo 360°, feedback is not reinforcing.

Correct condition

If phase and magnitude conditions are met, oscillation can build from a small disturbance.

What to watch for

  • Do not treat 12 dB as a linear factor of 12.
  • Use 20 log10 for amplitude ratios, not 10 log10.
  • The phase condition applies to the whole loop.
  • 360° is equivalent to 0° in a feedback loop.
Math Insight: The oscillator condition can be written as a self-consistency relation x = Aβx. Rearranging gives (1 - Aβ)x = 0. A nonzero solution requires Aβ = 1 in the ideal threshold case.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry

  • RF and communications: local oscillators, clock references, mixers.
  • Digital electronics: timing circuits for CPUs, MCUs, FPGAs, and communication buses.
  • Instrumentation: function generators, sensor excitation, test equipment.

Why engineers care

Engineers care because oscillator design affects start-up reliability, frequency accuracy, power consumption, distortion, and manufacturing yield.

Typical measurement and validation methods

  • Gain/phase measurement versus frequency with a network analyzer.
  • Startup waveform and steady oscillation checks with an oscilloscope and spectrum analyzer.
  • Tolerance and temperature validation by simulation and lab testing.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • More startup gain improves reliability but may increase distortion.
  • Real components have tolerances and parasitics.
  • Temperature and supply changes shift gain and phase.
From lab to product: In real hardware, engineers usually design with some extra gain margin above the ideal threshold because component spread and environmental variation can prevent startup if the loop gain is exactly 1 only on paper.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

Problem restated clearly

At the target frequency ω0, the feedback network introduces a phase shift of 180° and a loss of 12 dB. We must find the amplifier phase shift and the smallest amplifier gain that allows oscillation to begin.

Given quantities

  • ∠β(jω0) = 180°
  • Feedback loss = 12 dB

Unknowns

  • ∠A(jω0)
  • |A(jω0)|min

Relevant principles

  • Barkhausen phase condition.
  • Barkhausen magnitude condition.
  • dB-to-linear conversion.

Assumptions

  • Linear, small-signal startup analysis.
  • The given 12 dB refers to amplitude loss at ω0.
  • The minimum gain corresponds to the threshold case |Aβ| = 1.

Possible approaches

Approach Idea Comment
Direct Barkhausen method Use loop phase and magnitude immediately Best here
Closed-loop denominator Use 1 - Aβ = 0 Equivalent but more abstract
Round-trip picture Track one signal circulation around the loop Good intuition

The direct Barkhausen method is the clearest choice because the problem gives exactly the phase and loss of the feedback network at the oscillation frequency.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

  1. Write the phase condition for the total loop.
  2. Use the given network phase to find the amplifier phase.
  3. Convert the 12 dB loss into linear magnitude |β|.
  4. Apply the threshold magnitude condition |Aβ| = 1.
  5. Solve for the minimum amplifier gain.
  6. Check the result in both linear and dB form.
Common mistakes
  • Using 10 log instead of 20 log.
  • Forgetting that 180° + 180° = 360°.
  • Assuming positive feedback means the amplifier itself must have 0° phase shift.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Physical intuition first

The feedback network inverts the signal and attenuates it. Therefore the amplifier must also invert it and amplify it enough so that, after a full loop, the signal comes back with the same phase and at least the same magnitude.

Step 1: Write the loop condition

L(jω0) = A(jω0)β(jω0) At threshold start-up: |L(jω0)| = 1 Phase condition: ∠A(jω0) + ∠β(jω0) = 360°n

This is valid because we are using linear phasor analysis at a single frequency.

Step 2: Find the amplifier phase shift

Given:

∠β(jω0) = 180°

Apply the phase condition:

∠A(jω0) + 180° = 360°

Therefore,

∠A(jω0) = 180°

So the amplifier must provide a 180° phase shift.

Step 3: Convert the 12 dB loss into a linear ratio

Since the network has a 12 dB loss,

20 log10 |β| = -12

Divide by 20:

log10 |β| = -12/20 = -0.6

Exponentiate:

|β| = 10^-0.6 ≈ 0.2512

Step 4: Find the minimum amplifier gain

At the threshold of oscillation,

|Aβ| = 1

Thus,

|A|min = 1 / |β|

Substitute the value of |β|:

|A|min = 1 / 0.2512 ≈ 3.98
Final Answer

Amplifier phase shift at ω0: 180°

Minimum amplifier gain magnitude: 3.98 ≈ 4

Equivalent amplifier gain in dB: ≈ +12 dB

Sanity checks

  • Units: gains are dimensionless, so the result is dimensionally consistent.
  • Phase: 180° + 180° = 360°, so the loop is reinforcing.
  • Magnitude: if the network returns only about 0.251 of the amplitude, the amplifier must multiply by about 4 to recover unity loop gain.
Optional deeper note

In practice, designers often choose the amplifier gain slightly above this threshold value so that oscillation starts reliably despite tolerances and device non-idealities. Later, nonlinear behavior reduces the effective gain to unity at steady amplitude.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

Interpretation of the final result

The formula |A|min = 1/|β| means the amplifier must exactly compensate the attenuation in the feedback path. The phase condition means the amplifier must also exactly compensate the feedback-network phase so the signal reproduces itself after one round trip.

Parameter dependence

  • If the feedback loss increases, the required amplifier gain increases.
  • If the feedback-network phase changes, the amplifier phase requirement changes accordingly.
  • In dB form, losses and gains add directly around the loop.

Alternative derivation

Using the positive-feedback transfer form T = A/(1 - Aβ), oscillation corresponds to 1 - Aβ = 0, so Aβ = 1. The same magnitude and phase conditions follow immediately.

Concept check Q&A

  • Q: Why is the required amplifier phase not 0°?
    A: Because the network already contributes 180°, so the amplifier must add another 180° to make 360° total.
  • Q: Why is 12 dB not equal to a gain of 12?
    A: Because dB is logarithmic. Here 12 dB corresponds to a linear ratio of about 3.98.
  • Q: What happens if |Aβ| < 1?
    A: The oscillation dies out instead of growing.
  • Q: What happens if |Aβ| > 1?
    A: The oscillation grows until nonlinear effects reduce the effective loop gain.
