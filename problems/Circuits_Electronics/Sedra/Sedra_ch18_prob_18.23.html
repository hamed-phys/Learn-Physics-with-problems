Estimating the Harmonic Distortion of a Resonator-Filtered Square Wave

We are told that the diode-clipped waveform is approximately an ideal square wave, and that the resonator has quality factor Q = 20. The goal is to estimate how much unwanted harmonic content survives in the output sine wave. The central physics idea is simple: the square wave contains many harmonics, but a high-Q resonator passes the fundamental strongly and suppresses the off-resonant harmonics.

Quick Summary

  • The problem is about harmonic distortion after resonant filtering of an approximately square-wave excitation.
  • The input square wave contains only odd harmonics: 1st, 3rd, 5th, 7th, ... ; the 2nd harmonic is exactly zero for an ideal symmetric square wave.
  • The resonator is modeled as a linear second-order band-pass resonator tuned to the fundamental angular frequency ω₀.
  • The key transfer-magnitude formula is |H(r)| / |H(1)| = 1 / sqrt(1 + Q²(r - 1/r)²), where r = ω/ω₀.
  • Each output harmonic amplitude equals the input harmonic amplitude multiplied by the resonator response at that harmonic.
  • Because the square-wave harmonic amplitudes scale like 1/n, and the resonator suppresses off-resonant components even more, the output becomes very nearly sinusoidal.
  • The results are numerical ratios relative to the output fundamental: 2nd = 0, 3rd ≈ 0.625%, 5th ≈ 0.208%, and the RMS of harmonics through the 10th ≈ 0.670%.
  • Physically, this means the resonator cleans up the clipped waveform very effectively, leaving only a small residual distortion.

PART 0 — Concept Primer

Core definitions

Symbol Meaning Typical unit
V Amplitude of the ideal square wave V
ω₀ Fundamental angular frequency and resonant angular frequency rad/s
Q Quality factor of the resonator; measures selectivity dimensionless
n Harmonic index, so the harmonic frequency is nω₀ dimensionless
H(jω) Frequency-response transfer function of the resonator dimensionless here

Physical meaning of the key quantities

A square wave is not a single-frequency signal. It is a superposition of sinusoids. In Fourier language, it is expanded into harmonics of the fundamental frequency. A resonator, by contrast, strongly prefers one frequency range near its resonance and rejects others. So the output spectrum is simply the input spectrum multiplied by the resonator spectrum.

Square wave Fourier series: v_in(t) = (4V/π) [ sin(ω₀t) + (1/3)sin(3ω₀t) + (1/5)sin(5ω₀t) + ... ]

The coefficient 1/n tells us that higher harmonics are already smaller at the source. The resonator then attenuates them further according to how far they are from resonance.

Key laws and validity conditions

  • Linearity: the resonator must operate in its linear regime, so superposition applies. Each harmonic can be treated separately and then combined.
  • Time-invariant steady-state response: once transients die out, each sinusoidal input at frequency nω₀ produces a sinusoidal output at the same frequency.
  • Ideal square-wave approximation: the problem explicitly says the diode-clipped waveform is nearly ideal, so even harmonics vanish.
  • Resonator tuned to the fundamental: we interpret the resonator as centered at the desired output frequency ω₀.

Math Insight

The resonator response is a frequency-domain filter, and the problem is mathematically a product of two spectral objects: the discrete Fourier spectrum of the square wave and the continuous transfer function of a second-order system. In operator language, the resonator can be viewed as a linear ODE system whose eigenmodes respond strongly near resonance. Because the input is periodic, Fourier decomposition diagonalizes the linear problem: each harmonic is an eigenfunction of the time-shift operator, so it is scaled independently by H(jnω₀).

Common resonator model

A standard normalized second-order resonator has magnitude response

|H(jω)| / |H(jω₀)| = 1 / sqrt(1 + Q²(r - 1/r)²), r = ω/ω₀

This formula is dimensionally consistent because Q and r - 1/r are both dimensionless. Therefore the whole denominator is dimensionless, as it must be for a gain ratio.

Mini intuition examples

  • If r = 1, then r - 1/r = 0, so the response is maximal.
  • If r = 3, the harmonic is already three times higher in frequency, and a high-Q resonator suppresses it strongly.
  • If Q increases, the resonance gets narrower, so distortion decreases.
What to watch for
  • Do not confuse the input harmonic amplitudes with the output harmonic amplitudes.
  • For an ideal square wave, all even harmonics are zero; the resonator cannot create a second harmonic if the system is linear.
  • “Relative to the fundamental” means divide by the output fundamental amplitude, not just the input fundamental coefficient.
  • When combining several harmonics into an RMS distortion estimate, square the ratios, add them, and then take the square root.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

This topic matters because real engineering systems frequently create signals that are easier to generate in a non-sinusoidal form, then shape into cleaner spectral content with resonant or filtering structures.

Application area
Oscillators & RF
Clipped or limited waveforms are filtered by resonant tanks to produce low-distortion carriers.
Application area
Power electronics
Square-like inverter outputs are shaped by LC networks to reduce harmonics and EMI.
Application area
Sensors & instrumentation
Resonant readout chains isolate a desired tone from broadband or harmonic-rich excitation.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: low distortion improves spectral purity, measurement accuracy, and communication quality.
  • Safety and compliance: unwanted harmonics can create electromagnetic interference and regulatory failure.
  • Reliability: high harmonic content can increase heating and stress in downstream components.
  • Efficiency and cost: designers must balance a higher-Q resonator against tolerances, startup behavior, physical size, and losses.

Typical measurement and validation methods

  • Spectrum analyzer measurement of harmonic amplitudes.
  • Oscilloscope plus FFT for quick laboratory verification.
  • Network-analyzer measurement of resonator bandwidth and Q.
  • Production testing with pass/fail thresholds on total harmonic distortion or spur levels.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Higher Q reduces distortion, but narrows bandwidth and increases sensitivity to component tolerances.
  • Real inductors, capacitors, and resonant structures have losses, temperature drift, and parasitic elements.
  • Non-ideal clipping may produce asymmetry, which can introduce even harmonics in practice.
  • Manufacturability often pushes designs away from ideal textbook values.
From lab to product

In an ideal model, the resonator is perfectly linear and exactly tuned. In a real product, engineers add trimming, automatic level control, guard margins on Q and bandwidth, and tolerance analysis to make sure harmonic distortion remains acceptable across temperature, aging, and part variation.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

Problem restated clearly

A nearly ideal square wave drives a resonator whose quality factor is Q = 20. The resonator is tuned to the square wave’s fundamental frequency. Estimate how much harmonic content remains in the output by finding the output harmonic magnitudes relative to the output fundamental for the 2nd, 3rd, and 5th harmonics, and also the RMS of all harmonics up to the 10th.

Given quantities

  • Input waveform: ideal square wave approximation
  • Fourier coefficients: odd harmonics only, magnitude proportional to 1/n
  • Resonator quality factor: Q = 20
  • Resonator tuned to the fundamental ω₀

Unknowns

  • A₂/A₁
  • A₃/A₁
  • A₅/A₁
  • sqrt(Σ[n=2..10](A_n/A_1)^2)

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Fourier decomposition: applies because the input is periodic.
  • Linear frequency response: applies because the resonator is assumed linear.
  • Steady-state sinusoidal response: each harmonic is treated independently at its own frequency.
  • Symmetry of ideal square wave: eliminates even harmonics immediately.
Assumptions
  • The clipped waveform is close enough to an ideal symmetric square wave that even harmonics are neglected.
  • The resonator is a second-order linear resonator with standard Q-dependent amplitude response.
  • The output is measured in steady state.
  • No additional nonlinear distortion is introduced by the resonator.

Possible approaches

Approach Idea Pros Cons
Direct time-domain ODE Drive the resonator differential equation with a square wave Fundamental physics is explicit Cumbersome because many Fourier components still appear
Fourier series + transfer function Multiply each harmonic by the resonator response Fast, transparent, best for distortion estimates Requires knowing the resonator magnitude formula
Narrowband asymptotic estimate Use large-detuning approximations for high harmonics Good intuition and scaling Less exact numerically

The best choice is the Fourier series + transfer function method, because the problem explicitly gives the square-wave Fourier series and asks only for harmonic magnitudes relative to the fundamental.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

  1. Write the input spectrum. Goal: identify which harmonics are present. Tool: Fourier series of a square wave.
  2. Set the resonator frequency ratio. Goal: evaluate the response at harmonic n. Tool: r=n because ω=nω₀.
  3. Compute the relative resonator gain. Goal: find how much the resonator suppresses the nth harmonic. Tool: resonator magnitude formula.
  4. Multiply source harmonic by filter response. Goal: obtain output harmonic amplitude relative to the output fundamental.
  5. Use symmetry for the 2nd harmonic. Goal: avoid unnecessary calculation; it is zero for an ideal square wave.
  6. Sum RMS contributions through the 10th harmonic. Goal: estimate total residual distortion from harmonics 2 through 10.
  7. Check reasonableness. Goal: confirm that high-Q filtering leaves only small residual harmonic content.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting that the ideal square wave has no even harmonics.
  • Using only 1/n and ignoring the resonator attenuation.
  • Adding harmonic amplitudes directly instead of using an RMS sum.
  • Using frequency f in one place and angular frequency ω in another without consistency.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Physical intuition before calculation

A square wave is rich in harmonics, but a resonator with Q = 20 is fairly selective. So we expect the fundamental to dominate the output strongly. Since the square wave is symmetric, the 2nd harmonic should be absent altogether. The 3rd and 5th should survive only as small residual terms.

Step 1: Write the harmonic amplitudes of the input

For an ideal square wave of amplitude V,

v_in(t) = (4V/π) Σ[m=0..∞] [ 1/(2m+1) ] sin[(2m+1)ω₀ t]

Therefore the input amplitude of the nth harmonic is proportional to 1/n for odd n, and exactly zero for even n.

Step 2: Resonator model and mathematical structure

A standard linear second-order resonator can be written as an ODE of the form

d²x/dt² + (ω₀/Q) dx/dt + ω₀² x = F(t)

Here x(t) is the resonator output coordinate or voltage-like response, and F(t) is the drive. This is a linear ODE with constant coefficients. By Fourier decomposition, if F(t) contains a component e^(j n ω₀ t), then the steady-state response at that frequency is multiplied by the transfer function.

Optional deeper note: why this frequency-domain method is valid

The operator L = d²/dt² + (ω₀/Q)d/dt + ω₀² is linear. Complex exponentials e^(jωt) are eigenfunctions of differentiation: d/dt → jω and d²/dt² → -ω². So each Fourier component reduces the ODE to an algebraic response factor. That is the mathematical reason superposition works so cleanly here.

Step 3: Frequency response at the nth harmonic

Let the dimensionless frequency ratio be

r = ω/ω₀

At the nth harmonic, ω = nω₀, so r = n. The normalized resonator magnitude is

G(n) = |H(jnω₀)| / |H(jω₀)| = 1 / sqrt(1 + Q²(n - 1/n)²)

Since the output fundamental is the input fundamental multiplied by |H(jω₀)|, the harmonic amplitude relative to the output fundamental is

A_n/A_1 = 0, for even n A_n/A_1 = (1/n) G(n), for odd n A_n/A_1 = (1/n) / sqrt(1 + Q²(n - 1/n)²)

This formula is dimensionless on both sides, which is a good dimensional check.

Step 4: Evaluate the requested harmonics for Q = 20

(a) Second harmonic

Because the input is an ideal symmetric square wave,

A₂/A₁ = 0

The resonator cannot generate the 2nd harmonic by itself because we assume a linear system.

(b) Third harmonic

For n = 3,

A₃/A₁ = (1/3) / sqrt(1 + 20²(3 - 1/3)²) 3 - 1/3 = 8/3 A₃/A₁ = (1/3) / sqrt(1 + 400(64/9)) = (1/3) / sqrt(1 + 2844.444...) A₃/A₁ ≈ 0.0062489

Thus the 3rd harmonic is about 0.625% of the fundamental.

(c) Fifth harmonic

For n = 5,

A₅/A₁ = (1/5) / sqrt(1 + 20²(5 - 1/5)²) 5 - 1/5 = 24/5 A₅/A₁ = (1/5) / sqrt(1 + 400(24/5)²) A₅/A₁ ≈ 0.0020832

Thus the 5th harmonic is about 0.208% of the fundamental.

(d) RMS of harmonics up to the 10th

Up to the 10th harmonic, the ideal square wave contributes only the odd harmonics n = 3, 5, 7, 9. So the RMS harmonic ratio is

D₁₀ = sqrt[(A₃/A₁)² + (A₅/A₁)² + (A₇/A₁)² + (A₉/A₁)²]

The additional terms are

A₇/A₁ = (1/7)/sqrt(1 + 20²(7 - 1/7)²) ≈ 0.00104164 A₉/A₁ = (1/9)/sqrt(1 + 20²(9 - 1/9)²) ≈ 0.00062499

Therefore,

D₁₀ = sqrt[(0.0062489)² + (0.0020832)² + (0.00104164)² + (0.00062499)²] D₁₀ ≈ 0.0066981

So the RMS of harmonics through the 10th is about 0.670% of the fundamental.

Final Answer

Relative to the output fundamental,

  • (a) Second harmonic: A₂/A₁ = 0
  • (b) Third harmonic: A₃/A₁ ≈ 0.00625 = 0.625%
  • (c) Fifth harmonic: A₅/A₁ ≈ 0.00208 = 0.208%
  • (d) RMS of harmonics through the 10th: D₁₀ ≈ 0.00670 = 0.670%

Sanity checks

  • Units: all reported quantities are ratios, so they are dimensionless.
  • Limiting case: if Q were smaller, harmonic suppression would be weaker and distortion larger.
  • Large-harmonic asymptotics: for large odd n, n - 1/n ≈ n, so A_n/A_1 ≈ 1/(Q n²); the output harmonics decay faster than the square-wave source terms.
  • Physical meaning: the dominant residual distortion is the 3rd harmonic; all higher terms are even smaller.
Optional deeper note: a useful asymptotic estimate

For odd n >> 1,

A_n/A_1 = (1/n)/sqrt(1 + Q²(n - 1/n)²) ≈ (1/n)/(Qn) = 1/(Qn²)

This shows mathematically why a high-Q resonator is so effective: the square-wave source already gives a 1/n decay, and the resonator adds essentially another 1/n decay for far-off harmonics.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

Re-interpreting the final formula

The formula

A_n/A_1 = (1/n) / sqrt(1 + Q²(n - 1/n)²)

contains two suppression mechanisms:

  • 1/n comes from the square-wave physics itself.
  • 1/sqrt(1 + Q²(... )²) comes from resonant selectivity.

Parameter dependence

  • Increasing Q: makes the denominator larger for off-resonant harmonics, so distortion decreases.
  • Increasing harmonic order n: reduces amplitude both because the square wave has smaller coefficients and because detuning grows.
  • Breaking square-wave symmetry: would make the 2nd harmonic nonzero in real hardware.

Alternative derivation idea

One could start from the resonator ODE, insert each input harmonic F_n sin(nω₀ t), solve for the steady-state amplitude using phasors, and then divide by the corresponding fundamental amplitude. That route gives the same result but is algebraically longer.

Concept checks

  • Q: Why is the second harmonic zero here?
    A: Because an ideal symmetric square wave has only odd harmonics, and the linear resonator does not create new frequencies.
  • Q: Why is the 3rd harmonic the dominant residual distortion?
    A: It is the nearest nonzero harmonic to the resonance, so it is least suppressed among the unwanted terms.
  • Q: Would the distortion increase or decrease if Q were 100 instead of 20?
    A: It would decrease because the resonator would be more selective.
  • Q: Why use RMS to combine several harmonics?
    A: Because power-like contributions add in quadrature for orthogonal sinusoidal components.

Closing Note

This is a classic example of how Fourier analysis and resonance work together in applied physics and engineering: a waveform may look badly distorted in time, yet a selective resonator can recover an almost pure sine wave by rejecting the undesired harmonics.
