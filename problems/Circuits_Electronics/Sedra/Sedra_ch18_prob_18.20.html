Loop Gain of the RC–Op-Amp Oscillator Network

We are asked to break the loop at node X and find the loop gain of the circuit. The cleanest route is to treat the op-amp as an ideal inverting stage and analyze the three-capacitor RC ladder that feeds it. This is a classic phase-shift oscillator structure, and the loop gain tells us whether self-oscillation is possible.

Result: symbolic transfer function
Method: nodal analysis in the s-domain
Physics lens: phase shift + energy storage

Quick Summary

  • The problem is about the loop gain of an RC feedback network wrapped around an inverting op-amp.
  • The key physics idea is that capacitors store and delay energy, creating a frequency-dependent phase shift and attenuation.
  • The governing tool is linear circuit theory in the Laplace domain, using impedances and nodal equations.
  • With an ideal op-amp, the inverting input is at virtual ground, which greatly simplifies the RC ladder analysis.
  • If we define x = sRC, the ladder transfer from the break point to the last RC node is v3/vx = 1 / (x3 + 6x2 + 10x + 4).
  • The op-amp stage contributes vo = -(Rf/R) v3.
  • Therefore the loop gain is L(s) = - (Rf/R) / [(sRC)3 + 6(sRC)2 + 10(sRC) + 4].
  • This is a symbolic result. Physically, it tells us the frequency-dependent gain and phase around the loop.
  • As a useful check, the zero-phase condition occurs at ω0RC = √10, and unity loop-gain then requires Rf = 56R.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions

Symbol Meaning SI unit
R Resistance of each ladder resistor and the op-amp input resistor Ω
Rf Feedback resistor of the inverting op-amp stage Ω
C Capacitance of each shunt capacitor F
s Laplace variable, s = σ + jω s-1
vx Injected test voltage at the broken loop at node X V
v1, v2, v3 Intermediate node voltages in the RC ladder V
vo Op-amp output voltage; also the returned signal after one loop traversal V
L(s) Loop gain dimensionless

Physical meaning of the key quantities

A resistor dissipates energy, while a capacitor stores electric-field energy U = (1/2)CV2. Because a capacitor current is i = C dv/dt, a capacitor resists sudden voltage change. In sinusoidal steady state, that storage process becomes a frequency-dependent impedance, so the network both attenuates amplitude and shifts phase.

The op-amp stage is an inverting amplifier. Its negative sign contributes 180° phase shift. The RC ladder contributes the rest. Oscillation is possible only when the total phase shift around the loop is effectively 0° modulo 360°, and the magnitude of the loop gain is at least unity.

Key laws and validity conditions

Key laws used here
  • Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL): charge conservation at each node.
  • Linear capacitor model: iC = C dv/dt, or IC(s)=sCV(s) for zero initial conditions.
  • Ohm’s law: i = v/R.
  • Ideal op-amp assumptions: infinite gain, zero input current, and with negative feedback the inverting input sits at virtual ground.

Common models and approximations

  • Ideal op-amp approximation: valid when the real op-amp open-loop gain is very large at the frequency of interest and the device is not saturating.
  • Zero initial energy: in Laplace analysis we assume capacitors start from zero stored voltage unless otherwise stated.
  • Lumped-element model: valid when circuit size is much smaller than the electromagnetic wavelength, so transmission-line effects are negligible.

Mini intuition examples

Single RC stage One resistor and one capacitor form a low-pass stage. At high frequency the capacitor pulls the node toward ground.
Three RC stages Cascading stages increases attenuation and phase lag. This makes phase-shift oscillators possible.
Inverting amplifier The op-amp provides gain and a sign reversal. The RC network then selects the oscillation frequency.

What to watch for

  • Do not treat the three RC stages as completely independent cascades unless the loading is negligible. Here it is not negligible.
  • Do not forget that the last resistor goes into a virtual ground, so it loads the third RC node.
  • Loop gain is a signed complex quantity, not just a magnitude.
  • Keep the node labeling consistent. A small sign mistake can flip the phase conclusion.
Math Insight: The core equation is a linear algebraic system in the Laplace domain. Physically it comes from conservation of charge at each node; mathematically it is a finite-dimensional linear operator problem. The denominator polynomial is the characteristic response of the coupled RC ladder.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry

  • Signal generation and instrumentation: RC oscillators are used in audio-frequency generators, calibration sources, and low-cost waveform circuits.
  • Sensors and industrial electronics: oscillatory circuits appear in filtering, timing, impedance measurement, and analog front-ends.
  • Embedded and control hardware: feedback networks with defined phase characteristics are central to compensation, stability, and timing.
  • Test equipment and manufacturing fixtures: simple oscillators help in diagnostics, tone injection, self-test, and fault localization.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: the loop gain determines whether oscillation starts, sustains, or dies out.
  • Reliability: too much gain can cause clipping and distortion; too little gain prevents startup.
  • Cost and simplicity: RC oscillators avoid inductors, which can reduce size and manufacturing cost.
  • Tolerances: resistor and capacitor spread shifts the oscillation frequency and startup margin.

Typical measurement and validation methods

  • Network analyzer or frequency-response measurement of magnitude and phase.
  • Oscilloscope startup observation to verify amplitude growth and steady-state behavior.
  • Impedance and tolerance checks on passive components.
  • SPICE simulation with parametric sweeps for worst-case design.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Large R reduces power draw but increases noise sensitivity and bias-current error.
  • Large C can improve low-frequency operation but costs more area and may have worse tolerance.
  • Real op-amps have finite bandwidth, slew rate, output swing limits, and input offset.
  • Temperature drift shifts both R and C, moving the oscillation condition.
From lab to product: The ideal model gives the correct architecture and first-pass design values. A real product then adds amplitude stabilization, tolerance analysis, op-amp nonidealities, startup margin, noise considerations, PCB parasitics, and production test limits.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement

We must open the feedback loop at node X, apply a test signal there, and compute the signal that returns to the other side of the break after passing through the RC ladder and the inverting op-amp stage. That returned-signal-to-injected-signal ratio is the loop gain.

Given quantities

  • Three identical shunt capacitors, each of value C
  • Four resistors of value R in the forward ladder path
  • One op-amp with grounded noninverting input
  • Feedback resistor Rf from output to inverting input
  • Loop broken at node X

Unknowns

  • The symbolic loop gain L(s)
  • Optionally, as a check, the oscillation frequency condition and the required Rf/R ratio

What must be found

We want L(s) = vreturned(s) / vtest(s). Since the returned signal at the broken top wire is the op-amp output, this is simply L(s) = vo(s)/vx(s).

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Nodal analysis: the circuit is linear and has several shunt elements to ground, so KCL is the most direct method.
  • Laplace-domain impedances: because the loop gain is frequency dependent and capacitors are present.
  • Ideal op-amp virtual ground: because the inverting input is in a negative-feedback configuration and the noninverting input is grounded.

What we do not need:

  • No magnetic-field or wave equation treatment: this is a lumped low-frequency circuit.
  • No nonlinear large-signal model: the loop-gain calculation is small-signal and linear.
  • No time-domain differential-equation solving directly: the Laplace method is cleaner.
Assumptions
  • Ideal op-amp: infinite gain, zero input current, output not saturated.
  • Zero initial capacitor voltages, so IC(s)=sCV(s).
  • All three capacitors equal to C, and the forward-path resistors equal to R.
  • Lumped-element approximation is valid.

Possible approaches

Approach Idea Pros Cons
Nodal analysis Write KCL at each RC node Systematic, exact, least ambiguity Requires solving 3 equations
Backward reduction Express each upstream node in terms of the downstream one Elegant for this ladder Still rooted in KCL; easy to slip signs
Matrix / determinant method Set up admittance matrix Generalizable More formal than needed here

Best choice: nodal analysis, but performed working backward from the last RC node, because that matches the problem hint and makes the algebra cleaner.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Define the test input.
    Goal: Let the broken-loop voltage at node X be vx.
    Tool: Loop-gain definition.
    Meaning: This is the signal we inject to probe the loop.
  2. Label the RC nodes.
    Goal: Call them v1, v2, v3 from left to right.
    Tool: Clear notation.
    Meaning: These are the internal state-like variables of the ladder.
  3. Use virtual ground at the op-amp input.
    Goal: Replace the inverting-input node by 0 V.
    Tool: Ideal op-amp with negative feedback.
    Meaning: The last resistor loads node v3 to ground through R.
  4. Write KCL at each node in the s-domain.
    Goal: Convert capacitor currents into sCV terms.
    Tool: Laplace transform with zero initial conditions.
    Meaning: Frequency dependence enters through sRC.
  5. Nondimensionalize with x = sRC.
    Goal: Simplify the algebra and make the polynomial dimensionless.
    Tool: Scaling argument.
    Meaning: The natural frequency scale is set by 1/RC.
  6. Solve backward: first v2 in terms of v3, then v1, then vx.
    Goal: Find v3/vx.
    Tool: Linear substitution.
    Meaning: This is the RC-network transfer factor.
  7. Multiply by the op-amp stage gain.
    Goal: Use vo = -(Rf/R)v3.
    Tool: Ideal inverting-amplifier formula.
    Meaning: This closes the full loop.
  8. Check the result.
    Goal: Verify dimensions, sign, and oscillation condition.
    Tool: Substitute s=jω and inspect phase/magnitude.
    Meaning: This confirms the symbolic result is physically reasonable.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting the resistor from the last RC node into virtual ground.
  • Using 1/(sC) but then mixing impedance and admittance forms inconsistently.
  • Dropping the minus sign of the inverting amplifier.
  • Assuming the ladder transfer is the cube of a single-stage response. It is not, because of loading.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition before calculating

Before doing algebra, we expect two things:

  1. The RC ladder will strongly attenuate the signal and add phase lag.
  2. The op-amp will invert the signal and add gain.

So the loop gain should be a negative rational function in sRC, with a cubic denominator because there are three capacitors.

Step 1: Mathematical model

Let the injected voltage at the break be vx(t). The node voltages after each resistor-capacitor stage are v1(t), v2(t), and v3(t). The op-amp output is vo(t).

The circuit is a finite-dimensional lumped linear dynamical system. In time domain, each node equation is a first-order ODE arising from charge conservation: 

At each node:  Σ I_resistor + C dv/dt = 0

Since the problem asks for loop gain, the Laplace domain is more convenient. With zero initial conditions, 

I_C(s) = s C V(s)

Also, for an ideal op-amp in negative feedback, the inverting input is at virtual ground: 

v_- = 0

This means the resistor connected from node v3 to the inverting input is effectively a resistor from v3 to ground.

Step 2: Define the dimensionless variable

Introduce the nondimensional parameter x = sRC. This is valid because s has units s-1 and RC has units s, so the product is dimensionless: 

x = s R C

This scaling is mathematically useful because it compresses the response into a polynomial in one dimensionless variable. Physically, it shows that the natural frequency scale is set by 1/RC.

Step 3: Write KCL at each node

We now work in the Laplace domain. Currents leaving each node are taken as positive.

Node 1

Currents go left through R to vx, right through R to v2, and down through C to ground: 

(v1 - vx)/R + (v1 - v2)/R + s C v1 = 0

Multiply by R: 

(2 + x) v1 - v2 = vx

Node 2

Likewise, 

(v2 - v1)/R + (v2 - v3)/R + s C v2 = 0

Multiply by R: 

-v1 + (2 + x) v2 - v3 = 0

Node 3

The right-hand resistor goes to virtual ground, so: 

(v3 - v2)/R + v3/R + s C v3 = 0

Multiply by R: 

-v2 + (2 + x) v3 = 0

Step 4: Solve backward, as suggested

The last equation gives the cleanest starting point: 

v2 = (2 + x) v3

Substitute this into the node-2 equation: 

-v1 + (2 + x) v2 - v3 = 0

Therefore, 

v1 = (2 + x) v2 - v3

Insert v2 = (2+x)v3: 

v1 = (2 + x)^2 v3 - v3 = [(2 + x)^2 - 1] v3

Expand the square: 

(2 + x)^2 - 1 = x^2 + 4x + 3

Hence 

v1 = (x^2 + 4x + 3) v3

Now use the node-1 equation: 

(2 + x) v1 - v2 = vx

Substitute the expressions for v1 and v2: 

vx = (2 + x)(x^2 + 4x + 3) v3 - (2 + x) v3

Factor out (2+x)v3: 

vx = (2 + x)[(x^2 + 4x + 3) - 1] v3 = (2 + x)(x^2 + 4x + 2) v3

Multiply out: 

(2 + x)(x^2 + 4x + 2) = x^3 + 6x^2 + 10x + 4

Therefore the ladder transfer is 

v3 / vx = 1 / (x^3 + 6x^2 + 10x + 4)

Returning to x = sRC, 

v3 / vx = 1 / [(sRC)^3 + 6(sRC)^2 + 10(sRC) + 4]

Step 5: Include the op-amp stage

The inverting amplifier has input resistor R and feedback resistor Rf. Because the inverting node is at virtual ground, the standard inverting-gain formula applies: 

vo = -(Rf / R) v3

So the loop gain is 

L(s) = vo / vx = - (Rf / R) / [(sRC)^3 + 6(sRC)^2 + 10(sRC) + 4]

Final Answer

The loop gain, with the loop broken at node X, is 

L(s) = - (Rf / R) / [(sRC)^3 + 6(sRC)^2 + 10(sRC) + 4]

Equivalently, with x = sRC: L(s) = - (Rf/R) / (x3 + 6x2 + 10x + 4).

Sanity checks

1) Dimensional analysis

Every term in the denominator uses powers of sRC, which is dimensionless. The ratio Rf/R is also dimensionless. So L(s) is dimensionless, as any gain must be.

2) Low-frequency limit

At s → 0, capacitors are open circuits, so the ladder becomes purely resistive: 

L(0) = - (Rf / R) / 4

That is reasonable: there is finite attenuation through the resistor chain, followed by inversion by the op-amp.

3) High-frequency limit

For large |s|, the capacitors act like short circuits, strongly grounding the ladder nodes, so the ladder transfer should vanish like 1/s3. Our result indeed behaves as 

L(s) ~ - (Rf / R) / (sRC)^3 as |s| -> infinity

4) Oscillation-condition check

Set s = jω and define u = ωRC. Then 

L(jω) = - (Rf / R) / [(ju)^3 + 6(ju)^2 + 10(ju) + 4]

Since (ju)2 = -u2 and (ju)3 = -ju3, the denominator becomes 

(4 - 6u^2) + j(10u - u^3)

For zero net phase shift, the imaginary part must vanish: 

10u - u^3 = 0 => u(10 - u^2) = 0 => u = sqrt(10)

We discard u = 0 because that does not produce oscillation. So 

ω0 = sqrt(10) / (RC)

At this frequency, 

4 - 6u^2 = 4 - 60 = -56

Hence 

L(jω0) = - (Rf / R) / (-56) = (Rf / R) / 56

For unity loop gain, |L(jω0)| = 1, so 

Rf = 56 R

This is a well-known check for this phase-shift oscillator topology, so the loop-gain expression is consistent.

Optional deeper mathematical note

The three node equations can be written in matrix form as 

[ 2+x   -1     0 ] [v1]   [vx]
[ -1   2+x   -1 ] [v2] = [ 0]
[  0    -1   2+x] [v3]   [ 0]

except that the right-most loading is already built into the final diagonal entry. Solving this linear system is equivalent to inverting an admittance operator. The cubic denominator appears because there are three energy-storage elements.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the final formula

The final loop gain is 

L(s) = - (Rf / R) / [(sRC)^3 + 6(sRC)^2 + 10(sRC) + 4]

Each part has a physical meaning:

  • - sign: the op-amp stage inverts the signal.
  • Rf/R: the adjustable amplifier strength.
  • (sRC)3 term: the highest-order dynamic effect from three capacitors.
  • Lower-order terms: coupling and loading between the ladder stages.
  • Constant 4: the purely resistive low-frequency loading structure.

Parameter dependence

  • Increasing Rf increases the loop-gain magnitude linearly.
  • Increasing R with fixed C lowers the characteristic frequency scale 1/RC.
  • Increasing C also lowers the characteristic frequency scale.
  • The oscillation frequency scales as ω0 ∝ 1/(RC).
  • The startup threshold is set by Rf/R; if it is below 56, oscillations decay in the ideal small-signal model.

Alternative derivation idea

Another valid route is to derive the transfer function using a chain of loaded voltage dividers or by writing the admittance matrix and evaluating the determinant ratio. Those methods are mathematically equivalent because the circuit is linear and time-invariant.

Concept check Q&A

  • Q: Why is the denominator cubic?
    A: Because the network has three capacitors, hence three first-order energy-storage contributions.
  • Q: Why can the last resistor be treated as going to ground?
    A: The ideal op-amp with grounded noninverting input and negative feedback makes the inverting node a virtual ground.
  • Q: Why is the loop gain negative at low frequency?
    A: The RC ladder is positive at low frequency, while the op-amp stage inverts.
  • Q: Why is it wrong to cube a single RC low-pass transfer function here?
    A: Because each stage loads the previous one; the stages are coupled, not isolated.
  • Q: What sets the oscillation frequency?
    A: The phase condition of the entire loop, which here gives ω0RC = √10.

PART 5 — Optional Plot Guide

No plot is necessary for this problem type because the task is purely symbolic: find the loop gain. The essential learning value lies in the structure of the transfer function and the phase-condition check, both of which are fully visible from the algebra.

Key takeaway: Break the loop at X, analyze the loaded three-stage RC ladder with virtual ground at the op-amp input, and then multiply by the inverting amplifier gain.
