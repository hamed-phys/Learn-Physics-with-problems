Colpitts Oscillator: Governing Equation, Oscillation Frequency, and Start-Up Condition

We analyze the transistor Colpitts oscillator of the given figure using a linear small-signal model about the bias point. The collector is at AC ground, the current source is an AC open circuit, and the bias/load network is represented by RL, which also includes ro. We first derive the governing equation of motion in the Laplace domain, then obtain the oscillation frequency and the condition for self-starting oscillations. Finally, we simplify the expressions for rπ → ∞.

Quick Summary

  • The problem asks for the characteristic equation of a Colpitts oscillator built from a BJT plus an L–C1–C2 feedback network.
  • The key idea is to use the small-signal hybrid-π model and impose the condition for a nontrivial natural response: the nodal coefficient matrix must have zero determinant.
  • The mathematical object is a homogeneous linear dynamical system whose poles satisfy a cubic characteristic equation.
  • With finite rπ, the governing equation is
    L C1C2s3 + L(C1/rπ + C2/RL)s2 + (C1+C2 + L/(RLrπ))s + (gm + 1/RL + 1/rπ) = 0.
  • For rπ large, this reduces to
    L C1C2s3 + (L C2/RL)s2 + (C1+C2)s + (gm + 1/RL) = 0.
  • The oscillation frequency is symbolic:
    ω02 = (C1+C2)/(L C1C2) = 1/(L Ceq), where Ceq = C1C2/(C1+C2).
  • The start-up threshold for large rπ is
    gmRL = C2/C1, so oscillations start when gmRL > C2/C1.
  • Physically, gm must be large enough to cancel the resonator’s effective loss represented by RL.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions and symbols

Symbol Meaning SI unit Physical interpretation
L Inductance H Stores magnetic energy, resists changes in current.
C1, C2 Feedback capacitors F Store electric energy and form the capacitive divider of the Colpitts network.
RL Effective shunt resistance at the emitter node Ω Represents damping/loading, including transistor output resistance ro as specified.
gm Transconductance of the transistor S Converts small base-emitter voltage into collector current; this is the active gain source.
rπ Input resistance of the hybrid-π model Ω Represents base current. The problem later asks us to simplify for rπ large.
s Laplace variable s−1 Used to encode growth/decay and oscillation: s = σ + jω.
vb, ve Base and emitter small-signal node voltages V The two independent node variables in the AC model.

Physical meaning of a Colpitts oscillator

A Colpitts oscillator is an LC-tuned feedback oscillator. The inductance L and the capacitive divider C1, C2 define a resonant frequency. The transistor supplies energy to compensate the losses of the resonator. When the active device provides exactly the needed negative resistance or loop gain, the circuit can sustain a sinusoidal oscillation.

Key laws and principles used here

1) Linearized small-signal circuit theory

We linearize the transistor around a DC operating point. This turns the nonlinear transistor into a linear time-invariant element with parameters gm, rπ, and ro. This is valid for sufficiently small oscillation amplitude near start-up.

2) Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL)

At each node, the algebraic sum of currents is zero. In the Laplace domain, capacitors contribute sC and inductors contribute 1/(sL).

3) Natural-response condition

Since we are looking for self-sustained oscillation, there is no external driving source in the AC small-signal problem. Therefore we solve a homogeneous system, and nontrivial solutions exist only when the system determinant is zero.

4) Barkhausen-style interpretation

Although we use nodal analysis, the final threshold condition has the same meaning as Barkhausen: at the oscillation frequency, the active device must provide enough gain to exactly balance losses.

Common approximations in this topic

  • Collector at AC ground: because the collector is tied to the supply, which is an AC ground in small-signal analysis.
  • Bias current source is AC open: an ideal DC current source has infinite small-signal resistance.
  • Large coupling capacitor is AC short: therefore RL appears directly at the emitter node for AC.
  • Large rπ limit: base current is negligible, simplifying the algebra and revealing the classic Colpitts result.

Mini intuition examples

  1. If gm = 0, the network is passive. Losses dominate, so any transient decays.
  2. If RL → ∞, the resonator is lightly damped; it becomes easier for the transistor to maintain oscillation.
  3. If C2/C1 increases, the required gain increases. This reflects a feedback-divider effect.
Math Insight. The governing equation is a cubic characteristic polynomial in s. Mathematically, this is the eigenvalue condition of a linear dynamical system. Oscillation corresponds to a root on the imaginary axis, s = jω0. Start-up corresponds to the real part becoming positive, meaning the oscillation amplitude initially grows.

What to watch for

  • Do not forget that the collector is at signal ground, not “removed” from the circuit.
  • Be careful with the sign of the controlled source gmvπ.
  • The oscillation condition is a threshold equality; start-up requires a strict inequality beyond that threshold.
  • At start-up, analysis is linear. Once amplitude grows, nonlinear device behavior limits the oscillation amplitude; that nonlinear regime is not part of this problem.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in real engineering

RF transmitters and local oscillators

Colpitts-style LC oscillators are used in frequency generation, frequency conversion, and signal synthesis in radios and communication front ends.

Instrumentation and sensors

Oscillators convert component changes into frequency shifts, which are easy to measure accurately in sensing systems.

Clocking and timing subsystems

Tuned oscillators underpin reference generation, phase-locked loops, and analog timing blocks in mixed-signal electronics.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: oscillation frequency, phase noise, spectral purity, and amplitude stability all matter.
  • Reliability: the oscillator must start under worst-case temperature, process, and supply variations.
  • Cost and manufacturability: component tolerances shift the resonant frequency and gain margin.
  • Efficiency: only enough active-device gain should be used to guarantee start-up without excessive distortion or power loss.

Typical measurement and validation methods

  • Measure startup on an oscilloscope: does the waveform grow from noise?
  • Measure frequency on a spectrum analyzer or frequency counter.
  • Use vector network analysis or impedance analysis to estimate resonator Q and loss.
  • Run Monte Carlo simulation to assess the spread in ω0 and startup margin.

Design trade-offs and constraints

In practice, L, C1, and C2 are nonideal. Inductors have series resistance and parasitic capacitance. Capacitors have equivalent series resistance and tolerance. The transistor has finite rπ, finite ro, junction capacitances, and a frequency-dependent gm. Therefore the ideal threshold gmRL > C2/C1 becomes a design rule with margin rather than a razor-sharp boundary.

From lab to product. In a real oscillator, engineers begin with the ideal symbolic model to choose nominal values, then include parasitics, temperature drift, transistor frequency response, noise, startup margin, and amplitude-limiting nonlinearities before releasing a product design.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restated clearly

We must analyze the given transistor Colpitts oscillator, derive the equation governing its small-signal operation, and then use that equation to find:

  1. the oscillation frequency, and
  2. the condition on gmRL that ensures oscillations will start.

We are told that RL includes the transistor output resistance ro, and we are finally asked to simplify the results assuming rπ is large.

Given quantities

  • Inductance L
  • Capacitances C1 and C2
  • Transistor small-signal parameters gm, rπ
  • Effective load RL including ro

Unknowns

  • The characteristic/governing equation in s
  • The oscillation frequency ω0 (or f0)
  • The startup condition on gmRL

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Small-signal transistor model: valid because startup is a small-amplitude phenomenon.
  • KCL in the Laplace domain: efficient because the circuit is linear after small-signal linearization.
  • Determinant-zero condition: necessary for a homogeneous network to have a nonzero natural response.

Assumptions

Assumptions used in the derivation
  • All DC supplies are AC grounds.
  • The ideal bias current source contributes zero small-signal current, so it is an AC open circuit.
  • The large coupling capacitor to the external node is an AC short, so RL is directly connected to the emitter node in AC.
  • The transistor is represented by the hybrid-π model with gm, rπ, and ro absorbed into RL.
  • Linear time-invariant analysis is valid near startup.

Possible approaches

Approach Idea Pros Cons
Nodal analysis Write KCL at base and emitter nodes. Direct, systematic, produces characteristic equation cleanly. Requires careful sign handling of the controlled source.
Loop-gain/Barkhausen Separate amplifier and feedback network. Good physical intuition. Can be ambiguous when loading and transistor internal resistances are included.
Equivalent negative resistance Translate transistor action into an effective negative conductance. Very insightful for startup threshold. Less straightforward for the full finite-rπ characteristic equation.

Best choice: nodal analysis. It gives the full governing equation first, and then the oscillation frequency and threshold condition follow by setting s = jω.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Build the AC small-signal model.
    Tool: transistor hybrid-π model, AC-ground rules.
    Physical meaning: reduce the circuit to a linear network around its operating point.
  2. Choose node voltages.
    Tool: define vb at the base node and ve at the emitter node.
    Physical meaning: these are the two state-like variables of the oscillator.
  3. Write KCL at the base node.
    Tool: admittances 1/(sL), sC2, and 1/rπ.
    Physical meaning: current splitting among the inductor and the base-emitter branch.
  4. Write KCL at the emitter node.
    Tool: admittances sC1, 1/RL, sC2, 1/rπ, and the controlled source gmvπ.
    Physical meaning: balance of passive losses and active power injection.
  5. Form the coefficient matrix and set its determinant to zero.
    Tool: linear algebra.
    Physical meaning: condition for a self-sustained natural mode.
  6. Substitute s = jω.
    Tool: separate real and imaginary parts.
    Physical meaning: the imaginary part sets frequency, the real part sets loss/gain balance.
  7. Take the large-rπ limit.
    Tool: asymptotic simplification by neglecting 1/rπ terms.
    Physical meaning: negligible base current, which is the classic Colpitts limit.
Common mistakes
  • Using the wrong sign for the gmvπ controlled source.
  • Forgetting that RL is present in AC because of the large coupling capacitor.
  • Confusing the equality condition for sustained oscillation with the strict inequality for startup.
  • Dropping rπ too early before deriving the general equation.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition before calculating

The LC network wants to oscillate at its natural resonant frequency. However, RL and finite transistor resistances dissipate energy. The transistor, through gmvπ, can feed energy back into the resonator. So we expect:

  • a resonance frequency determined mainly by L, C1, and C2, and
  • a threshold condition where transistor gain just cancels the loss.

Step 1: Mathematical model

Let the base-node voltage be vb(t) and the emitter-node voltage be ve(t). We work in the Laplace domain: 

State variables: v_b(t), v_e(t)
Laplace variable: s = σ + jω

Admittances:
Y_L  = 1/(sL)
Y_π  = 1/r_π
Y_C1 = sC1
Y_C2 = sC2
Y_RL = 1/RL

Because the collector is at AC ground, the transistor controlled source injects a current into the emitter equal to gm(vb - ve). Equivalently, the current leaving the emitter through that branch is gm(ve - vb). This sign is the only subtle point.

Step 2: KCL at the base node

Currents leaving the base node go through the inductor to ground and through the base-emitter branch: 

(Y_L + Y_π + Y_C2) v_b - (Y_π + Y_C2) v_e = 0

that is,

(1/(sL) + 1/r_π + sC2) v_b - (1/r_π + sC2) v_e = 0      (1)

This equation is linear because each branch current is linear in the node voltages under the small-signal model.

Step 3: KCL at the emitter node

Currents leaving the emitter node go:

  • to ground through C1,
  • to ground through RL,
  • to the base through rπ and C2,
  • and through the controlled source branch toward AC ground.
(Y_C1 + Y_RL) v_e + (Y_π + Y_C2)(v_e - v_b) + g_m(v_e - v_b) = 0

or

-(Y_π + Y_C2 + g_m) v_b + (Y_C1 + Y_RL + Y_π + Y_C2 + g_m) v_e = 0   (2)

Step 4: Matrix form and determinant condition

Equations (1) and (2) form a homogeneous linear system: 

| Y_L + Y_π + Y_C2              -(Y_π + Y_C2)          | |v_b| = |0|
| -(Y_π + Y_C2 + g_m)   Y_C1 + Y_RL + Y_π + Y_C2 + g_m | |v_e|   |0|

A nonzero solution exists only if the determinant vanishes: 

(Y_L + Y_π + Y_C2)(Y_C1 + Y_RL + Y_π + Y_C2 + g_m)
- (Y_π + Y_C2)(Y_π + Y_C2 + g_m) = 0                           (3)

This is the governing equation in compact admittance form.

Step 5: Expand into a polynomial in s

Substitute YL = 1/(sL), Yπ = 1/rπ, YC1 = sC1, YC2 = sC2, YRL = 1/RL.

After straightforward algebra, equation (3) becomes 

L C1 C2 s^3
+ L(C1/r_π + C2/RL) s^2
+ (C1 + C2 + L/(RL r_π)) s
+ (g_m + 1/RL + 1/r_π) = 0                                  (4)

This is the full characteristic equation, valid before taking the large-rπ approximation.

Dimensional check. Every term in (4) has units of conductance (siemens): L C1C2s3, L(C1/rπ)s2, C1s, and gm all have units of S. This consistency is a strong algebra check.

Step 6: Oscillation condition from s = jω

At the boundary of sustained oscillation, roots lie on the imaginary axis, so set s = jω. Using j2 = -1 and j3 = -j, equation (4) becomes: 

-j ω^3 L C1 C2
- ω^2 L(C1/r_π + C2/RL)
+ j ω (C1 + C2 + L/(RL r_π))
+ (g_m + 1/RL + 1/r_π) = 0

Separate real and imaginary parts.

Imaginary part

-ω^3 L C1 C2 + ω(C1 + C2 + L/(RL r_π)) = 0

Since ω ≠ 0 for oscillation, divide by ω: 

ω0^2 = (C1 + C2 + L/(RL r_π)) / (L C1 C2)                     (5)

Real part

-ω0^2 L(C1/r_π + C2/RL) + (g_m + 1/RL + 1/r_π) = 0

Therefore the threshold condition is 

g_m + 1/RL + 1/r_π = ω0^2 L(C1/r_π + C2/RL)                   (6)

Equations (5) and (6) together describe the oscillation boundary for the general finite-rπ case.

Step 7: Simplify for large rπ

Now apply the requested approximation rπ → ∞. Mathematically, this means 1/rπ &to; 0, so all terms proportional to 1/rπ are neglected.

Equation (4) simplifies to 

L C1 C2 s^3 + (L C2/RL)s^2 + (C1 + C2)s + (g_m + 1/RL) = 0   (7)

This is the desired governing equation in the classic large-rπ limit.

Frequency of oscillation

From (5), when 1/rπ &to; 0, 

ω0^2 = (C1 + C2)/(L C1 C2) = 1/(L C_eq)                      (8)

where

C_eq = C1 C2/(C1 + C2)

So the oscillation frequency is 

ω0 = √[(C1 + C2)/(L C1 C2)]

f0 = (1/(2π)) √[(C1 + C2)/(L C1 C2)]                         (9)

This is exactly the resonance frequency of the inductor L with the series-equivalent capacitance of C1 and C2.

Start-up condition

From (6), in the large-rπ limit, 

g_m + 1/RL = ω0^2 L (C2/RL)

Now substitute (8): 

g_m + 1/RL = [(C1 + C2)/(L C1 C2)] L (C2/RL)
            = (C1 + C2)/(C1 RL)

Therefore 

g_m = C2/(C1 RL)

hence

g_m R_L = C2/C1                                                (10)

Equation (10) is the threshold equality for sustained oscillation. For oscillations to start, the small-signal growth rate must be positive, so the transistor gain must slightly exceed the threshold: 

Start-up condition:  g_m R_L > C2/C1                          (11)

Sanity checks

1) Units

  • ω02 in (8) has units s−2, as required.
  • gmRL in (10) is dimensionless, matching C2/C1.

2) Limiting-case asymptotics

  • If C2 ≪ C1, then Ceq ≈ C2 and the required gain ratio gmRL ≈ C2/C1 is small.
  • If C2 ≫ C1, then Ceq ≈ C1 and the required gain becomes large, since gmRL ≫ 1.
  • If RL → ∞, loss vanishes and the threshold on gm tends toward zero in absolute terms, consistent with weak damping.

3) Physical interpretation

The oscillation frequency is set by the resonant tank, while the ratio C2/C1 controls how much transistor gain is required. Larger C2 relative to C1 means the feedback division demands more active transconductance to overcome loss.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Reinterpreting the final formulas

  • ω02 = 1/(LCeq) says the oscillator frequency is that of an inductor with the series-equivalent capacitance of the two capacitors.
  • gmRL = C2/C1 says the active-device gain must match the feedback-divider requirement.
  • RL quantifies damping; gm quantifies energy replenishment.

Parameter dependence

Parameter Effect on frequency Effect on startup threshold
L Increasing L lowers ω0. No direct effect on the simplified threshold gmRL > C2/C1.
C1 Increasing C1 lowers ω0. Increasing C1 lowers the required gain.
C2 Increasing C2 lowers ω0. Increasing C2 raises the required gain.
RL No direct effect on the ideal large-rπ frequency. Larger RL means lower absolute threshold on gm.
rπ Finite rπ slightly perturbs frequency upward via (5). Finite rπ changes the exact threshold condition through extra loading.

Alternative derivation idea

One can also derive the threshold by treating the transistor plus feedback network as an amplifier-loop system and applying a Barkhausen-type gain-phase condition. This gives strong intuition, but the nodal determinant method used above is more precise when internal transistor resistances and loading are included.

Concept checks

  • Q: Why does the frequency not explicitly depend on gm in the large-rπ ideal result?
    A: Because gm mainly compensates loss; the reactive resonance is set by L, C1, and C2.
  • Q: What does gmRL = C2/C1 mean physically?
    A: It is the exact balance point where active gain equals the loss requirement of the feedback network.
  • Q: Why is the final condition for startup an inequality rather than an equality?
    A: Equality gives neutral sustained oscillation. Startup requires a slight excess gain so that noise or a transient grows.
  • Q: Why do we solve a homogeneous system?
    A: Because a self-oscillator has no external AC forcing; the oscillation is a natural mode of the active network.
Deeper mathematical note: what kind of equation is this?

Equation (4) is a cubic characteristic polynomial obtained from a linear state-space problem with two reactive energy-storage coordinates and one effective dissipative/active balance. In systems language, the circuit is a linear dynamical system whose poles are eigenvalues of the small-signal Jacobian. The threshold of oscillation is a Hopf-like boundary in the linearized sense: a complex-conjugate pole pair crosses the imaginary axis as the control parameter gm increases.

Why no vector operators like ∇ or Laplacian here?

This problem is a lumped-circuit problem, so the mathematical model is an ODE/algebraic network model, not a field PDE. Therefore operators such as ∇, ∇·, ∇×, and the Laplacian do not naturally appear in the reduced model. The relevant operator here is the time derivative d/dt, represented in the Laplace domain by multiplication by s.
