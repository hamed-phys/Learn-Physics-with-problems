Modified RC Phase-Shift Oscillator: Loop Gain, Start-Up Condition, and Oscillation Frequency

We analyze a three-section RC phase-shift oscillator after adding an extra resistor in series with the rightmost capacitor. The task is to find the loop gain Aβ, the feedback resistor Rf needed for oscillation to begin, and the oscillation frequency f0. The result is symbolic, then specialized to R = 10 kΩ.

Linear small-signal analysis Barkhausen criterion RC ladder network Applied electronics

Quick Summary

  • This problem is about a phase-shift oscillator: an inverting amplifier plus a frequency-selective RC feedback network.
  • The key physical idea is the Barkhausen condition: at the oscillation frequency, the loop phase must be 0° (or 360°) and the loop magnitude must be 1.
  • The governing quantity is the loop gain, Aβ(s), where A is the amplifier gain and β(s) is the RC-network transfer function.
  • Because the circuit is linearized and analyzed in sinusoidal steady state, we use impedances and nodal equations in the complex frequency variable s.
  • With the added resistor in series with the rightmost capacitor, the RC network changes from the textbook equal-section case, so both the required gain and the frequency shift.
  • The transfer function of the modified feedback network is
    β(s) = (C^3R^3 s^3) / (1 + 6CRs + 9C^2R^2s^2 + 2C^3R^3s^3)
  • The oscillation condition gives ω0RC = 1/3, so
    ω0 = 1/(3RC), f0 = 1/(6πRC).
  • At that frequency, the feedback factor is β(jω0) = -1/52, so the amplifier must provide a magnitude of 52.
  • If the op-amp stage is the usual inverting stage with |A| = Rf/R, then the start-up threshold is
    Rf = 52R = 520 kΩ (for R = 10 kΩ).

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions

R — resistance, SI unit: Ω.

C — capacitance, SI unit: F.

s — complex frequency, s = σ + jω.

ω — angular frequency, rad/s.

f — ordinary frequency, Hz, with ω = 2πf.

A(s) — amplifier gain.

β(s) — feedback-network transfer function.

Aβ(s) — loop gain.

Node voltages — unknown phasor voltages found by KCL.

Impedance of capacitor: ZC = 1/(sC).

Physical meaning of the key quantities

An oscillator does not need an external periodic input; instead, it recirculates a signal around a feedback loop. The amplifier provides energy, while the RC network selects the frequency and phase. In a phase-shift oscillator, the amplifier contributes 180° phase inversion and the RC ladder contributes another 180° at one special frequency. Then the total phase shift around the loop is 360°, which is equivalent to positive feedback.

Key laws and validity conditions

Small-signal linear model used here:
  • The op-amp (or inverting amplifier block) is treated as ideal in the frequency range of interest.
  • The amplitude stabilization circuitry is ignored, as requested.
  • All analysis is done in the linear regime before saturation becomes important.
  • Kirchhoff’s current law applies at each node because we are using lumped-element circuit theory.
  • Phasor/impedance analysis is valid because we are studying sinusoidal steady-state behavior.

Common models and approximations

  • Lumped-element approximation: physical dimensions are small compared with the signal wavelength, so voltages and currents are well described by ordinary circuit variables rather than full electromagnetic field equations.
  • Ideal amplifier approximation: very large input resistance and negligible output resistance.
  • Linear start-up analysis: we determine when oscillations begin; this is a threshold condition, not the final large-amplitude waveform.

Mini intuition examples

  1. If three identical RC sections are cascaded, loading between sections shifts the required amplifier gain above the naive isolated-stage estimate.
  2. If one section is modified by adding resistance, the phase condition occurs at a new frequency because the network time constants are redistributed.
  3. If the amplifier gain is slightly larger than the threshold, oscillations start and grow; if it is smaller, they decay.

What to watch for

  • Do not confuse β(s) with the full loop gain Aβ(s).
  • Do not apply the Barkhausen criterion before deriving the correct loaded transfer function.
  • Be careful with signs: the amplifier is inverting, so A = -K with K > 0.
  • At oscillation, the loop gain must be real and positive when written in the conventional closed-loop form.
  • Use ω in radians per second during algebra, then convert to f at the end.

Mathematical model

This is a finite-dimensional linear dynamical system. In time-domain language, each capacitor current obeys iC = C dv/dt, and each resistor current obeys iR = v/R. Because the system is linear and time-invariant, Laplace analysis converts the coupled ODE system into algebraic equations in s.

Math Insight: the core object here is a rational transfer function. The denominator is the characteristic polynomial of a third-order linear network, and the oscillation condition is equivalent to placing a closed-loop pole pair on the imaginary axis. In other words, we are solving a marginal stability problem for a linear system.
Optional deeper note: why no ∇, ∇·, or Laplacian appears here

This problem belongs to lumped circuit theory, not distributed field theory. The full electromagnetic description would involve Maxwell’s equations, including operators such as ∇· and ∇×, but under the quasi-static lumped approximation the system reduces to node voltages and branch currents. Thus the governing mathematics is a system of ODEs or algebraic equations in the phasor domain rather than PDEs.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Although this is a textbook oscillator problem, the underlying ideas matter strongly in real engineering. Oscillators are timing sources, signal generators, tone sources, sensor interfaces, and test-reference blocks.

Where it appears

  • Low-frequency instrumentation and lab signal generation
  • Audio oscillators and tone generation circuits
  • Embedded analog subsystems and sensor excitation
  • Educational/test platforms for analog design

Why engineers care

  • Frequency accuracy and drift
  • Reliable start-up across tolerances
  • Low distortion and amplitude stability
  • Cost, simplicity, and manufacturability

Practical validation

  • Oscilloscope phase and amplitude measurement
  • Frequency counter or FFT/spectrum analyzer
  • Tolerance/Monte-Carlo verification in simulation
  • Temperature and supply-voltage testing

Design trade-offs and constraints

In a real product, resistors and capacitors have tolerances, temperature coefficients, parasitics, and aging. The op-amp contributes finite bandwidth, excess phase lag, input bias currents, output swing limits, and noise. A design that barely satisfies the Barkhausen threshold in theory may fail to start in production. Engineers therefore design for start-up margin and then control amplitude with nonlinear stabilization.

From lab to product

The idealized model gives the frequency-selective skeleton of the oscillator. A practical design then adds: amplitude control, biasing, supply decoupling, component tolerancing, frequency trimming, and sometimes buffering so that the load does not pull the oscillation frequency. This is exactly where applied physics meets industrial engineering: you move from a mathematically elegant loop condition to a manufacturable, reliable, low-variation circuit.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restated clearly

We start from a three-section RC phase-shift oscillator. The modification is to add one more resistor, equal to R = 10 kΩ, in series with the rightmost capacitor C. Ignoring the amplitude stabilization network, we must:

  1. Break the loop at node X and derive the loop gain Aβ.
  2. Find the feedback resistor Rf required for oscillation to begin.
  3. Find the oscillation frequency f0.

Given quantities

  • Three capacitors of value C
  • Three shunt resistors of value R
  • Additional resistor in series with the rightmost capacitor, also R
  • Inverting amplifier block with gain A = -K
  • For the actual circuit, R = 10 kΩ

Unknowns

  • The modified feedback transfer function β(s)
  • The full loop gain Aβ(s)
  • The threshold amplifier gain K
  • The corresponding Rf
  • The oscillation frequency ω0 and f0

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Nodal analysis: the network is linear and lumped, so KCL gives an exact symbolic transfer function.
  • Impedance model: capacitors are handled naturally in Laplace/phasor form as 1/(sC).
  • Barkhausen criterion: for oscillation onset, the loop phase must be zero modulo 360° and the magnitude must be unity.

Assumptions

  • Ideal inverting amplifier in the small-signal regime
  • Amplitude control omitted, exactly as instructed
  • No loading from outside the oscillator
  • All resistors labeled R are equal, including the added series resistor
  • All capacitors are equal to C

Possible approaches

Approach 1

Direct nodal analysis of the RC ladder and then apply Barkhausen.

Pros: exact, systematic, least ambiguous.

Cons: algebra is longer.

Approach 2

ABCD / chain-matrix treatment of cascaded sections.

Pros: elegant for ladder networks.

Cons: less transparent for many students.

Approach 3

State-space / characteristic equation approach.

Pros: ties directly to dynamical systems.

Cons: more abstract than needed here.

Best choice: direct nodal analysis. It gives the cleanest path to the exact transfer function and keeps every physical current path visible.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Label the nodes of the three RC sections and choose the break point at node X.
    Goal: define the input and output of the feedback network.
  2. Replace capacitors by impedances 1/(sC).
    Goal: convert the circuit to algebraic form in s.
  3. Write KCL at each internal node.
    Tool: nodal analysis.
  4. Solve for β(s) = vout,network/vin,network.
    Meaning: this is the phase-shift network response.
  5. Form the loop gain Aβ(s) = (-K)β(s).
    Meaning: complete one loop around the oscillator.
  6. Set s = jω and enforce zero loop phase.
    Meaning: find the candidate oscillation frequency.
  7. Evaluate β(jω0).
    Meaning: determine the attenuation of the network at the phase-correct frequency.
  8. Set |Aβ| = 1 for start-up threshold.
    Meaning: solve for the minimum gain K and hence Rf.
  9. Check units and limiting behavior.
    Meaning: verify the result is physically reasonable.
Common mistakes
  • Dropping the extra series resistor from the rightmost section
  • Using the standard equal-section result K = 29 without re-deriving the modified network
  • Solving for magnitude before phase
  • Forgetting that the amplifier is inverting, so the feedback network itself must contribute another 180°

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition before calculation

In the usual three-section RC phase-shift oscillator, the ladder must attenuate the signal strongly while also providing the needed phase lag. By adding an extra resistor in series with the rightmost capacitor, we increase the “difficulty” of transmitting signal through the last section. So we expect two things qualitatively:

  • a changed oscillation frequency, because the phase condition moves, and
  • a larger required amplifier gain, because the feedback network attenuates more strongly.

Step 1: Define the network variables

Break the loop at node X. Let the injected test voltage be v0. Let the three internal node voltages of the RC ladder be v1, v2, and v3. The feedback factor is

β(s) = v3 / v0

The impedances of the three series branches are:

First section: Z1 = 1/(sC) Second section: Z2 = 1/(sC) Third section (modified): Z3 = R + 1/(sC)

Step 2: Write KCL at each node

Because the network is linear and passive, KCL is exact. Currents leaving each node sum to zero.

At node v1: (v1 - v0)/Z1 + v1/R + (v1 - v2)/Z2 = 0 At node v2: (v2 - v1)/Z2 + v2/R + (v2 - v3)/Z3 = 0 At node v3: (v3 - v2)/Z3 + v3/R = 0

Substitute Z1 = Z2 = 1/(sC) and Z3 = R + 1/(sC), then solve the three linear equations for v3/v0.

Step 3: Obtain the transfer function β(s)

β(s) = (C^3 R^3 s^3) / (1 + 6CRs + 9C^2R^2s^2 + 2C^3R^3s^3)

It is convenient to nondimensionalize using the dimensionless variable

x = sRC

Then

β(s) = x^3 / (1 + 6x + 9x^2 + 2x^3), where x = sRC.
Dimensional analysis check: every factor of sRC is dimensionless because [s] = 1/s, [R] = Ω, [C] = F, and Ω·F = s. So the transfer function is correctly dimensionless.

Step 4: Form the loop gain

Let the inverting amplifier gain be

A = -K

where K > 0. Therefore

Aβ(s) = -K (C^3 R^3 s^3) / (1 + 6CRs + 9C^2R^2s^2 + 2C^3R^3s^3)

Step 5: Apply the phase condition

Set s = jω and define the dimensionless frequency u = ωRC. Then

β(jω) = (ju)^3 / (1 + 6ju + 9(ju)^2 + 2(ju)^3) Since (ju)^2 = -u^2 and (ju)^3 = -ju^3, β(jω) = (-ju^3) / [(1 - 9u^2) + j(6u - 2u^3)].

For the loop gain Aβ to be real and positive, the feedback factor β(jω) must be real and negative, because the amplifier contributes the minus sign. The imaginary part of β vanishes when the real part of the denominator, (1 - 9u^2), is zero.

1 - 9u^2 = 0 ⇒ u = 1/3 ⇒ ω0 RC = 1/3

Since frequency is positive, we take

ω0 = 1/(3RC), f0 = 1/(6πRC)

Step 6: Evaluate β at ω = ω0

At u = 1/3, the denominator becomes purely imaginary:

6u - 2u^3 = 6(1/3) - 2(1/27) = 2 - 2/27 = 52/27.

The numerator is

-ju^3 = -j(1/27).

Therefore

β(jω0) = [-j(1/27)] / [j(52/27)] = -1/52.
This is the critical attenuation result. The feedback network provides exactly the required 180° phase shift at ω0 = 1/(3RC), but its magnitude there is only 1/52.

Step 7: Start-up condition for the amplifier gain

At the threshold of oscillation,

|Aβ| = 1.

Since |β(jω0)| = 1/52, we need

K · (1/52) = 1 ⇒ K = 52.

Thus the loop gain at the oscillation condition is

Aβ(jω0) = (+1) when K = 52

Step 8: Convert K into Rf

For the usual inverting op-amp realization of this oscillator, the amplifier magnitude is

K = Rf / R.

Therefore the minimum value for oscillation to begin is

Rf(min) = 52R

With R = 10 kΩ,

Rf(min) = 52 × 10 kΩ = 520 kΩ.

Sanity checks

1) Units

ω0 = 1/(3RC) has units of 1/s, correct. f0 = 1/(6πRC) has units of Hz, correct. Rf = 52R has units of Ω, correct.

2) Physical trend

The required gain is larger than the standard three-equal-section result. That matches intuition: the added series resistance increases attenuation.

3) Limiting-case reasoning

If the extra resistor were absent, the network would reduce to the standard equal-capacitor, equal-resistor phase-shift ladder, which needs less gain. So moving from 29 to 52 is qualitatively reasonable.

4) Sign reasoning

At the oscillation frequency, β is negative real. Multiplying by the amplifier’s minus sign makes the loop gain positive real, exactly what Barkhausen requires.

Final Answer

Modified feedback network: β(s) = (C^3 R^3 s^3) / (1 + 6CRs + 9C^2R^2s^2 + 2C^3R^3s^3) Loop gain: Aβ(s) = -K (C^3 R^3 s^3) / (1 + 6CRs + 9C^2R^2s^2 + 2C^3R^3s^3) Oscillation condition: ω0 = 1/(3RC) f0 = 1/(6πRC) At ω0: β(jω0) = -1/52 Therefore the amplifier must provide: K = 52 If K = Rf/R, then: Rf(min) = 52R For R = 10 kΩ: Rf(min) = 520 kΩ
Optional deeper note: a compact algebra trick

Once β(jω) is written as (-ju^3)/(a + jb) with a = 1 - 9u^2 and b = 6u - 2u^3, multiplying numerator and denominator by a - jb gives β = (-bu^3 - jau^3)/(a^2 + b^2). The imaginary part is zero when a = 0, immediately yielding the frequency condition.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Interpret the final formula

  • R and C appear only through the product RC, the characteristic time constant.
  • The oscillation frequency is inversely proportional to RC: larger R or larger C means slower charging/discharging and thus lower frequency.
  • The threshold gain K = 52 tells you how much the passive network attenuates at the phase-correct frequency.

Parameter dependence

If R increases

f0 ∝ 1/R, so frequency decreases.

Rf(min) = 52R, so the feedback resistor must also increase.

If C increases

f0 ∝ 1/C, so frequency decreases.

The gain threshold K = 52 does not change because it is dimensionless.

If gain exceeds 52

Oscillations grow initially.

In practice, nonlinear amplitude control eventually reduces the effective gain to unity.

Alternative derivation idea

A state-space derivation is also possible. Each capacitor voltage can be chosen as a state variable, producing a third-order linear ODE system. The oscillation condition then corresponds to a pair of closed-loop eigenvalues crossing onto the imaginary axis. This is mathematically elegant, but for this network, nodal analysis is more direct.

Concept checks

  • Q: Why is the oscillation threshold found from the linear model?
    A: Because start-up happens at small signal amplitude, before nonlinear stabilization matters.
  • Q: Why is the frequency found from the phase condition first?
    A: Because the oscillator can only sustain a sinusoid where the loop phase closes properly.
  • Q: Why does the added resistor raise the required gain?
    A: It makes the passive ladder more lossy at the needed phase condition.
  • Q: Is K = 52 the recommended operating value?
    A: It is the ideal threshold. Real designs usually choose a slightly larger value for reliable start-up.
