Design of a Cross-Coupled LC Oscillator

We determine the tank capacitance needed for a specified oscillation frequency and the minimum transistor transconductance required to overcome tank loss in a cross-coupled LC oscillator. The treatment is physics-friendly, mathematically explicit, and connected to real engineering practice.

Quick Summary

  • What the problem is about: designing a differential cross-coupled LC oscillator to oscillate at a target angular frequency ω0.
  • Key physics idea: the LC tank stores and exchanges electric and magnetic energy, while the cross-coupled transistors provide negative conductance to cancel losses.
  • Governing equations: ω0 = 1/√(LC), and startup occurs when transistor negative conductance equals or exceeds the tank loss conductance.
  • Inductor loss model: an inductor with finite quality factor Q is represented by an equivalent parallel resistance RP ≈ Qω0L.
  • Role of transistor output resistance: finite ro adds extra positive conductance, so it increases the required gm.
  • Mathematical model: a linear small-signal differential-mode admittance balance at each resonant node.
  • Result type: numeric. We find the required capacitance and the minimum transconductance per transistor.
  • Physical meaning: the oscillator starts reliably only if the active devices can replenish at least as much energy per cycle as the lossy tank dissipates.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions

L — inductance, unit: henry (H). It measures magnetic-energy storage.

C — capacitance, unit: farad (F). It measures electric-energy storage.

ω0 — oscillation angular frequency, unit: rad/s.

Q — quality factor, dimensionless. A measure of how weakly damped the resonator is.

RP — equivalent parallel resistance representing tank loss.

gm — transistor transconductance, unit: siemens (S = A/V).

ro — transistor small-signal output resistance, unit: ohm (Ω).

Physical meaning

In an ideal LC tank, energy sloshes back and forth between the electric field of the capacitor and the magnetic field of the inductor. No net energy is lost.

In a real tank, metal loss, substrate loss, and device nonidealities dissipate energy. This damping can be represented by a conductance.

The cross-coupled transistor pair acts like a source of negative conductance, injecting energy back into the tank.

Key law

For a lossless resonator, the natural frequency follows from the harmonic-oscillator equation:

d²v/dt² + (1/LC) v = 0

Thus:

ω0 = 1/√(LC)
Math Insight: the LC tank is a second-order linear oscillator. Mathematically, it is the same type of object as the mass-spring harmonic oscillator. The undamped equation d²x/dt² + ω02x = 0 has oscillatory eigenmodes. Real losses add damping; the active devices must make the net damping zero or negative at startup.

Key laws, assumptions, and validity conditions

Assumptions
  • Small-signal startup analysis is used. This means we analyze the oscillator just at the moment oscillation begins, before large-signal amplitude limiting appears.
  • The oscillation mode is differential, so the common-source node is treated as an AC virtual ground.
  • The inductor loss is represented by a parallel resistance at the operating frequency.
  • The target frequency is set mainly by the LC tank, not by transistor parasitics.
  • Linearized transistor model: drain current variation is represented by gmvgs plus vds/ro.

Common models and why they are used

Model Use Why it is helpful
Ideal LC resonator Set target frequency Captures the dominant storage physics with one simple closed-form relation.
Parallel-loss tank model Account for finite Q Startup is easiest to analyze by comparing positive and negative conductances in parallel.
Small-signal transistor model Find minimum gm Lets us express the active pair as an effective negative conductance.

Mini intuition examples

  1. Higher L or higher C means lower frequency: because the oscillator exchanges energy more slowly.
  2. Lower Q means more damping: therefore more transistor gain is needed to start oscillation.
  3. Smaller ro hurts startup: because it behaves like an additional leakage path that drains energy from the tank.

What to watch for

  • Do not confuse ordinary frequency f (Hz) with angular frequency ω (rad/s).
  • Do not forget that finite transistor ro adds positive conductance.
  • Do not mix series and parallel Q formulas without stating the approximation.
  • The startup condition is a small-signal criterion, not the final large-signal operating condition.
Optional deeper note: exact versus approximate inductor-loss conversion

If an inductor is modeled as a series pair L and Rs, then Q = ωL/Rs. The exact equivalent parallel resistance at that frequency is

RP,exact = Rs(1 + Q²)

Since Rs = ωL/Q, this becomes

RP,exact = (ωL/Q)(1 + Q²) = ωL(Q + 1/Q)

For Q ≫ 1, the term 1/Q is small, so RP ≈ QωL. Here Q = 10, so the approximation is quite reasonable.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry

  • RF transceivers: local oscillators and voltage-controlled oscillators in wireless chips for phones, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, satellite links, and radar front ends.
  • Clock generation: on-chip frequency synthesis and timing references for processors, serializers, high-speed links, and mixed-signal SoCs.
  • Instrumentation: precision signal sources, test equipment, spectrum analyzers, and sensor interfaces.

Why engineers care

Performance

Oscillators affect phase noise, startup margin, power consumption, tuning range, and frequency accuracy.

Reliability and cost

Poor startup margin can cause production failures across process, voltage, and temperature corners. Oversizing devices raises current and chip area.

Measurement and validation in practice

  • S-parameter and impedance extraction for inductors and tanks.
  • Phase-noise and startup-time measurements in lab characterization.
  • Corner simulation across process-voltage-temperature variations.
  • Parasitic extraction from layout to capture real on-chip wiring and substrate effects.

Design trade-offs and constraints

Constraint Effect Design consequence
Finite inductor Q More loss Requires larger gm or more current
Smaller transistor ro Extra tank loading Raises startup requirement
Process tolerances Frequency shift Usually needs tuning elements such as varactors or switched capacitors
Power budget Limits bias current Pushes design toward high-Q passives and efficient device sizing

From lab to product

The ideal LC model is only the starting point. Real products include parasitic capacitances, bond-pad effects, package inductance, substrate loss, temperature variation, and amplitude stabilization. Engineers therefore design with margin: the startup condition in production is usually stronger than the bare mathematical minimum.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restated clearly

We are given a cross-coupled LC oscillator. The required oscillation angular frequency is ω0 = 20 Grad/s = 20 × 109 rad/s. The available integrated inductor has L = 5 nH and Q = 10. Each transistor has output resistance ro = 5 kΩ. We must find:

  1. the required capacitance C for resonance at the specified frequency,
  2. the minimum transconductance gm per transistor needed for startup.
Given quantities
  • ω0 = 20 × 109 rad/s
  • L = 5 × 10-9 H
  • Q = 10
  • ro = 5 × 103 Ω
Unknowns
  • Tank capacitance C
  • Minimum startup transconductance gm,min

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • LC resonance: applies because the oscillation frequency is determined primarily by the tank.
  • Quality-factor loss model: applies because the inductor is nonideal and its Q is specified.
  • Small-signal startup criterion: applies because the question asks for the minimum required gm, which is a startup threshold quantity.

We do not need a large-signal nonlinear amplitude analysis, because the problem does not ask for steady-state amplitude or waveform distortion.

Mathematical model

Model statement

Let v1(t) and v2(t) be the two tank node voltages. In differential oscillation, v2(t) = -v1(t). The relevant domain is time t ≥ 0. The initial condition is a small noise or perturbation that seeds startup.

The tank obeys a lumped-element ODE, not a field PDE. Thus operators such as ∇ or Δ are not needed here because the system is modeled as spatially lumped rather than distributed.

At small signal, the node-admittance balance is the essential mathematical object:

Ytank(ω) + Yloss + Yactive = 0

At resonance, the susceptances cancel, leaving a conductance-balance condition.

Assumptions

  • Differential symmetry is used, so one half-circuit represents the full oscillator mode.
  • The tail current source is ideal enough to keep the source node approximately at AC ground for differential signals.
  • The loss of the tank is dominated by the inductor Q and transistor output resistance.
  • Parasitic capacitances are neglected unless already absorbed into the stated C.

Possible approaches

Approach Idea Pros Cons
Direct resonance + conductance balance Use ω0 = 1/√(LC) and compare positive and negative conductances Fast, transparent, standard for design Depends on small-signal assumptions
Differential negative-resistance derivation Compute the equivalent negative resistance of the cross-coupled pair seen by the tank Gives strong physical intuition More algebra than needed
Full state-space eigenvalue analysis Form linear ODE system and require a nondecaying mode Most rigorous Overkill for this numeric design problem

Best choice: the direct resonance + conductance-balance approach. It is mathematically clean, physically transparent, and exactly matched to the asked quantities.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Set the resonant frequency relation.
    Tool: ω0 = 1/√(LC).
    Meaning: links the desired oscillation rate to the energy-storage elements.
  2. Solve for the required capacitor.
    Tool: algebraic rearrangement.
    Meaning: determines the needed electric-energy storage for the chosen inductor.
  3. Translate inductor Q into an equivalent parallel loss resistance.
    Tool: RP ≈ Qω0L.
    Meaning: converts “how lossy the inductor is” into an actual conductance seen by the tank.
  4. Add transistor output-resistance loading.
    Tool: parallel conductance addition.
    Meaning: captures extra energy leakage from finite ro.
  5. Write the startup condition.
    Tool: negative conductance from cross-coupled pair must at least cancel total positive conductance.
    Meaning: zero net damping at threshold.
  6. Compute the minimum gm.
    Tool: solve the conductance balance numerically.
    Meaning: the smallest small-signal device strength that lets oscillation start.
  7. Do sanity checks.
    Tool: unit analysis, limiting cases, physical interpretation.
    Meaning: confirms the result is not just algebraically correct but physically meaningful.
Common mistakes
  • Using f instead of ω without the factor 2π.
  • Forgetting that conductances add in parallel: Gtot = 1/RP + 1/ro.
  • Using the wrong sign for the active-device contribution.
  • Reporting capacitance in farads without converting to picofarads.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition before calculating

The given inductor is small, only 5 nH, while the desired angular frequency is very high, 20 × 109 rad/s. So we expect the required capacitance to be small, in the sub-picofarad range. Also, since Q = 10 is not extremely high, the tank has noticeable loss. Therefore the cross-coupled pair must supply a nontrivial negative conductance. Finite ro adds extra loading, so the required gm should be somewhat larger than the tank-loss conductance alone.

Step 1: Find the required capacitance

For an LC resonator, the resonance condition is

ω0 = 1/√(LC)

Solve this for C. Since each symbol is defined:

  • ω0 = target angular frequency,
  • L = given inductance,
  • C = unknown capacitance.
ω0 = 1/√(LC) ω02 = 1/(LC) C = 1/(ω02L)

Substitute the numerical values:

C = 1 / [ (20 × 109)2 (5 × 10-9) ]

Now calculate carefully:

(20 × 109)2 = 400 × 1018 = 4 × 1020 (ω02L) = (4 × 1020)(5 × 10-9) = 20 × 1011 = 2 × 1012 Therefore, C = 1 / (2 × 1012) F = 0.5 × 10-12 F = 5 × 10-13 F
Capacitance result:
C = 0.5 pF

Step 2: Convert inductor Q into an equivalent parallel resistance

The tank loss is represented by an equivalent parallel resistor RP. For a reasonably high-Q inductor, the standard approximation at the operating frequency is

RP ≈ Q ω0 L

This is valid because at a fixed frequency the inductor loss can be mapped from series form to parallel form, and for Q ≫ 1, the leading behavior is proportional to QωL. Here Q = 10, so the approximation is acceptable for design-level work.

Substitute the numbers:

RP ≈ (10)(20 × 109)(5 × 10-9) Ω
(20 × 109)(5 × 10-9) = 100 Therefore, RP ≈ 10 × 100 = 1000 Ω
Equivalent tank-loss resistance:
RP ≈ 1.0 kΩ
Optional deeper note: exact value with the series-to-parallel conversion

Since Q = ωL/Rs, the series loss is

Rs = ωL/Q = 100/10 = 10 Ω

Then the exact parallel equivalent at that frequency is

RP,exact = Rs(1 + Q2) = 10(101) = 1010 Ω

This is very close to 1.0 kΩ, so the approximation is fully consistent here.

Step 3: Build the conductance balance for startup

At resonance, the reactive parts cancel. What remains is a comparison of conductances:

  • Positive conductance from the tank loss: 1/RP
  • Positive conductance from finite transistor output resistance: 1/ro
  • Negative conductance supplied by the cross-coupled action: approximately -gm per node in the differential half-circuit

Why is the active contribution -gm here? Because by symmetry, in differential mode v2 = -v1. For the left transistor, id1 = gmvgs1 + vds1/ro. Since the gate of the left transistor is tied to the right node, vgs1 = v2 = -v1, and vds1 = v1. Hence:

id1 = gm(-v1) + v1/ro = (-gm + 1/ro)v1

Therefore the transistor pair appears, at each node in the differential half-circuit, as a conductance (-gm + 1/ro). Adding the tank loss gives total small-signal conductance

Gnet = 1/RP + 1/ro - gm

The startup threshold is the point of zero net damping:

Gnet = 0

So the minimum transconductance is

gm,min = 1/RP + 1/ro

Step 4: Compute the minimum transconductance

Now substitute the numbers:

1/RP = 1/1000 = 0.001 S = 1.0 mS 1/ro = 1/5000 = 0.0002 S = 0.2 mS
gm,min = 1.0 mS + 0.2 mS = 1.2 mS
Minimum startup transconductance per transistor:
gm,min = 1.2 mS

Dimensional analysis check

Capacitance

From C = 1/(ω02L):

[ω02L] = s-2 · H = s-2 · (V s/A) = V/(A s) = 1/F

So C indeed has units of farads.

Transconductance

From gm,min = 1/RP + 1/ro, each term has units

1/Ω = S

So the result is correctly in siemens.

Limiting-case sanity checks

  • If the inductor were ideal (Q → ∞), then RP → ∞ and the tank-loss term would vanish. The required gm would be reduced.
  • If the transistor output resistance were infinite (ro → ∞), then the extra loading term disappears and gm,min → 1/RP = 1.0 mS.
  • If Q were lower, then RP would be smaller, so the required gm would be larger. That is physically consistent.

Plain-language interpretation

The oscillator needs a capacitance of 0.5 pF to resonate with the given 5 nH inductor at the required angular frequency. Because the tank and the transistors both dissipate energy, each transistor must be biased to provide at least 1.2 mS of transconductance so that the cross-coupled pair can cancel the total damping and allow oscillation to start.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the final formulas

Frequency-setting formula
C = 1/(ω02L)

This says the required capacitance scales inversely with both inductance and the square of frequency. Doubling the target frequency cuts the required capacitance by a factor of four.

Startup formula
gm,min = 1/RP + 1/ro

This is a conductance-balance law. Every dissipative path adds a positive conductance, and the active device must match that total with negative conductance.

Parameter dependence

Parameter Increase it Effect on result
ω0 Higher target frequency C decreases as 1/ω02; RP rises as ω0 if Q and L are fixed
L Larger inductance C decreases as 1/L; RP increases linearly if Q and ω0 are fixed
Q Better inductor quality RP increases, so gm,min decreases
ro Larger output resistance Less loading, so gm,min decreases

Alternative derivation idea

One can also derive the startup condition by writing a second-order ODE for the tank voltage with an effective damping coefficient. In that form, the active pair enters as a term that reduces the damping. Startup occurs when the damping coefficient crosses zero. This is mathematically the same condition as the conductance balance used above.

Math Insight: the startup threshold is an eigenvalue crossing. In a linearized dynamical system, the time dependence is of the form est. At the threshold, the real part of s changes sign from negative (decay) to zero and then positive (growth). The condition gm = 1/RP + 1/ro is exactly the boundary between damped and growing oscillatory solutions.

Concept checks

  • Q: Why is gm,min not just 1/RP?
    A: Because finite ro provides an additional loss path, adding conductance 1/ro.
  • Q: If the oscillator does not start in silicon but the math says it should, what is a likely reason?
    A: Parasitics, process spread, and layout-induced loss can reduce the real startup margin, so designers usually choose gm above the ideal minimum.
  • Q: Does the formula for C depend on transistor gm?
    A: Not in the ideal first-order resonance design. gm sets startup, while LC sets frequency.
  • Q: What kind of mathematical system is this?
    A: A lumped, linearized, second-order dynamical system with gain and damping.

Boxed result:

C = 0.5 pF gm,min = 1.2 mS

This is the minimum small-signal value for startup. In practical design, engineers usually choose a larger value to ensure robust operation over process, voltage, temperature, and parasitic variation.
