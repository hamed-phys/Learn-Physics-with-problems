Focus: small-signal Colpitts oscillator, nodal/Laplace analysis, startup criterion, and physical interpretation.

Colpitts Oscillator in Fig. P18.27

We derive the governing small-signal equation, extract the oscillation frequency, and find the startup condition on gmRL. The solution is symbolic, physically interpreted, and mathematically justified.

Topic: Electronics / Applied Physics Model: Lumped linearized small-signal circuit Method: Nodal analysis in the Laplace domain Result: Symbolic threshold + resonance formula

Quick Summary

  • This problem is about a Colpitts oscillator: an active transistor plus an LC resonant network with capacitive feedback.
  • The key physics idea is that the transistor supplies negative resistance / energy compensation to balance tank losses, while the inductor and split capacitors set the natural oscillation frequency.
  • Using the small-signal model and nodal analysis, the governing characteristic equation is a third-order polynomial in s.
  • The oscillation frequency is found by setting s = jω and requiring the imaginary part of the characteristic equation to vanish.
  • With rπ large, the frequency becomes the standard Colpitts result: ω02 = (C1 + C2)/(LC1C2).
  • The startup condition simplifies to gmRL > C2/C1. Equality gives the threshold for sustained sinusoidal oscillation in the linear model.
  • Physically, increasing gm or RL helps startup; changing the capacitor ratio changes both feedback strength and threshold.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Physical meaning of the topic

A Colpitts oscillator is a self-excited resonant circuit. The LC tank prefers to oscillate near its natural frequency, but real losses would normally damp the motion. The transistor supplies energy back into the tank in phase with the oscillation so that the total loop gain reaches unity and the net damping becomes zero.

Intuition: the inductor and capacitors form the “swing,” while the transistor acts like the periodic push. If the push is too weak, oscillation dies out. If it is just right, a sustained sinusoid is possible.

Key laws and validity conditions

  • KCL (Kirchhoff’s current law): valid because we use a lumped circuit model at frequencies where dimensions are small compared with wavelength.
  • Linearized transistor small-signal model: valid around a DC bias point and during startup when signal amplitudes are small.
  • Laplace / phasor analysis: valid for linear time-invariant small-signal dynamics.
  • Neglect of Rf: given by the problem as Rf ≫ ω0L, so it does not affect the AC loop significantly.

Mathematical model

We treat the circuit as a lumped-parameter dynamical system on the time domain t ≥ 0. Let:

  • v1(t) = small-signal voltage at the upper resonant node (base node / top of L)
  • v2(t) = small-signal voltage at the lower resonant node (collector / output node / bottom of L)
  • iL(t) = inductor current from node 1 to node 2

The state vector can be taken as x(t) = [v1(t), v2(t), iL(t)]T. Initial conditions are the initial capacitor voltages and inductor current: v1(0), v2(0), iL(0).

In operator form, the linearized homogeneous dynamics can be written as M(d/dt)\,x(t)=0, or equivalently after Laplace transform, A(s)X(s)=0. Nontrivial solutions exist only when det A(s)=0, which is the governing characteristic equation.

Math Insight: the core equation is a characteristic polynomial of a third-order linear dynamical system. Oscillation corresponds to a pair of roots on the imaginary axis, s = ±jω0. In control language, startup occurs when active-device gain cancels passive damping; in dynamical-systems language, a stable focus moves to the imaginary axis.

Common approximations and why they are used

  • Large coupling/bypass capacitors as shorts: this isolates the AC behavior from the DC bias network.
  • Large rπ approximation: simplifies algebra and reflects weak loading of the tank by the transistor input.
  • Small-signal startup analysis: ignores amplitude limiting; later nonlinearities clamp the amplitude in a real oscillator.

Mini intuition examples

Example 1: If gm=0, the tank is passive only. The mode is damped because loading remains, so oscillation cannot self-start.
Example 2: If C1 is made very small, the capacitor ratio changes strongly. The feedback fraction changes and the startup condition becomes harder to satisfy.
Example 3: If RL increases, loading decreases. That means less loss and an easier startup condition.

What to watch for

  • Do not mix up the threshold condition for neutral oscillation with the strict startup inequality.
  • Do not forget that RL already includes ro as stated.
  • Use the correct transistor current direction: the dependent current source is gmvπ from collector to emitter.
  • The final symbolic frequency is determined from the imaginary part when s=jω, not from the real part.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry

  • RF communication front-ends: local oscillators in transmitters, receivers, mixers, and frequency synthesizers.
  • Sensor electronics: inductive and resonant sensing circuits, wireless tags, proximity systems.
  • Test and measurement equipment: reference oscillators, signal injection stages, tunable resonant sources.
  • Industrial controls: clocking, timing, carrier generation, and resonant converters in some analog subsystems.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: frequency stability, phase noise, amplitude stability.
  • Reliability: startup must occur robustly across temperature, aging, and transistor spread.
  • Cost: capacitor ratio and inductor quality factor affect BOM and manufacturability.
  • Efficiency: enough gain for startup, but not excessive gain that worsens distortion and power consumption.

Typical measurement and validation methods

  • Small-signal AC analysis and loop-gain estimation in simulation.
  • Network-analyzer or impedance measurements of the resonant network.
  • Startup transient measurement on an oscilloscope to verify positive amplitude growth.
  • Spectrum-analyzer measurement of oscillation frequency and phase-noise behavior.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Real inductors have finite Q, parasitic resistance, and self-capacitance.
  • Capacitors have tolerances and temperature coefficients, shifting ω0.
  • Transistor gm varies with bias current and temperature.
  • Too much excess gain improves startup but increases waveform distortion and can raise phase noise.
From lab to product: the ideal symbolic model gives the first design target, but real products add parasitic resistances, device capacitances, PCB stray inductance/capacitance, bias stabilization, and amplitude-control mechanisms. Engineers usually design for margin, not just the exact startup threshold.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restated clearly

We are given the Colpitts oscillator of Fig. P18.27 and asked to derive the equation governing its small-signal operation, then use that equation to find:

  1. the oscillation frequency, and
  2. the condition on gmRL that ensures oscillations will start.

We must include ro inside RL, neglect Rf, and simplify the final expressions by assuming rπ is large.

Given quantities

  • L, C1, C2
  • Transistor small-signal parameters gm, rπ
  • Collector load RL including output resistance effects
  • Rf negligible in AC because Rf ≫ ω0L

Unknowns

  • The governing characteristic equation in s
  • The oscillation frequency ω0
  • The startup condition on gmRL

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Small-signal hybrid-π model: because the oscillator is analyzed near startup, where the transistor is linearized about its bias point.
  • Nodal analysis: because the resonant network is naturally described by node voltages v1, v2.
  • Laplace-domain characteristic equation: because oscillation is a property of the natural modes of the homogeneous linear system.

Assumptions

  • All large coupling/bypass capacitors marked ∞ are AC shorts.
  • Rf is neglected for AC analysis.
  • The DC current source is AC open, so it does not load the tank in small signal.
  • The transistor is modeled by gmvπ, rπ, and ro absorbed into RL.
  • Linear small-signal analysis determines startup threshold and frequency; nonlinear amplitude limiting is not included.

Possible approaches

Approach A: Nodal analysis in the Laplace domain
Pros: direct, systematic, gives governing polynomial immediately.
Cons: algebra can be longer.
Approach B: Barkhausen loop-gain decomposition
Pros: intuitive for startup and feedback ratio.
Cons: easier to miss loading by rπ and RL.
Approach C: State-space matrix and eigenvalues
Pros: mathematically elegant and general.
Cons: more abstract than needed here.

Best choice: Approach A. It is the cleanest way to obtain the exact governing equation, and from that equation the oscillation frequency and startup condition follow transparently.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Identify AC nodes: define the two resonant node voltages v1 and v2.
  2. Replace components by admittances: use sC for capacitors and 1/(sL) for the inductor branch.
  3. Insert the transistor small-signal model: use rπ from base to ground and current source gmvπ from collector to emitter.
  4. Write KCL at both nodes: this creates two homogeneous linear equations in V1 and V2.
  5. Set the determinant to zero: nontrivial natural modes require det A(s)=0.
  6. Substitute s=jω: separate real and imaginary parts.
  7. Use the imaginary part for frequency: this gives ω0.
  8. Use the real part for threshold gain: this gives the required gm or gmRL.
  9. Apply the large-rπ simplification: recover the standard Colpitts formulas.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting that the large capacitor ties the base AC node to the upper tank node.
  • Using jωL instead of 1/(jωL) in admittance form.
  • Confusing threshold equality with startup inequality: startup requires “greater than” threshold.
  • Dropping rπ too early before forming the exact equation.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

1) Physical intuition before calculation

We expect the resonant frequency to be the usual Colpitts value based on the inductor in parallel with the series-equivalent of C1 and C2, at least when transistor loading is weak. We also expect a threshold on gm because the transistor must compensate the losses represented by RL and the finite input loading rπ.

2) AC small-signal model

Let the upper tank node be V1 and the lower tank/output node be V2. Because the left large capacitor is an AC short, the base is at node 1, so vπ = V1.

The relevant admittances are:

  • YL = 1/(sL) between nodes 1 and 2
  • YC2 = sC2 from node 1 to ground
  • YC1 = sC1 from node 2 to ground
  • Gπ = 1/rπ from node 1 to ground
  • GL = 1/RL from node 2 to ground

3) Write KCL at node 1

Currents leaving node 1 are through C2, through rπ, and through the inductor branch:

sC2V1 + GπV1 + YL(V1 - V2) = 0
( sC2 + Gπ + YL )V1 - YLV2 = 0

This is valid because KCL is linear and each branch current is proportional to node voltage in the small-signal model.

4) Write KCL at node 2

Currents leaving node 2 are through C1, through RL, through the inductor branch, and through the transistor controlled current source gmV1 to ground:

sC1V2 + GLV2 + YL(V2 - V1) + gmV1 = 0
( gm - YL )V1 + ( sC1 + GL + YL )V2 = 0

5) Matrix form and governing equation

The two nodal equations can be written in matrix form:

[
  ( sC2 + Gπ + YL )     -YL
  ( gm - YL )       ( sC1 + GL + YL )
] [
  V1
  V2
] = 0
det A(s)=0

Now evaluate the determinant:

( sC2 + Gπ + YL )(sC1 + GL + YL) - (-YL)(gm - YL) = 0
( sC2 + Gπ )(sC1 + GL) + YL[s(C1+C2) + Gπ + GL + gm] = 0
Since YL=1/(sL), multiplying by sL gives
sL(sC2 + Gπ)(sC1 + GL) + s(C1 + C2) + Gπ + GL + gm = 0

Expanding the polynomial gives the governing characteristic equation explicitly:

s3LC1C2 + s2L(C2/RL + C1/rπ) + s(C1 + C2 + L/(rπRL)) + (gm + 1/RL + 1/rπ) = 0

Dimensional check: each term must have the same dimensions because they are added. For example, s3LC1C2 has dimension s-3·H·F·F = s/Ohm, and the constant term gm + 1/RL + 1/rπ also has dimension 1/Ohm; since the characteristic equation comes from a determinant of admittances after multiplying by sL, the overall scale is consistent.

Optional deeper note: time-domain interpretation

The Laplace polynomial corresponds to a third-order homogeneous ODE. If one projects the dynamics onto any state component, the characteristic operator is

LC1C2d3y/dt3 + L(C2/RL + C1/rπ)d2y/dt2 + (C1 + C2 + L/(rπRL))dy/dt + (gm + 1/RL + 1/rπ)y=0

Oscillation corresponds to undamped sinusoidal modes, so the characteristic roots must lie on the imaginary axis.

6) Find the oscillation frequency

For a sustained sinusoid in the linear model, set s = jω. Then separate real and imaginary parts. Using (jω)3 = -jω3 and (jω)2 = -ω2, the polynomial becomes:

-jω3LC1C2 - ω2L(C2/RL + C1/rπ) + jω(C1 + C2 + L/(rπRL)) + (gm + 1/RL + 1/rπ)=0

The imaginary part must vanish:

-ω3LC1C2 + ω(C1 + C2 + L/(rπRL))=0
ω[-ω2LC1C2 + C1 + C2 + L/(rπRL)] = 0
For the nonzero oscillatory solution ω≠0,
ω02 = (C1 + C2 + L/(rπRL)) /(LC1C2)

This is the exact frequency condition from the linearized model.

Large-rπ simplification

If rπ is large, then L/(rπRL) is negligible, so:

ω02 ≈ (C1 + C2)/(LC1C2) = 1/(LCeq)
where Ceq = C1C2/(C1 + C2)

This is exactly the standard Colpitts resonance, since the two capacitors act as a series equivalent across the inductor.

7) Find the condition on gmRL

Now use the real part:

-ω02L(C2/RL + C1/rπ) + (gm + 1/RL + 1/rπ)=0
Therefore the threshold value is
gm,th = ω02L(C2/RL + C1/rπ) - 1/RL - 1/rπ

This equality corresponds to the ideal threshold for sustained sinusoidal oscillation in the linear model. For actual startup from noise or a perturbation, we need the active term to be slightly larger:

gm > gm,th

Large-rπ simplification of the threshold

When rπ is large, drop all terms containing 1/rπ. Then:

gm,th ≈ ω02L(C2/RL) - 1/RL
Using ω02L = (C1+C2)/(C1C2),
gm,th ≈ ((C1+C2)/(C1C2))(C2/RL) - 1/RL
gm,th ≈ (C1+C2)/(C1RL) - 1/RL = C2/(C1RL)
Hence the threshold equality is
gm,thRL ≈ C2/C1
and the startup condition is
gmRL > C2/C1

Final Answer

Governing characteristic equation:

sL(sC2 + 1/rπ)(sC1 + 1/RL) + s(C1 + C2) + 1/rπ + 1/RL + gm = 0
equivalently
s3LC1C2 + s2L(C2/RL + C1/rπ) + s(C1 + C2 + L/(rπRL)) + (gm + 1/RL + 1/rπ)=0

Exact oscillation frequency condition:

ω02 = (C1 + C2 + L/(rπRL)) /(LC1C2)

Threshold condition (exact form):

gm,th = ω02L(C2/RL + C1/rπ) - 1/RL - 1/rπ

With large rπ:

ω0 ≈ sqrt((C1+C2)/(LC1C2))
gm,thRL ≈ C2/C1
startup requires gmRL > C2/C1

8) Sanity checks

  • Limiting case, large rπ: the result reduces to the textbook Colpitts frequency using the series-equivalent capacitance. Good.
  • Loading trend: if RL gets smaller (heavier loading), the required gm increases. Physically correct.
  • Capacitor-ratio trend: if C2/C1 increases, the startup condition becomes harder. This matches the idea that the feedback division ratio matters.
  • Sign reasoning: the active device appears in the constant term as a conductance-like contribution; the threshold condition exactly balances loss.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

What each term controls

  • LC1C2 in the cubic term reflects the three energy-storage elements, so the system is third order.
  • C1+C2 controls the main resonant scale; together they form the series-equivalent capacitance seen by L.
  • 1/RL and 1/rπ are damping/load terms.
  • gm is the active compensation term that can cancel damping and enable oscillation.

Parameter dependence

  • Increase L: lowers ω0.
  • Increase either capacitor: generally lowers ω0 because the equivalent tank capacitance increases.
  • Increase RL: reduces loading, so startup is easier.
  • Increase gm: increases loop activity and promotes startup.
  • Make rπ finite: adds input loading, slightly perturbs frequency, and raises the startup requirement.

Nondimensionalization / scaling viewpoint

A useful dimensionless gain parameter is Γ = gmRL. Then, in the large-rπ limit, the startup criterion becomes Γ > C2/C1. This is powerful because it separates the design into:

  • a transistor/load strength parameter Γ, and
  • a passive feedback ratio C2/C1.

That is a compact engineering design rule.

Limiting-case asymptotics

  • As rπ &to; ∞: ω02 = (C1+C2 + L/(rπRL))/(LC1C2) = (C1+C2)/(LC1C2) + O(rπ-1) .
  • As RL &to; ∞: loss vanishes, so the threshold on gm tends toward zero in the idealized model.

Alternative derivation idea

A brief alternative is to treat the transistor as providing an effective negative conductance and combine that with the resonator’s equivalent loss conductance. Then the startup condition is found by requiring the net conductance at resonance to be negative. This is physically elegant, but the nodal method used above is more exact and makes the role of rπ explicit.

Concept checks

  • Q: Why does the frequency depend mainly on L, C1, and C2?
    A: Because those are the energy-storage elements that set the natural mode of the tank.
  • Q: Why is the characteristic equation third order?
    A: Two capacitors plus one inductor give three independent energy-storage states.
  • Q: Why is startup an inequality and not an equality?
    A: Equality gives neutral balance; real startup from small noise requires slight net gain.
  • Q: What does a large rπ physically mean?
    A: The transistor input draws little current, so it barely loads the tank.
