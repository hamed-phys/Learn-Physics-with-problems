Sinusoidal Oscillator with a Second-Order Bandpass Filter

We analyze an oscillator made from a frequency-independent amplifier of gain A > 0 and a second-order bandpass filter with pole frequency ω0, pole quality factor Q, and positive center-frequency gain K. The goals are to find the oscillation frequency and the condition for sustained oscillation.

Quick Summary

  • The problem is about a feedback oscillator built from an amplifier and a second-order bandpass network.
  • The key physics and engineering idea is the Barkhausen oscillation condition: loop phase must match, and loop magnitude must equal unity.
  • The governing object is the loop transfer function L(s) = A H(s).
  • For a standard second-order bandpass filter, the phase becomes exactly suitable for oscillation at ω = ω0.
  • The result is fully symbolic.
  • Physically, the filter selects the oscillation frequency, while the amplifier level determines whether oscillations die out, grow, or remain sustained.
Key result preview: the oscillation frequency is ωosc = ω0, and the sustained-oscillation condition is A K = 1 for the standard positive-feedback interpretation.

PART 0 — Concept Primer

Core Definitions

Symbol Meaning Typical SI Unit
A Amplifier gain, assumed frequency-independent and positive dimensionless
H(s) Bandpass filter transfer function dimensionless
K Center-frequency gain of the filter dimensionless
ω0 Pole frequency / natural frequency of the second-order section rad/s
Q Quality factor, measuring resonance sharpness dimensionless
s Laplace variable, s = σ + jω s-1
L(s) Loop gain, L(s)=A H(s) dimensionless

Physical Meaning

A sinusoidal oscillator is a system that creates a periodic output without an external periodic input. In linearized analysis, it is understood through feedback. The amplifier provides gain, while the bandpass filter provides frequency selection. Only at frequencies where the loop returns the signal with the correct phase and the correct magnitude can the waveform maintain itself.

Key Laws / Principles

Barkhausen condition for sustained sinusoidal oscillation at angular frequency ω: 
L(jω) = A H(jω) = 1
Equivalently:
  • Phase condition: ∠L(jω) = 2πn
  • Magnitude condition: |L(jω)| = 1
This is the standard steady-state criterion for linear feedback oscillators.

The second-order bandpass filter is commonly modeled in canonical form as 

H(s) = K [(ω0/Q)s] / [s^2 + (ω0/Q)s + ω0^2]

This form is chosen because:

  • it is the standard normalized second-order bandpass structure,
  • its gain at ω = ω0 is exactly K,
  • it exhibits resonance behavior governed by Q.

Mathematical Model

We work in the frequency-domain / Laplace-domain setting for a linear time-invariant system:

  • Variable: complex frequency s, and later s = jω for steady sinusoidal response.
  • Domain: ω > 0 because the problem asks for positive oscillation frequency.
  • Model type: algebraic frequency-response problem derived from a second-order linear ODE.
  • Boundary/initial conditions: not needed explicitly, because steady-state sinusoidal oscillation is determined by the closed-loop characteristic condition rather than a forced initial-value solution.
Math Insight: the core equation is not a PDE or field equation here; it is a complex-frequency eigencondition for a feedback loop. In effect, we are finding a frequency at which the loop reproduces its own sinusoidal eigenmode without attenuation or phase mismatch.

Common Approximations / Modeling Choices

  • Linear small-signal analysis: used to determine the oscillation frequency and threshold condition.
  • Frequency-independent amplifier gain: valid over the narrow band near oscillation.
  • Idealized bandpass form: real circuits have parasitics and finite amplifier bandwidth, but this canonical model is the first-order design model.

Mini Intuition Examples

  1. If AK < 1, each loop trip shrinks the signal: oscillations decay.
  2. If AK > 1, each loop trip amplifies the signal: amplitude grows until nonlinear saturation appears.
  3. If AK = 1, the loop exactly replaces losses: ideal sustained oscillation.
What to watch for:
  • Do not confuse ω0 with a 3-dB cutoff. Here it is the second-order pole frequency and also the resonance center frequency of the canonical bandpass form.
  • Do not impose only the magnitude condition. The phase condition is equally necessary.
  • Do not let Q distract you into thinking it changes the oscillation frequency here; it changes resonance sharpness, but the phase condition still selects ω0.
Deeper note: relation to a time-domain ODE

The canonical denominator s^2 + (ω0/Q)s + ω0^2 corresponds to a damped second-order system. In time domain, that structure comes from a linear ODE of the form 

x¨ + (ω0/Q) x˙ + ω0^2 x = input-dependent forcing

The bandpass numerator introduces a derivative-type weighting, which is why the filter favors midband frequencies and suppresses both very low and very high frequencies.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Oscillator analysis matters far beyond textbook circuits. A bandpass-based oscillator is a core example of how selective resonance + gain compensation produce stable periodic signals in real engineering systems.

Where this appears in industry

  • Communication systems: carrier generation, IF stages, local oscillators.
  • Instrumentation: signal sources, lock-in amplifiers, test equipment.
  • Sensors and industrial electronics: resonant sensing, timing references, filter-based tone generation.
  • Control and mechatronics: frequency-selective feedback loops and resonant compensation networks.

Why engineers care

  • Frequency accuracy affects interoperability and measurement precision.
  • Loop-gain margin affects startup reliability and amplitude stability.
  • Component tolerances affect manufacturability and calibration cost.
  • Noise and nonlinearity affect spectral purity and total system performance.

Typical measurement / validation methods

  • Bode or network analysis of the filter.
  • Loop-gain estimation near startup.
  • Oscilloscope and spectrum analyzer measurement of steady-state waveform and phase noise.
  • Production test under temperature and supply variation.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Higher Q improves selectivity but may worsen sensitivity to tolerances.
  • Higher startup gain helps oscillation begin, but too much causes distortion and clipping.
  • Real amplifiers have finite bandwidth and phase shift, so the ideal condition AK=1 is only approximate in practice.
From lab to product: the ideal model uses constant A, exact K, and ideal second-order dynamics. A real product adds automatic amplitude control, device parasitics, temperature drift, supply sensitivity, and manufacturing spread. Engineers therefore design for startup margin and then rely on nonlinearity or control circuitry to settle the amplitude.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

Problem restated

We are given an oscillator loop made of a positive constant-gain amplifier and a second-order bandpass filter. We must determine:

  1. the frequency at which oscillation occurs,
  2. the condition relating A and K for sustained oscillation.
Given Unknowns What must be shown
A > 0,
second-order bandpass filter,
ω0, Q, K > 0 		ωosc,
oscillation condition on A and K 		Apply the loop condition and solve symbolically

Relevant principles

  • Barkhausen criterion applies because this is a linear feedback oscillator problem.
  • Frequency response of a second-order bandpass applies because the filter determines phase and magnitude versus frequency.
  • Dimensional scaling using x = ω/ω0 is useful because it nondimensionalizes the algebra.

Principles that are not central here:

  • Transient initial-condition analysis is not needed, because the question asks for steady sinusoidal oscillation conditions.
  • Distributed-field operators such as ∇, ∇·, ∇×, or a Laplacian are not relevant because this is a lumped-parameter circuit model, not a spatial field problem.
Assumptions
  • Linear time-invariant small-signal model.
  • Positive-feedback interpretation of the oscillator loop.
  • The canonical second-order bandpass transfer function is used.
  • Amplifier gain A is constant near the oscillation frequency.
  • We seek a positive angular frequency ω > 0.

Possible approaches

Approach 1: Direct Barkhausen with complex algebra

Write L(jω)=A H(jω), separate real and imaginary parts, solve the phase and magnitude conditions exactly.

Pros: systematic, rigorous, clear. Cons: more algebra.

Approach 2: Use known bandpass property at center frequency

Recognize that the standard bandpass has zero phase shift and gain K at ω0.

Pros: fast. Cons: less explicit mathematically.

Approach 3: Closed-loop characteristic equation

Set the denominator of the closed-loop response to zero and look for purely imaginary roots.

Pros: closely tied to stability theory. Cons: slightly less transparent for beginners here.

Chosen approach: Approach 1, supported by Approach 2 as a physical shortcut. It is the best balance between rigor and intuition.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Physical intuition before calculation

A bandpass filter strongly favors a narrow frequency region centered near ω0. Since the amplifier has no frequency preference, the oscillation frequency should be selected by the filter. Therefore, before doing any algebra, we expect the oscillation to occur at the filter center frequency. The only remaining question is whether the loop reproduces the signal with exactly the right amplitude, which should involve the product AK.

Step 1: Write the bandpass filter model

The standard second-order bandpass transfer function with center-frequency gain K is 

H(s) = K [(ω0/Q)s] / [s^2 + (ω0/Q)s + ω0^2]

Why this form is valid:

  • The denominator is the canonical second-order polynomial.
  • The numerator is proportional to s, which enforces bandpass behavior: low response at very low and very high frequencies.
  • At s = jω0, the magnitude becomes exactly K, matching the problem statement.

Step 2: Form the loop gain

Since the amplifier has constant gain A, the loop gain is 

L(s) = A H(s) = AK [(ω0/Q)s] / [s^2 + (ω0/Q)s + ω0^2]

For sustained oscillation, Barkhausen requires 

L(jω) = 1

Step 3: Evaluate at s = jω

Substitute s = jω into L(s): 

L(jω) = AK [(ω0/Q) jω] / [ (jω)^2 + (ω0/Q)jω + ω0^2 ]

Since (jω)^2 = -ω^2, this becomes 

L(jω) = AK [j(ω0ω/Q)] / [ω0^2 - ω^2 + j(ω0ω/Q)]

Step 4: Nondimensionalize

Define the dimensionless frequency ratio 

x = ω / ω0

This scaling is useful because frequency appears only relative to ω0. Divide numerator and denominator by ω0^2: 

L(jω) = AK [j(x/Q)] / [1 - x^2 + j(x/Q)]

This form is dimensionally clean: every quantity is now dimensionless except the already-scaled ratio x.

Step 5: Convert to explicit real and imaginary parts

Multiply numerator and denominator by the complex conjugate of the denominator: 

L(jω) = AK [j(x/Q)] [1 - x^2 - j(x/Q)] / [ (1 - x^2)^2 + (x/Q)^2 ]

Expand the numerator carefully: 

j(x/Q)(1 - x^2) - j^2(x^2/Q^2)
= j(x/Q)(1 - x^2) + x^2/Q^2

Therefore, 

L(jω) = AK [ x^2/Q^2 + j(x/Q)(1 - x^2) ] / [ (1 - x^2)^2 + x^2/Q^2 ]

So the imaginary part is proportional to 

Im{L(jω)} ∝ x(1 - x^2)

For sustained oscillation, L(jω) must be real and positive, so we require 

x(1 - x^2) = 0

The solutions are 

x = 0,  x = +1,  x = -1

We reject:

  • x = 0 because a bandpass filter has zero output at zero frequency, so it cannot sustain sinusoidal oscillation there.
  • x = -1 because we seek a positive angular frequency.

Hence the physically relevant solution is 

x = 1  ⇒  ωosc = ω0

Step 6: Apply the magnitude condition

At x = 1, substitute into the loop gain: 

L(jω0) = AK [j(1/Q)] / [1 - 1 + j(1/Q)]
       = AK [j(1/Q)] / [j(1/Q)]
       = AK

The Barkhausen magnitude condition requires 

L(jω0) = 1  ⇒  AK = 1

Therefore the sustained oscillation condition is

Oscillation frequency: ωosc = ω0 Sustained oscillation condition: AK = 1

Sanity checks

1) Dimensional check

ωosc and ω0 both have units of rad/s, so ωosc = ω0 is dimensionally correct. The gain condition AK = 1 is dimensionless, also correct.

2) Limiting-case check in Q

The solution does not depend on Q for the oscillation frequency. This is sensible: Q changes bandwidth and phase slope around resonance, but the center-frequency phase match still occurs at ω0.

3) Physical startup interpretation

If AK > 1, oscillations grow from noise or perturbations. If AK < 1, they decay. Exactly AK = 1 is the ideal sustained condition.

4) Plain-language interpretation

The filter chooses ω0. The amplifier must supply exactly enough gain to compensate the loop losses at that frequency.

Alternative closed-loop viewpoint

If the oscillator is modeled as a positive-feedback loop, the closed-loop denominator is 1 - L(s). Oscillation onset corresponds to roots on the imaginary axis, i.e. 1 - L(jω) = 0. This is the same as L(jω) = 1. Thus the Barkhausen approach and the characteristic-equation approach are equivalent here.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

Interpret the final formula

  • ω0 controls the selected oscillation frequency because it is built into the resonance structure of the filter.
  • K measures how much of the signal the filter returns at the center frequency.
  • A measures how much the amplifier boosts that returned signal.
  • The product AK is therefore the net loop replacement factor at the resonant frequency.

Parameter dependence

Parameter Effect on frequency Effect on oscillation condition
ω0 Directly sets ωosc No direct change to threshold product except through the filter design itself
Q No change to ideal oscillation frequency here Does not change the ideal threshold AK=1, but affects resonance sharpness and startup dynamics in practice
K No change to frequency Higher K means smaller required A
A No change to frequency Must satisfy A = 1/K for ideal sustained oscillation
Equivalent form: the sustained oscillation condition can also be written as A = 1/K. This is often the most convenient design expression.

Alternative derivation idea

A brief shortcut is to evaluate the canonical bandpass directly at ω = ω0. The filter then becomes purely real and equal to K. Since the amplifier is also purely real and positive, the loop gain is AK. Therefore, resonance gives the only plausible oscillation frequency, and unity loop gain then requires AK=1. The longer derivation above proves that no other positive frequency satisfies the phase requirement.

Concept checks

  • Q: If AK = 0.9, what happens?
    A: The oscillation decays; loop gain is below threshold.
  • Q: If AK = 1.2, is the final steady state truly linear?
    A: No. The linear model predicts growth, but real circuits enter nonlinear amplitude limiting.
  • Q: Does increasing Q move the ideal oscillation frequency away from ω0?
    A: No, not in this canonical ideal model.
  • Q: Why must the loop gain be real and positive?
    A: Because the signal must reproduce itself without phase mismatch after one loop traversal.

A plot is not necessary for this problem type because the requested answer is purely symbolic and follows directly from the exact phase and magnitude conditions of the loop gain. The decisive facts are algebraic: the phase condition forces ω = ω0, and the magnitude condition forces AK = 1.
