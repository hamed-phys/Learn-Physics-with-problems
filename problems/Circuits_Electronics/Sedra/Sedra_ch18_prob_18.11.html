Wien-Bridge Oscillator Feedback Network as a Bandpass Filter

We analyze the passive feedback network of the Wien-bridge oscillator and prove that its transfer function Va(s)/Vo(s) is a second-order bandpass filter. Then we extract the pole frequency ω0, the quality factor Q, and the center-frequency gain, while interpreting the result physically and mathematically.

Quick Summary

  • This problem is about the passive RC feedback network inside a Wien-bridge oscillator.
  • The key idea is to model the series RC branch and the parallel RC branch as impedances in the Laplace domain.
  • The governing transfer function is β(s) = Va(s)/Vo(s) = Zp / (Zs + Zp).
  • After simplification, β(s) = (sRC)/[(sRC)2 + 3sRC + 1], which is the form of a second-order bandpass filter.
  • The pole frequency is ω0 = 1/(RC).
  • The pole quality factor is Q = 1/3.
  • The center-frequency gain is |β(jω0)| = 1/3; in fact β(jω0) = 1/3 is real and positive.
  • The result is fully symbolic, and physically it means that the network selects one frequency band and attenuates both low and high frequencies.

PART 0 — Concept Primer

Core definitions

Symbol Meaning SI unit
R Resistance Ω
C Capacitance F
s Laplace variable, s = σ + jω s−1
Z(s) Impedance in the Laplace domain Ω
ω Angular frequency rad/s
ω0 Center / natural frequency rad/s
Q Quality factor; measure of selectivity dimensionless
β(s) Feedback transfer function V_a(s)/V_o(s) dimensionless

Physical meaning

  • Resistance dissipates energy.
  • Capacitance stores electric-field energy.
  • A series RC branch tends to block very low frequencies because the capacitor impedance becomes large there.
  • A parallel RC branch tends to shunt very high frequencies because the capacitor impedance becomes small there.
  • Combining the two gives a bandpass effect: low frequencies are rejected, high frequencies are rejected, and an intermediate band passes best.

Key laws and why they apply

  • Kirchhoff’s laws apply because we are using a lumped-element circuit model.
  • Laplace-domain impedance relations apply because the circuit is linear and time-invariant, and we assume zero initial capacitor voltage.
  • Voltage division applies because the feedback path from V_o to ground is a series connection of two equivalent impedances, with the output node V_a between them.
  • The op-amp is treated as ideal and non-loading at the positive input, so the passive network can be analyzed independently.

Common approximations

  • Ideal op-amp input current ≈ 0.
  • Ideal resistors and capacitors.
  • No parasitic capacitance or inductance.
  • Zero initial conditions in Laplace analysis.

Mini intuition example 1

At ω → 0, the capacitor in the series branch behaves like an open circuit, so almost no signal reaches V_a. Hence the gain tends to zero.

Mini intuition example 2

At ω → ∞, the capacitor in the shunt branch behaves like a short to ground, so V_a is strongly pulled toward ground. Again the gain tends to zero.

What to watch for

  • Do not confuse the oscillator loop gain with the passive feedback factor β(s).
  • Do not forget that the parallel branch is R || 1/(sC), not R + 1/(sC).
  • Q = 1/3 is small, so the poles are real, not complex conjugates. This is still a bandpass transfer function.
  • The frequency ω0 here is the natural / center frequency of the canonical denominator, not necessarily a sharp resonance in the underdamped sense.

Math Insight

The transfer function is a second-order rational function in the Laplace variable s. Mathematically, this is the transfer function of a linear time-invariant second-order system. In the time domain, such a denominator corresponds to a second-order linear ODE with constant coefficients. In the frequency domain, the numerator has one factor of s, which forces the gain to vanish at ω = 0; the denominator grows like s² at high frequency, which forces the gain to vanish again as ω → ∞. That “zero-low / zero-high / finite-mid” structure is exactly what makes the system a bandpass filter.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry

  • Signal generators and function generators: Wien-bridge oscillators are classic low-distortion sine-wave sources.
  • Instrumentation and calibration equipment: precise sinusoidal references are needed for testing sensors, amplifiers, and filters.
  • Audio electronics: low-frequency sinusoidal oscillators are used for tone generation and test equipment.
  • Control and monitoring systems: RC filter sections appear in conditioning chains for vibration, temperature, and process signals.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: the center frequency sets the oscillation frequency.
  • Reliability: drift in R or C changes the operating frequency.
  • Cost: tighter tolerances improve accuracy but increase manufacturing cost.
  • Manufacturability: standard-value components and thermal stability matter in production.
  • Safety and compliance: stable signal sources are critical in calibration and medical/industrial instrumentation.

Measurement and validation

  • Frequency-response measurement with a network analyzer.
  • Oscilloscope measurement of oscillator start-up and steady-state amplitude.
  • LCR meters for checking actual R and C values.
  • Temperature chamber tests for drift characterization.

Design trade-offs

  • Small Q means broad passband and modest selectivity.
  • Component tolerances shift ω₀ = 1/(RC).
  • Parasitics and finite op-amp bandwidth alter the ideal transfer function.
  • Amplitude stabilization is needed in real oscillators to avoid clipping or decay.

From lab to product

The ideal model assumes exact equal values R and C, no loading, and a perfect op-amp. Real designs add amplitude-control elements, account for op-amp gain-bandwidth limits, model parasitic impedances, and often use precision components or trimming to hold the target frequency.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

Restatement: We must analyze the passive RC feedback network connected between the output V_o and the non-inverting input node V_a of the Wien-bridge oscillator, show that the ratio V_a(s)/V_o(s) has bandpass form, then identify the natural frequency ω₀, the pole quality factor Q, and the gain at the center frequency.

Given quantities

  • A series branch from V_o to V_a containing C then R, equivalent impedance Z_s.
  • A shunt branch from V_a to ground containing R || C, equivalent impedance Z_p.
  • Equal resistor values R and equal capacitor values C.
  • Ideal op-amp input, so the network is unloaded.

Unknowns / targets

  • The symbolic transfer function β(s)=V_a(s)/V_o(s).
  • Proof that this has the form of a bandpass filter.
  • The canonical pole frequency ω₀.
  • The canonical quality factor Q.
  • The center-frequency gain β(jω₀).

Mathematical model

  • Variables: input V_o(t), node voltage V_a(t).
  • Domain: linear lumped circuit, time domain t ≥ 0, transformed to Laplace domain s ∈ ℂ.
  • Initial conditions: capacitor voltages initially zero, so Laplace impedance formulas apply directly.
  • Impedance operators: Z_R = R, Z_C(s) = 1/(sC).
  • Boundary / interconnection structure: a series impedance Z_s connects V_o to V_a, and a shunt impedance Z_p connects V_a to ground.

Why these principles apply — and why others do not

  • Kirchhoff + impedance algebra is best because the network is small and purely lumped.
  • Phasor analysis would work for sinusoidal steady state, but the problem asks for V_a(s)/V_o(s), so Laplace form is more general.
  • Field equations such as ∇·E, ∇×E, or wave equations are not needed because the circuit is already idealized as a lumped network.

Approach 1 — Direct impedance division

Pros: Fastest, clearest, most physical.
Cons: Requires careful algebra.

Approach 2 — Nodal analysis at Va

Pros: Systematic and robust.
Cons: Slightly longer than impedance division.

Approach 3 — Time-domain ODE

Pros: Gives strong mathematical insight.
Cons: More cumbersome for this symbolic problem.

Chosen approach

We choose direct impedance division because the node V_a lies between a series impedance and a shunt impedance. That makes the network exactly a voltage divider in the Laplace domain: V_a/V_o = Z_p/(Z_s + Z_p). This is the shortest route and also makes the bandpass structure immediately visible.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

  1. Identify the two equivalent impedances
    Tool: series/parallel impedance formulas.
    Meaning: compress the network into a two-element divider.
  2. Write the voltage-divider formula
    Tool: β(s)=Z_p/(Z_s+Z_p).
    Meaning: relate output node to input node.
  3. Substitute the capacitor impedance
    Tool: Z_C=1/(sC).
    Meaning: encode dynamics in the algebraic variable s.
  4. Simplify into a rational function
    Tool: algebra with common denominators.
    Meaning: expose the filter’s zeros and poles.
  5. Nondimensionalize using x = sRC
    Tool: scaling argument.
    Meaning: collapse the algebra into a universal form independent of units.
  6. Compare with the canonical second-order bandpass form
    Tool: coefficient matching.
    Meaning: read off ω₀ and Q.
  7. Evaluate at s = jω₀
    Tool: substitution into frequency response.
    Meaning: get the center-frequency gain.
  8. Check limiting cases
    Tool: asymptotics as ω → 0 and ω → ∞.
    Meaning: verify bandpass behavior physically.

Common mistakes

  • Writing Z_p = R + 1/(sC) instead of the correct parallel form.
  • Forgetting to divide the denominator by (RC)^2 before matching to canonical second-order form.
  • Assuming that because it is a bandpass, the poles must be complex. Here Q = 1/3, so the poles are real.
  • Reporting ω₀ = 1/(3RC). The coefficient 3 belongs to the damping term, not the natural-frequency term.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Physical intuition first: the feedback network should pass an intermediate frequency best. At low frequency the series capacitor blocks transmission, and at high frequency the shunt capacitor dumps the signal to ground. So before doing any algebra, we expect β(jω) to be small at both extremes and largest somewhere near ω ~ 1/(RC).

Step 1 — Define the branch impedances

Let Z_s be the series branch from V_o to V_a, and Z_p the shunt branch from V_a to ground.

Z_s = R + 1/(sC)
Z_p = R || 1/(sC) = 1 / (1/R + sC) = R/(1 + sRC)

These relations are valid because the resistor and capacitor are ideal linear elements, and we are working in the Laplace domain with zero initial conditions.

Step 2 — Write the transfer function by voltage division

The node V_a is the voltage across Z_p in the series combination Z_s + Z_p. Therefore

β(s) = V_a(s)/V_o(s) = Z_p / (Z_s + Z_p)

This is the core network equation. It is dimensionless, as every transfer function must be.

Step 3 — Substitute and simplify carefully

Substitute the expressions for Z_s and Z_p:

β(s) = [R/(1 + sRC)] / [R + 1/(sC) + R/(1 + sRC)]

To simplify cleanly, define the dimensionless variable

x = sRC

This nondimensionalization is useful because RC has units of time, so sRC is unitless. Then

Z_s = R + 1/(sC) = R + R/x = R(1 + 1/x) = R(1 + x)/x
Z_p = R/(1 + x)

Hence

β(s) = [R/(1 + x)] / [R(1 + x)/x + R/(1 + x)]

Cancel the common factor R:

β(s) = [1/(1 + x)] / [(1 + x)/x + 1/(1 + x)]

Combine terms in the denominator:

[(1 + x)/x + 1/(1 + x)] = [ (1 + x)^2 + x ] / [ x(1 + x) ]

Therefore

β(s) = [1/(1 + x)] · [x(1 + x)] / [ (1 + x)^2 + x ] = x / [ (1 + x)^2 + x ]

Expand the denominator:

β(s) = x / (1 + 3x + x^2)

Returning to x = sRC,

β(s) = (sRC) / [ (sRC)^2 + 3sRC + 1 ]

Why this is a bandpass filter

The numerator is proportional to s, so β(0)=0. Thus the gain vanishes at DC. For large |s|, the denominator behaves like (sRC)^2, so

β(s) ~ (sRC)/(sRC)^2 = 1/(sRC) → 0 as |s| → ∞

So the transfer function is small at both low and high frequency and has a finite middle-frequency maximum. That is exactly bandpass behavior.

Step 4 — Put the denominator into canonical second-order form

Start from

β(s) = (sRC) / [ (RC)^2 s^2 + 3RC s + 1 ]

Divide numerator and denominator by (RC)^2 in the denominator sense, equivalently write

β(s) = (s/RC) / [ s^2 + (3/RC)s + 1/(RC)^2 ]

The standard second-order denominator is

s^2 + (ω₀/Q)s + ω₀²

Match coefficients:

ω₀² = 1/(RC)² ⇒ ω₀ = 1/(RC)
(ω₀/Q) = 3/(RC)

Using ω₀ = 1/(RC),

1/(RCQ) = 3/(RC) ⇒ Q = 1/3

So the poles are characterized by ω₀ = 1/(RC) and Q = 1/3.

Step 5 — Find the center-frequency gain

Evaluate the transfer function at s = jω and then set ω = ω₀ = 1/(RC). Since sRC = jωRC, at ω = ω₀ we get sRC = j. Therefore

β(jω₀) = j / (j² + 3j + 1)

Because j² = -1,

β(jω₀) = j / (-1 + 3j + 1) = j/(3j) = 1/3
β(jω₀) = 1/3

Thus the center-frequency gain is 1/3.

Step 6 — Explicit pole locations (extra mathematical check)

The poles satisfy

(sRC)^2 + 3sRC + 1 = 0

Let u = sRC. Then

u² + 3u + 1 = 0
u = [-3 ± √(9 - 4)]/2 = (-3 ± √5)/2

Hence

s = [ -3 ± √5 ] / (2RC)

Both poles are real and negative. This is consistent with Q = 1/3 < 1/2. So the filter is bandpass without having underdamped complex poles.

Dimensional and limiting-case checks

  • Dimension check: sRC is dimensionless, so β(s) is dimensionless. Good.
  • Low-frequency limit: for |sRC| ≪ 1, β(s) ≈ sRC. The gain rises linearly from zero, as expected for a bandpass numerator.
  • High-frequency limit: for |sRC| ≫ 1, β(s) ≈ 1/(sRC). The gain falls inversely with frequency.
  • Physical interpretation: the network transmits best near ω = 1/(RC), with attenuation factor 1/3.

Final Answer

V_a(s)/V_o(s) = β(s) = (sRC)/[(sRC)^2 + 3sRC + 1]

Therefore the feedback network is a second-order bandpass filter.

  • Pole frequency: ω₀ = 1/(RC)
  • Quality factor: Q = 1/3
  • Center-frequency gain: β(jω₀) = 1/3

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

Re-interpret the final formula

The transfer function β(s) = (sRC)/[(sRC)^2 + 3sRC + 1] has three important structural pieces:

  • Numerator ∝ s: forces a zero at DC.
  • Constant term 1 in the denominator: sets the low-frequency scale.
  • Quadratic term (sRC)²: dominates at high frequency and suppresses gain there.

What each parameter controls

  • R or C larger → larger time constant RC → smaller ω₀ = 1/(RC).
  • R or C smaller → smaller time constant → higher center frequency.
  • In this equal-component Wien network, Q is fixed at 1/3; it does not change independently unless the network ratios are altered.

Alternative derivation idea

A nodal-analysis derivation starts by summing currents at node V_a:

(V_a - V_o)/Z_s + V_a/Z_p = 0

Rearranging gives V_a/V_o = Z_p/(Z_s + Z_p), so it collapses to the same expression. This route is useful when the network is more complicated and a simple divider is not visually obvious.

Concept check Q&A

  • Q1: Why is the transfer zero at DC?
    A: Because the series capacitor blocks low-frequency current, and mathematically the numerator contains a factor s.
  • Q2: Why does the transfer also vanish at very high frequency?
    A: The shunt capacitor shorts the node toward ground, and the denominator grows one power of s faster than the numerator.
  • Q3: Does a bandpass filter always have complex poles?
    A: No. Here it is bandpass even though the poles are real because the numerator and asymptotics produce midband transmission.
  • Q4: Why is the gain at the center frequency exactly 1/3?
    A: At ω = 1/(RC), the denominator becomes purely imaginary and equals 3j, while the numerator is j.
Optional deeper note: canonical bandpass form

A standard second-order bandpass transfer function can be written as

H(s) = K [s/(Qω₀)] / [ (s/ω₀)^2 + s/(Qω₀) + 1 ]

For the Wien network, if we set ω₀ = 1/(RC) and Q = 1/3, then s/(Qω₀) = 3sRC. Since our numerator is only sRC, the prefactor must be K = 1/3. That is another direct way to see the center-frequency gain.
