Colpitts Oscillator: Governing Equation, Oscillation Frequency, and Start-Up Condition

We derive the small-signal characteristic equation for the MOSFET Colpitts oscillator shown, then obtain the oscillation frequency and the condition on the transistor gain factor gmRL required for oscillations to start. The derivation is done carefully from nodal equations and interpreted physically as a resonance-plus-negative-resistance problem.

Topic: Electronic oscillations Method: Small-signal nodal analysis Result: Symbolic Lens: Physics + engineering practice

Quick Summary

  • The problem asks for the governing small-signal equation of a Colpitts oscillator using a MOSFET, then the oscillation frequency and the start-up condition.
  • The key physics idea is that the inductor L resonates with the series-equivalent capacitance of C1 and C2, while the transistor provides active feedback to cancel losses.
  • The mathematical model is a linear two-node circuit eigenvalue problem in the Laplace domain, with the oscillation condition obtained by setting the determinant of the nodal-admittance matrix to zero.
  • The characteristic equation is L RL C1C2s3 + L C2s2 + RL(C1+C2)s + (1+gmRL) = 0.
  • The oscillation angular frequency is ω0 = √[(C1+C2)/(L C1C2)], or equivalently ω0 = 1/√(L Ceq) with Ceq = C1C2/(C1+C2).
  • The minimum condition at the edge of sustained oscillation is gmRL = C2/C1.
  • For start-up from noise, the gain must be slightly larger: gmRL > C2/C1.
  • Physically, C2/C1 is the feedback ratio the transistor must overcome; larger transistor transconductance or larger effective load resistance makes oscillation easier to start.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

What is a Colpitts oscillator?

A Colpitts oscillator is an LC sinusoidal oscillator in which the feedback network uses a capacitive divider rather than an inductive divider. In this circuit, the pair C1 and C2 split voltage in a way that feeds part of the resonant signal back to the transistor input. The transistor contributes energy to compensate dissipation, while the LC network selects the oscillation frequency.

Physical meaning

  • L stores magnetic energy: UL = ½Li².
  • C1, C2 store electric energy and form the feedback divider.
  • gmvgs is the transistor-controlled current that pumps energy into the resonator.
  • RL models energy loss from the resonator/output side.
  • Oscillation occurs when reactive energy exchange sets the frequency and active gain cancels loss.

Key laws and validity conditions

Small-signal linear model used here
  • We linearize the MOSFET around its bias point: the incremental drain-source current is modeled by gmvgs.
  • The coupling capacitor labeled “∞” is treated as an AC short circuit.
  • The ideal bias current source has infinite small-signal resistance, so it is an AC open circuit.
  • The drain is tied to the DC supply, and the supply is treated as AC ground.
  • The effective resistance RL is assumed to include the MOSFET output resistance ro, exactly as requested.

Common models and approximations

  • Phasor/Laplace-domain linearization: valid because we seek the condition for the onset of oscillation, where amplitudes are still small.
  • Lumped-element approximation: valid when the physical circuit size is much smaller than the wavelength, so distributed electromagnetic effects can be neglected.
  • Ignore amplitude limiting initially: start-up is a linear instability question. Large-signal saturation determines final amplitude, but not the threshold condition.

Mini intuition examples

Example 1: Pure LC resonance

If there were no transistor and no losses, L and a net capacitance C would oscillate naturally at ω = 1/√(LC).

Example 2: Loss kills oscillation

Adding a resistor drains energy each cycle, so the oscillation decays unless some active device puts the energy back.

Example 3: Feedback ratio matters

If only a small fraction of the tank voltage is returned to the controlling node, the transistor must supply more gain for start-up.

What to watch for

  • Do not treat C1 and C2 as parallel; they combine into a series-equivalent capacitance for the resonance seen by L.
  • Be careful with AC grounds: the supply node is AC ground, but the bias current source is AC open.
  • Keep the sign of the transconductance current consistent with the MOSFET small-signal model.
  • The equality condition gives the edge of sustained oscillation; the actual start-up requirement is a strict inequality.
Math Insight

The core equation of this oscillator is a characteristic polynomial in s. Mathematically, this is an eigenvalue condition for a linear dynamical system. Oscillation begins when an eigenvalue reaches the imaginary axis: s = jω. Thus the problem is not just “find a frequency,” but “find when the system’s linear operator has a purely imaginary eigenvalue.”

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in real engineering

  • RF signal generation: local oscillators in radios, transmitters, and receivers.
  • Instrumentation: test sources, clock generation, waveform generation, sensor interrogation circuits.
  • Integrated circuits: on-chip LC VCOs, PLL building blocks, and frequency-reference subsystems.
  • Industrial electronics: resonant drive stages, timing subsystems, and communication front ends in automation hardware.

Why engineers care

Performance

Frequency accuracy, phase noise, start-up margin, power consumption, and output amplitude all depend on the tank quality factor, transistor gain, and parasitics.

Reliability and cost

Insufficient start-up margin causes intermittent failure. Excess gain can distort the waveform and waste power. In mass production, designers must tolerate process spread in gm, L, C, and parasitic losses.

Typical measurement and validation methods

  • Spectrum analyzer: checks oscillation frequency, harmonics, and phase noise.
  • Oscilloscope: verifies time-domain start-up and steady-state waveform.
  • Network or impedance analysis: estimates tank resonance and loss.
  • Small-signal simulation: AC, pole-zero, and periodic steady-state analyses are common design tools.

Design trade-offs and real constraints

  • Larger gm helps start-up but can raise power consumption and nonlinearity.
  • Capacitor ratio C2/C1 sets the feedback fraction; this affects both start-up threshold and amplitude distribution.
  • Real inductors have series resistance and limited Q, shifting the ideal result.
  • MOSFET parasitic capacitances add to C1 and C2, changing the actual oscillation frequency.

From lab to product

The ideal derivation gives the first design equation. In an actual product, engineers replace L, C1, C2, RL, gm by effective values that include device parasitics, package effects, temperature variation, and manufacturing tolerance. The symbolic threshold remains the design anchor, but the final product is tuned with margins, simulation, and measurement.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restated clearly

We must model the shown MOSFET Colpitts oscillator in small signal, derive the characteristic equation governing its AC behavior, and then extract:

  1. the oscillation frequency, and
  2. the condition the product gmRL must satisfy for oscillations to start.

Given quantities

  • Inductor L
  • Capacitors C1 and C2
  • MOSFET with transconductance gm
  • Effective load resistance RL, including ro
  • Coupling capacitor of infinite value → AC short
  • Bias current source → AC open

Unknowns to find

  • Characteristic / governing equation in s
  • Oscillation frequency ω0 (and optionally f0)
  • Threshold and start-up condition on gmRL

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Kirchhoff’s current law (KCL): ideal for writing node equations in a linear small-signal circuit.
  • Laplace-domain admittances: capacitors become sC, inductors become 1/(sL), simplifying the algebra.
  • Characteristic determinant = 0: nontrivial node voltages require the admittance matrix to be singular.
  • Oscillation condition: set s = jω and separate real and imaginary parts to get resonance and gain threshold.

Assumptions

  • Small-signal linear operation about the bias point.
  • The supply rail is AC ground.
  • The current source has infinite AC resistance.
  • The output coupling capacitor is an AC short.
  • All parasitic loss not shown explicitly is absorbed into RL.
  • No large-signal clipping or amplitude limiting is needed to find the start-up threshold.

Possible approaches

Approach Idea Pros Cons
Two-node nodal analysis Write KCL at the gate node and source/output node Direct, systematic, least ambiguity Requires careful sign handling for gmvgs
Barkhausen loop-gain method Identify amplifier and feedback network separately Fast physical interpretation Can be ambiguous in multi-node transistor circuits
Equivalent negative-resistance view Reflect transistor action into tank loss compensation Excellent intuition Harder to derive the exact polynomial cleanly

Best choice: two-node nodal analysis. It yields the governing equation rigorously and lets the oscillation condition emerge cleanly from the determinant.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Identify AC grounds and opens. Replace the infinite coupling capacitor by a short and the ideal current source by an open. This simplifies the topology.
  2. Choose node voltages. Let vg be the upper resonant node and vs the source/output node.
  3. Write KCL at the gate node. Use inductor and capacitor admittances. This captures resonance.
  4. Write KCL at the source node. Include C1, RL, C2, and the transistor current gm(vg-vs).
  5. Arrange into matrix form. A homogeneous linear system in vg, vs is obtained.
  6. Set the determinant to zero. That is the characteristic equation.
  7. Substitute s = jω. Separate real and imaginary parts.
  8. Imaginary part → frequency. This enforces reactive balance.
  9. Real part → gain threshold. This enforces loss cancellation.
  10. Interpret physically. Frequency comes from the LC tank; threshold comes from transistor gain overcoming loss through the feedback ratio.
Common mistakes
  • Using C1 + C2 directly in the resonance instead of the series-equivalent capacitance.
  • Forgetting that the source node is loaded by RL because the coupling capacitor is an AC short.
  • Confusing the equality condition for sustained oscillation with the strict inequality needed for start-up.
  • Losing the sign of the transconductance term when forming the source-node KCL.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Step 1: Small-signal circuit interpretation

Before calculating, we expect the following qualitatively:

  • The oscillation frequency should be set mainly by L and some equivalent capacitance built from C1 and C2.
  • The transistor should not determine the frequency strongly at threshold; instead, it should determine the start-up condition.
  • The capacitor ratio should appear in the gain condition, because the Colpitts oscillator uses a capacitive divider for feedback.

Step 2: Define node voltages and the mathematical model

Let vg(t) be the small-signal voltage at the upper node connected to L and the top plate of C2, and let vs(t) be the small-signal voltage at the source/output node connected to C1, the bottom plate of C2, and RL.

The domain is a lumped, finite-dimensional linear dynamical system, so the governing equations are ordinary differential equations. In the Laplace domain, with s as the transform variable, the element laws are:

Y_L = 1/(sL), Y_C = sC, i_transistor = g_m(v_g - v_s)

This is valid because the system is linearized about a bias point. Linearity lets us use superposition and homogeneous-matrix methods.

Step 3: KCL at the gate-side resonant node

The gate node sees the inductor to ground and capacitor C2 to the source node. Summing currents leaving the gate node:

v_g/(sL) + sC_2(v_g - v_s) = 0

Rearranging:

(1/(sL) + sC_2)\,v_g - sC_2\,v_s = 0

Physically, this equation expresses the reactive balance between the inductor current and the current through the coupling capacitor.

Step 4: KCL at the source/output node

At the source node, currents can leave through:

  • C1 to ground: sC1vs
  • RL to ground: vs/RL
  • C2 to the gate node: sC2(vs-vg)

The transistor controlled source injects current gm(vg-vs) into the source node from AC ground. Therefore, as a current leaving the source node it contributes -gm(vg-vs).

sC_1 v_s + v_s/R_L + sC_2(v_s - v_g) - g_m(v_g - v_s) = 0

Now expand and collect coefficients of vs and vg:

-(sC_2 + g_m)\,v_g + (sC_1 + sC_2 + 1/R_L + g_m)\,v_s = 0

Step 5: Matrix form

The two homogeneous equations can now be written as:

[ (1/(sL) + sC_2) -sC_2 ] [v_g] [0] [ -(sC_2 + g_m) (sC_1 + sC_2 + 1/R_L + g_m)] [v_s] = [0]

A nonzero oscillatory solution requires the determinant to vanish. This is the linear-algebra statement that a homogeneous system has nontrivial solutions only when its coefficient matrix is singular.

Step 6: Characteristic equation

Set the determinant equal to zero:

(1/(sL) + sC_2)(sC_1 + sC_2 + 1/R_L + g_m) - sC_2(sC_2 + g_m) = 0

Now expand carefully. First distribute the first product:

[(1/(sL))(sC_1 + sC_2 + 1/R_L + g_m)] + [sC_2(sC_1 + sC_2 + 1/R_L + g_m)] - sC_2(sC_2 + g_m) = 0

The two bracketed terms involving sC2(sC2 + gm) cancel. What remains is:

C_1/L + C_2/L + (1 + g_m R_L)/(s L R_L) + s^2 C_1 C_2 + s C_2 / R_L = 0

Multiplying through by sLRL to remove denominators gives the governing cubic:

L R_L C_1 C_2 s^3 + L C_2 s^2 + R_L(C_1 + C_2)s + (1 + g_m R_L) = 0
Governing equation

The small-signal operation of the circuit is governed by L RL C1C2s3 + L C2s2 + RL(C1+C2)s + (1+gmRL) = 0.

Step 7: Find the oscillation frequency by setting s = jω

At the threshold of oscillation, the system has a purely imaginary root. Substitute s = jω into the cubic:

L R_L C_1 C_2 (jω)^3 + L C_2 (jω)^2 + R_L(C_1 + C_2)(jω) + (1 + g_m R_L) = 0

Use j² = -1 and j³ = -j:

(1 + g_m R_L - ω^2 L C_2) + j[ωR_L(C_1 + C_2) - ω^3 L R_L C_1 C_2] = 0

Since a complex number is zero only if both its real and imaginary parts are zero, we obtain two equations.

Imaginary part = 0

ωR_L(C_1 + C_2) - ω^3 L R_L C_1 C_2 = 0

Assuming the nontrivial oscillation case ω ≠ 0, divide by ωRL:

C_1 + C_2 - ω^2 L C_1 C_2 = 0
ω_0^2 = (C_1 + C_2)/(L C_1 C_2)

Therefore,

ω_0 = √[(C_1 + C_2)/(L C_1 C_2)] = 1/√(L C_eq), C_eq = (C_1 C_2)/(C_1 + C_2)

So the inductor resonates with the series-equivalent capacitance of C1 and C2, exactly as expected physically.

Dimensional check

Since L Ceq has units of H·F = s², the quantity 1/√(L Ceq) indeed has units s-1, as required for angular frequency.

Step 8: Find the gain threshold from the real part

Now set the real part to zero:

1 + g_m R_L - ω^2 L C_2 = 0

Insert the value of ω² found above:

1 + g_m R_L - [(C_1 + C_2)/(L C_1 C_2)] L C_2 = 0

Simplify:

1 + g_m R_L - (C_1 + C_2)/C_1 = 0
1 + g_m R_L - 1 - C_2/C_1 = 0
g_m R_L = C_2/C_1

This is the edge of sustained oscillation. To actually start from noise, the loop must exhibit net positive growth initially, so the required condition is:

Start-up condition: g_m R_L > C_2/C_1

Final Answer

Governing characteristic equation

L R_L C_1 C_2 s^3 + L C_2 s^2 + R_L(C_1 + C_2)s + (1 + g_m R_L) = 0

Oscillation angular frequency

ω_0 = √[(C_1 + C_2)/(L C_1 C_2)] = 1/√(L C_eq), C_eq = (C_1 C_2)/(C_1 + C_2)

Edge of sustained oscillation

g_m R_L = C_2/C_1

Condition for oscillations to start

g_m R_L > C_2/C_1

Sanity checks

1) Units

The expression for ω0 has units of s-1. The product gmRL is dimensionless, matching the capacitor ratio C2/C1.

2) Physical meaning

The frequency is set by the tank; the transistor enters the threshold equation, not the ideal resonance formula. This is exactly what we expect for a lightly loaded sinusoidal oscillator near start-up.

3) Limiting case: very large C1

If C1 → ∞, then Ceq → C2, so ω0 → 1/√(LC2), and the threshold tends to gmRL → 0. That means very strong feedback, so little active gain is needed.

4) Limiting case: very small C1

If C1 is very small, then Ceq ≈ C1 and the threshold gmRL = C2/C1 becomes large. Weak feedback requires much higher transistor gain.

Optional deeper note: time-domain interpretation

Because the determinant gives a cubic polynomial in s, the corresponding time-domain small-signal variable obeys a third-order linear ODE. The oscillation threshold is the point where one conjugate pair of roots crosses from the left half-plane to the imaginary axis. In dynamical-systems language, start-up is a linear instability of the equilibrium.

Optional deeper note: nondimensionalization

A convenient dimensionless gain parameter is μ = gmRL. A convenient dimensionless feedback ratio is κ = C2/C1. The start-up criterion is simply μ > κ. This is useful in engineering design because it separates the active-device strength from the passive-network feedback ratio.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Interpretation of the final formula

  • ω0 depends on L and the series-equivalent capacitance Ceq. This is the resonance-selection part.
  • gmRL measures how much effective active reinforcement the transistor can provide relative to loss.
  • C2/C1 is the capacitive-divider ratio that sets the required feedback gain.

Parameter dependence

Parameter Effect on frequency Effect on start-up
L ↑ ω0 decreases No direct change in threshold formula
C1 ↑ Ceq increases, so ω0 decreases Threshold C2/C1 decreases → easier start-up
C2 ↑ Ceq increases, so ω0 decreases Threshold increases → harder start-up
gm ↑ Little direct effect at threshold in ideal model Easier start-up
RL ↑ No direct effect on ideal resonance Easier start-up because gmRL increases

Alternative derivation idea

One can also derive the same result using a loop-gain/Barkhausen viewpoint. The capacitive divider provides a feedback fraction roughly set by the ratio of capacitor voltages, while the source follower with transconductance gm and effective load RL provides the forward gain. Setting the loop phase to zero gives the resonance; setting the loop magnitude to unity gives gmRL = C2/C1. The nodal method is cleaner because it avoids ambiguity about where to “break the loop.”

Concept checks

  • Q: Why does the frequency not explicitly depend on gm in the ideal threshold formula?
    A: Because at start-up, the active device mainly compensates losses; the reactive network sets the resonance.
  • Q: Why do C1 and C2 combine as a series capacitance for resonance?
    A: The inductor sees the capacitive path through the divider, and the energy exchange is governed by the equivalent capacitance between the resonant node and ground through that series path.
  • Q: Why is the start-up condition an inequality and not just an equality?
    A: Equality corresponds to zero net damping. To grow from noise, the circuit needs slightly negative damping initially, hence a strict “greater than.”
  • Q: What role does RL play physically?
    A: It represents energy dissipation or loading. A larger effective resistance means less damping and therefore easier oscillation start-up.
