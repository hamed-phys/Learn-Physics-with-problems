Wien-Bridge Oscillator: Closed-Loop Poles, Pole Q, and the Condition R2/R1 > 2

We analyze the Wien-bridge oscillator using its loop-gain expression, derive the closed-loop characteristic equation, identify the poles in standard second-order form, obtain the pole quality factor Q, and prove that the poles move into the right-half s-plane only when R2/R1 > 2. The treatment is physics-friendly, but mathematically explicit and rigorous.

Quick Summary

  • This problem is about the dynamics of a Wien-bridge oscillator, not just its steady-state oscillation frequency.
  • The key idea is that oscillation start-up is determined by the closed-loop poles: if they lie in the right-half plane, the amplitude grows exponentially from noise or disturbance.
  • The given loop gain is L(s) = (1 + R2/R1) / (3 + sCR + 1/(sCR)).
  • The closed-loop characteristic equation comes from 1 − L(s) = 0.
  • After algebra, the pole equation becomes (sCR)2 + (2 − R2/R1)(sCR) + 1 = 0.
  • In standard second-order form, the natural frequency is ω0 = 1/(RC).
  • The pole Q is Q = 1 / (2 − R2/R1).
  • The poles are in the right-half plane when their real part is positive, which requires R2/R1 > 2.
  • The result is symbolic, and physically it means the amplifier must provide enough positive feedback to overcome damping and create start-up growth.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

1) What kind of system is this?

A Wien-bridge oscillator is a linear feedback system built from an amplifier and a frequency-selective RC network. In its idealized small-signal form, it behaves like a second-order dynamical system. The RC network selects a preferred angular frequency, while the amplifier gain determines whether oscillations decay, stay constant, or grow.

Math Insight: The core equation we will obtain is a second-order linear ordinary differential equation in characteristic-polynomial form. In the Laplace domain, the poles are the roots of a quadratic polynomial in s. This is mathematically the same structure as a damped harmonic oscillator, except the “damping” can become negative.

2) Mathematical model

We work in the Laplace domain, where the complex frequency variable is s = σ + jω, with:

  • σ = exponential growth/decay rate, units s−1
  • ω = angular frequency, units rad/s

The system is modeled as a lumped-parameter linear time-invariant (LTI) circuit. There are no spatial coordinates, so operators such as ∇, ∇·, ∇×, and the Laplacian do not appear here; this is an ODE/system-theory problem, not a field PDE problem.

Symbol Meaning SI unit
R Resistor in the Wien frequency-selective network Ω
C Capacitor in the Wien frequency-selective network F
R1, R2 Amplifier feedback resistors that set closed-loop amplifier gain Ω
s Laplace complex frequency variable s−1
L(s) Loop gain dimensionless
ω0 Natural angular frequency of the pole pair rad/s
Q Pole quality factor; inverse measure of damping dimensionless

3) Key laws and principles

Feedback principle

For a feedback loop with loop gain L(s), the closed-loop poles satisfy the characteristic equation:

1 − L(s) = 0

This is the condition under which the closed-loop transfer function denominator vanishes.

Second-order dynamics

A standard second-order characteristic polynomial is

s2 + (ω0/Q)s + ω02 = 0

Comparing any quadratic with this form lets us identify ω0 and Q.

4) Validity conditions and assumptions

Assumptions used in this topic:
  • Ideal op-amp in the small-signal sense: very large gain, negligible input current, negligible output impedance.
  • Linear RC elements with constant R and C.
  • We analyze start-up / pole location, not amplitude stabilization.
  • No nonlinear limiter or automatic gain control is included.
  • The model is time-invariant and valid near equilibrium.

5) Common approximations and why they are used

In oscillator theory, a frequent approximation is to separate the analysis into:

  1. Linear small-signal analysis to determine whether oscillation can start.
  2. Nonlinear amplitude analysis to determine the final steady amplitude.

This problem is purely of the first type. Therefore, using a linear characteristic equation is appropriate.

6) Mini intuition examples

  • If feedback is too weak, the poles lie in the left-half plane, so any oscillation dies out.
  • If feedback is exactly balanced, the poles lie on the imaginary axis, so oscillation is marginal.
  • If feedback is slightly too strong, the poles move into the right-half plane, so oscillation grows until nonlinearity stops it.

7) What to watch for

Pitfalls and misconceptions:
  • Do not confuse oscillation frequency with pole location. Frequency alone does not tell you whether oscillation grows or decays.
  • Do not forget that the closed-loop poles come from 1 − L(s) = 0, not from the denominator of L(s) alone.
  • The sign of the s coefficient matters: it determines left-half or right-half plane location.
  • Q can become negative in this unstable linear model. That is not a passive resonator Q; it is a pole-parameter indicating negative damping.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Why this matters in real engineering

Oscillators are fundamental timing and signal-generation blocks. The Wien-bridge oscillator is historically important because it produces a low-distortion sine wave using only resistors, capacitors, and an amplifier.

Application 1

Test and measurement instruments

Used in audio-frequency generators, calibration equipment, and lab signal sources.

Application 2

Industrial electronics

Used where stable sinusoidal excitation is needed for sensors, impedance measurements, or analog diagnostics.

Application 3

Embedded analog subsystems

Appears in low-frequency analog front ends, educational instrumentation, and control-system test loops.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: pole placement determines start-up speed and waveform purity.
  • Reliability: too much loop gain causes saturation and distortion; too little causes failure to start.
  • Cost: simple RC oscillators are cheaper than precision crystal or DDS solutions in many low-frequency uses.
  • Manufacturability: resistor/capacitor tolerances shift frequency and start-up margin.
  • Efficiency: choosing gain just above threshold avoids unnecessary clipping and power loss.

How the model is validated in practice

  • Measure the start-up waveform on an oscilloscope and infer the exponential envelope.
  • Use network analysis or simulation to inspect the loop transfer function and phase condition.
  • Perform tolerance sweeps in SPICE to verify robust start-up over resistor/capacitor variation.
  • Check total harmonic distortion (THD) once amplitude stabilization circuitry is added.

Design trade-offs and non-idealities

Real circuits include finite op-amp bandwidth, input bias currents, capacitor loss, resistor tolerance, temperature drift, and amplitude stabilization elements such as lamps, diodes, or AGC loops. These modify the ideal equation slightly, but the core insight remains: start-up is a pole-location problem.

From lab to product: the ideal model predicts the required gain threshold; a real product then adds nonlinear amplitude control so that the start-up poles are slightly unstable at very small amplitude, but settle to effective marginal stability at the desired output level.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Restating the problem

We are given the loop gain of the Wien-bridge oscillator: 

L(s) = (1 + R2/R1) / (3 + sCR + 1/(sCR))

We must use this expression to:

  1. Find the closed-loop poles.
  2. Write the expression for the pole Q.
  3. Use that result to show that the poles are in the right half of the s-plane only when R2/R1 > 2.

Given quantities

  • Loop gain: L(s) as above
  • Resistors: R, R1, R2
  • Capacitance: C
  • Implicitly, the system is linear and idealized

Unknowns

  • The pole locations s = s1, s2
  • The pole quality factor Q
  • The condition for right-half-plane poles

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Closed-loop characteristic equation: because poles are roots of the denominator of the closed-loop transfer function, so we set 1 − L(s) = 0.
  • Polynomial comparison with standard second-order form: because the oscillator dynamics reduce to a quadratic in s.
  • Stability criterion from pole real part: because the sign of Re(s) determines exponential growth or decay.

We do not need spatial field equations, energy density integrals, or PDE eigenmodes here, because the circuit is a finite-dimensional lumped network.

Assumptions

  • Ideal op-amp and ideal RC network.
  • Linearized start-up analysis only.
  • No amplitude stabilization mechanism is active.
  • All components are positive real constants: R > 0, C > 0, R1 > 0, R2 > 0.

Possible approaches

Approach A: Direct characteristic equation

Set 1 − L(s) = 0, clear fractions, derive quadratic, compare with standard form.

Pros: shortest and most transparent.

Cons: requires careful sign handling.

Approach B: Root-locus viewpoint

Treat R2/R1 as a gain parameter and inspect pole motion.

Pros: strong physical intuition.

Cons: still needs algebra for an exact proof.

Approach C: Time-domain ODE

Convert the quadratic polynomial in s into a second-order differential equation.

Pros: physics intuition via damping.

Cons: more steps than needed for this question.

Best approach

Approach A is best because the question explicitly gives L(s) and asks for poles and Q. The most direct route is to derive the quadratic characteristic equation and then compare it with the standard second-order form.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Write the closed-loop condition.
    Use: 1 − L(s) = 0.
    Physical meaning: poles are the self-sustained internal modes of the closed system.
  2. Substitute the given L(s).
    Use algebra only.
    Physical meaning: combine amplifier gain and RC frequency selection into one dynamic equation.
  3. Clear the fractional term 1/(sCR).
    Multiply through by sCR.
    Physical meaning: convert the characteristic condition into a polynomial in s.
  4. Write the result as a quadratic in s.
    Use dimensional grouping with RC.
    Physical meaning: identify a second-order oscillator-like system.
  5. Compare with the standard form s2 + (ω0/Q)s + ω02 = 0.
    Physical meaning: read off natural frequency and effective damping.
  6. Find the poles or their real part.
    Use the quadratic formula or symmetry of a second-order pair.
    Physical meaning: determine whether oscillation decays or grows.
  7. Establish the inequality for right-half-plane poles.
    Use sign of the real part.
    Physical meaning: derive the start-up condition.
Common mistakes:
  • Using 1 + L(s) = 0 instead of the correct sign convention given by the loop formulation here.
  • Dropping the factor sCR incorrectly when clearing fractions.
  • Comparing with the wrong standard second-order form.
  • Forgetting that R and C are positive, so the sign of the pole real part comes only from the numerator coefficient.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition before calculating

The Wien network selects a preferred frequency near ω = 1/(RC). The amplifier gain parameter 1 + R2/R1 determines whether the selected oscillation mode is damped or anti-damped. We therefore expect a second-order polynomial whose middle coefficient changes sign as the gain is increased. When that coefficient becomes negative, the poles should move into the right-half plane.

Step 1 — Start from the loop gain

The given loop gain is

L(s) = (1 + R2/R1) / (3 + sCR + 1/(sCR))

For the closed-loop poles, we use the characteristic equation 

1 - L(s) = 0

This step is valid because in linear feedback theory, the closed-loop denominator is proportional to 1 − L(s) for the sign convention used here.

Step 2 — Substitute L(s) into the characteristic equation

1 - (1 + R2/R1) / (3 + sCR + 1/(sCR)) = 0

Move the fraction to the other side: 

(1 + R2/R1) / (3 + sCR + 1/(sCR)) = 1

Multiply both sides by the denominator. This is valid provided we are solving away from singularities of the algebraic expression; equivalently, we are seeking roots of the characteristic equation. 

1 + R2/R1 = 3 + sCR + 1/(sCR)

Step 3 — Rearrange into a dimensionless form

Bring all terms to one side: 

sCR + 1/(sCR) + 3 - (1 + R2/R1) = 0

Simplify the constant terms:

sCR + 1/(sCR) + 2 - R2/R1 = 0

This form is already useful physically: sCR is dimensionless, so the entire equation is dimensionless. That is a good dimensional check.

Step 4 — Nondimensionalization and polynomial form

Define the dimensionless variable 

x = sCR

Then the characteristic equation becomes 

x + 1/x + 2 - R2/R1 = 0

Multiply by x to eliminate the reciprocal term: 

x^2 + (2 - R2/R1)x + 1 = 0

Returning to x = sCR, 

(sCR)^2 + (2 - R2/R1)(sCR) + 1 = 0

This is the characteristic polynomial of the closed-loop system. Because it is quadratic in s, the system has two poles.

Step 5 — Write explicitly as a quadratic in s

Expand the powers of sCR: 

s^2 C^2 R^2 + (2 - R2/R1)sCR + 1 = 0

Divide by R^2 C^2: 

s^2 + [(2 - R2/R1)/(RC)] s + 1/(R^2 C^2) = 0

This form is now ready for comparison with the standard second-order polynomial.

Step 6 — Identify the natural frequency and pole Q

The standard second-order form is 

s^2 + (ω0/Q)s + ω0^2 = 0

Comparing term by term: 

ω0^2 = 1/(R^2 C^2)  =>  ω0 = 1/(RC)
ω0/Q = (2 - R2/R1)/(RC)

Since ω0 = 1/(RC), substitute into the second relation: 

(1/(RC))/Q = (2 - R2/R1)/(RC)

Multiply both sides by RC: 

1/Q = 2 - R2/R1

Therefore, 

Q = 1 / (2 - R2/R1)

This is the requested pole quality factor.

Step 7 — Solve for the poles

Apply the quadratic formula to 

s^2 + [(2 - R2/R1)/(RC)] s + 1/(R^2 C^2) = 0

The poles are 

s = { -[(2 - R2/R1)/(RC)] ± sqrt( [(2 - R2/R1)^2/(R^2C^2)] - 4/(R^2C^2) ) } / 2

Factor out 1/(RC): 

s = [ -(2 - R2/R1) ± sqrt( (2 - R2/R1)^2 - 4 ) ] / (2RC)

For many values near the oscillation threshold, the roots are a complex-conjugate pair. Their real part is 

Re(s) = -(2 - R2/R1)/(2RC) = (R2/R1 - 2)/(2RC)

Step 8 — Condition for poles in the right-half plane

The poles lie in the right-half plane exactly when 

Re(s) > 0

Since R > 0 and C > 0, the denominator 2RC is positive. Therefore the sign of Re(s) is determined only by R2/R1 − 2. Hence 

Re(s) > 0  ⇔  R2/R1 - 2 > 0  ⇔  R2/R1 > 2

This proves the required result.

Final Answer

The closed-loop characteristic equation is 

(sCR)^2 + (2 - R2/R1)(sCR) + 1 = 0

or equivalently 

s^2 + [(2 - R2/R1)/(RC)] s + 1/(R^2 C^2) = 0

Therefore the poles are 

s = [ -(2 - R2/R1) ± sqrt((2 - R2/R1)^2 - 4) ] / (2RC)

Comparing with s^2 + (ω0/Q)s + ω02 = 0 gives 

ω0 = 1/(RC),   Q = 1 / (2 - R2/R1)

The real part of the poles is 

Re(s) = (R2/R1 - 2)/(2RC)

Hence the poles are in the right half of the s-plane when and only when 

R2/R1 > 2

Sanity checks

Dimensional check

In s^2 + [(2 - R2/R1)/(RC)]s + 1/(R^2C^2) = 0, each term has units of s−2. This is consistent.

Threshold check

If R2/R1 = 2, then the s coefficient vanishes, so s = ±j/(RC). The poles lie on the imaginary axis, meaning marginal oscillation.

Below threshold

If R2/R1 < 2, then Re(s) < 0, so oscillations decay.

Above threshold

If R2/R1 > 2, then Re(s) > 0, so oscillations grow. This is exactly the start-up condition.

Optional deeper note: time-domain interpretation

Replacing s by the differential operator d/dt, the homogeneous characteristic polynomial corresponds to a second-order ODE of the form 

d^2x/dt^2 + [(2 - R2/R1)/(RC)] dx/dt + [1/(R^2C^2)] x = 0

This is mathematically identical to a harmonic oscillator with effective damping coefficient (2 - R2/R1)/(RC). When this coefficient becomes negative, the system has negative damping, so amplitude grows exponentially.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpreting the final formula

The characteristic equation 

s^2 + [(2 - R2/R1)/(RC)] s + 1/(R^2 C^2) = 0

shows three distinct controls:

  • 1/(RC) sets the natural frequency scale.
  • 2 − R2/R1 sets the damping sign and magnitude.
  • Q = 1/(2 − R2/R1) becomes very large in magnitude near threshold, indicating very weak effective damping.

Parameter dependence

  • Increase R or C → ω0 = 1/(RC) decreases, so the oscillation frequency scale decreases.
  • Increase R2/R1 → the s-coefficient decreases, then changes sign, turning damping into anti-damping.
  • At R2/R1 = 2 → the system is at the start-up boundary.
  • Far above 2 → the growth rate increases, but in a real oscillator nonlinearity will soon dominate and distort the waveform.

Limiting-case asymptotics

Near-threshold expansion: let ε = R2/R1 − 2, with |ε| << 1. Then Re(s) = ε/(2RC), while the imaginary part remains close to 1/(RC). So near threshold the oscillation frequency changes little, but the envelope growth/decay rate changes linearly with gain error.

Alternative derivation idea

Another route is to write the quadratic directly in the dimensionless variable x = sRC: 

x^2 + (2 - R2/R1)x + 1 = 0

The sum of roots is −(2 − R2/R1). Since the roots are either both real or a complex-conjugate pair, their average real part is half the sum: 

average Re(x) = (R2/R1 - 2)/2

Dividing by RC gives the average pole real part in s. This gives the instability criterion without fully solving the quadratic.

Concept checks

  • Q: What happens when R2/R1 = 2?
    A: Then Q → ∞ in the linear formula, corresponding to zero damping and poles on the imaginary axis.
  • Q: Why can Q become negative here?
    A: Because this is not a passive resonator; it is an active feedback system, so the effective damping can be negative.
  • Q: Does R2/R1 > 2 mean the output stays bounded forever?
    A: No. In the linear model it grows without bound; in real circuits, nonlinearity limits the amplitude.
  • Q: What primarily sets the oscillation frequency scale?
    A: The product RC, through ω0 = 1/(RC).
