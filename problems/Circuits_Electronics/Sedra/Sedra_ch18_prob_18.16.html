Quick Summary

  • This problem is about finding the loop gain of an op-amp RC oscillator network and using the oscillation condition.
  • The key physics/electronics idea is the Barkhausen criterion: at the oscillation frequency, the loop phase must be 0° (or 360°) and the loop magnitude must be 1.
  • The amplifier is a non-inverting ideal op-amp stage with gain A = 1 + R2/R1.
  • The feedback network connected to the non-inverting input is a frequency-selective RC network whose transfer function is derived by nodal analysis in the Laplace domain.
  • The governing equations come from KCL and capacitor impedance ZC = 1/(sC), or equivalently admittance YC = sC.
  • The loop gain is symbolic: L(s) = (1 + R2/R1) · [sRC / (s²R²C² + 3sRC + 1)].
  • Zero loop phase occurs at ω0 = 1/(RC).
  • At that frequency, the RC network magnitude is 1/3, so oscillation requires the amplifier gain to be 3.
  • Therefore the required resistor ratio is R2/R1 = 2.
  • Physically, the RC network selects one frequency while the op-amp provides just enough gain to compensate for losses.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions

vo(t) — output voltage of the op-amp, in volts (V).

v+(t) — non-inverting input voltage, in volts (V).

v-(t) — inverting input voltage, in volts (V).

R, R1, R2 — resistances, in ohms (Ω).

C — capacitance, in farads (F).

s = σ + jω — Laplace variable, units s-1.

ω — angular frequency, in rad/s.

L(s) — loop gain, dimensionless.

β(s) — feedback network transfer from output to non-inverting input.

A — amplifier gain from v+ to vo when the op-amp is ideal and used with the resistive divider.

Physical meaning of the key quantities

In oscillator analysis, the circuit is viewed as a loop. One part is an amplifier, and the other part is a frequency-selective feedback network. The amplifier supplies energy; the feedback network decides which frequency receives reinforcement. If, after one full loop, a sinusoidal signal returns with the same phase and the same amplitude, the signal can sustain itself without external forcing.

Key laws and principles

Essential laws used here
  • Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL): sum of currents leaving any node is zero.
  • Ideal op-amp assumptions: infinite input resistance, zero output resistance, infinite open-loop gain.
  • Linear time-invariant modeling: resistors and capacitors are linear, so Laplace-domain algebra applies.
  • Barkhausen condition: for sustained sinusoidal oscillation at ω = ω0, the loop must satisfy |L(jω0)| = 1 and ∠L(jω0) = 0° (mod 360°).

Validity conditions and assumptions

  • The op-amp is ideal, exactly as the problem states.
  • We analyze the linear startup condition. Real amplitude stabilization is not modeled.
  • All components labeled R are equal to R, and both capacitors labeled C are equal to C.
  • The oscillation is sinusoidal and studied in steady-state frequency-domain form.

Common models/approximations used in this topic

Oscillator circuits are often modeled by splitting the loop into:

  1. an amplifier block with gain A, and
  2. a feedback block β(s), usually frequency dependent.

This works because the circuit is linearized around small-signal operation. The RC network is analyzed exactly in the Laplace domain; no approximation is needed there.

Mini intuition examples

Example 1

If the loop phase is not zero, the returned signal does not line up with itself, so reinforcement fails.

Example 2

If the loop magnitude is less than one, noise dies out instead of building into oscillation.

Example 3

If the loop magnitude is greater than one, oscillation amplitude grows until nonlinearities limit it.

What to watch for

  • Do not confuse the op-amp closed-loop gain with the total loop gain.
  • Do not forget that the non-inverting RC network itself contributes phase.
  • Be careful with capacitor placement: Fig. P18.16 is not the same topology as Fig. P18.15, even though the final oscillation condition turns out to be the same.
  • In the Laplace domain, sRC is dimensionless. This is a useful dimensional check.
Math Insight

The core object here is a rational transfer function in the complex variable s. Its numerator and denominator encode zeros and poles. Oscillation selection occurs where the feedback transfer function becomes purely real and positive, so the loop phase vanishes. Mathematically, this is a frequency-selection condition on a second-order linear network.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this matters in industry

  • Instrumentation and sensors: RC oscillators are used in test equipment, function generation, and timing references.
  • Industrial control electronics: oscillators provide clocks, excitation signals, and reference waveforms for filters and measurement chains.
  • Consumer and embedded electronics: low-cost waveform generation often uses op-amp or comparator-based oscillators.
  • Audio and signal conditioning: Wien-type oscillators are classic sources for low-distortion sine waves in calibration and lab instruments.

Why engineers care

Performance: frequency accuracy and low distortion matter in measurement systems.

Reliability: startup must occur robustly despite tolerances and temperature drift.

Cost: simple RC oscillators are cheaper than precision crystal systems in many applications.

Manufacturability: choosing component ratios that are tolerant to real-world spreads reduces reject rates.

Typical measurement and validation methods

  • Oscilloscope measurement of output waveform and startup transient.
  • Frequency counter or FFT measurement of oscillation frequency.
  • Bode measurement of the loop or network transfer during design validation.
  • Monte Carlo simulation for resistor/capacitor tolerances.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Real op-amps have finite bandwidth, finite slew rate, input bias currents, and noise.
  • Capacitors have tolerances and temperature coefficients; this shifts ω0.
  • If gain is too low, the oscillator does not start. If gain is too high, distortion rises because amplitude grows until clipped or limited.

From lab to product

The idealized model gives the target frequency and nominal gain condition. In real products, engineers add amplitude-control mechanisms such as lamp stabilization, diodes, AGC, or digital regulation so the oscillator starts reliably but settles to low distortion.

Restatement of the problem

We must repeat the analysis of Problem 18.15 for the modified circuit in Fig. P18.16. Specifically, we need the loop gain L(s), its sinusoidal form L(jω), the frequency at which the loop phase is zero, and the resistor ratio R2/R1 required for oscillation.

Given quantities

  • Ideal op-amp.
  • Non-inverting amplifier formed by R1 and R2.
  • Positive-feedback RC network with two equal resistors R and two equal capacitors C arranged as in Fig. P18.16.

Unknowns

  • L(s)
  • L(jω)
  • ω such that ∠L(jω)=0
  • R2/R1 for oscillation

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Nodal analysis in the s-domain: the RC network is linear and time-invariant, so it is the cleanest exact method.
  • Ideal non-inverting gain: since the op-amp is ideal, the amplifier block gain is directly 1 + R2/R1.
  • Barkhausen criterion: once L(jω) is known, the oscillation condition follows immediately.

Assumptions

Assumptions used
  • Linear small-signal analysis around startup.
  • No loading of the feedback network by the op-amp input because input current is zero for an ideal op-amp.
  • No parasitics, no saturation, no finite-bandwidth effects.

Possible approaches

Approach Idea Pros Cons
Direct nodal analysis Write KCL at the two RC-network nodes in the s-domain. Exact, systematic, and transparent. Requires some algebra.
Impedance reduction Try to collapse parts of the RC ladder before solving. Can be shorter if done carefully. Easy to make topology mistakes.
State-space/ODE view Write time-domain differential equations and transform to s-domain. Good for deeper theory. Longer than needed for this problem.

Best approach

The best method is direct nodal analysis in the Laplace domain. It uses the ideal-op-amp structure cleanly, exposes the transfer function of the feedback network, and makes the zero-phase condition easy to evaluate.

  2. Label the RC-network nodes: define the non-inverting input node and the intermediate node.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition before calculation

Because the RC network has one series capacitor and one shunt capacitor, we expect it to behave like a frequency-selective network: at very low frequency, the series capacitor tends to block transmission; at very high frequency, the shunt capacitor heavily diverts signal to ground. Therefore, only an intermediate frequency should produce the correct phase and a significant transmitted amplitude. That is exactly the behavior needed for oscillation selection.

Step 1: Identify the amplifier gain

The inverting input is connected to the standard divider made of R2 from output to v-, and R1 from v- to ground. Since the op-amp is ideal and operates in the non-inverting configuration, the closed-loop gain from v+ to vo is 

A = vo / v+ = 1 + R2/R1

This is dimensionless, as it must be.

Step 2: Define the RC-network variables

Let

  • va = voltage at the non-inverting input node, so va = v+,
  • vb = voltage at the intermediate node between the series capacitor, the shunt capacitor, and the series resistor to the output,
  • vo = output voltage.

The RC topology in Fig. P18.16 is:

  • node a to ground through R,
  • node a to node b through C,
  • node b to ground through C,
  • node b to output through R.

Because the op-amp input current is zero, node a is not otherwise loaded.

Step 3: Mathematical model in the Laplace domain

Mathematical model

We model the network on the complex-frequency domain s ∈ ℂ using linear circuit equations. The unknowns are va(s) and vb(s). The input is vo(s). Since this is lumped-circuit theory rather than a field problem, no spatial operators such as ∇ or ∇² are needed here. The governing equations are algebraic KCL relations obtained from the constitutive laws iR = v/R and iC = C dv/dt, which become iC(s) = sCv(s) in the Laplace domain.

Step 4: Write KCL at node a

Currents leaving node a:

  • through the resistor to ground: va/R,
  • through the capacitor to node b: sC(va - vb).

So KCL gives

va/R + sC(va - vb) = 0

Rearrange:

(1/R + sC)va - sC vb = 0

This step is valid because KCL is linear, and both the resistor and capacitor relations are linear in the Laplace domain.

Step 5: Write KCL at node b

Currents leaving node b:

  • through the resistor to the output node: (vb - vo)/R,
  • through the capacitor to ground: sCvb,
  • through the capacitor to node a: sC(vb - va).

Thus,

(vb - vo)/R + sC vb + sC(vb - va) = 0

Rearrange:

-sC va + (1/R + 2sC)vb = vo/R

Step 6: Solve for β(s) = va/vo

From the node-a equation, 

(1/R + sC)va = sC vb

So

vb = ((1/R + sC)/(sC)) va

Substitute this into the node-b equation: 

-sC va + (1/R + 2sC) ((1/R + sC)/(sC)) va = vo/R

Multiply both sides by sC to simplify: 

-(sC)^2 va + (1/R + 2sC)(1/R + sC) va = (sC/R) vo

Expand the product: 

(1/R + 2sC)(1/R + sC) = 1/R^2 + 3sC/R + 2s^2C^2

Therefore,

[-s^2C^2 + 1/R^2 + 3sC/R + 2s^2C^2] va = (sC/R) vo

Combine the s² terms:

(1/R^2 + 3sC/R + s^2C^2) va = (sC/R) vo

Multiply numerator and denominator by R²: 

va/vo = (sCR)/(1 + 3sCR + s^2C^2R^2)

Hence the feedback transfer function is 

β(s) = v+/vo = sRC / (s^2R^2C^2 + 3sRC + 1)

Notice the elegant dimensionless structure: every term in the denominator is dimensionless because sRC has no units.

Step 7: Form the loop gain L(s)

The loop gain is amplifier gain times feedback transfer: 

L(s) = A β(s) = (1 + R2/R1) · [sRC / (s^2R^2C^2 + 3sRC + 1)]
Key equation

This is the symbolic answer for L(s).

Step 8: Evaluate L(jω)

Set s = jω. Then 

L(jω) = (1 + R2/R1) · [jωRC / (1 - ω^2R^2C^2 + j3ωRC)]

since (jω)² = -ω².

Step 9: Find the frequency for zero loop phase

The factor (1 + R2/R1) is real and positive, so it contributes zero phase. Therefore, the phase of L(jω) comes entirely from 

jωRC / (1 - ω^2R^2C^2 + j3ωRC)

Let x = ωRC. This nondimensionalization is useful because the transfer function depends only on the combination ωRC. Then 

L(jω) ∝ jx / (1 - x^2 + j3x)

The numerator has phase +90°. For the overall phase to be zero, the denominator must also have phase +90°, so it must be purely imaginary and positive. That happens when its real part vanishes: 

1 - x^2 = 0  ⇒  x = 1  (take x > 0 for positive frequency)

Thus,

ω0 = 1/(RC)

In ordinary frequency, 

f0 = 1/(2πRC)

Step 10: Evaluate the loop gain magnitude at ω0

Substitute x = 1 into β(jω): 

β(jω0) = j / (0 + j3) = 1/3

Therefore, 

L(jω0) = (1 + R2/R1) · (1/3)

For sustained oscillation, |L(jω0)| = 1, so 

(1 + R2/R1)/3 = 1

Hence,

1 + R2/R1 = 3  ⇒  R2/R1 = 2

Sanity checks

1) Dimensional analysis

  • sRC is dimensionless because s has units s-1 and RC has units s.
  • Therefore β(s) and L(s) are dimensionless, which is correct for gains.
  • ω0 = 1/(RC) has units rad/s, also correct.

2) Limiting-case asymptotics

Let x = ωRC.

  • Low frequency, x ≪ 1: β(jω) ≈ jx. Magnitude tends to zero, so low frequency is suppressed.
  • High frequency, x ≫ 1: β(jω) ≈ jx / (-x² + j3x) ~ O(1/x). Again the magnitude tends to zero, so high frequency is suppressed.
  • Intermediate frequency, x = 1: the phase condition is exactly satisfied and the feedback magnitude peaks at a useful nonzero value 1/3.

3) Physical interpretation

The network passes neither very low nor very high frequency effectively. Only near ω = 1/(RC) does the returned signal line up in phase with the original signal. The op-amp must then provide gain 3 so that the one-third attenuation of the RC network is exactly compensated.

Final boxed result

L(s) = (1 + R2/R1) · [sRC / (s^2R^2C^2 + 3sRC + 1)]

L(jω) = (1 + R2/R1) · [jωRC / (1 - ω^2R^2C^2 + j3ωRC)]

Zero loop phase occurs at:
ω0 = 1/(RC)
and therefore
f0 = 1/(2πRC)

At ω0, β(jω0) = 1/3, so oscillation requires:
1 + R2/R1 = 3

Hence:
R2/R1 = 2
Optional deeper note: time-domain ODE viewpoint

Because the feedback network is second order, one can also derive a second-order linear ODE relating v+(t) and vo(t). In operator form, the denominator (R²C² d²/dt² + 3RC d/dt + 1) acts on v+(t), while the numerator RC d/dt acts on vo(t). This makes the network look like a linear filter governed by a constant-coefficient ODE.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpreting the final formula

The loop gain 

L(s) = (1 + R2/R1) · [sRC / (s^2R^2C^2 + 3sRC + 1)]

has two factors:

  • 1 + R2/R1 controls the overall loop magnitude.
  • sRC / (s²R²C² + 3sRC + 1) controls both the frequency selection and the loop phase.

Parameter dependence

R or C increase ω0 = 1/(RC) decreases, so oscillation frequency goes down.
R2/R1 increase Amplifier gain rises. Startup becomes easier, but distortion risk rises in real circuits.
R2/R1 decrease Loop gain falls below unity and oscillation cannot sustain.

Alternative derivation idea

An alternative route is to derive the two-node state equations in time domain, then take the Laplace transform. This gives the same transfer function but emphasizes that the network is a second-order dynamical system. For pure circuit work, however, nodal analysis is faster and less error-prone.

Concept check Q&A

  • Q: Why is the oscillation frequency not set by R1 and R2?
    A: Because R1 and R2 set only the amplifier gain, not the RC phase-selective condition.
  • Q: Why is β(jω0) real and positive?
    A: At ω0, the numerator and denominator both have +90° phase, so their ratio has 0° phase.
  • Q: Why does the network reject very low frequency?
    A: The series capacitor between the two nodes impedes transmission as ω → 0.
  • Q: Why does the network reject very high frequency?
    A: The shunt capacitor at node b strongly pulls signal toward ground at high ω.
  • Q: What happens in a real circuit if gain is slightly above 3?
    A: Oscillation starts and amplitude grows until nonlinearity reduces the effective loop gain to unity.
