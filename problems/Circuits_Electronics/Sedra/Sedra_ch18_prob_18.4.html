Frequency Sensitivity of the RLC Feedback Oscillator in Fig. 18.3(a)

We are asked to determine how the oscillation frequency changes when one component at a time is increased by +1%: the inductor L, the capacitor C, and the resistor R. The key idea is that the feedback network oscillates at the parallel LC resonance, so the oscillation frequency is governed by the resonance condition rather than by the amplifier gain magnitude.

Key Equations Resonance condition: ω0 = 1 / √(LC) Oscillation frequency: f0 = 1 / (2π√(LC)) Sensitivity form: Δf/f ≈ -(1/2)(ΔL/L) -(1/2)(ΔC/C) Dependence on R: f0 is independent of R for the ideal circuit

Quick Summary

  • This problem is about parameter sensitivity: how much the oscillation frequency changes when a circuit component changes slightly.
  • The central physics idea is resonance of a parallel LC branch inside a feedback loop.
  • The governing frequency condition is ω0 = 1/√(LC), hence f0 = 1/(2π√(LC)).
  • The resistor R affects the feedback magnitude and startup/amplitude behavior, but not the ideal oscillation frequency.
  • For a +1% change in L, the frequency decreases by about 0.5% (more precisely, about 0.496%).
  • For a +1% change in C, the frequency also decreases by about 0.5% (again, about 0.496% exactly).
  • For a +1% change in R, the ideal oscillation frequency changes by 0%.
  • The result is symbolic and physically means that frequency is set by the energy exchange between electric and magnetic storage, not by the series resistor in this idealized model.

PART 0 — Concept Primer

Before solving, let us review the theory of feedback oscillators and RLC resonance. This is fundamentally an interplay between circuit theory, linear systems, and frequency-domain analysis.

Core definitions

  • L: inductance, SI unit henry (H).
  • C: capacitance, SI unit farad (F).
  • R: resistance, SI unit ohm (Ω).
  • ω: angular frequency, unit rad/s.
  • f: ordinary frequency, unit Hz, with ω = 2πf.
  • A(s): amplifier transfer function.
  • β(s): feedback network transfer function.
  • s: Laplace variable, s = σ + jω.

Physical meaning

  • The capacitor stores electric-field energy: EC = (1/2)CV².
  • The inductor stores magnetic-field energy: EL = (1/2)LI².
  • Oscillation occurs when energy sloshes back and forth between these two storage mechanisms.
  • The resonant frequency is the natural exchange rate of that energy.
  • The resistor mainly controls dissipation and feedback attenuation, not the ideal LC natural frequency.
Validity conditions and assumptions We treat the op-amp and the passive network using the standard linear, time-invariant model in the frequency domain. The op-amp input current is neglected, parasitic resistance of the inductor and capacitor is neglected, and the oscillator frequency is determined from the phase condition of the loop. These are the usual textbook assumptions for first-pass oscillator analysis.

The usual oscillator criterion is the Barkhausen condition:

Barkhausen criterion |A(jω)β(jω)| = 1, ∠A(jω)β(jω) = 2πn, where n is any integer.

The second condition is the one that fixes the oscillation frequency. The first condition fixes whether the loop can sustain the oscillation amplitude.

Math Insight The feedback network here is a linear rational transfer function of the Laplace variable s. The oscillation frequency is found by solving a phase-matching condition, which is equivalent to finding where the loop transfer function becomes real and positive. Mathematically, this is a root/constraint problem in the complex-frequency plane.

Common models and approximations used here

  • Phasor analysis: valid for steady sinusoidal operation.
  • Laplace-domain impedance: ZL = sL, ZC = 1/(sC).
  • Ideal op-amp noninverting gain: A = 1 + r2/r1, treated as real and frequency-independent over the narrow range of interest.
  • Small percentage change approximation: for sensitivity, Δf/f can be obtained by logarithmic differentiation.

Mini intuition examples

Example 1

If L becomes larger, the magnetic energy storage is “heavier,” so the energy exchange slows down. Thus frequency decreases.

Example 2

If C becomes larger, more electric charge is needed for a given voltage swing, so oscillation also slows down.

Example 3

Changing R alters how strongly the output drives the resonator, but the LC branch still resonates at the same ideal frequency.

What to watch for
  • Do not confuse resonance frequency with bandwidth or gain.
  • Do not assume every component affects frequency. Some affect only amplitude or startup.
  • Use angular frequency ω and ordinary frequency f consistently.
  • For percentage changes, distinguish between the exact finite change and the differential approximation.
  • The sign matters: increasing L or C lowers f0.
Optional deeper note: ODE viewpoint

An ideal LC oscillator can also be understood in the time domain. For example, the voltage across the capacitor in an isolated LC system obeys

d²v/dt² + (1/LC)v = 0.

This is the differential equation of a harmonic oscillator with natural angular frequency ω0 = √(1/LC). The feedback amplifier compensates losses and maintains the motion.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Resonant oscillators are not just textbook circuits. They are a foundation of timing, sensing, filtering, and communication systems. In industrial engineering and applied physics, the frequency sensitivity to component tolerances matters because it directly affects performance, reliability, manufacturability, and calibration cost.

Where this appears in industry

  • Communication electronics: local oscillators, RF references, frequency-selective front ends.
  • Instrumentation: resonant sensors, impedance analyzers, bridge circuits, frequency-based measurement systems.
  • Power electronics and control: resonant converters, gate-drive timing circuits, feedback compensation networks.
  • Embedded systems: clock generation, timing references, low-cost oscillator modules.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: wrong frequency means mistuned filters, off-channel transmission, or inaccurate sensing.
  • Reliability: temperature drift and component aging shift L and C, causing long-term deviation.
  • Cost: tighter tolerances cost more, so sensitivity analysis helps decide where precision is worth paying for.
  • Manufacturability: tolerance stack-up predicts production spread in oscillator frequency.

Typical measurement and validation methods

  • Frequency counter or spectrum analyzer measurement of oscillation frequency.
  • LCR meter characterization of L and C.
  • Monte Carlo tolerance analysis in circuit simulation.
  • Environmental testing for temperature and aging drift.
Design trade-offs and constraints Real inductors have series resistance and parasitic capacitance. Real capacitors have equivalent series resistance, dielectric loss, and temperature coefficient. Op-amps have finite bandwidth, finite slew rate, input bias currents, and phase shift. In product design, the ideal formula f0 = 1/(2π√(LC)) is a starting point, then engineers add parasitics, tolerance distributions, thermal coefficients, and amplitude control circuits.

From lab to product: the ideal model is adapted by replacing ideal L, C, and R with measured or fitted equivalent circuits. Then the nominal oscillation frequency, tolerance band, startup margin, and temperature drift are verified experimentally.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

In plain words, the problem asks: For the oscillator built from a noninverting amplifier and an RLC feedback network, how much does the oscillation frequency change if one element at a time is increased by +1%?

Category Content
Given The oscillator in Fig. 18.3(a), with feedback resistor R and parallel reactive branch L || C.
Unknowns The percentage change in oscillation frequency due to +1% change in L, C, and R.
What must be found (%Δf) for each one-parameter perturbation.

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Barkhausen phase condition: determines oscillation frequency.
  • Impedance of parallel LC network: determines the feedback phase and magnitude.
  • Resonance condition: when the net susceptance of the parallel LC branch vanishes.
  • Sensitivity analysis: useful because the change is only +1%, so differential and exact finite-change methods are both convenient.
Assumptions used in the solution
  • Ideal op-amp input: no loading of the resonator node.
  • Ideal passive components: no parasitic loss beyond the shown resistor R.
  • Linear small-signal frequency selection determines f0.
  • The shown resistor R does not alter the phase-zero condition of the ideal LC resonance.

Possible approaches

Approach A: Direct resonance argument

Use the fact that the parallel LC branch resonates when its reactive admittance is zero, giving ω0 = 1/√(LC).

Pros: fastest and physically transparent.

Cons: does not display the full loop function explicitly.

Approach B: Full loop transfer function

Compute A(s) and β(s), then impose zero loop phase.

Pros: rigorous and shows why R drops out of the frequency condition.

Cons: more algebra.

Approach C: Time-domain differential equation

Derive the resonator equation and infer the natural frequency.

Pros: strong physics interpretation.

Cons: less directly tied to the shown feedback topology.

Chosen method We will use Approach B as the main derivation because it is the most rigorous for this exact circuit, and then use the resonance formula and sensitivity analysis to obtain the requested percentage changes cleanly.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

  1. Write the amplifier gain A(s). Goal: isolate the active block. Tool: ideal noninverting op-amp gain formula. Physical meaning: how much the amplifier multiplies the feedback signal.
  2. Write the resonator impedance seen at the feedback node. Goal: model the L and C branch mathematically. Tool: parallel combination using Laplace impedances. Physical meaning: frequency-dependent loading of the node.
  3. Construct β(s). Goal: find the fraction of output voltage returned to the op-amp input. Tool: voltage division. Physical meaning: the frequency-selective feedback path.
  4. Apply the phase condition for oscillation. Goal: determine ω0. Tool: Barkhausen phase criterion. Physical meaning: the returned signal must reinforce itself.
  5. Convert ω0 to f0. Goal: express the result in ordinary frequency. Tool: ω = 2πf. Physical meaning: the actual oscillation rate in hertz.
  6. Differentiate or perturb the formula for f0. Goal: get the change due to +1% in each component. Tool: logarithmic differentiation and exact finite ratio check. Physical meaning: tolerance sensitivity.
  7. Interpret the result. Goal: explain why R does not change the ideal frequency. Tool: physical reasoning from the phase condition. Physical meaning: distinguish resonance-setting components from loss-setting components.
Common mistakes
  • Using amplitude condition instead of phase condition to find frequency.
  • Forgetting that f0 depends on √(LC), not on LC directly.
  • Reporting +0.5% instead of -0.5% for an increase in L or C.
  • Claiming R changes frequency in the ideal model merely because it appears in the circuit.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Physical intuition before calculation

We expect the oscillation frequency to be set by the LC resonator. Increasing either L or C should slow the oscillation and reduce the frequency. The resistor R dissipates energy and changes feedback strength, so it should affect startup and amplitude conditions, but not the ideal resonant frequency.

Step 1: Amplifier transfer function

The op-amp is configured as a noninverting amplifier. Therefore

A(s) = Vo(s) / Vi(s) = 1 + r2/r1.

This is a real positive constant under the ideal op-amp model. Hence the amplifier contributes no frequency-selective phase shift in this simplified analysis.

Step 2: Impedance of the parallel LC branch

Let the feedback-node voltage be Vr(s). The branch from that node to ground is a parallel combination of L and C.

Using Laplace impedances:

ZL = sL, ZC = 1/(sC).

The equivalent impedance of the parallel branch is

Zp(s) = ZL || ZC = (ZL ZC)/(ZL + ZC) = (sL · 1/(sC)) / (sL + 1/(sC)) = (L/C) / (sL + 1/(sC)).

Multiply numerator and denominator by sC:

Zp(s) = sL / (1 + s²LC).

This is valid because the network is linear and the parallel-combination formula applies directly in the Laplace domain.

Step 3: Feedback transfer function β(s)

The resistor R is in series between the output and the resonator node. Thus, by voltage division,

β(s) = Vr(s) / Vo(s) = Zp(s) / (R + Zp(s)).

Substitute Zp(s) = sL/(1 + s²LC):

β(s) = [sL/(1 + s²LC)] / [R + sL/(1 + s²LC)] = sL / [R(1 + s²LC) + sL].

Therefore

β(s) = sL / (R + sL + Rs²LC).

Step 4: Loop phase condition

The loop gain is

A(s)β(s) = (1 + r2/r1) · [sL / (R + sL + Rs²LC)].

Since A(s) is real and positive, the oscillation frequency is determined by the phase of β(jω). Set s = jω:

β(jω) = jωL / [R + jωL + R(jω)²LC] = jωL / [R + jωL - Rω²LC] = jωL / [R(1 - ω²LC) + jωL].

For β(jω) to have zero phase, the denominator must have the same phase as the numerator. The numerator jωL is purely imaginary and has phase +90°. Therefore the denominator must also be purely imaginary, which means its real part must vanish:

R(1 - ω²LC) = 0.

Since R ≠ 0, we obtain

1 - ω²LC = 0 ω0² = 1/(LC) ω0 = 1/√(LC).

Converting to ordinary frequency,

f0 = ω0/(2π) = 1/(2π√(LC)).
Why this step is mathematically valid We imposed the zero-phase condition by setting the real part of the denominator to zero. This works because the numerator is purely imaginary, so equal phase requires the denominator to lie on the same ray in the complex plane. This is a standard phasor/complex-analysis argument in circuit theory.

Step 5: Dimensional check

The product LC has units

[H·F] = s².

Therefore 1/√(LC) has units 1/s, exactly as required for angular frequency. Then f0 = ω0/(2π) has units of hertz. So the formula is dimensionally correct.

Step 6: Sensitivity to L and C

Write the frequency explicitly:

f0 = (1/2π)L^(-1/2)C^(-1/2).

Take the logarithm:

ln f0 = -ln(2π) - (1/2)ln L - (1/2)ln C.

Differentiate:

df0/f0 = -(1/2)(dL/L) - (1/2)(dC/C).

So the normalized sensitivities are

SL^f = (d f0 / f0) / (dL / L) = -1/2, SC^f = (d f0 / f0) / (dC / C) = -1/2, SR^f = 0.

For a +1% change in L alone:

Δf/f ≈ -(1/2)(+1%) = -0.5%.

For a +1% change in C alone:

Δf/f ≈ -(1/2)(+1%) = -0.5%.

For a +1% change in R:

Δf/f = 0.

Step 7: Exact finite-change check

Because the problem specifies a finite +1% change, we can also compute the exact ratios.

(a) +1% in L: replace L → 1.01L.

f' = 1 / [2π√((1.01L)C)] = f0 / √1.01.

Hence

(f' - f0)/f0 = 1/√1.01 - 1 ≈ -0.0049628 = -0.4963%.

(b) +1% in C: replace C → 1.01C.

f' = 1 / [2π√(L(1.01C))] = f0 / √1.01.

Therefore

(f' - f0)/f0 = 1/√1.01 - 1 ≈ -0.4963%.

(c) +1% in R: the frequency formula contains no R.

f' = f0, (f' - f0)/f0 = 0.
Final Answer (a) If L increases by +1%, the oscillation frequency decreases by about 0.5% Exact value: -0.4963% (b) If C increases by +1%, the oscillation frequency decreases by about 0.5% Exact value: -0.4963% (c) If R increases by +1%, the ideal oscillation frequency changes by 0%

Sanity checks

  • Units: correct, because LC has units of s².
  • Limiting case: if L or C grows very large, frequency should decrease; the formula predicts exactly that.
  • Sign check: increasing an energy-storage element slows the oscillation, so the negative sign is physically sensible.
  • R check: since R does not enter the phase-zero condition, it does not shift the ideal oscillation frequency.
Optional deeper note: nondimensionalization

A useful dimensionless variable is x = ω√(LC). Then the real part of the denominator of β(jω) is proportional to 1 - x². Oscillation occurs when x = 1. This shows directly that the frequency condition is controlled by the single dimensionless combination ω√(LC), not by R.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

Re-interpret the final formula

The result

f0 = 1/(2π√(LC))

says that frequency is controlled by the geometric mean of the two reactive parameters. Neither L nor C acts alone; the relevant quantity is the product LC.

Parameter dependence

  • L increases → f0 decreases as L^(-1/2).
  • C increases → f0 decreases as C^(-1/2).
  • R increases → startup margin and loop magnitude may change, but ideal f0 does not.
Applied interpretation In a real product, R can affect the observed frequency slightly through non-ideal amplifier phase shift, parasitic resistance, and amplitude-dependent nonlinearities. But in the ideal textbook model used here, the frequency is strictly independent of R.

Alternative derivation idea

Instead of using the feedback transfer function, one could derive the resonance condition from the parallel LC admittance:

Yp(jω) = jωC + 1/(jωL) = j(ωC - 1/(ωL)).

Resonance occurs when the imaginary part vanishes:

ωC - 1/(ωL) = 0 ⇒ ω² = 1/(LC).

This is shorter and highlights the physical meaning: at resonance, inductive and capacitive susceptances cancel.

Concept checks

  • Q: Why is the sensitivity to L equal to the sensitivity to C? A: Because f0 depends symmetrically on L and C through √(LC).
  • Q: Why is the sign negative? A: Larger reactive storage slows the natural oscillation.
  • Q: What does R mainly affect here? A: Feedback magnitude, damping, startup condition, and practical amplitude behavior.
  • Q: Is -0.5% exact for a +1% change? A: No. It is the linear approximation. The exact value is 1/√1.01 - 1 ≈ -0.4963%.

PART 5 — Optional Plot Guide

No plot is necessary for this problem type because the answer follows directly from a symbolic resonance relation and a simple sensitivity calculation. A graph would add little beyond the compact formula f0 ∝ (LC)^(-1/2).
