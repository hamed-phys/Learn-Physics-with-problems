Active-Filter-Tuned Oscillator: Frequency, Amplitude, and How to Double It

We analyze the practical active-filter-tuned oscillator of Fig. 18.12, find the resistor value that gives a 10 kHz sinusoidal output for C = 1.6 nF, estimate the output sine-wave peak-to-peak amplitude when the limiter diodes drop 0.7 V, and explain how to modify the limiter so the sine-wave amplitude doubles.

Quick Summary

  • The problem is about a tuned active oscillator whose frequency is set by an RC resonator and whose amplitude is stabilized by diode limiting.
  • The key physics idea is selective resonance + nonlinear amplitude control: the loop favors one frequency, while the diodes clip the internal waveform and prevent unlimited growth.
  • The governing equation for the oscillation frequency is ω0 = 1/(RC), hence f0 = 1/(2πRC).
  • The limiter makes the internal waveform at v2 nearly a square wave with approximately V2,pp ≈ 2VD.
  • The square-wave fundamental obeys the given hint Vfund,pp = 4Vpp/π.
  • For this oscillator, the final sine output is about twice that fundamental component, giving Vo,pp ≈ 2·(4V2,pp/π).
  • Numerically, R ≈ 9.95 kΩ ≈ 10 kΩ and Vo,pp ≈ 3.6 V.
  • To double the output amplitude, double the limiter threshold, for example by using two diodes in series in each clipping branch.

Physical meaning of the key quantities

The oscillator is a feedback system that turns DC power into a periodic AC output. The RC network chooses the preferred oscillation frequency, while the diodes stop the amplitude from growing without bound.

In steady operation, one internal node becomes close to a square wave because of clipping. The active filter then extracts mainly the fundamental Fourier component, so the output node is approximately sinusoidal.

Key laws and validity conditions
  • Kirchhoff’s current law (KCL) and Kirchhoff’s voltage law (KVL) apply to the lumped RC-op-amp network.
  • Ideal op-amp model is used for the design equation: input currents are negligible and the feedback forces the needed terminal relation.
  • Linear small-signal analysis determines the oscillation frequency because the limiter mainly affects amplitude, not the tuning law.
  • Fourier-series analysis is valid because a periodic square-like waveform can be decomposed into harmonics, and the tuned network favors the first harmonic.

Math Insight

Ignoring the limiter for a moment, the oscillator behaves like a linear time-invariant second-order dynamical system. The oscillation condition corresponds to a characteristic equation with purely imaginary roots s = ±jω0. This is mathematically the same structure as an ideal undamped harmonic oscillator.

Mathematical model (linearized frequency-setting stage): d x / d t = A x
Oscillation threshold: det(sI − A) = 0 with s = ±jω0
Result for this topology: ω0 = 1/(RC)
Thus: f0 = 1/(2πRC)

Mathematical-physics viewpoint

This is not a field problem, so operators such as ∇, ∇·, ∇×, and the Laplacian do not naturally appear in the governing model. The relevant mathematical structure is an ordinary differential equation system, not a PDE. The capacitor law introduces time derivatives:

i_C(t) = C d v_C(t) / d t

Because the network is lumped and spatial propagation is neglected, the state variables depend on time only, not on position. Thus the correct language is ODE/state-space analysis rather than wave-equation or diffusion-equation analysis.

Common approximations used here and why

  • Ideal diode clipping: treat each diode as turning on near a constant drop VD.
  • Square-wave approximation: after clipping, v2(t) is modeled as nearly square.
  • Fundamental-only approximation: the filter strongly suppresses higher harmonics and passes mainly the first harmonic.
  • Nominal component values: tolerances and finite op-amp bandwidth are neglected in the first-pass calculation.

Mini intuition examples

Example 1: why frequency scales like 1/RC

The only time constant formed from R and C is τ = RC. Therefore the characteristic frequency must scale as 1/τ = 1/(RC).

Example 2: why clipping creates harmonics

A pure sine has only one frequency. Once the waveform flattens at the top and bottom, it is no longer sinusoidal, so it must contain harmonics.

Example 3: why the output can still be a sine

A tuned network acts like a frequency selector. Even if the internal signal is square-like, the output taken from the tuned section can remain close to sinusoidal.

What to watch for
  • Do not mix up peak and peak-to-peak values.
  • The hint gives the fundamental component of a square wave, not the full output sine directly.
  • Use ω = 2πf carefully.
  • The frequency comes from the linear tuned network; the amplitude comes from the nonlinear limiter.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry

  • Test and measurement equipment: function generators, calibrators, and low-distortion signal sources.
  • Industrial instrumentation: AC excitation for sensors, impedance bridges, and resonant measurement systems.
  • Communications and embedded electronics: local analog tone generation and reference waveform creation.
  • Process automation: waveform generation in control modules and diagnostic electronics.

Why engineers care

Engineers care because oscillators directly affect performance (frequency accuracy, distortion), reliability (guaranteed startup, thermal stability), cost (component count and tolerance requirements), and manufacturability (repeatability from unit to unit).

Typical measurement and validation in practice

  • Oscilloscope measurement of frequency, peak-to-peak amplitude, and startup behavior.
  • FFT or spectrum-analyzer measurement of harmonic distortion.
  • Temperature and tolerance testing to evaluate drift and robustness.
  • Production-line checks with acceptable windows for amplitude and frequency.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Tighter-tolerance resistors and capacitors improve accuracy but cost more.
  • Simple diode limiting is cheap and robust, but it introduces distortion.
  • Finite op-amp bandwidth and slew rate can shift the frequency or distort the wave.
  • Real diode forward drops vary with temperature and current, so amplitude is not perfectly fixed.
From lab to product

In a product, the ideal model is refined by including finite op-amp gain-bandwidth, diode dynamic resistance, component tolerances, supply limits, and thermal drift. If low distortion is crucial, designers may replace hard diode limiting with a smoother automatic-gain-control scheme.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restated in plain words

We must design the resistor value in the active-filter-tuned oscillator so that it oscillates at 10 kHz when C = 1.6 nF. Then we must estimate the output sine-wave amplitude when the limiter diodes each have a forward drop of 0.7 V. Finally, we must explain how to change the circuit so that the output amplitude becomes twice as large.

Given quantities

  • C = 1.6 nF = 1.6 × 10−9 F
  • f0 = 10 kHz = 104 Hz
  • VD = 0.7 V

Unknowns

  • R
  • Vo,pp, the output sine-wave peak-to-peak amplitude
  • The circuit modification needed to double the amplitude

What must be found

  • A numerical value for R.
  • A numerical estimate for the steady-state output sine-wave amplitude.
  • A practical design modification that doubles that amplitude.

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Oscillation tuning law: the RC resonator sets the oscillation frequency, so we use f0 = 1/(2πRC).
  • Diode limiter model: the diodes clamp an internal node to roughly ±0.7 V.
  • Fourier-series hint: the square-wave fundamental determines the sine component that the tuned network can sustain.
Assumptions
  • Ideal op-amps for the design calculation.
  • Matched components as labeled in the figure.
  • Oscillation is steady and periodic.
  • The clipped node is close to an ideal square wave.
  • The output node is dominated by the square wave’s fundamental after filtering.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition before calculating

Since the oscillator is built from repeated R and C sections, the oscillation frequency should be controlled by the time constant RC. Also, because the diodes clip the internal waveform, the steady-state amplitude should be set by the diode threshold rather than by the op-amp open-loop behavior.

Step 1: Frequency-setting model

For the practical active-filter-tuned oscillator of Fig. 18.12, the standard oscillation condition gives

ω0 = 1/(RC)

Since ω = 2πf, we convert to ordinary frequency:

2πf0 = 1/(RC)
f0 = 1/(2πRC)

This is valid because the frequency is set by the linear tuned portion of the loop. The limiter is a nonlinear stabilization element and mainly affects amplitude.

Step 2: Solve explicitly for R

Rearranging the previous equation,

R = 1/(2πf0C)

Insert the given values:

R = 1 / [2π(10,000)(1.6 × 10^−9)]
R = 1 / [2π × 1.6 × 10^−5]
R = 1 / [1.0053 × 10^−4]
R = 9.95 × 10^3 Ω
R ≈ 9.95 kΩ

Therefore, the practical design choice is

R ≈ 10 kΩ

Dimensional analysis check

Check the units of R = 1/(2πfC):

[f] = s^−1
[C] = F
[1/(fC)] = 1/(s^−1·F) = s/F = Ω

So the equation is dimensionally correct.

Step 3: Model the limiter mathematically

Let the clipped internal voltage be v2(t). Under the constant-drop diode approximation, the waveform is limited to roughly

v2(t) ≈ ±VD

with VD = 0.7 V. Therefore

V2,peak ≈ 0.7 V
V2,pp ≈ 2 × 0.7 = 1.4 V

In a more formal periodic model, we can write the approximate square wave as

v2(t) ≈ VD · sgn[sin(ω0 t)]

where sgn is the sign function. This is a mathematically convenient idealization of the clipped waveform.

Deeper mathematical note: Fourier-series structure

The square wave has the Fourier series

v2(t) = (4VD/π)[sin(ω0 t) + (1/3)sin(3ω0 t) + (1/5)sin(5ω0 t) + ... ]

Because the tuned network strongly favors ω0, the dominant transmitted part is the first term. That is the mathematical reason the final output is approximately sinusoidal.

Step 4: Use the hint to get the fundamental component

The problem gives the result directly in peak-to-peak form:

Vfund,pp = 4Vpp(square)/π

Here the square-wave peak-to-peak amplitude is V2,pp = 1.4 V, so

V2,fund,pp = 4(1.4)/π
V2,fund,pp = 5.6/π
V2,fund,pp ≈ 1.78 V

This number is the peak-to-peak amplitude of the first harmonic contained in the clipped internal waveform.

Step 5: Convert that to the final output sine-wave amplitude

For this active-filter-tuned oscillator, the sinusoidal output node is approximately twice the first-harmonic component at the clipped node. Therefore,

Vo,pp ≈ 2V2,fund,pp
Vo,pp ≈ 2(1.78 V)
Vo,pp ≈ 3.56 V

So the output sine-wave amplitude is

Vo,pp ≈ 3.6 V

Step 6: How to double the output amplitude

The output amplitude is proportional to the limiter level. Therefore, if we double the limiter threshold, we double the square-wave amplitude, which doubles its fundamental, which then doubles the final sine-wave output.

The simplest circuit modification is:

Replace each diode by two identical diodes in series in the same orientation.

Then the clipping level becomes approximately

Vclip,new ≈ 2VD = 1.4 V (peak)
V2,pp,new ≈ 2.8 V

The new output amplitude is therefore approximately

Vo,pp,new ≈ 2 × 3.56 V
Vo,pp,new ≈ 7.12 V
Vo,pp,new ≈ 7.2 V

Scaling and asymptotic insight

The result has a useful scaling form:

f0 ∝ 1/(RC)
Vo,pp ∝ VD

So a small relative change obeys

δf0/f0 ≈ −δR/R − δC/C
δVo,pp/Vo,pp ≈ δVD/VD

This tells us immediately that a 1% increase in either R or C causes about a 1% decrease in frequency, while doubling the effective diode threshold doubles the steady-state amplitude.

Sanity checks
  • If C becomes larger, the oscillator should slow down. Our formula gives exactly that.
  • If the limiter threshold increases, the clipped internal swing increases, so the sine-wave output should increase. Our result gives exactly that.
  • The amplitude result is only a few volts peak-to-peak, which is very reasonable for diode-limited op-amp oscillators.

Final Answer

R ≈ 10 kΩ
Output sine-wave amplitude
Vo,pp ≈ 3.6 V
To double amplitude

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Reinterpreting the final formula

  • R and C control the time scale, so they determine the frequency.
  • VD controls the nonlinear saturation level, so it determines the steady amplitude.
  • The tuned network acts as a harmonic selector, turning a clipped internal waveform into an approximately sinusoidal external output.

Parameter dependence

ChangeEffect on frequencyEffect on output amplitude
Increase RDecreasesLittle direct effect in the ideal model
Increase CDecreasesLittle direct effect in the ideal model
Increase diode thresholdAlmost none ideallyIncreases almost proportionally
Finite op-amp bandwidthCan shift slightlyCan reduce and distort

Alternative derivation idea

A fuller derivation would write the circuit as a state equation dx/dt = Ax, then solve the eigenvalue problem det(sI − A) = 0. The oscillation condition occurs when the roots are purely imaginary, s = ±jω0. The limiter can then be treated as a nonlinear boundary on the state amplitude.

Concept check Q&A

  • Q: Why is the output nearly sinusoidal if an internal node is square-like?
    A: Because the tuned active filter suppresses higher harmonics and retains mainly the fundamental.
  • Q: What sets the frequency most directly?
    A: The RC time constant of the tuned network.
  • Q: What sets the amplitude most directly?
    A: The limiter threshold set by the diodes.
  • Q: If R doubles and C stays fixed, what happens?
    A: The oscillation frequency is cut in half.
  • Q: Why is linear analysis still useful though the circuit is nonlinear?
    A: Because the nonlinearity mainly stabilizes amplitude, while the oscillation frequency comes from the linear resonant structure.
