Second-Order Bandpass Oscillator: Oscillation Condition, Pole Geometry, and Stability Interpretation

We analyze a sinusoidal oscillator made from an amplifier of constant gain A and a second-order bandpass filter with pole frequency ω₀, pole quality factor Q, and center-frequency gain K. The goal is to determine the oscillation frequency, the sustained-oscillation condition, and then use the closed-loop poles to solve the follow-up stability questions.

Symbolic solution Barkhausen criterion Laplace-domain analysis Applied electronics relevance

Quick Summary

  • This problem is about a linear feedback oscillator built from an amplifier and a second-order bandpass network.
  • The key physics and engineering idea is that sustained sinusoidal oscillation occurs when the loop phase is zero and the loop-gain magnitude is unity.
  • The governing transfer function for the bandpass stage is H(s)=K[(ω₀/Q)s]/[s²+(ω₀/Q)s+ω₀²], which has zero phase at ω=ω₀ and magnitude K there.
  • The oscillation frequency is therefore ωosc = ω₀.
  • The sustained-oscillation condition is AK = 1.
  • The closed-loop characteristic equation becomes s² + (ω₀/Q)(1−AK)s + ω₀² = 0.
  • Its poles always satisfy |s| = ω₀, so they lie on a circle of radius ω₀ in the s-plane, independent of A and K.
  • For poles on the jω axis, AK = 1. For poles in the right half-plane at horizontal distance ω₀/(2Q) from the axis, AK = 2.
  • The final result is symbolic, and physically it tells us exactly when the oscillator is stable, marginal, or growing.

PART 0 — Concept Primer

Core definitions

Symbol Meaning Units
A Frequency-independent amplifier gain in the loop dimensionless
K Center-frequency gain of the bandpass filter dimensionless
ω₀ Pole frequency / natural angular frequency of the second-order section rad/s
Q Quality factor of the pole pair dimensionless
s = σ + jω Complex Laplace variable; σ measures growth/decay, ω measures oscillation s−1
H(s) Bandpass transfer function dimensionless

Physical meaning of the key quantities

A bandpass filter selects a narrow range of frequencies around a preferred frequency. In a feedback oscillator, that preferred frequency becomes the natural candidate for self-sustained oscillation because it is the frequency at which the loop can satisfy the phase condition most naturally.

The quality factor Q measures how sharp the resonance is. A larger Q generally means a narrower passband and weaker damping. The gain K tells us how strongly the bandpass network transmits the signal at resonance. The amplifier gain A compensates for loop losses.

Key laws and principles

Barkhausen idea for sinusoidal oscillation.
For a loop transfer function L(s)=A H(s), a sustained sinusoid is possible when, at some frequency ω, |L(jω)|=1 and ∠L(jω)=2πm for some integer m.

This is a frequency-domain viewpoint. The equivalent pole-based viewpoint is that sustained oscillation occurs when the closed-loop poles lie exactly on the imaginary axis. If the poles are in the left half-plane, oscillations decay. If they are in the right half-plane, oscillations grow.

Mathematical model

We model the bandpass filter as the standard second-order transfer function with positive gain at resonance:

H(s) = K ((ω₀/Q)s) / (s² + (ω₀/Q)s + ω₀²)

This is a linear, time-invariant, second-order system. The corresponding time-domain differential equation for the output y(t) driven by input x(t) is

d²y/dt² + (ω₀/Q) dy/dt + ω₀² y = K (ω₀/Q) dx/dt

The oscillator feedback loop closes this system through x(t)=A y(t) for positive feedback. Because the system is linear and time-invariant, Laplace methods apply directly. No field operator such as ∇, ∇·, or ∇× is needed here because the model is an ordinary differential equation, not a spatial field equation.

Math Insight.
The closed-loop characteristic equation is a quadratic eigenvalue condition in s. Its roots are the system eigenvalues (poles). Their real part sets damping or growth, while their imaginary part sets oscillation frequency. In this problem, the root geometry in the complex plane reveals the physics almost immediately.

Common models and approximations used here

  • Linear small-signal model: we assume the amplifier and filter remain in their linear operating region while deriving the oscillation condition.
  • Frequency-independent amplifier gain: A is treated as constant with respect to frequency.
  • Ideal positive feedback sign convention: the loop is arranged so that the bandpass phase shift at the selected frequency yields net zero loop phase.
  • No nonlinear amplitude limiting in the model: real oscillators need nonlinear saturation to settle to a finite amplitude, but the textbook problem only asks for the linear condition for sustained oscillation.

Mini intuition examples

  • If AK < 1, the loop cannot fully replace the energy lost each cycle, so oscillations decay.
  • If AK = 1, loss is exactly balanced; the oscillation is neutrally sustained.
  • If AK > 1, the loop adds more energy than it loses, so oscillation amplitude grows until nonlinearity eventually limits it in a real circuit.

What to watch for

Pitfalls and hidden assumptions
  • Do not confuse ω₀ as merely the frequency of maximum amplitude with the pole frequency; here the standard form makes them coincide with the center-frequency condition used in the problem.
  • Do not write the loop condition as A=K; the relevant quantity is the product AK.
  • The phase condition matters just as much as the magnitude condition.
  • In the pole calculation, the sign of the feedback term matters. For positive feedback, the characteristic equation is 1-AH(s)=0, not 1+AH(s)=0.
Optional deeper note: nondimensionalization

Let p = s/ω₀. Then the closed-loop characteristic equation becomes

p² + ((1 - AK)/Q)p + 1 = 0

This shows that the entire problem depends only on the dimensionless combinations AK and Q. The radius condition later becomes |p|=1, which is especially elegant: the poles lie on the unit circle in the nondimensional p-plane.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Although the present problem is symbolic and idealized, its structure is directly relevant to practical oscillator design across electronics, sensing, control, and communications.

1) Communications

RF and IF oscillators in radios, mixers, and local oscillator chains rely on resonance, loop gain, and phase conditions to generate stable carrier signals.

2) Instrumentation and sensors

Resonant sensing circuits, lock-in detection stages, and frequency-selective measurement electronics use bandpass dynamics to isolate weak signals from noise.

3) Industrial control and power electronics

Feedback loops in converters and active filters must avoid unintended oscillation; the same pole-gain logic appears in stability margins, ringing, and startup behavior.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: frequency accuracy, phase noise, selectivity, and startup margin matter.
  • Reliability: too much loop gain can cause runaway oscillation or excessive distortion.
  • Cost and manufacturability: real component tolerances shift ω₀, Q, and effective loop gain.
  • Efficiency: gain must be enough for startup but not so large that amplitude control wastes power or drives components nonlinear too early.

How this is measured in practice

  • Frequency response measurement with a network analyzer or frequency response analyzer.
  • Ring-down or step-response testing to estimate damping and infer pole locations.
  • Spectrum analysis to verify oscillation frequency and spectral purity.
  • Monte Carlo tolerance analysis in simulation to assess spread due to component variations.

Design trade-offs and constraints

Increasing Q sharpens frequency selectivity but may slow startup or heighten sensitivity to tolerances. Raising loop gain improves startup certainty but can worsen distortion once nonlinear amplitude limiting begins. Real op-amps and active devices also introduce finite bandwidth, noise, temperature drift, parasitic capacitances, and saturation effects.

From lab to product.
The ideal model uses a constant gain A and exact second-order bandpass form. In real products, engineers add amplitude control, bias stabilization, component trimming, automatic gain control, or digital calibration to keep the oscillator frequency and amplitude within specification.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

Problem restated clearly

We are given a positive-feedback oscillator loop made of:

  1. a frequency-independent amplifier with gain A > 0, and
  2. a second-order bandpass filter with pole frequency ω₀, pole quality factor Q, and center-frequency gain K > 0.

First, we must find the oscillation frequency and the condition on A and K for sustained oscillation. Then, using that same oscillator, we must show that the poles lie at a constant radial distance ω₀ from the origin in the complex plane, independent of A and K, and determine the values of AK for two specific pole locations.

Given

  • A: frequency-independent amplifier gain
  • H(s): second-order bandpass filter
  • ω₀: pole frequency
  • Q: pole quality factor
  • K: positive center-frequency gain

Unknowns

  • Oscillation frequency ωosc
  • Sustained oscillation condition on A and K
  • Closed-loop pole locations
  • Value of AK for each pole-location requirement

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Barkhausen criterion: directly applicable because the problem asks for oscillation frequency and sustained-oscillation condition in a linear feedback loop.
  • Closed-loop characteristic equation: needed because Problem 18.3 explicitly asks about poles and their locations in the s-plane.
  • Second-order pole geometry: useful because the characteristic polynomial is quadratic and can be fully interpreted geometrically.

Methods from wave physics, PDEs, or spatial field theory do not apply because the system has no distributed spatial coordinate; this is a lumped, linear, time-invariant dynamical system described by an ODE and its Laplace transform.

Assumptions

  • Ideal positive feedback with loop equation 1 - A H(s) = 0.
  • Amplifier gain A is frequency independent.
  • Bandpass transfer function is in standard second-order form.
  • Small-signal linear analysis is valid for finding the threshold of oscillation.
  • Nonlinear amplitude stabilization is ignored because the problem only asks for the linear oscillation condition and pole placement.

Possible approaches

Approach How it works Pros Cons
Frequency-domain Barkhausen Evaluate H(jω), apply phase and magnitude conditions Fast and physically intuitive for oscillation frequency Less direct for full pole geometry unless followed by characteristic equation
Closed-loop polynomial Set 1 - A H(s)=0, derive quadratic, inspect roots Best for Problem 18.3 and stability interpretation A little more algebra
Time-domain ODE eigenvalue method Write homogeneous ODE and solve for exponential modes e^{st} Deep physical meaning Equivalent to Laplace method, but less compact

Chosen approach

The best route is a combined method: first use Barkhausen to identify the oscillation frequency quickly, then derive the closed-loop characteristic equation to prove the pole-circle result and answer the numerical conditions on AK. This gives both physical intuition and rigorous mathematical control.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

  1. Write the standard bandpass transfer function.
    Tool: standard second-order form.
    Physical meaning: identifies resonance and phase behavior.
  2. Substitute s=jω.
    Tool: sinusoidal steady-state analysis.
    Physical meaning: finds loop phase and gain versus frequency.
  3. Check the special frequency ω=ω₀.
    Tool: direct evaluation of H(jω).
    Physical meaning: resonance often gives the correct oscillation frequency.
  4. Apply Barkhausen.
    Tool: |AH(jω)|=1, phase zero.
    Physical meaning: loop exactly compensates losses without phase mismatch.
  5. Form the closed-loop characteristic equation.
    Tool: 1-AH(s)=0.
    Physical meaning: poles determine decay, growth, or sustained oscillation.
  6. Solve the quadratic for the poles.
    Tool: quadratic formula.
    Physical meaning: real part gives growth/decay, imaginary part gives oscillation.
  7. Compute the pole radius.
    Tool: product/sum of roots or direct magnitude calculation.
    Physical meaning: shows invariant geometry in the s-plane.
  8. Impose the two requested pole-location conditions.
    Tool: match the real part to the specified horizontal distance.
    Physical meaning: connects gain margin with stability location.
Common mistakes
  • Using the wrong bandpass numerator. The standard numerator must be proportional to s.
  • Forgetting that the center-frequency gain is H(jω₀)=K, not 1.
  • Confusing “on the jω axis” with “at the origin.” On the jω axis the real part is zero, but the imaginary part need not be zero.
  • Missing the sign in the real part of the poles: the sign tells whether the system is stable or unstable.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Qualitative expectation before calculation

A second-order bandpass stage has a preferred frequency near its center frequency. Since the amplifier is frequency independent, the loop phase and magnitude are mainly shaped by the bandpass filter. Therefore, we expect oscillation to occur at the filter’s center frequency ω₀, and the gain condition should compare the amplifier gain A with the filter’s center-frequency gain K.

Step 1 — Write the bandpass transfer function

The standard second-order bandpass transfer function with pole frequency ω₀, pole quality factor Q, and center-frequency gain K is

H(s) = K ((ω₀/Q)s)/(s² + (ω₀/Q)s + ω₀²)

Why this form is valid: for a second-order bandpass, the numerator must be first order in s so that low frequencies and high frequencies are both attenuated, while a midband region is passed. The coefficient is chosen so that the gain at ω=ω₀ becomes exactly K.

Step 2 — Evaluate the filter at sinusoidal steady state

Set s=jω. Then

H(jω) = K ((ω₀/Q)jω)/((jω)² + (ω₀/Q)jω + ω₀²)

Since (jω)^2=-ω^2, the denominator becomes

H(jω) = K ((ω₀/Q)jω)/((ω₀² - ω²) + j(ω₀ω/Q))

Step 3 — Evaluate at ω = ω₀

At the center frequency, ω₀² - ω² = 0, so

H(jω₀) = K ((ω₀/Q)jω₀)/(jω₀²/Q) = K

Therefore, at ω=ω₀, the bandpass filter has:

  • magnitude |H(jω₀)|=K,
  • phase ∠H(jω₀)=0.

This is exactly what we need in a positive-feedback oscillator: the filter contributes no net phase shift at that frequency.

Step 4 — Apply the oscillation criterion (Problem 18.1)

The loop transfer function is

L(s) = A H(s)

At ω=ω₀,

L(jω₀) = A K

Since A>0 and K>0, this is real and positive, so the phase condition is already satisfied:

∠L(jω₀) = 0

The magnitude condition for sustained oscillation is therefore

|L(jω₀)| = AK = 1
Result for Problem 18.1
Oscillation frequency: ωosc = ω₀ Sustained oscillation condition: AK = 1

Step 5 — Derive the closed-loop characteristic equation (Problem 18.3)

For positive feedback, the closed-loop poles satisfy

1 - A H(s) = 0

Substitute the expression for H(s):

1 - A K ((ω₀/Q)s)/(s² + (ω₀/Q)s + ω₀²) = 0

Multiply through by the denominator:

s² + (ω₀/Q)s + ω₀² - AK(ω₀/Q)s = 0

Combine the linear terms in s:

s² + (ω₀/Q)(1 - AK)s + ω₀² = 0

This is the closed-loop characteristic polynomial.

Step 6 — Solve for the poles

Apply the quadratic formula to s² + bs + c = 0 with b=(ω₀/Q)(1-AK) and c=ω₀².

s = [-(ω₀/Q)(1-AK) ± √((ω₀/Q)²(1-AK)² - 4ω₀²)]/2

Rearranging slightly,

s = -ω₀(1-AK)/(2Q) ± jω₀ √(1 - (1-AK)²/(4Q²))

whenever the roots are complex. Even if the roots become purely real in some parameter range, the constant-radius property below still follows from the polynomial coefficients.

Step 7 — Show the poles lie at radial distance ω₀

Let s = σ ± jω_d where

σ = -ω₀(1-AK)/(2Q), ω_d = ω₀ √(1 - (1-AK)²/(4Q²))

The radial distance from the origin is

|s| = √(σ² + ω_d²)

Substitute the expressions:

|s|² = [ω₀²(1-AK)²/(4Q²)] + ω₀²[1 - (1-AK)²/(4Q²)] = ω₀²

Hence

|s| = ω₀

So the poles always lie on a circle of radius ω₀ centered at the origin of the s-plane, independent of A and K.

There is also a very short proof using the product of roots. For s² + bs + ω₀²=0, the root product is s₁ s₂ = ω₀². For complex-conjugate roots, s₁ s₂ = |s|², so immediately |s|=ω₀.

Step 8 — Case (a): poles on the jω axis

Poles on the imaginary axis have zero real part:

Re(s) = -ω₀(1-AK)/(2Q) = 0

Since ω₀>0 and Q>0, we must have

1 - AK = 0 ⇒ AK = 1

Step 9 — Case (b): poles in the right half-plane at horizontal distance ω₀/(2Q)

A horizontal distance from the jω axis means the magnitude of the real part. Since the poles are in the right half-plane, the real part is positive:

Re(s) = +ω₀/(2Q)

But

-ω₀(1-AK)/(2Q) = +ω₀/(2Q)

Multiply both sides by 2Q/ω₀:

-(1-AK) = 1

Therefore

AK = 2
Final answer for Problems 18.1 and 18.3
Problem 18.1: Oscillation frequency: ωosc = ω₀ Sustained oscillation condition: AK = 1 Problem 18.3: Closed-loop characteristic equation: s² + (ω₀/Q)(1 − AK)s + ω₀² = 0 Closed-loop poles: s = −ω₀(1−AK)/(2Q) ± jω₀√(1 − (1−AK)²/(4Q²)) Therefore |s| = ω₀, independent of A and K. (a) Poles on the jω axis: AK = 1 (b) Poles in the right half-plane at horizontal distance ω₀/(2Q): AK = 2

Sanity checks

  • Dimensions: each term in the characteristic equation has units of s−2. For example, (ω₀/Q)s has units (s−1)(s−1)=s−2, and ω₀² also has units s−2.
  • Threshold behavior: when AK=1, the real part of the poles is zero, so the oscillation is neither decaying nor growing.
  • Stable side: if AK<1, then 1-AK>0, so Re(s)<0, which means decay.
  • Unstable side: if AK>1, then Re(s)>0, so the amplitude grows in the linear model.
  • Physical interpretation: the loop-gain product AK acts like a damping-control knob. It does not change the pole radius, but it shifts the poles horizontally across the complex plane.
Optional deeper note: limiting cases and asymptotics

Near threshold, let AK = 1 + ε with |ε| \ll 1. Then

Re(s) = ω₀ ε/(2Q)

to first order in ε. This means the growth or decay rate changes linearly with the gain error away from threshold. The imaginary part is

Im(s) ≈ ±ω₀ (1 - ε²/(8Q²))

so the oscillation frequency changes only at second order near threshold. That is a useful engineering insight: the gain error mainly affects growth/decay rate before it significantly perturbs frequency.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

Re-interpreting the final formula

The central formula is

s = -ω₀(1-AK)/(2Q) ± jω₀ √(1 - (1-AK)²/(4Q²))

Each part has a clean interpretation:

  • ω₀ sets the basic scale of the dynamics and the pole-circle radius.
  • Q controls sensitivity of the real-part shift to the gain error 1-AK.
  • AK determines whether the system is under-compensated (AK<1), exactly compensated (AK=1), or over-compensated (AK>1).

Parameter dependence

Parameter What changes when it increases? Physical effect
A Raises loop gain linearly through AK Moves poles rightward; promotes oscillation growth
K Also raises loop gain linearly Same effect as increasing A
Q Reduces real-part sensitivity since Re(s)\propto 1/Q For fixed gain error, growth/decay rate is smaller
ω₀ Scales both real and imaginary parts Faster time scale and higher oscillation frequency

Alternative derivation idea

Instead of starting from Barkhausen, one can directly write the homogeneous closed-loop ODE by substituting x(t)=A y(t) into the bandpass differential equation:

d²y/dt² + (ω₀/Q)(1-AK) dy/dt + ω₀² y = 0

Then seek modal solutions of the form y(t)=e^{st}. Substitution gives the same quadratic characteristic equation. This is mathematically equivalent to the Laplace-domain method and highlights that the feedback changes the effective damping term while leaving the constant term ω₀² unchanged.

Concept checks

  • Q: Why is ωosc not changed by A in this ideal model?
    A: Because the phase condition is met at the bandpass center frequency, and the amplifier is frequency independent, so it does not shift the preferred phase-zero point.
  • Q: What does AK>1 mean physically?
    A: The loop injects more energy per cycle than it loses, so the linear model predicts exponentially growing amplitude.
  • Q: Why do the poles keep the same radius ω₀?
    A: Because the constant term of the characteristic equation remains ω₀², fixing the product of the roots.
  • Q: What would a real oscillator do when AK>1?
    A: It would not grow without bound forever; nonlinear effects reduce effective gain until the average loop gain settles near unity.

PART 5 — Optional Plot Guide

A plot is not necessary for this problem type because the result is fundamentally symbolic and geometric in the complex s-plane. The key learning point is the exact pole-circle relation |s|=ω₀, which is conveyed more clearly by algebra than by a numerical plot using arbitrary example values.
