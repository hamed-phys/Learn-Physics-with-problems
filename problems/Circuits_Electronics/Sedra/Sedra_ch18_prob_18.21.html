Quadrature Oscillator Without Limiter: Pole Location and Right-Half-Plane Growth

We analyze the linearized quadrature-oscillator circuit of Fig. 18.10 with the limiter removed and with Rf = 2R/(1+Δ), where Δ << 1. The goal is to prove that the characteristic poles lie in the right-half complex plane and are approximately s ≃ (1/CR)[(Δ/4) ± j].

Quick Summary

  • This problem is about a quadrature oscillator, a two-state linear circuit that ideally produces sinusoidal outputs 90° out of phase.
  • The key physics idea is that two coupled first-order dynamical elements can form a harmonic-oscillator-like system in state space.
  • The governing equations are two linear ODEs: one from the inverting integrator and one from the op-amp-2 input-node KCL.
  • The small parameter Δ slightly changes the effective feedback resistance, making the real part of the poles positive.
  • The characteristic equation gives complex-conjugate poles, so the circuit still oscillates near angular frequency ω0 ≈ 1/(RC).
  • The result is symbolic, not numeric: s ≃ (1/CR)[(Δ/4) ± j].
  • Because Re(s) = Δ/(4CR) > 0, the poles lie in the right-half plane; hence the oscillation amplitude grows exponentially until a nonlinearity would limit it.
  • Physically, removing the limiter means there is no amplitude stabilization; a tiny excess loop gain causes start-up growth.

Key Governing Pair

d vo1/dt = -(1/RC) vo2
d vo2/dt = (1/RC) vo1 + (Δ/2RC) vo2

Final Pole Location

s ≃ (1/CR)[(Δ/4) ± j]

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions

Symbol Meaning SI Unit
R, Rf Resistance and feedback resistance Ω
C Capacitance F
vo1, vo2 Op-amp output voltages (state variables of the linear system) V
v Input node voltage of op amp 2; from the figure v = vo2/2 V
s Complex growth/frequency variable in Laplace space s−1
Δ Small dimensionless detuning/excess-gain parameter, with Δ << 1 dimensionless

Physical meaning of the key quantities

In this oscillator, the capacitor does not just “store charge”; it creates a time derivative relation between current and voltage, iC = C dv/dt. Because the two op-amp stages couple the state variables vo1 and vo2, the circuit becomes a linear dynamical system. The imaginary part of a pole corresponds to oscillation frequency; the real part corresponds to amplitude growth or decay.

Key laws and validity conditions

Ideal op-amp assumptions

Infinite open-loop gain, infinite input resistance, zero output resistance, and operation within the linear region for the purpose of small-signal analysis. With the limiter removed, we analyze only the linear differential equations.

KCL and constitutive laws

Kirchhoff’s current law applies at the input node of op amp 2. Capacitor current follows i = C dv/dt. Resistor current follows i = v/R.

Small-parameter approximation

Since Δ << 1, we may neglect terms of order Δ² when extracting the poles. This is a first-order asymptotic expansion.

Common models/approximations used in this topic

  • State-space model: write the circuit as dX/dt = AX, where X = [vo1, vo2]T.
  • Eigenvalue model: assume X(t) = X0est; then poles are eigenvalues of A.
  • Linearized start-up analysis: before clipping or limiting occurs, oscillation amplitude is governed by pole location.

Mini intuition examples

Example 1: Pure integrator

If dv/dt = -k x, the capacitor introduces a 90° phase shift in sinusoidal steady behavior. This is why integrator chains are natural oscillator building blocks.

Example 2: Coupled states

A pair like ẋ = -ωy, ẏ = ωx gives circular motion in state space and purely imaginary poles ±jω.

Example 3: Small positive damping reversed

Replacing damping by anti-damping moves poles from the left-half plane to the right-half plane, causing amplitude growth instead of decay.

What to watch for

  • Do not forget that in the figure v = vo2/2, not v = vo2.
  • The branch labeled −Rf behaves like a negative resistance, so its current sign is opposite to an ordinary resistor branch.
  • Keep the time constant dimensions straight: RC has units of seconds, so 1/(RC) has units of s−1.
  • For Δ << 1, retain only first-order terms in Δ unless a higher-order correction is explicitly needed.

Math Insight

The core equation is a linear autonomous ODE system, dX/dt = AX. Mathematically, this is an eigenvalue problem for the linear operator L = d/dt − A. Oscillation occurs because the eigenvalues are complex; growth occurs because their real part is positive. This is the circuit analogue of a weakly anti-damped harmonic oscillator.

Optional deeper note: operator viewpoint

If we eliminate one variable, the system reduces to a second-order scalar ODE: d²v/dt² − (Δ/2RC) dv/dt + (1/R²C²)v = 0. This has the same algebraic structure as a harmonic oscillator with negative damping. The characteristic polynomial is the dispersion relation for temporal modes.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry

  • Signal generation and instrumentation: quadrature oscillators are used to generate sine/cosine reference signals for test equipment, lock-in amplifiers, and sensor excitation.
  • Communications and modulation: I/Q (in-phase and quadrature) signal paths rely on precise 90° phase relationships in mixers, modulators, and demodulators.
  • Motor control and mechatronics: quadrature signals appear in resolver interfaces, encoder processing, and control references for rotating systems.
  • Measurement electronics: phase-sensitive detection, impedance spectroscopy, and precision analog front ends often use oscillator cores derived from RC state-space designs.

Why engineers care

In real products, oscillators must satisfy competing goals: reliable start-up, low distortion, stable frequency, temperature robustness, and manufacturability at low cost. A small change in loop gain can decide whether an oscillator fails to start, starts cleanly, or saturates violently. The present problem directly models that start-up sensitivity.

Measurement and validation in practice

  • Measure start-up rate from the envelope eσt, where σ = Re(s).
  • Measure oscillation frequency using spectrum analyzers or zero-crossing timing.
  • Validate component spread with Monte Carlo or tolerance analysis in SPICE.
  • Check waveform purity and limiting action on an oscilloscope under temperature and supply variation.

Design trade-offs and non-idealities

Start-up margin vs distortion

A larger positive real part helps start-up but causes stronger amplitude growth, which then demands more aggressive limiting and can increase distortion.

Tolerances vs frequency accuracy

Since ω0 ≈ 1/(RC), resistor and capacitor tolerances directly perturb frequency. Matching often matters more than absolute values.

Ideal model vs op-amp bandwidth

Real op-amps have finite gain-bandwidth, slew rate, noise, and output swing limits, all of which perturb the ideal pole locations.

Negative resistance implementation

The effective negative resistance is a synthesized behavior, not a passive component. Real implementations are sensitive to biasing and device nonlinearities.

From lab to product

The idealized linear model is used first to place the poles and guarantee start-up. Then engineers add amplitude stabilization (diode limiter, AGC loop, lamp, JFET, or digital control), include op-amp finite-bandwidth models, and perform tolerance plus thermal analysis. So the textbook model is not the whole design—but it is the essential first step.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restated in plain language

We are given a quadrature oscillator with its limiter removed. The feedback resistance in the second stage is adjusted to Rf = 2R/(1+Δ), where Δ is a small positive number. We must derive the characteristic poles of the linear circuit and show that they lie in the right-half plane, approximately at s ≃ (1/CR)[(Δ/4) ± j].

Given quantities

  • R, C
  • Rf = 2R/(1+Δ)
  • Δ << 1
  • Equivalent node relation from the figure: v = vo2/2

Unknowns / what must be shown

  • Derive the linear differential equations of the circuit.
  • Obtain the characteristic equation.
  • Show the poles have positive real part.
  • Show the approximate pole formula s ≃ (1/CR)[(Δ/4) ± j].

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • KCL at the input node of op amp 2: the equivalent circuit in part (b) is expressly designed for this.
  • Ideal integrator relation for op amp 1: the first stage is an inverting integrator, so it provides a first-order differential relation.
  • Laplace/eigenvalue analysis: because the circuit is linear and time invariant in this approximation, exponential trial solutions are valid.

Principles that are not needed here: Maxwell PDE field analysis, distributed transmission-line theory, and nonlinear diode equations, because the problem explicitly asks for the circuit without the limiter and in a lumped linear regime.

Assumptions

  • Ideal op amps.
  • Limiter is absent, so no amplitude clipping or nonlinear conduction enters the equations.
  • Small-signal linear analysis about the quiescent regime.
  • Δ is small enough that first-order asymptotics are justified.

Possible approaches

Approach Idea Pros Cons
State-space Write two ODEs for vo1 and vo2, then find eigenvalues. Clean, systematic, physically transparent. Requires careful sign handling.
Direct elimination Eliminate one state and derive one second-order ODE. Fast once equations are known. Can hide physical meaning of each branch.
Loop-gain/Barkhausen style Infer oscillation and start-up from loop phase and magnitude. Good intuition. Less direct for exact pole extraction.

Best choice: the state-space / KCL approach, because the figure already supplies the equivalent node circuit, and the required result is explicitly a pole formula.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Identify the first stage relation: use the ideal inverting-integrator law to relate dvo1/dt to vo2.
  2. Write KCL at node v: use the equivalent circuit for op amp 2 input.
  3. Use the op amp 2 gain relation: replace v by vo2/2.
  4. Insert Rf = 2R/(1+Δ): simplify the coefficient of vo2.
  5. Build the 2×2 linear system: write dX/dt = AX.
  6. Find the characteristic equation: compute det(sI − A) = 0.
  7. Apply the small-Δ approximation: keep only first-order terms in Δ.
  8. Interpret the poles physically: positive real part means start-up growth; imaginary part gives oscillation frequency.

Common mistakes

  • Using v = vo2 instead of v = vo2/2.
  • Treating the −Rf branch like an ordinary resistor without reversing the sign.
  • Forgetting that Rf = 2R/(1+Δ) implies 1/Rf = (1+Δ)/(2R).
  • Dropping the factor of 1/2 from the capacitor term when converting v to vo2/2.
  • Expanding the square root incorrectly; here first order in Δ means no correction to the imaginary part at order Δ.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation before calculating

If the second stage were adjusted exactly to the marginal oscillation value, we would expect poles at approximately ±j/(RC), meaning steady oscillation with constant amplitude in the idealized model. Here Rf is slightly altered through the small parameter Δ, so we expect the poles to move slightly off the imaginary axis. Since the target answer has a positive real part, the circuit is expected to be weakly unstable, which is exactly what enables oscillator start-up.

Step 1: Equation for op amp 1 (inverting integrator)

The first op amp is an ideal inverting integrator with input through R and capacitive feedback C. Its input signal is the returned node labeled X, which is connected to vo2. For an ideal inverting integrator,

v_o1(t) = -(1/RC) ∫ v_o2(t) dt therefore d v_o1 / dt = -(1/RC) v_o2 .

This equation is valid because the capacitor current in the feedback path satisfies iC = C d(v- − vo1)/dt, and the inverting input is a virtual ground in the ideal op-amp limit.

d vo1/dt = -(1/RC) vo2

Step 2: KCL at the input node of op amp 2

From the equivalent circuit shown in the figure for op amp 2, the node voltage is v. Into this node enters a current source of value vo1/(2R). The node is connected to ground through:

  • a resistor 2R, carrying current v/(2R),
  • a capacitor C, carrying current C dv/dt,
  • an effective negative resistance −Rf, carrying current v/(−Rf) = −v/Rf.

Using KCL in the form “current injected into the node = sum of branch currents leaving according to their constitutive forms,” we get

v_o1/(2R) = v/(2R) + C (dv/dt) - v/R_f .

The negative sign in the last term is the critical signature of the negative-resistance branch.

Step 3: Use the op-amp-2 gain relation v = vo2/2

From the figure, the noninverting op amp 2 has closed-loop gain 2, so

v_o2 = 2v ⇔ v = v_o2/2 .

Substitute this into the KCL equation:

v_o1/(2R) = (v_o2/2)/(2R) + C d(v_o2/2)/dt - (v_o2/2)/R_f v_o1/(2R) = v_o2/(4R) + (C/2) d v_o2/dt - v_o2/(2R_f).

Multiply the entire equation by 2:

v_o1/R = v_o2/(2R) + C d v_o2/dt - v_o2/R_f .

Now solve for d vo2/dt:

C d v_o2/dt = v_o1/R - v_o2/(2R) + v_o2/R_f d v_o2/dt = (1/RC) v_o1 + (1/C)(1/R_f - 1/(2R)) v_o2 .

Step 4: Insert Rf = 2R/(1+Δ)

Since

R_f = 2R/(1+Δ) ⇒ 1/R_f = (1+Δ)/(2R),

the coefficient of vo2 becomes

1/R_f - 1/(2R) = (1+Δ)/(2R) - 1/(2R) = Δ/(2R).

Therefore, the second state equation is

d v_o2/dt = (1/RC) v_o1 + (Δ/(2RC)) v_o2 .
d vo2/dt = (1/RC) vo1 + (Δ/2RC) vo2

Step 5: Write the state-space system

Define the state vector X(t) = [vo1(t), vo2(t)]T. Then the two equations become

dX/dt = A X, A = (1/RC) [ [ 0 , -1 ], [ 1 , Δ/2 ] ] .

This is a linear autonomous ODE system on the state space R². Its temporal modes are exponential: X(t) = X_0 e^{st}. Substituting this ansatz is valid because the system is linear with constant coefficients.

Step 6: Characteristic equation

The poles are the eigenvalues of A, found from

det(sI - A) = 0.

Compute the determinant carefully:

sI - A = [ [ s, 0 ], [ 0, s ] ] - (1/RC)[ [0,-1],[1,Δ/2] ] = [ [ s, 1/(RC) ], [ -1/(RC), s - Δ/(2RC) ] ] .
det(sI - A) = s ( s - Δ/(2RC) ) - (1/(RC))( -1/(RC) ) = s^2 - (Δ/(2RC)) s + 1/(R^2 C^2) .

Hence the characteristic equation is

s^2 - (Δ/(2RC)) s + 1/(R^2 C^2) = 0 .
s² − (Δ/2RC)s + 1/(R²C²) = 0

Step 7: Solve the quadratic exactly

Using the quadratic formula,

s = [ Δ/(2RC) ± sqrt( (Δ/(2RC))^2 - 4/(R^2 C^2) ) ] / 2 .

Rewrite the square root by factoring out 1/(RC):

s = Δ/(4RC) ± (1/2) sqrt( Δ^2/(4R^2 C^2) - 4/(R^2 C^2) ) = Δ/(4RC) ± (1/(RC)) (1/2) sqrt( Δ^2/4 - 4 ) = Δ/(4RC) ± (1/(RC)) sqrt( Δ^2/16 - 1 ).

Since Δ << 1, the quantity inside the square root is negative, so

sqrt( Δ^2/16 - 1 ) = j sqrt( 1 - Δ^2/16 ) .

Thus the exact pole form is

s = Δ/(4RC) ± j (1/RC) sqrt(1 - Δ^2/16) .

Step 8: Small-Δ asymptotic approximation

Because Δ << 1, we use the expansion

sqrt(1 - ε) = 1 - ε/2 + O(ε^2), where ε = Δ^2/16 .

Notice that ε is already of order Δ², so to first order in Δ we simply take sqrt(1 - Δ²/16) ≈ 1.

Therefore, s ≃ Δ/(4RC) ± j/(RC) = (1/CR)[ (Δ/4) ± j ] .

Final Answer

s ≃ (1/CR)[(Δ/4) ± j]

Since Δ > 0, the real part is Re(s) = Δ/(4CR) > 0, so the poles lie in the right-half plane.

Sanity checks

  • Dimensions: 1/(RC) has units of s−1, so the pole location has the correct unit.
  • Limiting case Δ → 0: s → ±j/(RC), which corresponds to an ideal marginal oscillator.
  • Sign reasoning: if Δ > 0, then Rf is slightly smaller than 2R, strengthening the effective negative resistance and pushing the poles rightward.
  • Physical interpretation: the waveform oscillates at approximately ω ≈ 1/(RC), but its envelope grows like eΔt/(4RC) until nonlinear effects would intervene.
Optional deeper note: direct second-order ODE derivation

Differentiate the first equation: d²vo1/dt² = -(1/RC) dvo2/dt. Substitute the second equation: d²vo1/dt² = -(1/R²C²) vo1 - (Δ/2RC) (1/RC) vo2. Then use vo2 = -RC dvo1/dt from the first equation to get d²vo1/dt² − (Δ/2RC) dvo1/dt + (1/R²C²) vo1 = 0. The same characteristic equation follows immediately.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the final formula

The pole pair s ≃ (1/CR)[(Δ/4) ± j] separates naturally into:

  • Real part: Δ/(4CR), which controls amplitude growth rate.
  • Imaginary part: ±1/(CR), which controls oscillation frequency.

So Δ does not significantly shift the frequency at first order; instead, it primarily controls start-up growth. The frequency correction appears only at order Δ² through sqrt(1 − Δ²/16).

Parameter dependence

Parameter Effect on pole location Physical meaning
R Larger R makes both real and imaginary parts smaller in magnitude. Slower dynamics; lower oscillation frequency and slower envelope growth.
C Larger C also scales both parts down by 1/C. Again slows the circuit time scale.
Δ Changes the real part linearly, with only second-order effect on the imaginary part. Acts like a weak anti-damping / excess loop-gain control.

Nondimensionalization insight

Let the dimensionless time be τ = t/(RC). Then d/dt = (1/RC) d/dτ, and the system becomes

d/dτ [ v_o1 ] = - v_o2 d/dτ [ v_o2 ] = v_o1 + (Δ/2) v_o2 .

This shows clearly that RC sets the overall time scale, while Δ is the sole small dimensionless control parameter. The dimensionless poles are λ ≃ (Δ/4) ± j, and the dimensional poles are s = λ/(RC).

Alternative derivation idea

Another meaningful derivation is a loop-transfer analysis: one stage contributes an integration and phase shift, while the second stage plus RC network supplies the complementary phase and gain. At the nominal value Rf = 2R, the system is marginal. Replacing that by 2R/(1+Δ) slightly increases effective loop gain, moving the poles rightward by a small positive amount. This gives the same start-up conclusion, though not as directly as the state-space method.

Concept checks

  • Q: Why are the poles complex? A: Because the two coupled first-order states form an oscillator-like second-order system with restoring coupling.
  • Q: Why is the real part positive? A: The effective negative resistance exceeds the exact marginal value slightly, creating anti-damping.
  • Q: Does Δ change the frequency at first order? A: No; the first correction to the imaginary part is of order Δ².
  • Q: What happens physically without the limiter? A: The amplitude grows exponentially until the op amp leaves the linear regime or saturates.
