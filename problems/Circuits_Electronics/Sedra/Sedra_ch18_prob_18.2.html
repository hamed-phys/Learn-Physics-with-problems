Second-Order Bandpass Oscillator: Frequency of Oscillation, Sustained Condition, and Phase-Sensitivity

This article solves Problems 18.1 and 18.2 in a teaching-first way. We use the Barkhausen criterion, a standard second-order bandpass transfer function, and a small-signal phase perturbation analysis to determine the oscillation frequency, the gain condition for sustained oscillation, and the sensitivity of the oscillation frequency to an amplifier phase shift.

Quick Summary

  • The problem is about an oscillator built from a frequency-independent amplifier of gain A > 0 and a second-order bandpass filter having pole frequency ω0, pole quality factor Q, and center-frequency gain K > 0.
  • The key physics/electronics idea is the Barkhausen criterion: sustained oscillation requires total loop phase equal to an integer multiple of 2π and loop-magnitude equal to 1.
  • A convenient bandpass model is H(jω) = K / [1 + jQ(ω/ω0 - ω0/ω)], which has gain K and zero phase at ω = ω0.
  • Therefore the oscillation frequency is ωosc = ω0.
  • The sustained-oscillation condition is AK = 1.
  • The bandpass phase is φ(ω) = -tan^-1{Q(ω/ω0 - ω0/ω)}, so dφ/dω |ω=ω0 = -2Q/ω0.
  • If the amplifier introduces a small extra phase shift Δφ (in radians), the new oscillation frequency shifts by Δω/ω0 = Δφ/(2Q).
  • The result is symbolic and physically means that higher-Q oscillators are less frequency-sensitive to amplifier phase errors.

PART 0 — Concept Primer

Core definitions

Oscillator: a feedback system that produces a periodic output without an external periodic input once start-up noise or an initial disturbance is present.

Loop gain: product of gains around the feedback loop. Here it is the amplifier gain times the bandpass transfer function.

Bandpass filter: a system that passes a band of frequencies around a center frequency and suppresses frequencies far below and far above that center.

Quality factor Q: a dimensionless measure of frequency selectivity. Larger Q means a narrower passband and steeper phase slope near resonance.

Symbol Meaning SI Unit Physical interpretation
ω Angular frequency rad/s Independent frequency variable
ω0 Pole or center angular frequency rad/s Natural frequency at which the bandpass phase is zero
Q Quality factor dimensionless Controls selectivity and phase slope near resonance
K Center-frequency gain of filter dimensionless Magnitude of filter response at ω = ω0
A Frequency-independent amplifier gain dimensionless Constant real positive gain
φ Phase of filter transfer function rad Angle contribution to loop-phase condition

Mathematical model

System type: linear time-invariant lumped-parameter feedback system analyzed in the frequency domain.

Variables and domain:

  • Frequency variable: ω ∈ (0, ∞).
  • Loop transfer function: L(jω) = A H(jω).
  • The filter is a second-order bandpass section with a single resonance scale ω0 and damping encoded by Q.

Standard transfer-function form:

H(s) = K [(ω0/Q)s] / [s^2 + (ω0/Q)s + ω0^2]

Setting s = jω gives the steady-state sinusoidal response. This is an ODE-derived rational transfer function. The underlying time-domain model is a second-order linear constant-coefficient ODE, but since the problem asks about sinusoidal oscillation conditions, the frequency-domain form is the natural tool.

Why this model is valid

  • Linearity: We assume the amplifier and filter are operating in a small-signal linear regime for the purpose of finding the oscillation condition.
  • Time invariance: Parameters do not change with time during the analysis.
  • Frequency-domain analysis: For sinusoidal steady state, replacing s by jω is valid.
  • No PDEs needed: This is a lumped circuit problem, so the governing mathematics is a transfer function from an ODE, not a field equation like ∇²u = 0 or a wave PDE.

Key laws and principles

Barkhausen criterion

For sustained oscillation at frequency ωosc:

  • |L(jωosc)| = 1
  • ∠L(jωosc) = 2mπ, for integer m

Resonant bandpass phase property

A second-order bandpass filter has zero phase shift at its center frequency, while its phase changes rapidly near resonance. This rapid phase change is why the oscillation frequency becomes sensitive to additional phase errors in the loop.

Common approximations in this topic

  • Ideal constant amplifier gain: Here A is real, positive, and frequency-independent.
  • Small phase perturbation: In Part 18.2(b), the added amplifier phase shift Δφ is assumed small enough that linearization about ω = ω0 is valid.
  • Linear steady-state criterion: The Barkhausen criterion predicts oscillation condition and frequency, though real amplitudes are ultimately limited by nonlinear saturation.

Mini intuition examples

  1. If the filter phase is exactly zero at one frequency and the amplifier adds no phase shift, that frequency is the natural candidate for oscillation.
  2. If the loop magnitude is less than one, oscillations decay; if greater than one, they grow until nonlinearities clip them.
  3. If Q is large, the phase changes rapidly with frequency, so only a small frequency shift is needed to compensate a small phase error.
Math Insight. The central object here is a rational transfer function of a second-order linear system. Near resonance it behaves like a locally linear phase map: Δφ ≈ (dφ/dω) Δω. This is a first-order Taylor expansion, and it is the key mathematical reason that phase error converts into frequency error.

What to watch for

  • Do not confuse the filter’s center-frequency gain K with the total loop gain AK.
  • Phase must be handled in radians when using derivatives such as dφ/dω.
  • The sign of dφ/dω matters. Here it is negative.
  • “Sustained oscillation” means magnitude exactly one in the linear model, not merely greater than one.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry

Communications

RF local oscillators, IF oscillators, and selective tone-generation stages depend on resonant sections and phase conditions very similar to the one in this problem.

Instrumentation

Signal generators, lock-in amplifiers, and sensor excitation sources use oscillators whose frequency stability depends strongly on loop phase and resonator Q.

Industrial electronics

Motor drives, ultrasonic transducers, switching-control references, and embedded timing subsystems all rely on stable oscillation and predictable phase behavior.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: Oscillation frequency sets channel allocation, timing accuracy, and spectral purity.
  • Reliability: Small phase shifts from temperature, aging, or parasitics can move the oscillation frequency.
  • Cost: Higher-Q resonators often improve stability but may increase part cost or process complexity.
  • Manufacturability: Component tolerances change Q, K, and phase margins.
  • Efficiency: In RF and power systems, incorrect oscillation conditions can increase loss or create unstable behavior.

Typical measurement and validation methods

  • Frequency-response measurements with a network analyzer to get |H(jω)| and φ(ω).
  • Oscilloscope and frequency counter measurements to verify startup, steady oscillation, and drift.
  • Phase-noise and spectrum measurements for high-performance oscillators.
  • Parameter extraction from Bode plots to estimate Q, center frequency, and phase slope.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Higher Q gives lower frequency sensitivity to phase errors, but can slow startup and narrow tuning range.
  • Larger amplifier gain helps startup, but too much excess gain can increase distortion and nonlinear clipping.
  • Real amplifiers are not perfectly frequency-independent; parasitic capacitances introduce phase lag and shift the oscillation frequency.
  • Process spread, temperature coefficients, and PCB layout parasitics alter the effective loop phase.
From lab to product. In real systems, the ideal criterion AK = 1 is only the equilibrium condition of a nonlinear oscillator. Designers usually choose startup gain slightly greater than one, then rely on amplitude-limiting mechanisms—device saturation, AGC loops, or nonlinear resistive elements—to settle to a stable amplitude.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

Restating the problem

We have a feedback oscillator made from two blocks in the loop: a frequency-independent amplifier of gain A > 0 and a second-order bandpass filter with pole frequency ω0, quality factor Q, and center-frequency gain K > 0. We must:

  1. Find the oscillation frequency.
  2. Find the gain condition on A and K for sustained oscillation.
  3. For the same oscillator, derive dφ/dω at ω = ω0.
  4. Use that derivative to find the fractional change in oscillation frequency caused by a small amplifier phase-angle change Δφ.

Given quantities

  • Amplifier gain: A, real, positive, frequency-independent.
  • Bandpass pole frequency: ω0.
  • Bandpass quality factor: Q.
  • Bandpass center-frequency gain: K.

Unknowns

  • Oscillation frequency ωosc.
  • Sustained-oscillation condition linking A and K.
  • Phase slope dφ/dω evaluated at ω0.
  • Fractional frequency shift Δω/ω0 resulting from small Δφ.

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Barkhausen criterion applies because we are finding steady sinusoidal self-oscillation conditions in a linear feedback loop.
  • Bandpass phase formula applies because the filter is explicitly second-order bandpass with known ω0, Q, and K.
  • First-order Taylor expansion applies in Part 18.2(b) because the phase perturbation Δφ is assumed small.

Assumptions

  • The loop is analyzed in the linear regime.
  • The amplifier phase is initially zero because A is positive and frequency-independent.
  • The filter is described by the standard second-order bandpass form.
  • The extra amplifier phase-angle change Δφ is small enough for linearization.
  • Angles are measured in radians in derivative relations.

Possible approaches

Approach Idea Pros Cons
Direct Barkhausen on canonical bandpass form Write H(jω), inspect magnitude and phase, apply loop conditions Fast, transparent, best for symbolic results Requires knowing the standard bandpass form
Start from second-order denominator and normalize Substitute s=jω, separate real and imaginary parts More foundational Longer algebra
Pole-zero geometric reasoning Use resonance symmetry and argument of numerator/denominator Good intuition Less direct for derivative calculation

Best choice: the direct Barkhausen approach using the canonical bandpass response, because it gives both the frequency condition and the phase derivative with minimal ambiguity and clear physical interpretation.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

  1. Write the filter transfer function.
    Tool: standard second-order bandpass form.
    Meaning: fixes how magnitude and phase depend on frequency.
  2. Convert to the sinusoidal response.
    Tool: set s = jω.
    Meaning: expresses what happens to a sinusoid in the loop.
  3. Put the response into magnitude-phase form.
    Tool: algebraic normalization.
    Meaning: separates the Barkhausen magnitude and phase conditions.
  4. Apply the phase condition.
    Tool: ∠L(jω) = 0 modulo 2π.
    Meaning: selects the oscillation frequency.
  5. Apply the magnitude condition.
    Tool: |L(jω)|=1.
    Meaning: gives sustained oscillation, neither growing nor decaying.
  6. Differentiate the phase formula.
    Tool: derivative of tan^-1 using the provided hint.
    Meaning: measures how sharply phase changes near resonance.
  7. Linearize around resonance.
    Tool: first-order Taylor expansion.
    Meaning: converts a small phase error into a small frequency shift.
  8. Check units and limiting behavior.
    Tool: dimensional analysis and physical reasoning.
    Meaning: ensures the formula makes physical sense.
Common mistakes.
  • Using degrees instead of radians in Δφ.
  • Forgetting that the amplifier is positive, so it contributes zero phase in the nominal problem.
  • Writing AK > 1 as the sustained condition. That is a startup condition, not the exact sustained condition in the ideal linear model.
  • Losing the minus sign in dφ/dω.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Physical intuition before calculation

A bandpass filter has maximum transmission around its center frequency and zero phase shift exactly at that center. Since the amplifier gain A is positive and frequency-independent, it contributes no phase shift. Therefore, the only frequency where the loop phase can naturally be zero is expected to be the filter center frequency ω0. The magnitude condition will then simply require the product of amplifier gain and filter gain at that frequency to be one.

Step 1: Write the standard bandpass transfer function

For a second-order bandpass filter with center-frequency gain K, pole frequency ω0, and quality factor Q, a standard form is 

H(s) = K [(ω0/Q)s] / [s^2 + (ω0/Q)s + ω0^2]

This form is chosen because at s = jω0 the magnitude becomes exactly K. Let us verify that carefully.

Step 2: Evaluate the sinusoidal response

Set s = jω:

H(jω) = K [(ω0/Q)(jω)] / [ (jω)^2 + (ω0/Q)(jω) + ω0^2 ]
      = K [ j(ω0ω/Q) ] / [ -ω^2 + j(ω0ω/Q) + ω0^2 ]

Now factor the denominator in a way that exposes the phase structure. Divide numerator and denominator by j(ω0ω/Q): 

H(jω) = K / { 1 + [(-ω^2 + ω0^2)] / [j(ω0ω/Q)] }
      = K / { 1 - jQ(ω0^2 - ω^2)/(ω0ω) }

Since

(ω0^2 - ω^2)/(ω0ω) = (ω0/ω) - (ω/ω0),

we can write

H(jω) = K / [ 1 + jQ(ω/ω0 - ω0/ω) ]

This is the most useful form for both the phase and magnitude calculations.

Step 3: Magnitude and phase of the filter

For a complex number of the form 1 + jy, its magnitude is √(1+y²) and its angle is tan^-1(y). Since the number appears in the denominator, the filter phase is the negative of that angle. 

|H(jω)| = K / √{1 + [Q(ω/ω0 - ω0/ω)]^2}

φ(ω) = -tan^-1{ Q(ω/ω0 - ω0/ω) }

Step 4: Oscillation frequency from the phase condition

The amplifier gain A is positive and frequency-independent, so its phase is zero. Thus the loop transfer function is 

L(jω) = A H(jω)

The Barkhausen phase condition requires 

∠L(jωosc) = ∠A + ∠H(jωosc) = 0 (mod 2π)

Because A > 0, we have ∠A = 0. Therefore we need φ(ωosc) = 0. From the phase formula, 

-tan^-1{ Q(ωosc/ω0 - ω0/ωosc) } = 0

The arctangent is zero only when its argument is zero: 

Q(ωosc/ω0 - ω0/ωosc) = 0

Since Q ≠ 0, 

ωosc/ω0 - ω0/ωosc = 0

Multiply by ω0ωosc:

ωosc^2 - ω0^2 = 0

Since angular frequency is positive, 

ωosc = ω0

This solves Problem 18.1 for the oscillation frequency.

Step 5: Sustained oscillation condition from the magnitude condition

The Barkhausen magnitude condition is 

|L(jωosc)| = A |H(jωosc)| = 1

At ωosc = ω0, the term (ω/ω0 - ω0/ω) becomes 1 - 1 = 0, so 

|H(jω0)| = K

Hence

A K = 1

This is the sustained-oscillation condition.

Answer to Problem 18.1.
Oscillation occurs at the filter center frequency ω0, and sustained oscillation requires the loop gain magnitude to be unity, namely AK = 1.

Step 6: Derive dφ/dω at ω = ω0

The filter phase is 

φ(ω) = -tan^-1 y(ω),
where y(ω) = Q(ω/ω0 - ω0/ω)

Using the hint, 

dφ/dω = -(1 / (1 + y^2)) (dy/dω)

Now differentiate y(ω) term by term:

y(ω) = Q(ω/ω0 - ω0/ω)

dy/dω = Q[d/dω (ω/ω0) - d/dω (ω0/ω)]
      = Q[1/ω0 - ( -ω0/ω^2 )]
      = Q(1/ω0 + ω0/ω^2)

Evaluate at ω = ω0:

y(ω0) = Q(1 - 1) = 0

(dy/dω)|ω=ω0 = Q(1/ω0 + ω0/ω0^2)
              = Q(1/ω0 + 1/ω0)
              = 2Q/ω0

Substitute into the derivative formula:

(dφ/dω)|ω=ω0 = -(1/(1+0^2))(2Q/ω0)
                 = -2Q/ω0

This solves Problem 18.2(a).

Step 7: Use the phase slope to find the fractional frequency shift

Suppose the amplifier now has a small additional phase shift Δφ. The total loop phase must still be zero for oscillation: 

φfilter(ωosc,new) + Δφ = 0

Since the original oscillation was at ω0 and φ(ω0) = 0, we linearize the filter phase about ω0: 

φfilter(ω0 + Δω) ≈ φfilter(ω0) + (dφ/dω)|ω0 Δω
                  = 0 + (dφ/dω)|ω0 Δω

Thus the phase condition becomes

(dφ/dω)|ω0 Δω + Δφ = 0

Therefore

Δω = -Δφ / [(dφ/dω)|ω0]

Substitute (dφ/dω)|ω0 = -2Q/ω0:

Δω = -Δφ / (-2Q/ω0)
   = (ω0 Δφ)/(2Q)

Finally divide by ω0:

Δω/ω0 = Δφ/(2Q)

This solves Problem 18.2(b). Since f = ω/(2π), the same fractional change applies to ordinary frequency: 

Δf/f0 = Δω/ω0 = Δφ/(2Q)

Dimensional analysis and sanity checks

Units check

dφ/dω must have units of time because phase is dimensionless and ω has units of rad/s. Our result -2Q/ω0 indeed has units of seconds because Q is dimensionless.

Limiting-case check

If Q is very large, then Δω/ω0 = Δφ/(2Q) becomes very small. That is physically correct: a high-Q resonator pins the oscillation frequency more tightly.

Optional deeper note: nondimensionalization

Define the dimensionless frequency ratio x = ω/ω0. Then the phase becomes 

φ(x) = -tan^-1[Q(x - 1/x)]

This shows that the phase shape depends only on Q, not on the absolute frequency scale. Near resonance, let x = 1 + ε with |ε| << 1. Then 

1/x ≈ 1 - ε

x - 1/x ≈ (1 + ε) - (1 - ε) = 2ε

Hence near resonance, 

φ ≈ -tan^-1(2Qε) ≈ -2Qε

Since ε = Δω/ω0, the relation φ ≈ -2Q(Δω/ω0) immediately yields Δω/ω0 = Δφ/(2Q).

Final Answer

For the second-order bandpass oscillator,

1) Oscillation frequency:
   ωosc = ω0

2) Sustained-oscillation condition:
   AK = 1

3) Filter phase:
   φ(ω) = -tan^-1{Q(ω/ω0 - ω0/ω)}

4) Phase slope at resonance:
   (dφ/dω)|ω=ω0 = -2Q/ω0

5) Small frequency shift caused by an amplifier phase change Δφ:
   Δω/ω0 = Δφ/(2Q)

Equivalently, since f = ω/(2π),
   Δf/f0 = Δφ/(2Q)

with Δφ measured in radians.

Interpretation: the oscillation frequency sits at the bandpass center frequency, and larger Q makes the oscillator less sensitive to phase errors in the amplifier.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

Re-interpreting the final formulas

ωosc = ω0

The filter center frequency is where the phase is exactly zero, so the loop can satisfy the phase criterion with a positive amplifier.

AK = 1

This is the neutral stability condition of the linear model: each round trip reproduces the same amplitude.

(dφ/dω) = -2Q/ω0 at resonance

The negative slope means increasing frequency makes the filter phase more negative near resonance.

Δω/ω0 = Δφ/(2Q)

A given phase error causes a smaller fractional frequency error when Q is larger.

Parameter dependence

  • Increasing A alone does not change the nominal oscillation frequency in the ideal model; it affects the magnitude condition.
  • Increasing K also affects only the magnitude condition at the center frequency.
  • Increasing Q makes the passband narrower, increases the magnitude of the phase slope, and reduces frequency sensitivity to phase perturbations.
  • Increasing ω0 rescales the entire system in frequency; the relative sensitivity Δω/ω0 remains set by Δφ/(2Q).

Alternative derivation idea

Instead of differentiating the exact phase formula, one can use the dimensionless resonance expansion: let x = ω/ω0 = 1 + ε with |ε| << 1. Then x - 1/x ≈ 2ε, so φ ≈ -tan^-1(2Qε) ≈ -2Qε. This directly gives φ ≈ -2Q(Δω/ω0), and therefore Δω/ω0 = Δφ/(2Q).

Concept checks

  • Q: Why does a higher-Q oscillator resist frequency drift from amplifier phase errors?
    A: Because the phase-versus-frequency slope is steeper, so only a smaller frequency shift is needed to compensate a given phase change.
  • Q: If the amplifier were negative instead of positive, would the oscillation frequency still necessarily be ω0?
    A: Not with the same phase condition. A negative amplifier contributes an extra phase of π, so the required filter phase would be different.
  • Q: Is AK > 1 the sustained condition?
    A: No. In the ideal linear model, sustained oscillation requires AK = 1. Greater than one means growth, not steady amplitude.
  • Q: Why can we use a Taylor expansion in Part 18.2(b)?
    A: Because the problem asks about a small phase-angle change Δφ, so the resulting frequency shift is small and linearization is justified.
