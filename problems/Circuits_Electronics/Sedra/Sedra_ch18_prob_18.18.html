Wien-Bridge Oscillator with Op-Amp Phase Lag

We analyze a Wien-bridge oscillator designed for 10 kHz, then determine how a non-ideal op-amp phase lag shifts the oscillation frequency, and finally redesign the bridge and amplifier gain so the oscillator returns to exactly 10 kHz.

Quick Summary

  • This problem is about the frequency-selective positive-feedback network in a Wien-bridge oscillator.
  • The key physics idea is the Barkhausen phase condition: the total loop phase must be 0° (or 360°).
  • For the ideal equal-arm Wien bridge, the oscillation frequency is f0 = 1 / (2πRC).
  • If the op-amp adds an excess lag, the bridge must provide an equal lead, so the oscillator moves off its ideal center frequency.
  • The governing object is a linear complex transfer function β(jω).
  • The result is mainly numeric, but obtained from a fully symbolic derivation.
  • With a 5.7° op-amp lag, the oscillation frequency drops to about 8.61 kHz.
  • To restore 10 kHz, the shunt resistor must be changed from 10 kΩ to about 7.50 kΩ, and the amplifier ratio must become R2/R1 ≈ 2.35.
Math Insight. The Wien bridge here is a linear time-invariant first-order network composed of two RC sections. In the frequency domain, it becomes a complex rational function β(jω). The oscillation condition is a dispersion-like phase-selection condition: only frequencies satisfying the loop phase constraint can persist.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

1) What a Wien-bridge oscillator is

A Wien-bridge oscillator is a sinusoidal oscillator that uses an amplifier plus a frequency-selective RC bridge. The positive-feedback network is chosen so that at one particular frequency the feedback has the right phase and a known attenuation. The amplifier then supplies exactly the gain needed to sustain oscillation.

2) Core definitions

Symbol Meaning Typical SI Unit
Rs Series resistor in the lead-lag network Ω
Rp Shunt resistor in the parallel branch Ω
Cs Series capacitor F
Cp Shunt capacitor F
ω Angular frequency rad/s
f Ordinary frequency, f = ω/(2π) Hz
β(jω) Feedback transfer function of the Wien network dimensionless
A(jω) Amplifier gain dimensionless

3) Physical meaning of the key quantities

Resistance R

Sets the electrical time scale together with capacitance. Larger R means slower RC charging.

Capacitance C

Stores electric energy in the field. Together with R, it determines which frequency gets the correct phase.

Phase of β

Tells whether the bridge output leads or lags the amplifier output. Oscillation needs total loop phase = 0°.

4) Governing principles and validity conditions

Barkhausen criterion (steady sinusoidal oscillation):
  • Phase condition: arg[A(jω)β(jω)] = 0° mod 360°
  • Magnitude condition: |A(jω)β(jω)| = 1

This is a small-signal steady-state criterion. It assumes a linearized amplifier around the operating point and a sinusoidal mode of interest. Real oscillators start from noise, grow when loop gain exceeds unity, and then stabilize because some nonlinearity reduces the gain.

5) Mathematical model

We work in the frequency domain, using the phasor convention ejωt. No spatial field PDE is needed here, because the circuit is treated as a lumped-element system. The relevant mathematics is an algebraic complex-frequency model obtained from Kirchhoff laws.

Z_s = R_s + 1/(jωC_s) Z_p = R_p / (1 + jωR_pC_p) β(jω) = Z_p / (Z_s + Z_p)

These formulas are valid because the network is linear, time-invariant, and made of ideal lumped components. Impedance algebra follows from Ohm’s law in phasor form and the series/parallel combination rules.

6) Standard equal-arm approximation

For the classical Wien bridge, one chooses Rs = Rp = R and Cs = Cp = C. Then the bridge has zero phase at one frequency:

ω0 = 1/(RC) f0 = 1/(2πRC) |β(ω0)| = 1/3

Therefore the amplifier must supply gain 3 at oscillation, which in a non-inverting op-amp means A = 1 + R2/R1 = 3, so ideally R2/R1 = 2.

7) Mini intuition examples

  • At frequencies much lower than 1/(RC), the series capacitor strongly impedes the signal, so the feedback is small and phase-shifted.
  • At frequencies much higher than 1/(RC), the shunt capacitor tends to short the feedback node toward ground, again reducing useful feedback.
  • Near the middle frequency, the bridge produces the required phase and a moderate attenuation, selecting the sinusoidal oscillation frequency.

8) What to watch for

Pitfalls and hidden assumptions:
  • Do not confuse amplitude condition with phase condition; both are needed.
  • A lagging op-amp phase means the bridge must provide a leading phase to compensate.
  • For the equal Wien bridge, the bridge phase is positive below f0 and negative above f0.
  • The component values shown in a generic figure may not match the new design target; here we redesign for 10 kHz.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this matters in real engineering

  • Signal generators and instrumentation: low-distortion sine-wave sources for calibration and test benches.
  • Audio and acoustic electronics: tone generation, filter testing, vibration excitation.
  • Industrial sensing and control: bridge excitation, lock-in style measurements, analog front-end reference signals.
  • Embedded mixed-signal systems: local analog clocks, excitation sources for impedance or capacitance measurement.

Why engineers care

Performance

Frequency accuracy, distortion, start-up reliability, and amplitude stability determine whether the oscillator is useful in a product.

Reliability & Manufacturability

Tolerance spread in R and C, op-amp phase margin, temperature drift, and diode nonlinearity affect unit-to-unit consistency.

Typical validation methods

  • Frequency counter or oscilloscope measurement of output frequency.
  • Network analyzer or Bode plot measurement of loop phase/gain.
  • Spectrum analyzer measurement for harmonic distortion.
  • Temperature chamber testing for drift and start-up robustness.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Higher frequency demands a faster op-amp with small phase error.
  • Tighter frequency tolerance often requires precision capacitors and resistors.
  • Amplitude stabilization must be strong enough to prevent clipping, but gentle enough to avoid waveform distortion.
  • Real op-amps introduce finite open-loop gain, finite bandwidth, noise, and phase lag.
From lab to product. In textbook form, the bridge is perfectly symmetric and the op-amp is ideal. In real products, engineers often trim one resistor, choose lower-tolerance capacitors, and slightly retune the amplifier gain to offset op-amp phase shift and component spread.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restated clearly

We must design the Wien-bridge oscillator for 10 kHz using resistors of 10 kΩ in the RC network. Then we are told that the op-amp is non-ideal: at 10 kHz it introduces an extra phase lag of 5.7°. We must determine:

  1. What frequency the oscillator will actually choose under that phase lag.
  2. How to alter the bridge so that oscillation is restored to exactly 10 kHz.
  3. What the required gain ratio R2/R1 must then be.

Given quantities

  • Target design frequency: f0 = 10 kHz
  • Nominal RC resistors: R = 10 kΩ
  • Op-amp excess phase lag at 10 kHz: 5.7°
  • Assume this op-amp phase lag is approximately constant near 10 kHz

Unknowns

  • The capacitor value C for the original 10 kHz design
  • The shifted oscillation frequency f when the bridge is unchanged
  • The new shunt resistor Rp needed to restore 10 kHz
  • The new gain-setting ratio R2/R1

Relevant principles

  • Wien-bridge transfer function because the positive feedback is frequency selective.
  • Barkhausen phase condition because phase determines the oscillation frequency.
  • Magnitude condition because changing the bridge changes attenuation, so amplifier gain must be retuned.

Assumptions

Assumptions used in the solution:
  • The RC elements are ideal lumped components.
  • The op-amp phase lag is effectively constant in the small frequency interval of interest.
  • Amplitude stabilization does not significantly alter the linearized oscillation-frequency condition.
  • We keep Cs = Cp = C and Rs = 10 kΩ fixed, changing only the shunt resistor Rp when redesigning.

Possible approaches

Approach Idea Pros Cons
Direct phasor algebra Write β(jω), extract its phase and magnitude Exact, systematic, best for redesign Requires careful algebra
Bode-plot intuition Infer that positive bridge phase occurs below f0 Fast qualitative insight Not enough for precise numerical answers
Small-angle approximation Use tan 5.7° ≈ 0.1 for easy arithmetic Quick and elegant Still needs the exact phase formula first

The best route is direct phasor algebra, followed by a small-angle numerical simplification. This gives both the shifted frequency and the redesigned resistor/gain values cleanly.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Design the ideal bridge. Use f0 = 1/(2πRC) to find C. Physically, this sets the intended center frequency.
  2. Write β(jω) for equal R and C. This gives the exact frequency-dependent phase of the Wien network.
  3. Apply the phase condition. Since the op-amp lags by 5.7°, require the bridge to lead by +5.7°.
  4. Solve for the new frequency. This tells where oscillation moves when the bridge is left unchanged.
  5. Redesign the shunt resistor. Keep ω = 2π(10 kHz), keep Rs and C fixed, but choose Rp so bridge phase becomes +5.7° at 10 kHz.
  6. Compute the new attenuation. Evaluate |β| with the new Rp.
  7. Retune amplifier gain. Use A = 1/|β| and A = 1 + R2/R1.
Common mistakes.
  • Using 10 kΩ and 16 nF directly from a generic figure would give about 1 kHz, not 10 kHz.
  • Choosing a higher frequency for a lagging op-amp is wrong here; the bridge must lead, so the oscillator shifts below 10 kHz.
  • After changing Rp, forgetting to update the gain ratio R2/R1.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition before calculation

In the ideal Wien bridge, the network phase is 0° at the intended oscillation frequency. If the amplifier itself now adds a lag of 5.7°, the bridge must compensate by providing a lead of +5.7°. For the classical equal-arm Wien bridge, that positive phase occurs at frequencies below the ideal center frequency. So before doing any algebra, we already expect the oscillator frequency to move downward from 10 kHz.

Step 1 — Original design for 10 kHz

For the classical design, choose Rs = Rp = R = 10 kΩ and Cs = Cp = C. Then

f0 = 1/(2πRC)

Solve for C:

C = 1/(2πRf0) = 1/(2π·10^4·10^4) = 1.5915×10^-9 F ≈ 1.59 nF

So the 10 kHz design uses approximately C = 1.59 nF for each capacitor.

Dimensional check. Since Ω·F = s, the quantity 1/(RC) has units s-1, and therefore f = 1/(2πRC) has units Hz. The formula is dimensionally correct.

Step 2 — Transfer function of the equal-arm Wien bridge

Let the series arm be Zs = R + 1/(jωC), and the shunt arm be Zp = R/(1 + jωRC). The feedback factor is β = Zp / (Zs + Zp).

Carrying out the algebra gives the standard result:

β(jω) = (jωRC) / (1 - ω^2R^2C^2 + j3ωRC)

To interpret the phase more transparently, define the nondimensional frequency

x = ωRC

Then

β(x) = jx / (1 - x^2 + j3x)

Because the numerator jx has phase +90°, while the denominator has phase tan-1(3x/(1 - x2)), the net phase can be simplified to

tan φβ = (1 - x^2)/(3x)

This formula is especially useful because it tells us the sign immediately: if x < 1, then 1 - x2 > 0, so φβ > 0. Thus the bridge leads below the ideal frequency.

Optional deeper note: why this simplification is valid

The phase of a quotient is the phase of the numerator minus the phase of the denominator: arg(z1/z2) = arg z1 − arg z2. Since arg(jx) = 90° for x > 0, we obtain φβ = 90° − tan-1(3x/(1 − x²)). Using the complementary-angle identity tan(90° − θ) = 1/tan θ, we get tan φβ = (1 − x²)/(3x).

Step 3 — Actual oscillation frequency when the op-amp lags by 5.7°

The loop phase must be zero:

φA + φβ = 0

The op-amp contributes φA = −5.7°, so the bridge must contribute φβ = +5.7°. Therefore:

tan 5.7° = (1 - x^2)/(3x)

Now use the convenient small-angle numerical fact:

tan 5.7° ≈ 0.0997 ≈ 0.10

So

1 - x^2 = 3x(0.0997) ≈ 0.299x

Rearranging:

x^2 + 0.299x - 1 = 0

Solving the quadratic,

x = [-0.299 + sqrt(0.299^2 + 4)]/2 ≈ 0.861

Since x = ωRC and the ideal 10 kHz point corresponds to x = 1, the new frequency is

f = x f0 = 0.861 × 10 kHz ≈ 8.61 kHz

So the oscillator will run at approximately 8.61 kHz.

Sanity check. The result is below 10 kHz, exactly as expected from the sign of the required bridge phase lead.

Step 4 — Restore oscillation to 10 kHz by changing only the shunt resistor

Now keep the frequency fixed at 10 kHz and keep Rs = 10 kΩ, Cs = Cp = C = 1.59 nF. We change only the shunt resistor Rp.

Let

R_p = aR, with R = 10 kΩ

At 10 kHz we still have ωRC = 1 for the series branch because R = 10 kΩ and C was chosen for 10 kHz. Evaluating the bridge at that frequency gives

β = a / [(2a + 1) + j(a - 1)]

Since the numerator is real and positive, the phase of β is the negative of the denominator phase:

tan φβ = (1 - a)/(2a + 1)

To cancel the op-amp lag, require φβ = +5.7°. Hence

tan 5.7° = (1 - a)/(2a + 1)

Let t = tan 5.7° = 0.0997. Then

1 - a = t(2a + 1)

Expand and solve:

1 - t = a(1 + 2t) so a = (1 - t)/(1 + 2t)

Numerically,

a = (1 - 0.0997)/(1 + 2×0.0997) ≈ 0.750

Therefore

R_p = aR ≈ 0.750 × 10 kΩ ≈ 7.50 kΩ

So the shunt resistor should be changed from 10 kΩ to about 7.50 kΩ.

Step 5 — Find the new required amplifier gain and R2/R1

At the corrected 10 kHz operating point, the bridge magnitude is

|β| = a / sqrt((2a + 1)^2 + (a - 1)^2)

Substitute a = 0.750:

|β| ≈ 0.2986

Therefore the amplifier must supply

A = 1/|β| ≈ 3.349

For a non-inverting op-amp:

A = 1 + R2/R1

Hence

R2/R1 = A - 1 ≈ 3.349 - 1 ≈ 2.349

So the required ratio is approximately R2/R1 ≈ 2.35.

Sanity checks

  • Units: All gain and feedback factors are dimensionless, as they must be.
  • Direction: Because the op-amp lags, the bridge must lead, so the uncorrected oscillation frequency must be below 10 kHz. It is.
  • Magnitude logic: Changing Rp away from the symmetric case makes |β| smaller than 1/3, so the amplifier gain must be slightly larger than 3. That is exactly what we found.

Final Answer

Original 10 kHz design: C ≈ 1.59 nF for each capacitor. With op-amp excess lag of 5.7°, the oscillator frequency becomes approximately 8.61 kHz. To restore oscillation to 10 kHz, change the shunt resistor of the Wien bridge from 10 kΩ to about 7.50 kΩ. The amplifier gain must then be A ≈ 3.35, so R2/R1 ≈ 2.35.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

What the final formulas tell us

  • f = x f0: the oscillation frequency is scaled directly by the dimensionless parameter x = ωRC.
  • tan φβ = (1 - x²)/(3x): phase is controlled by how far the operating frequency is from the symmetric center point x = 1.
  • tan φβ = (1 - a)/(2a + 1) at fixed ωRC = 1: the resistor asymmetry parameter a = Rp/R modifies phase without changing C or the series resistor.

Parameter dependence

If op-amp lag increases

The uncompensated oscillation frequency drops further below the ideal value, or more asymmetry is needed in the bridge to keep the original frequency.

If Rp is reduced

At fixed 10 kHz, the bridge phase becomes more positive and the attenuation changes, requiring more amplifier gain.

Alternative derivation idea

Another way is to write the bridge node equation directly using Kirchhoff’s current law in phasor form and derive β(jω) from the node-voltage ratio. This yields the same rational function but emphasizes the network as a feedback filter rather than as a simple impedance divider.

Concept checks

  • Q: Why does a lagging op-amp make the oscillation frequency lower here?
    A: Because the Wien bridge provides positive phase only below its ideal center frequency.
  • Q: Why not keep Rp = 10 kΩ and just change gain?
    A: Gain alone fixes the amplitude condition, not the phase condition. The frequency error comes from phase mismatch.
  • Q: Why must R2/R1 become larger than 2?
    A: Because once the bridge is made asymmetric to supply phase lead, its attenuation is slightly greater than 1/3, so the amplifier needs slightly more than gain 3.
  • Q: Could we instead change capacitance?
    A: Yes. Any parameter that changes bridge phase at 10 kHz could be used, but the problem specifically asks for the shunt resistor change.
Optional deeper note: small-angle asymptotics

Since 5.7° is small, in radians it is about 0.0995. Thus tan 5.7° ≈ 5.7° in radians ≈ 0.10. This is why the arithmetic becomes especially clean. Small-angle expansions are mathematically justified because tan θ = θ + O(θ³) for |θ| ≪ 1.
