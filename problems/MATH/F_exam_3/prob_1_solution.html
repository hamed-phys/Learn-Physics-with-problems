Related Rates • Distance Growth

How fast is the particle moving away from \((0,3)\)?

A particle travels along the positive \(x\)-axis with constant speed \(5\) units/s. Find the instantaneous rate of increase of its distance from the point \((0,3)\) when that distance equals \(7\).

\( \dfrac{dx}{dt}=5 \) Point \((0,3)\) \( r=7 \)
Distance from \((x,0)\) to \((0,3)\) is the hypotenuse: \(r=\sqrt{x^2+3^2}\). Only \(x\) changes with time.

  • Given: particle on \(x\)-axis \((x(t),0)\), with \(\dfrac{dx}{dt}=5\) units/s.
  • Reference point: \((0,3)\).
  • Condition: at the instant of interest, \(r=7\).
  • Unknown: \(\dfrac{dr}{dt}\) (rate distance increases).
  • Key idea: geometry gives \(r\) as a function of \(x\), then differentiate w.r.t. time.

Approaches (and choice)

best: implicit
  • Direct: \(r=\sqrt{x^2+9}\), differentiate (fast).
  • Implicit: \(r^2=x^2+9\), differentiate (cleaner, fewer radicals early).
  • Vector: radial component of velocity (advanced, same result).

We use implicit differentiation because it keeps algebra tidy and reduces mistakes.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Geometry: express \(r\) using the distance formula \(r^2=x^2+9\).
  2. Differentiate: take \(\dfrac{d}{dt}\) of both sides using the chain rule.
  3. Solve for \(\dfrac{dr}{dt}\) in terms of \(x,r,\dfrac{dx}{dt}\).
  4. Use the condition \(r=7\) to find the matching \(x\) (positive root).
  5. Substitute to get the numerical value of \(\dfrac{dr}{dt}\).
  6. Check: units, sign, and whether the value is less than \(5\).

Tip: don’t differentiate \(3\) (it’s constant), and remember the particle stays on \(y=0\).

PART 3 — Full Solution

Intuition: The distance \(r\) is the hypotenuse of a right triangle with legs \(x\) (changing) and \(3\) (constant). Because the particle moves horizontally, the distance increases at the projection of its velocity onto the line from \((0,3)\) to \((x,0)\). That projection is a fraction \(\frac{x}{r}\) of the speed \(5\), so we expect \(\frac{dr}{dt} < 5\).

Derivation Steps related rates
Step 1 — Distance relation
\[ r=\sqrt{(x-0)^2+(0-3)^2}=\sqrt{x^2+9} \]
\[ \Rightarrow\quad r^2=x^2+9 \]
Step 2 — Differentiate w.r.t. time
\[ \frac{d}{dt}(r^2)=\frac{d}{dt}(x^2+9) \]
\[ 2r\frac{dr}{dt}=2x\frac{dx}{dt} \]
Step 3 — Solve for the desired rate
\[ \frac{dr}{dt}=\frac{x}{r}\,\frac{dx}{dt} \]
Step 4 — Use the condition \(r=7\)
\[ 7^2=x^2+9\ \Rightarrow\ 49=x^2+9\ \Rightarrow\ x^2=40 \]
\[ x=\sqrt{40}=2\sqrt{10}\quad (\text{positive }x\text{-axis}) \]
Step 5 — Substitute \(\frac{dx}{dt}=5\)
\[ \frac{dr}{dt}=\frac{2\sqrt{10}}{7}\cdot 5=\frac{10\sqrt{10}}{7}\ \text{units/s} \]

Final Result Boxed answer + meaning

\[ \boxed{\frac{dr}{dt}=\frac{10\sqrt{10}}{7}\ \text{units/s}} \]

The distance from \((0,3)\) is increasing at about \(4.52\) units/s at that instant (slower than \(5\) because the motion is not perfectly along the line connecting the points).

Sanity checks (units • limits • interpretation)
  • Units: \(x/r\) is dimensionless, so \(\frac{dr}{dt}\) has units of units/s, matching \(\frac{dx}{dt}\).
  • Limiting case \(x\to 0\): then \(r\to 3\) and \(\frac{dr}{dt}=\frac{x}{r}\frac{dx}{dt}\to 0\). Horizontal motion initially barely changes distance.
  • Limiting case \(x\to\infty\): then \(r\approx x\) so \(x/r\to 1\) and \(\frac{dr}{dt}\to 5\). Far away, the line of sight becomes almost horizontal.
  • Sign: all quantities are positive here, so \(\frac{dr}{dt}>0\): the particle is indeed moving away.

Compact Summary

one screen

Use \(r^2=x^2+9\). Differentiate: \(2r\,\frac{dr}{dt}=2x\,\frac{dx}{dt}\Rightarrow \frac{dr}{dt}=\frac{x}{r}\frac{dx}{dt}\).

When \(r=7\): \(x^2=40\Rightarrow x=2\sqrt{10}\). Then \(\frac{dr}{dt}=\frac{10\sqrt{10}}{7}\).

Common Mistakes

avoid
  • Using \(r=x\). (Not true because the point is \((0,3)\), not on the x-axis.)
  • Differentiating \(3\) as if it changes. (It’s constant.)
  • Taking \(x=-\sqrt{40}\). (Motion is on the positive x-axis.)
  • Forgetting chain rule: \(\frac{d}{dt}(r^2)=2r\frac{dr}{dt}\).

Numerical Feel

approx

\[ \frac{10\sqrt{10}}{7}\approx \frac{10\times 3.1623}{7}\approx 4.52 \]

Reasonable: it’s close to \(5\) but slightly smaller, since the distance line is slanted.
