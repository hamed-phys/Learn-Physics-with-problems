Calculus / ODEs IVP • Separable Problem 3

Find the unique function y(x)

Solve the initial value problem: dy/dx = x²y with y(1)=1.

Final Result
\[ y(x)=\exp\!\left(\frac{x^3-1}{3}\right) \]
Unique solution passing through (1,1).
PART 1

Problem Analysis

Restate the problem

Determine the function y(x) that satisfies the differential equation \(\frac{dy}{dx}=x^2y\) and also satisfies the initial condition \(y(1)=1\).

Given

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = x^2y\)
  • \(\displaystyle y(1)=1\)

Unknown

  • \(\displaystyle y(x)\)

Relevant physics intuition

This has the classic “growth law” structure: \[ \frac{dy}{dx}=k(x)\,y \] where the fractional growth rate is \(\frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx}=k(x)=x^2\). It behaves like exponential growth, except the “rate” increases with \(x\).

Candidate approaches

  1. Separation of variables — fastest and most transparent.
  2. Integrating factor — linear ODE method, same result.
  3. Pattern recognition — \(y=C\exp(\int x^2dx)\), quick but less instructive.

Choice: Separation of variables (best for step-by-step clarity).

PART 2

Strategy & Tips

Roadmap (no algebra first)

  1. Recognize separable form — identify \(y' = f(x)\,y\).
  2. Separate variables — move \(y\) to one side and \(x\) to the other.
  3. Integrate both sides — \(\int \frac{1}{y}dy\) and \(\int x^2dx\).
  4. Solve for \(y\) — exponentiate to remove \(\ln\).
  5. Apply \(y(1)=1\) — determine the constant.
  6. Verify — differentiate and check \(y'=x^2y\).

Common mistakes

  • Forgetting the integration constant.
  • Missing absolute value: \(\int \frac{1}{y}dy=\ln|y|+C\).
  • Exponent slip: \(e^{a+b}=e^a e^b\).
  • Not checking the initial condition at the end.
PART 3

Full Solution

Physical intuition

The equation \(\frac{dy}{dx}=x^2y\) says the slope is proportional to the current value of \(y\), with proportionality factor \(x^2\). Since \(x^2\ge 0\), the solution starting at \(y(1)=1>0\) stays positive and grows rapidly for large \(x>0\).

Step 1
\(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}=x^2y\)
Recognize it as separable: \(y' = f(x)\,y\).
Step 2
\(\displaystyle \frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx}=x^2 \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{1}{y}\,dy=x^2\,dx\)
Divide by \(y\) and multiply by \(dx\) to separate variables.
Step 3
\(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{y}\,dy = \int x^2\,dx\)
Integrate both sides.
Step 4
\(\displaystyle \ln|y|=\frac{x^3}{3}+C\)
\(\int \frac{1}{y}dy=\ln|y|\), \(\int x^2dx=x^3/3\), plus constant \(C\).
Step 5
\(\displaystyle |y|=e^{\frac{x^3}{3}+C}=e^C e^{x^3/3}=A e^{x^3/3}\)
Exponentiate; define \(A=e^C>0\).
Step 6
\(\displaystyle 1=y(1)=A e^{1/3}\;\Rightarrow\;A=e^{-1/3}\)
Use the initial condition to fix the constant.
Final
\[ y(x)=e^{-1/3}e^{x^3/3} =\exp\!\left(\frac{x^3-1}{3}\right) \]
This is the unique solution through \((1,1)\).

Sanity checks

Initial condition
\(y(1)=\exp((1-1)/3)=e^0=1\). ✓
ODE verification
If \(y=\exp((x^3-1)/3)\), then \(y'=\big(\frac{d}{dx}\frac{x^3-1}{3}\big)y=x^2y\). ✓
Units
The exponent must be dimensionless. In pure math, \(x\) is dimensionless. In physics, you would include a scale so \(\frac{x^3}{3}\) is dimensionless.
Limits & meaning
Large \(x>0\): \(y\sim e^{x^3/3}\) grows extremely fast.
\(x\to-\infty\): exponent \(\to -\infty\), so \(y\to 0^+\).
Fractional growth rate is \(x^2\): stronger growth as \(|x|\) increases.

Final boxed answer

\[ \boxed{\,y(x)=\exp\!\left(\frac{x^3-1}{3}\right)\,} \]
