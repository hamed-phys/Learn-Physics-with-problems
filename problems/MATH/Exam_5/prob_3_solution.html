PART 1 ‚Äî Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Goal Outcome

Build a reduction identity by differentiating \( \sec^{n-2}(\theta)\tan(\theta) \), rewriting everything in \(\sec(\theta)\) only, then integrating both sides to relate \( \int \sec^n(\theta)\,d\theta \) to \( \int \sec^{n-2}(\theta)\,d\theta \).

Restatement: You will (a) compute and simplify a derivative, (b) turn it into an integral recursion, and (c) use the recursion to evaluate \( \int \sec^4(\theta)\,d\theta\).

Given:

  • The expression \( \sec^{n-2}(\theta)\tan(\theta) \)
  • Derivative facts: \( \frac{d}{d\theta}\sec\theta=\sec\theta\tan\theta \), \( \frac{d}{d\theta}\tan\theta=\sec^2\theta \)
  • Identity: \( 1+\tan^2\theta=\sec^2\theta \)

Unknowns:

  • The derivative in a form involving only powers of \(\sec(\theta)\)
  • A recursion of the form \( \int \sec^n = F(\sec,\tan) + A\int \sec^{n-2} \)
  • The explicit antiderivative for \( \int \sec^4(\theta)\,d\theta \)

Why these principles apply: this is a calculus identity-building problem‚Äîproduct rule and chain rule generate the target \(\sec^n\) term, and the trig identity removes \(\tan\) so the result becomes a clean reduction relation.

Approaches & comparison 2‚Äì3 ways
  • Differentiate ‚Üí simplify ‚Üí integrate: matches the prompt and directly creates recursion (best).
  • Integration by parts on \( \int \sec^n\theta\,d\theta \): classic, but less aligned with the given structure.
  • Substitute \(u=\tan\theta\): leads to \((1+u^2)^{n/2}\) which is not simpler for general \(n\).

Chosen method: differentiate and convert into an integral identity, because it produces the requested recursion with minimal friction.

PART 2 ‚Äî Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Roadmap (no algebra yet)
  1. Differentiate \( \sec^{n-2}(\theta)\tan(\theta) \) using product rule.
  2. Apply chain rule to \( \sec^{n-2}(\theta) \) and insert trig derivatives.
  3. Factor/simplify to isolate terms containing \(\sec^n\) and \(\tan^2\).
  4. Eliminate \(\tan^2\) with \( \tan^2=\sec^2-1 \) to get only \(\sec\)-powers.
  5. Integrate both sides to convert the derivative identity into an integral identity.
  6. Rearrange to solve for \( \int \sec^n(\theta)\,d\theta \).
  7. Plug \(n=4\) and use \( \int \sec^2\theta\,d\theta=\tan\theta \) to finish.

Common mistakes:

  • Dropping the factor \((n-2)\) when differentiating \( \sec^{n-2} \).
  • Using \(1+\tan^2=\sec^2\) with the wrong rearrangement sign.
  • Forgetting to divide by \((n-1)\) when isolating \( \int \sec^n \).

Quick tip: Aim to rewrite everything into a linear combination of \( \sec^n \) and \( \sec^{n-2} \). That structure is what makes the recursion work.

PART 3 ‚Äî Full Solution

Intuition: We ‚Äúengineer‚Äù a derivative that naturally produces a \(\sec^n\) term. The leftover term drops the power by 2, creating a ladder/recurrence structure: hard integral ‚Üí easier integral.

(a) Differentiate and eliminate \(\tan(\theta)\)

Let \( g(\theta)=\sec^{n-2}(\theta)\tan(\theta) \). Use the product rule:

Product rule
\[ g'(\theta)=\big(\sec^{n-2}\theta\big)'\tan\theta+\sec^{n-2}\theta\,(\tan\theta)'. \]

Differentiate \( \sec^{n-2}\theta \) by chain rule:

Chain rule + trig derivatives
\[ \big(\sec^{n-2}\theta\big)'=(n-2)\sec^{n-3}\theta\cdot(\sec\theta)' =(n-2)\sec^{n-3}\theta\cdot(\sec\theta\tan\theta) =(n-2)\sec^{n-2}\theta\tan\theta. \] \[ (\tan\theta)'=\sec^2\theta. \]

Substitute back:

Substitution
\[ g'(\theta)=(n-2)\sec^{n-2}\theta\tan^2\theta+\sec^{n-2}\theta\sec^2\theta =(n-2)\sec^{n-2}\theta\tan^2\theta+\sec^n\theta. \]

Eliminate \( \tan^2\theta \) using \( \tan^2\theta=\sec^2\theta-1 \):

Identity: \(\tan^2=\sec^2-1\)
\[ (n-2)\sec^{n-2}\theta\tan^2\theta =(n-2)\sec^{n-2}\theta(\sec^2\theta-1) =(n-2)\sec^n\theta-(n-2)\sec^{n-2}\theta. \]

Therefore:

‚úì Result (a)
\[ \boxed{ \frac{d}{d\theta}\Big(\sec^{n-2}\theta\,\tan\theta\Big) =(n-1)\sec^n\theta-(n-2)\sec^{n-2}\theta. } \]

(b) Convert to an integral identity and solve for the recursion

Integrate both sides of the identity from (a) with respect to \(\theta\):

Integrate both sides
\[ \sec^{n-2}\theta\,\tan\theta =\int\left[(n-1)\sec^n\theta-(n-2)\sec^{n-2}\theta\right]\,d\theta. \]

Split the integral and isolate \( \int \sec^n\theta\,d\theta \):

Rearrange
\[ \sec^{n-2}\theta\,\tan\theta =(n-1)\int \sec^n\theta\,d\theta-(n-2)\int \sec^{n-2}\theta\,d\theta, \] \[ (n-1)\int \sec^n\theta\,d\theta =\sec^{n-2}\theta\,\tan\theta+(n-2)\int \sec^{n-2}\theta\,d\theta, \] \[ \boxed{ \int \sec^n\theta\,d\theta =\frac{\sec^{n-2}\theta\,\tan\theta}{n-1} +\frac{n-2}{n-1}\int \sec^{n-2}\theta\,d\theta. } \]
‚úì Match the requested form
\[ \int \sec^{n}(\theta)\,d\theta = F(\sec\theta,\tan\theta) + A\int \sec^{n-2}(\theta)\,d\theta \] with \[ \boxed{ F(\sec\theta,\tan\theta)=\frac{\sec^{n-2}\theta\,\tan\theta}{n-1}, \qquad A=\frac{n-2}{n-1}. } \]

(c) Use the recursion to compute \( \int \sec^4(\theta)\,d\theta \)

Set \(n=4\) in the reduction formula:

Plug in \(n=4\)
\[ \int \sec^4\theta\,d\theta =\frac{\sec^{2}\theta\,\tan\theta}{3} +\frac{2}{3}\int \sec^{2}\theta\,d\theta. \]

Use the standard antiderivative \( \int \sec^2\theta\,d\theta=\tan\theta + C\):

‚úì Final answer (c)
\[ \boxed{ \int \sec^4(\theta)\,d\theta =\frac{1}{3}\sec^{2}(\theta)\tan(\theta) +\frac{2}{3}\tan(\theta)+C. } \]

Sanity Checks

1) Units dimensionless consistency

\(\sec(\theta)\) and \(\tan(\theta)\) are dimensionless, so the antiderivative is dimensionless‚Äîas expected for an integral over an angle.

2) Limiting case near \(\theta=0\) small-angle behavior

For small \(\theta\), \(\sec\theta\approx 1\) and \(\tan\theta\approx \theta\). The integrand \(\sec^4\theta\approx 1\), so \(\int \sec^4\theta\,d\theta \approx \theta\). Our result becomes \(\frac{1}{3}(1)\theta+\frac{2}{3}\theta=\theta\), matching perfectly.

3) Quick derivative check does it differentiate back?

Differentiating \(\frac{1}{3}\sec^{2}\theta\tan\theta+\frac{2}{3}\tan\theta\) returns \(\sec^4\theta\). This is guaranteed by construction because the reduction formula was derived from an exact derivative identity.

4) ‚ÄúPhysics interpretation‚Äù why recursion matters

Reduction formulas act like a ladder: high-power expressions reduce to simpler base cases. In physics math, this mirrors how we reduce integrals and series to known primitives‚Äîsaving time and making solutions scalable.
