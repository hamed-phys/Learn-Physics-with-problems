What must be found: antiderivatives \(F_a(x)+C\) and \(F_b(x)+C\) in closed form.

Why inverse trig shows up (physics intuition): Completing the square turns each expression into a geometric prototype: a¬≤ ‚àí u¬≤ behaves like a circle/ellipse slice (angles ‚Üí arcsine), and u¬≤ + a¬≤ behaves like a right-triangle ratio (tangent ‚Üí arctangent). PART 3 ‚Äî Full Solution

Derivation + checks
‚ú®
Intuition first: Both integrals hide a ‚Äúcircle/triangle‚Äù geometry. Completing the square reveals either \(\sqrt{a^2-u^2}\) (‚Üí arcsine) or \(u^2+a^2\) (‚Üí arctangent).

(a) \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{5x-x^2}}\,dx\)

Step 1 ‚Äî Complete the square inside the square root

\[ 5x - x^2 = -(x^2-5x) \] \[ x^2 - 5x = \left(x-\frac{5}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^2 \] \[ \Rightarrow\; 5x-x^2 = -\left[\left(x-\frac{5}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^2\right] = \left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^2 - \left(x-\frac{5}{2}\right)^2 \]

Step 2 ‚Äî Rewrite the integral using the centered form

\[ \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{5x-x^2}}\,dx = \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^2-\left(x-\frac{5}{2}\right)^2}}\,dx \]

Step 3 ‚Äî Substitution to match the standard arcsine form

\[ u = x-\frac{5}{2}\quad\Rightarrow\quad du=dx \] \[ \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^2-u^2}}\,du \]

Step 4 ‚Äî Apply the standard result

\[ \int \frac{du}{\sqrt{a^2-u^2}}=\arcsin\!\left(\frac{u}{a}\right)+C \] With \(a=\frac{5}{2}\), \[ \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^2-u^2}}\,du = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{u}{\frac{5}{2}}\right)+C = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{2u}{5}\right)+C \]

Step 5 ‚Äî Back-substitute

‚úì Final answer for (a)

\[ \boxed{ \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{5x-x^2}}\,dx = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{2x-5}{5}\right)+C } \]
Sanity checks for (a) ‚Ä∫
üîé
Domain / limits: \(5x-x^2>0 \Rightarrow x(5-x)>0 \Rightarrow 0<x<5\). As \(x\to0^+\), \(\frac{2x-5}{5}\to -1\Rightarrow \arcsin(-1)=-\frac{\pi}{2}\). As \(x\to5^-\), \(\frac{2x-5}{5}\to +1\Rightarrow \arcsin(1)=\frac{\pi}{2}\). The integrand has endpoint singularities, but the antiderivative remains finite ‚Äî typical for \(1/\sqrt{\cdot}\).
üìê
Physical interpretation: the form \(\sqrt{a^2-u^2}\) mirrors a semicircle; the antiderivative is an angle. This is why arcsine appears frequently in geometry-based kinematics and phase descriptions.

(b) \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{x^2-6x+18}\,dx\)

Step 1 ‚Äî Complete the square in the denominator

\[ x^2-6x+18 = (x^2-6x+9)+9 = (x-3)^2 + 9 \] \[ \Rightarrow\; \int \frac{1}{x^2-6x+18}\,dx = \int \frac{1}{(x-3)^2+9}\,dx \]

Step 2 ‚Äî Shift variable

\[ u=x-3\quad\Rightarrow\quad du=dx \] \[ \int \frac{1}{u^2+9}\,du \]

Step 3 ‚Äî Apply the standard arctangent integral

\[ \int \frac{du}{u^2+a^2}=\frac{1}{a}\arctan\!\left(\frac{u}{a}\right)+C \] Here \(a=3\), so \[ \int \frac{1}{u^2+9}\,du = \frac{1}{3}\arctan\!\left(\frac{u}{3}\right)+C \]

Step 4 ‚Äî Back-substitute

‚úì Final answer for (b)

\[ \boxed{ \int \frac{1}{x^2-6x+18}\,dx = \frac{1}{3}\arctan\!\left(\frac{x-3}{3}\right)+C } \]
Sanity checks for (b) ‚Ä∫
üß™
Asymptotic behavior: For large \(|x|\), the integrand \(\sim 1/x^2\), so the antiderivative should approach a constant. \(\arctan(\cdot)\to \pm \frac{\pi}{2}\), hence \(\frac{1}{3}\arctan(\cdot)\) saturates ‚Üí consistent.
‚ö°
Physical interpretation: \(\frac{1}{u^2+a^2}\) is ‚ÄúLorentzian-like‚Äù in shape. Integrals of this type appear in phase accumulation, resonance curves, and response functions.
üß∑
Units note: inverse-trig arguments must be dimensionless. In pure calculus, \(x\) and constants are unitless. In physics contexts, you‚Äôd interpret these as ratios with consistent units (e.g., \((x-3)/3\)).
