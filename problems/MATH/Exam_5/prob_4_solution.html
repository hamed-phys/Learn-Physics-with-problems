Problem 4 ‚Äî Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{x^{2}\sqrt{x^{2}-1}}\,dx\) using trigonometric substitution

We‚Äôll solve the integral in a rigorous, beginner-friendly way: first analyzing what method fits the radical, then building a clean roadmap, and finally executing the full derivation with back-substitution and sanity checks.

Domain: Calculus Technique: \(x=\sec\theta\) Constraint: \(x>1\) Deliverables: \(\theta\)-form + \(x\)-form

PART 1 ‚Äî Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Concept Selection

1) Restate the task

Compute the indefinite integral \[ \int \frac{1}{x^{2}\sqrt{x^{2}-1}}\,dx, \] assuming \(x>1\), by first rewriting it using a trigonometric substitution \(x=f(\theta)\), then back-substituting to express the final answer in terms of \(x\).

2) Given / unknown / what must be found

  • Given: integrand \(\frac{1}{x^{2}\sqrt{x^{2}-1}}\) and condition \(x>1\).
  • Unknown: antiderivative \(F(x)\) such that \(F'(x)=\frac{1}{x^{2}\sqrt{x^{2}-1}}\).
  • Must find: (a) integral in \(\theta\), (b) final closed form in \(x\) + constant \(C\).

3) Why trig substitution applies

üß† Pattern match

The radical \(\sqrt{x^{2}-1}\) matches the identity \[ \sec^{2}\theta-1=\tan^{2}\theta. \] So choosing \(x=\sec\theta\) converts the square root into a single trig function, turning the integrand into something elementary.

4) Candidate approaches

  • Trig substitution: \(x=\sec\theta\) (directly targets \(\sqrt{x^2-1}\)).
  • Hyperbolic substitution: \(x=\cosh u\) (also works via \(\cosh^2u-1=\sinh^2u\), but not what‚Äôs asked).
  • Algebraic tricks: possible but usually messier for this exact structure.

5) Best approach & justification

‚úÖ Choose \(x=\sec\theta\). It is the cleanest route because it collapses \(\sqrt{x^{2}-1}\) immediately and produces a one-line trig integral after cancellation.

PART 3 ‚Äî Full Solution

Derivation + checks

Physical intuition (why the substitution ‚Äúwants‚Äù to work)

Expressions like \(\sqrt{x^{2}-1}\) behave like a Pythagorean difference. Trig identities are built to linearize that geometry: \(\sec^{2}\theta-1=\tan^{2}\theta\). By setting \(x=\sec\theta\), the radical becomes \(\tan\theta\), and the integral simplifies to a basic trigonometric form.

Step 1 ‚Äî Substitute \(x=\sec\theta\)

\[ x=\sec\theta,\qquad dx=\sec\theta\tan\theta\,d\theta. \]

Next rewrite each piece of the integrand:

\[ x^2=\sec^2\theta,\qquad \sqrt{x^2-1}=\sqrt{\sec^2\theta-1}=\sqrt{\tan^2\theta}=|\tan\theta|. \]

Since \(x>1\) we choose \(\theta\) in a range where \(\tan\theta\ge 0\), so \(|\tan\theta|=\tan\theta\). Hence: \[ \sqrt{x^2-1}=\tan\theta. \]

Step 2 ‚Äî Convert the integral to \(\theta\)

Start from \[ \int \frac{1}{x^{2}\sqrt{x^{2}-1}}\,dx. \] Substitute \(x^2=\sec^2\theta\), \(\sqrt{x^2-1}=\tan\theta\), and \(dx=\sec\theta\tan\theta\,d\theta\):

\[ \int \frac{1}{(\sec^2\theta)(\tan\theta)}\left(\sec\theta\tan\theta\,d\theta\right). \]

Step 3 ‚Äî Simplify

Cancel \(\tan\theta\) and reduce the \(\sec\) powers:

\[ \int \frac{\sec\theta}{\sec^2\theta}\,d\theta =\int \frac{1}{\sec\theta}\,d\theta =\int \cos\theta\,d\theta. \]

Step 4 ‚Äî Integrate in \(\theta\)

\[ \int \cos\theta\,d\theta=\sin\theta + C. \]

Step 5 ‚Äî Back-substitute to \(x\)

From \(x=\sec\theta\), we get \[ \cos\theta=\frac{1}{\sec\theta}=\frac{1}{x}. \] Then \[ \sin\theta=\sqrt{1-\cos^2\theta} =\sqrt{1-\frac{1}{x^2}} =\sqrt{\frac{x^2-1}{x^2}} =\frac{\sqrt{x^2-1}}{x}, \] using \(x>1 \Rightarrow x>0\).

‚úÖ Final Result
\[ \boxed{\int \frac{1}{x^{2}\sqrt{x^{2}-1}}\,dx=\frac{\sqrt{x^{2}-1}}{x}+C \qquad (x>1)} \]

Sanity checks

üß™ Check 1 ‚Äî Units / dimensional consistency

The expression \(\frac{\sqrt{x^2-1}}{x}\) is a ratio of ‚Äúsame-kind‚Äù terms in \(x\), so it‚Äôs dimensionless if \(x\) is. More broadly, it has the correct ‚Äúshape‚Äù for an antiderivative of a function that decays like \(1/x^3\) for large \(x\).

üìà Check 2 ‚Äî Limiting behavior
  • As \(x\to 1^+\): \(\sqrt{x^2-1}\to 0\), so \(F(x)\to 0\) (up to \(C\)). The integrand diverges but is integrable, so a finite limit is reasonable.
  • As \(x\to\infty\): \(\frac{\sqrt{x^2-1}}{x}\to 1\). Meanwhile the integrand behaves like \(1/x^3\), whose integral approaches a constant ‚Äî consistent.
üåç Check 3 ‚Äî Physical interpretation (intuition-level)

Radicals like \(\sqrt{x^2-1}\) often appear in geometry-driven constraints (e.g., hyperbolic/trigonometric relationships in kinematics or energy constraints). Trig substitution is essentially a coordinate change: it trades an algebraic constraint for an angular one, simplifying the mathematics while preserving the underlying structure.
