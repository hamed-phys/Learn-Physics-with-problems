Calculus · Polar Curves Exam Problem 3

Problem 3 — Polar Curve \( r=\sin(2\theta) \), \( 0\le\theta\le\frac{\pi}{2} \)

A rigorous, beginner-friendly solution with a clean roadmap, endpoint tangents, maximum radius, and the exact enclosed area using the polar area formula.

Given
\(r(\theta)=\sin(2\theta)\), \(\;\theta\in[0,\pi/2]\)
Find
Quadrant(s), endpoints, endpoint tangents, max \(r\), area enclosed

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

Copy

Restatement

You are given the polar curve \(\;r=\sin(2\theta)\;\) on \(\;0\le\theta\le\frac{\pi}{2}\). You must sketch it and label: (i) which quadrant(s) it lies in, (ii) endpoints, (iii) tangent slopes at endpoints, (iv) where \(r(\theta)\) is maximal. Then compute the enclosed area.

Given

  • \(r(\theta)=\sin(2\theta)\)
  • \(\theta\in\left[0,\frac{\pi}{2}\right]\)

Unknowns / Targets

  • Quadrant(s) of the traced curve
  • Endpoints \((x,y)\)
  • Endpoint tangents \(\left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|\)
  • Max radius angle(s) and value
  • Area enclosed by the loop
Relevant principles (why they apply)
  • Polar ↔ Cartesian mapping: \(\;x=r\cos\theta,\;y=r\sin\theta\;\) converts polar geometry into a plane sketch.
  • Extrema via derivatives: maximizing \(r(\theta)\) uses \(r'(\theta)=0\) (standard calculus).
  • Polar area formula: area swept by a radius in polar coordinates is \(\;A=\frac12\int r^2\,d\theta\;\).
Candidate approaches (and why we choose one)
  1. Direct polar reasoning: sign of \(r\), key angles, symmetry → fastest for sketching.
  2. Parametric method: \(x(\theta)=r\cos\theta,\;y(\theta)=r\sin\theta\) → best for slopes.
  3. Implicit Cartesian conversion: algebra-heavy; not needed here.

Chosen method: Polar reasoning + parametric slope formula + polar area integral (most direct and least algebraic overhead).

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

Solution
  1. Quadrant check: determine sign of \(r=\sin(2\theta)\) on the interval.
    Tool: sine sign on \([0,\pi]\).
  2. Endpoints: evaluate at \(\theta=0\) and \(\theta=\pi/2\), convert to \((x,y)\).
    Tool: \(x=r\cos\theta,\;y=r\sin\theta\).
  3. Maximum radius: solve where \(r(\theta)\) is maximal.
    Tool: \(r'(\theta)=0\) or max of sine.
  4. Shape inference: track the curve as \(\theta\) increases.
    Tool: key points at \(\theta=0,\pi/4,\pi/2\).
  5. Endpoint tangents: compute \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) in polar/parametric form at endpoints.
    Tool: \(\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{r'\sin\theta+r\cos\theta}{r'\cos\theta-r\sin\theta}\).
  6. Area: compute \(\frac12\int r^2\,d\theta\) over the tracing interval.
    Tool: polar area formula.
  7. Simplify integral: use \(\sin^2\) power-reduction (half-angle style).
    Tool: \(\sin^2 u=\frac{1-\cos(2u)}{2}\).

Common mistakes

  • Forgetting that negative \(r\) reflects the point by \(\pi\) (here \(r\ge 0\), so safe).
  • Thinking “endpoint” means where \(r\) is maximum—endpoints come from the interval endpoints.
  • At the origin, different parameter endpoints can give different tangent directions.
  • Dropping the factor \(\tfrac12\) in the polar area formula.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Copy

Physical intuition (geometry intuition)

As \(\theta\) sweeps from \(0\) to \(\pi/2\), the direction rotates through Quadrant I. Meanwhile \(r(\theta)=\sin(2\theta)\) starts at 0, rises to 1, then returns to 0. So the point leaves the origin, bulges outward, and returns to the origin — tracing one “petal” of a rose curve entirely in Quadrant I.

(a) Sketch + requested labels

\[ r(\theta)=\sin(2\theta),\qquad 0\le \theta \le \frac{\pi}{2}. \]

(i) Quadrant(s): On \(0\le\theta\le\pi/2\), we have \(0\le 2\theta\le \pi\), hence \(\sin(2\theta)\ge 0\). Therefore \(r\ge 0\) and the direction angle \(\theta\) is in Quadrant I, so the curve lies in Quadrant I (touching the axes at the origin).

\[ \boxed{\text{The curve (for the given interval) is contained in Quadrant I.}} \]

(ii) Endpoints: Endpoints correspond to the parameter endpoints \(\theta=0\) and \(\theta=\pi/2\). Convert using \(x=r\cos\theta,\;y=r\sin\theta\).

\[ r(0)=\sin 0=0 \;\Rightarrow\; (x,y)=(0,0), \qquad r\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)=\sin \pi=0 \;\Rightarrow\; (x,y)=(0,0). \]
\[ \boxed{\text{Endpoints: }(0,0)\text{ at }\theta=0 \;\;\text{and}\;\; (0,0)\text{ at }\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}.} \]

Important: these are the same point in Cartesian coordinates, but approached from different \(\theta\), so the tangents can be different.

(iv) Where \(r(\theta)\) is maximum: Since \(r(\theta)=\sin(2\theta)\), its maximum value is 1, achieved when \(\sin(2\theta)=1\), i.e. \(2\theta=\pi/2\).

\[ 2\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}\quad\Rightarrow\quad \theta=\frac{\pi}{4},\qquad r_{\max}=1. \]
\[ \boxed{\theta=\frac{\pi}{4}\ \text{gives the maximum radius } r_{\max}=1.} \]

(iii) Tangent slopes at the endpoints: Use the standard polar/parametric slope formula

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{\frac{dy}{d\theta}}{\frac{dx}{d\theta}} =\frac{r'(\theta)\sin\theta+r(\theta)\cos\theta}{r'(\theta)\cos\theta-r(\theta)\sin\theta}. \]

First compute \(r'(\theta)\):

\[ r(\theta)=\sin(2\theta)\quad\Rightarrow\quad r'(\theta)=2\cos(2\theta). \]
Tangent at \(\theta=0\)

At \(\theta=0\): \(r(0)=0\), \(r'(0)=2\cos 0=2\), \(\sin 0=0\), \(\cos 0=1\).

\[ \left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{\theta=0} =\frac{(2)(0)+(0)(1)}{(2)(1)-(0)(0)} =\frac{0}{2}=0. \]
\[ \boxed{\text{Tangent at }\theta=0:\ \text{horizontal, slope } m=0.} \]
Tangent at \(\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}\)

At \(\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}\): \(r=\sin(\pi)=0\), \(r'=2\cos(\pi)=-2\), \(\sin(\pi/2)=1\), \(\cos(\pi/2)=0\).

\[ \left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{\theta=\pi/2} =\frac{(-2)(1)+(0)(0)}{(-2)(0)-(0)(1)} =\frac{-2}{0}, \]

which indicates a vertical tangent (undefined slope).

\[ \boxed{\text{Tangent at }\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}:\ \text{vertical (undefined slope)}.} \]

(b) Area enclosed by the curve

The polar area formula for a region traced once from \(\theta=a\) to \(\theta=b\) is

\[ A=\frac12\int_a^b r^2\,d\theta. \]

Here the loop is traced on \([0,\pi/2]\), so

\[ A=\frac12\int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^2(2\theta)\,d\theta. \]

Use the power-reduction identity (consistent with the provided half-angle hint):

\[ \sin^2(u)=\frac{1-\cos(2u)}{2}. \]

With \(u=2\theta\), we get \(\sin^2(2\theta)=\frac{1-\cos(4\theta)}{2}\). Substitute:

\[ A=\frac12\int_0^{\pi/2}\frac{1-\cos(4\theta)}{2}\,d\theta =\frac14\int_0^{\pi/2}\left(1-\cos(4\theta)\right)\,d\theta. \]

Integrate term-by-term:

\[ \int\left(1-\cos(4\theta)\right)\,d\theta =\theta-\frac{\sin(4\theta)}{4}. \]

Evaluate from \(0\) to \(\pi/2\):

\[ A=\frac14\left[\theta-\frac{\sin(4\theta)}{4}\right]_{0}^{\pi/2} =\frac14\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\frac{\sin(2\pi)}{4}-0+\frac{\sin 0}{4}\right) =\frac14\cdot\frac{\pi}{2} =\frac{\pi}{8}. \]
Final results (boxed)
• Quadrant(s): Quadrant I (for \(0\le\theta\le\pi/2\)).
• Endpoints: \((0,0)\) at \(\theta=0\) and \((0,0)\) at \(\theta=\pi/2\).
• Tangent at \(\theta=0\): slope \(0\) (horizontal).
• Tangent at \(\theta=\pi/2\): vertical (undefined slope).
• Max radius: \(\boxed{\theta=\pi/4,\; r_{\max}=1}\).
• Enclosed area: \(\boxed{A=\pi/8}\).

Sanity checks

  • Units: \(r\) is dimensionless here, so area is dimensionless; \(\pi/8\) is consistent.
  • Magnitude: max radius is \(1\), so area must be smaller than \(\pi(1)^2=\pi\). Indeed, \(\pi/8\ll \pi\).
  • Interpretation: one petal is traced: \(r\) goes \(0\to 1\to 0\) while \(\theta\) sweeps Quadrant I.

Tip for your hand sketch: mark the origin, draw a smooth loop in Quadrant I reaching its farthest point on the line \(\theta=\pi/4\), and label the tangents at the origin (horizontal for \(\theta=0\), vertical for \(\theta=\pi/2\)).
