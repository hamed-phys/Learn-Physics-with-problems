CALCULUS • SOLIDS OF REVOLUTION

Problem 1 — Volume from Revolving \(y=h\left(\frac{x}{r}\right)^3\) about the \(y\)-axis

We compute the volume of the solid formed by rotating a first-quadrant region around the \(y\)-axis, then compare it to the containing cylinder \(\big(\text{radius } r,\; \text{height } h\big)\). The cleanest method here is the cylindrical shell method.

Axis \(y\)-axis
Curve \(y=h(x/r)^3\)
Region \(0\le x\le r,\; 0\le y\le h(x/r)^3\)
Goal Volume + fraction of cylinder
Score 25 pts

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

conceptual setup

1) Restate the problem

In the first quadrant, the region is bounded by the \(x\)-axis \((y=0)\), the vertical line \(x=r\), and the curve \(y=h\left(\frac{x}{r}\right)^3\). Revolve this region about the \(y\)-axis and find: (a) the resulting volume \(V\), and (b) the fraction of the cylinder \(\pi r^2 h\) occupied by this solid.

\[ \text{Curve: } y=h\left(\frac{x}{r}\right)^3,\qquad r>0,\;h>0 \]

2) Given, unknowns, required

  • Given: \(r,h>0\); axis of rotation is the \(y\)-axis; bounding curve \(y=h(x/r)^3\).
  • Unknowns: volume \(V\); fraction \(V/(\pi r^2 h)\).
  • Find: (a) \(V\), (b) \(\dfrac{V}{\pi r^2 h}\).

3) Principles / laws (why they apply)

Volume is obtained by accumulating infinitesimal elements: \(\;dV=(\text{cross-sectional area})\cdot(\text{thickness})\). Rotating about the \(y\)-axis creates circular geometry—either disks/washers (slicing in \(y\)) or cylindrical shells (slicing in \(x\)).

4) Candidate approaches

  • Cylindrical shells (in \(x\)): direct because \(y\) is already given as a function of \(x\).
  • Washers/disks (in \(y\)): valid but needs solving \(x\) in terms of \(y\) (extra step).
  • Pappus (centroid theorem): possible but requires centroid of the region (overkill here).

Chosen method

Shell method is best: it uses \(x\in[0,r]\) directly and avoids inverting the curve.

Common pitfalls

PART 3 — Full Solution

rigorous derivation

Physical intuition

Slice the region into thin vertical strips. Each strip at position \(x\) has height \(y(x)=h(x/r)^3\). Rotating around the \(y\)-axis turns that strip into a thin cylindrical shell of radius \(x\), height \(y(x)\), and thickness \(dx\). Summing all shells gives the volume.

A Part (a) — Compute the volume \(V\)

For a shell at position \(x\): radius \(=x\), height \(=h\left(\frac{x}{r}\right)^3\).

\[ dV = 2\pi(\text{radius})(\text{height})\,dx = 2\pi x\;h\left(\frac{x}{r}\right)^3\,dx \]

Integrate from \(x=0\) to \(x=r\):

\[ V=\int_0^r 2\pi x\;h\left(\frac{x}{r}\right)^3\,dx \]

Simplify the integrand: \(\;x\left(\frac{x}{r}\right)^3 = x\cdot\frac{x^3}{r^3}=\frac{x^4}{r^3}\).

\[ V=\int_0^r 2\pi h\frac{x^4}{r^3}\,dx =\frac{2\pi h}{r^3}\int_0^r x^4\,dx \]

Compute the power integral: \(\displaystyle \int_0^r x^4\,dx=\left[\frac{x^5}{5}\right]_0^r=\frac{r^5}{5}\).

\[ V=\frac{2\pi h}{r^3}\cdot\frac{r^5}{5} =\frac{2\pi h r^2}{5} \]

Final (a)

\[\boxed{V=\frac{2}{5}\,\pi r^2 h}\]
Volume of the solid of revolution about the \(y\)-axis.
shell method
B Part (b) — Fraction of the containing cylinder

The containing cylinder has base radius \(r\) and height \(h\), so:

\[ V_{\text{cyl}}=\pi r^2 h \]

The fraction occupied by the solid is:

\[ \frac{V}{V_{\text{cyl}}} =\frac{\frac{2}{5}\pi r^2 h}{\pi r^2 h} =\frac{2}{5} \]

Final (b)

\[\boxed{\text{Fraction}=\frac{2}{5}}\]
So the solid fills 40% of the cylinder volume.
comparison
✓ Sanity checks (units, limits, interpretation)

Units

\(\pi r^2 h\) has units of \((\text{length})^2(\text{length})=(\text{length})^3\). Our result \(V=\frac{2}{5}\pi r^2 h\) matches, so the dimensionality is correct.

Limiting cases

If \(h\to 0\), the region collapses vertically and \(V\to 0\). If \(r\to 0\), the region collapses toward the axis and \(V\to 0\). Our formula scales as \(V\propto h\) and \(V\propto r^2\), consistent with both limits.

Physical / geometric interpretation

The curve satisfies \(0\le y\le h\) on \(0\le x\le r\), so the solid must lie inside the cylinder. The constant fraction \(\frac{2}{5}\) indicates the “average” shell height relative to \(h\) is \(2/5\) in the shell-weighted sense.
