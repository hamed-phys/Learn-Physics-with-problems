CALCULUS • SURFACE OF REVOLUTION

Problem 2 — Surface Area by Revolving \(y=\tfrac{1}{2}x^2\) about the \(y\)-Axis

Compute the surface area generated when the curve \(y=\frac12 x^2\) for \(0\le x\le 1\) is rotated around the \(y\)-axis. The solution is presented with a physics-style “build it from infinitesimals” viewpoint: a surface is a sum of thin bands.

Given: \(y=\tfrac12 x^2\)
Interval: \(x\in[0,1]\)
Axis: \(y\)-axis
Find: surface area \(S\)
PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)
No solving yet

Restatement: We take the curve \(\,y=\tfrac12 x^2\,\) on \([0,1]\) and spin it around the \(y\)-axis. The rotation sweeps out a smooth surface. Your job is to compute its total surface area \(S\).

  • G
    Given: \(y=\tfrac12 x^2\), \(0\le x\le 1\); rotation about the \(y\)-axis.
  • U
    Unknown: \(S\) (the surface area of the surface of revolution).
  • F
    Find: an exact closed-form expression for \(S\).

Principle Why the calculus formula applies

A tiny curve segment of arc length \(ds\) at distance \(r\) from the axis sweeps a thin “belt” of area \(dS=(2\pi r)\,ds\). Integrating these belts over the curve gives the full surface area.

Arc length Geometry you need

For a graph \(y(x)\), the true curve element is \(ds=\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\,dx\). This corrects for tilt: slanted pieces are longer than their horizontal projection.

Candidate approaches

You can solve this in multiple equivalent ways:

  • 1
    Direct formula (best here): \[ S=2\pi\int_{0}^{1} x\sqrt{1+\left(y'(x)\right)^2}\,dx. \]
  • 2
    Parametric method: treat \((x,y(x))\) as a parametric curve and use \(S=2\pi\int r\,ds\). Same algebra, more general.
  • 3
    Rewrite as \(x(y)\): integrate in \(y\). Works, but slightly messier here.

Best choice Which approach and why?

Use the direct surface-of-revolution formula about the \(y\)-axis because the curve is already given as \(y(x)\) on \([0,1]\), so \(y'(x)\) and the integral are clean and standard.

Roadmap • No algebra

We’ll build the surface as a sum of thin rotational “bands”. Each band has circumference \(2\pi x\) and width \(ds\). Then we integrate from \(x=0\) to \(x=1\).

    Tool: \(r=x\).

Common mistakes Quick prevention tips

(i) About the \(y\)-axis, the radius is \(x\) (not \(y\)).
(ii) Keep the square root: \(\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\), not \(1+(y')^2\).
(iii) Don’t drop the \(2\pi\) factor (circumference).
(iv) Keep bounds consistent with the variable of integration.

PART 3 — Full Solution
Derivation • Final result

Intuition “Belt area” viewpoint

Slice the curve into tiny pieces of true length \(ds\). When rotated about the \(y\)-axis, each piece sweeps a thin belt whose area is circumference \((2\pi x)\) times width \((ds)\). Summing all belts gives the total surface area.

Step 1 — Set up the surface area integral
\[ S = 2\pi\int_{0}^{1} x\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2}\,dx \]

Here \(x\) is the radius to the \(y\)-axis and \(\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\,dx\) is the arc-length element \(ds\).

Step 2 — Compute the derivative
\[ y=\frac12 x^2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{dy}{dx}=x. \]
Step 3 — Substitute into the formula
\[ S = 2\pi\int_{0}^{1} x\sqrt{1+x^2}\,dx. \]
Step 4 — Evaluate the integral (substitution)

Use \(u=1+x^2\) because the square root contains \(1+x^2\) and we also have \(x\,dx\).

\[ u=1+x^2,\qquad du=2x\,dx\quad\Rightarrow\quad x\,dx=\frac12 du. \]

Change bounds: when \(x=0\), \(u=1\); when \(x=1\), \(u=2\).

\[ \int_{0}^{1} x\sqrt{1+x^2}\,dx =\frac12\int_{1}^{2} u^{1/2}\,du. \] \[ \frac12\int_{1}^{2} u^{1/2}\,du =\frac12\left[\frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}\right]_{1}^{2} =\frac{1}{3}\left(2^{3/2}-1\right) =\frac{1}{3}\left(2\sqrt2-1\right). \]

Final Result boxed

Exact closed form
\[ \boxed{ S=2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{3}\left(2\sqrt2-1\right) =\frac{2\pi}{3}\left(2\sqrt2-1\right) } \]
Check: \(S>\pi\) because \(\sqrt{1+x^2}>1\) on \((0,1]\). Method: dS=(2\pi x)\,ds with ds=\sqrt{1+(y')^2}dx

Sanity checks units • limits • meaning

Units: \(x\) is length, \(ds\) is length \(\Rightarrow (2\pi x)ds\) is area.
Limiting idea: If slopes were tiny (\(y'\approx 0\)), then \(S\approx 2\pi\int_0^1 x\,dx=\pi\). Here \(y'=x\) so \(S\) must be larger, which it is.
Interpretation: \(\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\) boosts the area because the curve’s true length exceeds its horizontal projection.

Final answer (compact)
\[ \boxed{S=\frac{2\pi}{3}\left(2\sqrt2-1\right)} \]
