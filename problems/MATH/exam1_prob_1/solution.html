Fermi-Dirac Distribution & Semiconductor Statistics

Detailed derivation and numerical implementation for carrier concentration analysis.

1. The Physical Problem

In Solid State Physics, the distribution of electrons over energy states is governed by the Pauli Exclusion Principle. The goal is to determine the probability that a given available energy state \(E\) is occupied by an electron at an absolute temperature \(T\).

2. Mathematical Model

The probability \(f(E)\) is defined by the Fermi-Dirac distribution function:

\[ f(E) = \frac{1}{e^{(E - E_F)/k_B T} + 1} \]

Where:

  • \(E_F\) is the Fermi Level (chemical potential).
  • \(k_B\) is the Boltzmann constant (\(8.617 \times 10^{-5} \, eV/K\)).
  • \(T\) is the absolute temperature in Kelvin.

3. Limiting Cases

At \(T \to 0K\)

The function becomes a perfect step function. All states below \(E_F\) are filled, and all above are empty.

At \(E = E_F\)

The probability is exactly \(1/2\), regardless of temperature (as long as \(T > 0\)).

4. Numerical Solver Implementation

To visualize this in the dashboard, we implement a discrete sampling of the energy spectrum surrounding the Fermi level. The JavaScript engine computes the distribution across a range of \(E_{min}\) to \(E_{max}\) using the following iterative logic: 

for(let e = 0; e <= 1.0; e += 0.01) {
    let exponent = (e - Ef) / (k * T);
    let probability = 1 / (Math.exp(exponent) + 1);
    points.push({ x: e, y: probability });
}
