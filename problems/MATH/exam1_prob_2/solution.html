PART 1 Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

1) Restate the problem (in my own words)

You are given the parabola \(\;y=x^2+2\;\). The point \(\;P=(0,1)\;\) lies on two distinct lines, and each of those lines is tangent to the parabola. The task is to find the equations of both tangent lines.

Extra credit. Pick two distinct points \(\;Q=(x_0,y_0)\;\) and \(\;R=(x_1,y_1)\;\) on the same parabola. Their tangents meet at \(\;S=(x_2,y_2)\;\). Prove that the vertical line through \(S\) bisects the chord \(QR\), i.e. \[ 2x_2=x_0+x_1. \]

2) List the information

Given

  • Parabola: \(\;y=x^2+2\)
  • External point: \(\;P=(0,1)\)

Unknowns

  • Equations of the two tangent lines through \(P\)
  • (Extra credit) The relationship between \(x_2\) (tangent intersection) and \(x_0,x_1\) (tangency x-coordinates)

What must be found / proved

  • Find both tangent lines to \(\;y=x^2+2\;\) that pass through \((0,1)\).
  • (Extra credit) Prove \(\;2x_2=x_0+x_1\;\) when tangents at \(Q\) and \(R\) meet at \(S\).

3) Relevant principles / laws (and why they apply)

  • Derivative = slope of tangent. For \(\;y=f(x)\;\), the slope at \(\;x=a\;\) is \(\;f'(a)\;\). This is the mathematical definition of tangency: the tangent line matches the curve’s instantaneous slope.
  • Point–slope form. A tangent at \(\;(a,f(a))\;\) is \[ y-f(a)=f'(a)(x-a). \]
  • Pass-through constraint. If the tangent must go through \(\;P=(0,1)\;\), substitute \(x=0,y=1\) into the tangent equation to solve for the tangency location(s).

Physics intuition: tangency is a local linearization (like approximating a potential near a point).

4) Possible approaches (2–3) and comparison

  • Parameter (tangency point) method (recommended): choose a tangency \(x=a\), write the tangent via derivative, force it to pass through \(P\), solve for \(a\).
  • Slope–intercept + discriminant method: any line through \(P\) is \(y=mx+1\). Substitute into the parabola and require a double intersection (discriminant \(=0\)).
  • Geometric parabola properties: possible but less direct in standard coordinates for this shifted parabola.

5) Best approach and why

Best choice: the tangency-parameter calculus method. It uses the core definition of tangency (slope = derivative), produces a clean tangent formula, and naturally yields two solutions.

PART 3 Full Solution

Physical intuition first

A tangent line is the best local straight-line approximation to the curve. The parabola \(\,y=x^2+2\,\) has its minimum at \((0,2)\), and the point \(P=(0,1)\) lies below that minimum. From such a point, you can “just touch” the parabola in two symmetric ways (left branch and right branch), so we expect two tangents.

A) Find both tangent lines through \(P=(0,1)\)

Step 1: Function and derivative

\[ f(x)=x^2+2, \qquad f'(x)=2x. \]

Step 2: Tangent line at \(x=a\)

The tangency point is \(\,(a,f(a))=(a,a^2+2)\,\) and the slope is \(\,m=f'(a)=2a\). Using point–slope form:

\[ y-(a^2+2)=2a(x-a). \]

Step 3: Enforce that the tangent passes through \(P=(0,1)\)

Substitute \(x=0\), \(y=1\):

\[ 1-(a^2+2)=2a(0-a). \]

Step 4: Solve for \(a\)

Simplify both sides carefully:

\[ 1-a^2-2=-a^2-1, \qquad 2a( -a)=-2a^2. \] \[ -a^2-1=-2a^2 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; a^2-1=0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; a=\pm 1. \]

Step 5: Write each tangent line

Tangent at \(a=1\)

Tangency point: \((1,3)\), slope \(m=2\).

\[ y-3 = 2(x-1) \;\Longrightarrow\; y = 2x+1. \]
Tangent at \(a=-1\)

Tangency point: \((-1,3)\), slope \(m=-2\).

\[ y-3 = -2(x+1) \;\Longrightarrow\; y = -2x+1. \]
Final answers
\[ \boxed{\,y=2x+1\,} \qquad\text{and}\qquad \boxed{\,y=-2x+1\,}. \]

Quick verification (touches + passes through \(P\))

  • Both lines pass through \((0,1)\): plug \(x=0\Rightarrow y=1\).
  • Tangency check: \[ 2x+1=x^2+2 \Rightarrow (x-1)^2=0 \quad\text{(double root)}, \] \[ -2x+1=x^2+2 \Rightarrow (x+1)^2=0 \quad\text{(double root)}. \]

Sanity checks

  • Units: pure math; derivative slope \(2a\) is consistent as “\(y/x\)”.
  • Limiting behavior: right branch slopes positive, left branch slopes negative; we got \(+2\) and \(-2\).

B) Extra credit — intersection of tangents bisects the chord (vertical midpoint property)

Setup

On the parabola \(y=x^2+2\), take \[ Q=(x_0, x_0^2+2), \quad R=(x_1, x_1^2+2), \quad x_0\neq x_1. \] Let the tangents at \(Q\) and \(R\) intersect at \(S=(x_2,y_2)\). Prove: \[ \boxed{\,2x_2=x_0+x_1\,}. \]

Step 1: Tangent line formula at \(x=x_0\) and \(x=x_1\)

Since \(f'(x)=2x\), the tangent at \(x=x_0\) is:

\[ y-(x_0^2+2)=2x_0(x-x_0) \;\Longrightarrow\; y=2x_0x-x_0^2+2. \]

Similarly at \(x=x_1\):

\[ y=2x_1x-x_1^2+2. \]

Step 2: Solve for the intersection \(S\)

At the intersection, the \(y\) values match, so:

\[ 2x_0x-x_0^2+2 = 2x_1x-x_1^2+2. \] \[ 2x_0x-2x_1x = x_0^2-x_1^2 \;\Longrightarrow\; 2(x_0-x_1)x = (x_0-x_1)(x_0+x_1). \]

Because \(x_0\neq x_1\), divide by \((x_0-x_1)\):

\[ 2x = x_0+x_1. \]

At the intersection, \(x=x_2\). Therefore:

\[ \boxed{\,2x_2=x_0+x_1\,}. \]

Geometric meaning

The chord midpoint of \(QR\) has x-coordinate \[ x_{\text{mid}}=\frac{x_0+x_1}{2}. \] The result says \(x_2=x_{\text{mid}}\), so the vertical line \(x=x_2\) passes through the midpoint of \(QR\), i.e. it bisects the chord.

Why the algebra is so clean: tangents to \(x^2\) have the special linear form \(\;y=2ax-a^2+\text{constant}\), so equating two tangents naturally produces an average \(\frac{x_0+x_1}{2}\).
