PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Conceptual setup

We’re given the curve \(y=x+\frac1x\) (defined for \(x\neq 0\)). The point \((0,4)\) is not on the curve, but lies on exactly one tangent line to it. We must find the tangency location \(x=a\) and then write the equation of that tangent line.

Given

  • Function: \(y(x)=x+\dfrac1x\), domain \(x\neq 0\).
  • External point: \((0,4)\).
  • Tangency point: \(\left(a,\;a+\dfrac1a\right)\).

Unknowns / Must find

  • Value \(a\) such that the tangent line at \(x=a\) passes through \((0,4)\).
  • The corresponding tangent line equation in explicit form.

Relevant principles (and why)

  • Derivative gives tangent slope: \(y'(a)\) is the slope of the tangent line at \(x=a\).
  • Point–slope line form: \(y-y(a)=y'(a)(x-a)\) constructs the tangent line at the contact point.
  • Point-on-line condition: if \((0,4)\) lies on the line, substituting \(x=0, y=4\) must satisfy it.

Approach A — Tangent equation + constraint

Differentiate, write tangent line at \(x=a\), then enforce it passes through \((0,4)\). Clean and standard.

Approach B — Slope matching

Tangent slope equals slope between \((0,4)\) and \(\left(a, y(a)\right)\). Equivalent to A, sometimes faster conceptually.

Best approach (chosen)

We use Approach A because it systematically produces both \(a\) and the line with minimal risk: compute \(y'(a)\), build the tangent line, then plug in \((0,4)\).

Common mistakes & quick tips

  • Domain: \(x=0\) is not allowed for the curve; \((0,4)\) is used only as a point on the line.
  • Derivative sign: \(\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac1x\right)=-\frac1{x^2}\).
  • Bookkeeping: keep \(y(a)=a+\frac1a\) and \(m=1-\frac1{a^2}\) clearly separated.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Derivation

Physical intuition

Think of the curve as a track and a tangent line as the instantaneous straight direction of motion at a point. We want the single line that “just kisses” the curve at \(x=a\) (same value and slope there) while also passing through \((0,4)\). The derivative gives the slope, and the pass-through condition pins down \(a\).

Step 1: Derivative (slope of the tangent)

\(y(x)=x+\dfrac1x \quad\Rightarrow\quad y'(x)=1-\dfrac1{x^2}\)

Therefore, the slope at \(x=a\) is

\(m=y'(a)=1-\dfrac1{a^2}\)

Step 2: Tangent line at \(\left(a, y(a)\right)\)

The point on the curve is \( \left(a,\;y(a)\right)=\left(a,\;a+\dfrac1a\right)\). Using point–slope form:

\(y-\left(a+\dfrac1a\right)=\left(1-\dfrac1{a^2}\right)(x-a)\)

Step 3: Enforce that \((0,4)\) lies on the tangent line

Substitute \(x=0\) and \(y=4\) into the tangent-line equation:

\(4-\left(a+\dfrac1a\right)=\left(1-\dfrac1{a^2}\right)(0-a)\)

Simplify the right-hand side:

\(\left(1-\dfrac1{a^2}\right)(-a)=-a+\dfrac1a\)

So we have:

\(4-a-\dfrac1a=-a+\dfrac1a\)

Add \(a\) to both sides (this cancels the \(-a\) terms cleanly):

\(4-\dfrac1a=\dfrac1a\)

Add \(\dfrac1a\) to both sides:

\(4=\dfrac{2}{a}\)

Solve for \(a\):

\(a=\dfrac{2}{4}=\dfrac12\)

Final answers (boxed)

Complete
\(\boxed{a=\tfrac12}\)
\(\boxed{y=-3x+4}\)

To get the line, compute the slope at \(a=\tfrac12\): \(m=1-\dfrac1{(1/2)^2}=1-4=-3\). Since the line passes through \((0,4)\), the intercept is \(4\), so \(y=-3x+4\).

Sanity checks

✓

Point check (touch point):
\(y(a)=\tfrac12+2=\tfrac52\). Line at \(x=\tfrac12\) gives \(y=-3(\tfrac12)+4=\tfrac52\). Matches.

✓

Slope check (true tangency):
\(y'(x)=1-\tfrac1{x^2}\Rightarrow y'(\tfrac12)=1-4=-3\), which equals the line slope.

✓

Pass-through check:
Plug \(x=0\) into \(y=-3x+4\) gives \(y=4\). So \((0,4)\) lies on the tangent line.

∿

Units & interpretation:
Pure math problem → no physical units. Geometrically, \((0,4)\) defines a unique “kissing” line to the curve.
