Problem 5 — Crash Test Motion (Piecewise Position)

A car accelerates for 5 seconds, then brakes until it hits a barrier. We enforce continuity of position and velocity at the switching time and compute the impact speed at \(s=33\text{ m}\).

Given: \(a_1=2\,\mathrm{m/s^2}\) for \(0\le t<5\)
Given: \(a_2=-4\,\mathrm{m/s^2}\) for \(t\ge 5\)
Barrier: \(s=33\,\mathrm{m}\)
Model: \(s(t)=\begin{cases}t^2,&0\le t<5\\-2t^2+At+B,&t\ge 5\end{cases}\)

1 PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Restatement translate → physics

The car starts from rest and follows a piecewise position function: it accelerates (concave up) until \(t=5\text{ s}\), then brakes (concave down). Find \(A,B\) so both position and velocity are continuous at \(t=5\). Then find the car’s velocity when it reaches the barrier at \(s=33\text{ m}\).

Giveninputs

  • Piecewise position: \[ s(t)=\begin{cases} t^2,& 0\le t<5\\ -2t^2+At+B,& t\ge 5 \end{cases} \]
  • Continuity at \(t=5\): \(s\) and \(s'(t)\) are continuous.
  • Barrier location: \(s=33\,\mathrm{m}\).

Unknownstargets

  • Constants \(A\) and \(B\).
  • Impact time \(t_{\text{hit}}\) (implicitly).
  • Impact velocity \(v_{\text{hit}}\).

Physics & Whyprinciples

  • Constant-acceleration kinematics: each phase has constant acceleration, so \(s(t)\) is quadratic and \(v(t)=s'(t)\) is linear.
  • Continuity of motion: position cannot jump (no teleporting), and velocity cannot jump at a finite switching instant; only acceleration changes abruptly.

Approachescompare

  • Calculus on the given \(s(t)\): differentiate to get \(v(t)\), apply continuity at \(t=5\), solve \(s(t)=33\), evaluate \(v\).
  • Pure kinematics formulas: compute \(s,v\) in each phase from \(a\), match at \(t=5\), then solve for barrier time.
  • Graph/monotonic reasoning: solve the quadratic, then choose the earliest root after \(t=5\).

Best choice: use the given \(s(t)\) directly — it minimizes setup and makes continuity conditions purely algebraic.

2 PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Differentiate to get velocity in each phase

    Goal: obtain \(v_1(t), v_2(t)\).
    Tool: \(v(t)=s'(t)\).

  2. Apply position continuity at \(t=5\)

    Goal: no jump in location.
    Tool: set \(s_1(5)=s_2(5)\).

  3. Apply velocity continuity at \(t=5\)

    Goal: no jump in speed.
    Tool: set \(v_1(5)=v_2(5)\).

  4. Solve for \(A\) and \(B\)

    Goal: fully determine the braking-phase model.
    Tool: solve two equations in two unknowns.

  5. Solve \(s(t)=33\) for \(t\ge 5\)

    Goal: find impact time candidates.
    Tool: quadratic equation / factoring.

  6. Pick the physically correct root

    Goal: choose the earliest time after braking begins.
    Tool: time ordering + physical meaning.

  7. Evaluate impact velocity

    Goal: compute \(v_{\text{hit}}=v_2(t_{\text{hit}})\).
    Tool: substitution into \(v_2(t)\).

Mistakes to avoidtips

  • Solving \(s(t)=33\) using the wrong branch (must use \(t\ge 5\)).
  • Forgetting that velocity is \(s'(t)\), not \(s(t)\).
  • Choosing the later root (the car hits the barrier at the first valid time).
  • Sign errors: braking means acceleration is negative → velocity decreases after \(t=5\).

3 PART 3 — Full Solution

Physical intuitionsense-making

From \(0\) to \(5\) seconds the car speeds up smoothly, so \(s(t)\) rises like a concave-up quadratic and \(v(t)\) increases linearly. After \(t=5\) the car brakes with constant negative acceleration, so \(v(t)\) must decrease linearly. At the switching moment, position and velocity cannot jump—only acceleration changes.

(a) Determine \(A\) and \(B\) from continuity at \(t=5\)

For \(0\le t<5\), \[ s_1(t)=t^2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad v_1(t)=s_1'(t)=2t. \] For \(t\ge 5\), \[ s_2(t)=-2t^2+At+B \quad\Rightarrow\quad v_2(t)=s_2'(t)=-4t+A. \]

Position continuity at \(t=5\): \(s_1(5)=s_2(5)\).

\[ s_1(5)=25,\qquad s_2(5)=-2(5^2)+5A+B=-50+5A+B. \] \[ -50+5A+B=25\quad\Rightarrow\quad 5A+B=75. \]

Velocity continuity at \(t=5\): \(v_1(5)=v_2(5)\).

\[ v_1(5)=10,\qquad v_2(5)=-4(5)+A=-20+A. \] \[ -20+A=10\quad\Rightarrow\quad A=30. \]

Substitute \(A=30\) into \(5A+B=75\) to solve for \(B\).

\[ 5(30)+B=75 \Rightarrow 150+B=75 \Rightarrow B=-75. \]
RESULT (a) continuity @ 5s
\[ \boxed{A=30,\quad B=-75} \] Hence, for \(t\ge 5\): \[ s_2(t)= -2t^2+30t-75,\qquad v_2(t)=-4t+30. \]

(b) Velocity at impact when \(s=33\text{ m}\)

Since \(s(5)=25\text{ m}\) and the barrier is at \(33\text{ m}\), the hit occurs after braking begins, so use \(s_2(t)\) with \(t\ge 5\):

\[ -2t^2+30t-75 = 33 \] \[ -2t^2+30t-108=0 \] \[ t^2-15t+54=0 \] \[ (t-6)(t-9)=0 \Rightarrow t=6\ \text{or}\ t=9. \]

The physically relevant impact time is the earliest valid time after \(t=5\): \(\;t_{\text{hit}}=6\text{ s}\).

Now evaluate the braking-phase velocity \(v_2(t)=-4t+30\) at \(t=6\).

\[ v_{\text{hit}}=v_2(6)=-4(6)+30=-24+30=6\ \text{m/s}. \]
RESULT (b) impact speed
\[ \boxed{t_{\text{hit}}=6\ \text{s},\qquad v_{\text{hit}}=6\ \text{m/s}} \]

Sanity checks

Unitsdimensional check

  • \(v(t)=s'(t)\) has units of \(\text{m/s}\). The final \(v_{\text{hit}}=6\,\text{m/s}\) is consistent.
  • At \(t=5\): \(s_1(5)=25\text{ m}\) matches \(s_2(5)=25\text{ m}\). Velocity also matches: \(v_1(5)=10\text{ m/s}\) and \(v_2(5)=10\text{ m/s}\).

Limiting casesbehavior

  • Immediately after braking starts, velocity should decrease at \(4\,\text{m/s}\) per second: from \(t=5\) to \(6\), \(10\to 6\ \text{m/s}\) — exactly as computed.
  • The second root \(t=9\) corresponds to reaching \(s=33\text{ m}\) again later (on the way back in the polynomial model). The actual “strike” happens at the first reach time \(t=6\text{ s}\).

Physical interpretationmeaning

The car begins braking at \(t=5\) s with speed \(10\) m/s, but the barrier is close enough that it still hits it one second later while moving forward at \(6\) m/s.
