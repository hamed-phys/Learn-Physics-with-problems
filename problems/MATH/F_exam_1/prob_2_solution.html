PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

We clarify what’s asked, what’s given, which math/physics ideas apply, and pick the cleanest method.

1) Restate the problem

You’re given the function \( f(x)=e^{-x^2/2} \). Compute its first, second, and third derivatives: \(f'(x)\), \(f''(x)\), and \(f^{(3)}(x)\). (Extra credit: prove \(f^{(n)}(x)=p_n(x)\,f(x)\) and give a recurrence.)

2) Given, unknowns, and goal

  • Given: \( f(x)=e^{-x^2/2} \)
  • Unknowns: \( f'(x), f''(x), f^{(3)}(x)\)
  • Goal: Differentiate correctly and simplify in a clear, reusable form.

3) Relevant principles (and why)

Chain rule applies because the exponent is a function of \(x\): \(-x^2/2\).
Product rule becomes essential after the first derivative because the result naturally becomes “polynomial × exponential.”
Gaussian structure: differentiating a Gaussian yields the same Gaussian times a polynomial, a pattern connected to Hermite polynomials and the quantum harmonic oscillator.

Chain rule Product rule Factoring Pattern recognition

4) Candidate approaches

  1. Direct differentiation each time (chain rule + product rule): straightforward for 1–3 derivatives.
  2. Factor-out method: keep \(e^{-x^2/2}\) factored and only evolve the polynomial prefactor—cleanest.
  3. General recurrence (extra credit): derive \(p_{n+1}\) from \(p_n\) to generate any order derivative.

5) Best approach

We choose the factor-out method because it minimizes algebra clutter and reduces sign mistakes: once you know \(f'(x)=-x f(x)\), higher derivatives become systematic.

PART 3 — Full Solution

We start with intuition, then derive \(f'(x)\), \(f''(x)\), and \(f^{(3)}(x)\) step-by-step, and box the result.

Physical intuition

A Gaussian \(e^{-x^2/2}\) is a “localized envelope” that decays very fast as \(|x|\) grows. Differentiation doesn’t destroy this envelope; instead it “pulls down” powers of \(x\) from the exponent. That’s why every derivative looks like:

\(\displaystyle f^{(n)}(x) = \big(\text{polynomial in }x\big)\,e^{-x^2/2}\)

1) First derivative \(f'(x)\)

Let \(g(x)=-x^2/2\). Then \(f(x)=e^{g(x)}\). By the chain rule:

\(\displaystyle g'(x)=\frac{d}{dx}\left(-\frac{x^2}{2}\right)=-x\) \(\displaystyle f'(x)=g'(x)e^{g(x)}=-x\,e^{-x^2/2}\) Key identity: \(\displaystyle f'(x)=-x\,f(x)\)

2) Second derivative \(f''(x)\)

Start from \(f'(x)=-x e^{-x^2/2}\) and apply the product rule:

\(\displaystyle f''(x)=\frac{d}{dx}\Big[-x\cdot e^{-x^2/2}\Big]\) \(\displaystyle f''(x)=(-1)e^{-x^2/2}+(-x)\frac{d}{dx}\big(e^{-x^2/2}\big)\) \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\big(e^{-x^2/2}\big)=f'(x)=-x e^{-x^2/2}\) \(\displaystyle f''(x)=-e^{-x^2/2}+x^2 e^{-x^2/2}=(x^2-1)e^{-x^2/2}\)

3) Third derivative \(f^{(3)}(x)\)

Start from \(f''(x)=(x^2-1)e^{-x^2/2}\) and use the product rule again:

\(\displaystyle f^{(3)}(x)=\frac{d}{dx}\Big[(x^2-1)e^{-x^2/2}\Big]\) \(\displaystyle f^{(3)}(x)=(2x)e^{-x^2/2}+(x^2-1)\frac{d}{dx}\big(e^{-x^2/2}\big)\) \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\big(e^{-x^2/2}\big)=-x e^{-x^2/2}\) \(\displaystyle f^{(3)}(x)=\big[2x-x(x^2-1)\big]e^{-x^2/2}\) \(\displaystyle 2x-x(x^2-1)=2x-(x^3-x)=3x-x^3\) \(\displaystyle f^{(3)}(x)=(3x-x^3)e^{-x^2/2}\)

Final boxed answers

\(\displaystyle \boxed{\,f'(x)=-x\,e^{-x^2/2}\,}\) \(\displaystyle \boxed{\,f''(x)=(x^2-1)\,e^{-x^2/2}\,}\) \(\displaystyle \boxed{\,f^{(3)}(x)=(3x-x^3)\,e^{-x^2/2}\,}\)

Extra Credit — Prove \(f^{(n)}(x)=p_n(x)\,f(x)\) and get a recurrence

We show why the “Gaussian × polynomial” structure persists for every derivative.

Claim: for every integer \(n\ge 0\), \(\displaystyle f^{(n)}(x)=p_n(x)\,f(x)\), where \(p_n(x)\) is a polynomial.

Proof idea (induction) + recurrence

Base case: \(n=0\): \(f^{(0)}(x)=f(x)\Rightarrow p_0(x)=1\) (a polynomial).

Inductive step: assume \(f^{(n)}(x)=p_n(x)f(x)\). Differentiate:

\(\displaystyle f^{(n+1)}(x) = (p_n f)' = p_n'(x)f(x)+p_n(x)f'(x)\) \(\displaystyle \text{but } f'(x)=-x f(x)\) \(\displaystyle f^{(n+1)}(x)=\big(p_n'(x)-x p_n(x)\big)f(x)\) \(\displaystyle \boxed{\,p_{n+1}(x)=p_n'(x)-x\,p_n(x)\,}\) \(\displaystyle \boxed{\,p_0(x)=1\,}\)

This guarantees \(p_{n+1}\) is a polynomial if \(p_n\) is, completing the induction.

Check the first few polynomials: \(p_0=1\), \(p_1=-x\), \(p_2=x^2-1\), \(p_3=3x-x^3\) — matching the derivatives above.

Sanity checks

Units, limits, and the physics-style interpretation that builds intuition.

1) Units

The exponent \(-x^2/2\) must be dimensionless. So \(x\) is either dimensionless, or (in physical contexts) it’s really \(e^{-x^2/(2\sigma^2)}\) with a length scale \(\sigma\). Under nondimensional \(x\), polynomial factors like \((-x)\), \((x^2-1)\) are consistent.

2) Limiting behavior

As \(|x|\to\infty\), \(e^{-x^2/2}\to 0\) extremely fast, so \(f'(x), f''(x), f^{(3)}(x)\to 0\) as well, despite the polynomial factors. This matches the “localized envelope” intuition.

3) Physical interpretation

“Gaussian × polynomial” is the hallmark of many physics systems (e.g., the quantum harmonic oscillator): the Gaussian provides confinement/decay, while the polynomial shapes the oscillations and sign changes (nodes).
