Problem 4 — Find \( \dfrac{d\theta}{dt} \) when \(x=5\), \(y=5\)

\(Q_1=(x(t),0)\) moves with \(x'=-20\) m/s on the x-axis, \(Q_2=(0,y(t))\) moves with \(y'=+10\) m/s on the y-axis. Use \(\tan\theta\).

file: prob4.html

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Restate: A line segment from \(Q_1\) to \(Q_2\) forms angle \(\theta(t)\) at \(Q_1\). Given \(x'=-20\), \(y'=10\), find \(\theta'\) at \(x=y=5\).
Given:
  • \(Q_1=(x(t),0)\), \(x'(t)=-20\ \text{m/s}\)
  • \(Q_2=(0,y(t))\), \(y'(t)=+10\ \text{m/s}\)
  • Instant: \(x=5\ \text{m},\ y=5\ \text{m}\)
Unknown: \(\theta'(t)=\dfrac{d\theta}{dt}\) at that instant.
Why this works: Geometry gives \(\tan\theta=\dfrac{y}{x}\). Differentiate with respect to time (related rates) to link \(\theta'\) with \(x',y'\).

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Write \(\tan\theta=\dfrac{y}{x}\).
  2. Differentiate: \(\dfrac{d}{dt}(\tan\theta)=\sec^2\theta\ \theta'\).
  3. Differentiate \(\dfrac{y}{x}\) using quotient rule.
  4. Solve for \(\theta'\).
  5. Use \(\sec^2\theta=1+\tan^2\theta\) with \(\tan\theta=y/x\) (avoid computing \(\theta\)).
  6. Substitute \(x=5,y=5,x'=-20,y'=10\).
Common mistakes:
  • Sign error: \(x'=-20\) means \(x\) is shrinking.
  • Forgetting \(\sec^2\theta\) factor when differentiating \(\tan\theta\).

PART 3 — Full Solution

Intuition: \(y\) increases and \(x\) decreases, so the segment becomes steeper ⇒ \(\theta\) increases ⇒ \(\theta'>0\).
Geometry: \[ \tan\theta=\frac{y}{x}. \] Differentiate both sides w.r.t. time: \[ \frac{d}{dt}(\tan\theta)=\sec^2\theta\ \theta',\qquad \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)=\frac{x y' - y x'}{x^2}. \] Hence: \[ \sec^2\theta\ \theta'=\frac{x y' - y x'}{x^2}. \] Use \(\sec^2\theta=1+\tan^2\theta=1+\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)^2=\frac{x^2+y^2}{x^2}\). Multiply both sides by \( \frac{x^2}{x^2+y^2} \) to get: \[ \theta'=\frac{x y' - y x'}{x^2+y^2}. \] Now plug in \(x=5,\ y=5,\ x'=-20,\ y'=10\): \[ x y' - y x' = 5(10)-5(-20)=50+100=150, \] \[ x^2+y^2=25+25=50, \] \[ \theta'=\frac{150}{50}=3. \]
Final Answer
\[ \boxed{\left.\frac{d\theta}{dt}\right|_{x=5,y=5}=3\ \text{rad/s}} \]
Sanity checks:
  • Units: numerator \(\sim m\cdot(m/s)=m^2/s\), denominator \(\sim m^2\) ⇒ \(1/s\) ⇒ rad/s ✅
  • Sign: positive, matches “segment gets steeper” intuition ✅
