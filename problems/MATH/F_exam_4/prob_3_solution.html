Surface Area from Rotating \(y=x^3\) about the \(x\)-axis

We revolve the curve \(y=x^3\) (for \(0\le x\le 1\)) about the \(x\)-axis. Each tiny arc element \(ds\) sweeps a thin circular band with area \(dS=(2\pi y)\,ds\). Below is a complete, structured solution: analysis → roadmap → full derivation → sanity checks.

Complete Solution (Structured)

Problem 3 • 25 pts
PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)
Restatement. Rotate the curve \(y=x^3\) on \([0,1]\) about the \(x\)-axis and compute the resulting surface area \(S\).

Given:

  • Curve: \(y=x^3\)
  • Interval: \(0\le x\le 1\)
  • Axis of revolution: \(x\)-axis

Unknown: surface area \(S\).

What must be found: an exact expression for \(S\) (and checks).

Principles (why):

  • Surface of revolution: a small arc segment \(ds\) sweeps a circular band whose circumference is \(2\pi y\), giving \(dS=(2\pi y)\,ds\).
  • Arc length differential: \(ds=\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\,dx\), accounting for the curve’s slope.

Approaches:

  • Direct formula (best): \(S=2\pi\int_0^1 y\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\,dx\).
  • Parametric: \(x=t,\;y=t^3\Rightarrow S=2\pi\int y\sqrt{(x')^2+(y')^2}\,dt\).
  • Frustum limit: approximate by truncated cones (intuitive but longer).
Chosen method: Use the direct surface-of-revolution formula because the curve is already a function \(y=f(x)\) and the integral simplifies cleanly with a single substitution.
PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Roadmap (no algebra yet):

  1. Set the surface area integral. Use \(S=2\pi\int_0^1 y\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\,dx\).
  2. Differentiate \(y=x^3\). Compute \(y'\) via the power rule.
  3. Assemble the integrand. Insert \(y\) and \(y'\) into \(\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\).
  4. Spot the substitution. Choose \(u=1+9x^4\) so that \(du\propto x^3dx\).
  5. Convert limits and integrate. Turn the integral into \(\int u^{1/2}\,du\).
  6. Simplify the exact expression. Write the final answer in radicals (e.g., \(10\sqrt{10}\)).
  7. Sanity checks. Units, comparison to \(\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\approx1\), interpretation.
Common mistakes:
  • Forgetting the factor \(\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\) (slope stretch).
  • Using the wrong radius (about \(x\)-axis ⇒ radius is \(y\), not \(x\)).
  • Derivative slip: \(y'=3x^2\Rightarrow (y')^2=9x^4\).
  • Picking a substitution that doesn’t match the \(x^3dx\) outside.
PART 3 — Full Solution
Intuition. A tiny arc segment of length \(ds\) at height \(y\) rotates into a thin band whose circumference is \(2\pi y\). Therefore \(dS=(2\pi y)\,ds\). Summing over the curve gives \(S=2\pi\int y\,ds\), and \(ds=\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\,dx\) accounts for the slope.

Step 1 — Write the surface area formula

\[ S = 2\pi\int_{0}^{1} y\,\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2}\,dx. \]

Step 2 — Substitute \(y=x^3\) and compute \(y'\)

\[ y=x^3 \quad\Rightarrow\quad y'=\frac{dy}{dx}=3x^2, \qquad (y')^2=9x^4. \]

Step 3 — Assemble the integral

\[ S = 2\pi\int_{0}^{1} x^3\,\sqrt{1+9x^4}\,dx. \]

Step 4 — Use a substitution

Choose \(u=1+9x^4\) because its differential produces the needed \(x^3dx\).

\[ u=1+9x^4 \quad\Rightarrow\quad du = 36x^3\,dx \quad\Rightarrow\quad x^3dx=\frac{1}{36}\,du. \]

Step 5 — Change limits

\[ x=0 \Rightarrow u=1,\qquad x=1 \Rightarrow u=10. \]

Step 6 — Integrate in \(u\)

\[ S = 2\pi\int_{1}^{10}\sqrt{u}\left(\frac{1}{36}\right)\,du = \frac{2\pi}{36}\int_{1}^{10}u^{1/2}\,du. \]

Step 7 — Evaluate the antiderivative

\[ \int u^{1/2}\,du=\frac{2}{3}u^{3/2} \quad\Rightarrow\quad S=\frac{2\pi}{36}\left[\frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}\right]_{1}^{10}. \]

Step 8 — Simplify

\[ \frac{2\pi}{36}\cdot\frac{2}{3}=\frac{4\pi}{108}=\frac{\pi}{27}, \qquad 10^{3/2}=10\sqrt{10},\quad 1^{3/2}=1. \]
Final Result exact closed form
\[ \boxed{\,S=\frac{\pi}{27}\left(10\sqrt{10}-1\right)\,} \]

Sanity Checks

Units: \(dS=(2\pi y)\,ds\) has units (length)\(\times\)(length) = (length\(^2\)). So \(S\) is an area, as required.
Limiting / comparison check: If the slope were negligible, \(\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\approx 1\), giving the underestimate \[ S \approx 2\pi\int_0^1 x^3dx = \frac{\pi}{2}\approx 1.57. \] Our exact result is larger (the slope increases with \(x\)), consistent with geometric “stretching.”
Physical interpretation: The factor \(\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\) measures how much longer the curve is than its horizontal projection. Larger slope near \(x=1\) contributes more surface area than near \(x=0\).
