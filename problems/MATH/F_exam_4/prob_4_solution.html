Sketch the Polar Curve: \(r(\theta)=\sin\theta\,\sin(\theta+\pi/2)\)

We analyze the curve only for \(0\le\theta\le\pi\) (half a revolution), then label the graph: quadrants, endpoints, endpoint tangents, and where \(r(\theta)\) is maximal.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Conceptual Setup
Restate the task
Given \(r(\theta)=\sin\theta\,\sin(\theta+\pi/2)\) for \(0\le\theta\le\pi\), sketch the curve and label:
  • quadrant(s) containing the curve
  • endpoints (for \(\theta=0\) and \(\theta=\pi\))
  • slopes of tangents at endpoints
  • angle(s) where \(r(\theta)\) is maximum
Given / Unknown / Find
  • Given: \(r(\theta)=\sin\theta\,\sin(\theta+\pi/2)\), \(0\le\theta\le\pi\).
  • Unknowns: shape/location in the plane, key labeled features.
  • Find: quadrant(s), endpoints, endpoint tangent slopes, maximizing \(\theta\).
Relevant math tools (and why)
  • Trig identity: simplifies \(r(\theta)\) so features become obvious.
  • Polar → Cartesian: \(x=r\cos\theta,\;y=r\sin\theta\) to determine quadrants.
  • Polar slope formula: \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{r'\sin\theta+r\cos\theta}{r'\cos\theta-r\sin\theta}\) for tangents.
  • Maximization: maximize \(r(\theta)\) via derivative or sine-range reasoning.
Candidate approaches (compare quickly)
  • Simplify \(r\) then analyze signs + slope + max — fastest for sketching.
  • Implicit Cartesian form (eliminate \(\theta\)) — can be heavy algebra.
  • Parametric view \(x(\theta),y(\theta)\) — systematic for endpoints and tangents.

Best choice: Simplify first, then use sign analysis and polar slope — minimal algebra, maximal insight.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Plan (no algebra)

Simplify \(r(\theta)\)

Goal: reveal structure for zeros/max. Tool: trig identity \(\sin(\theta+\pi/2)=\cos\theta\) (and optionally \(\sin 2\theta\)).

Find zeros & endpoints

Goal: locate origin crossings and interval endpoints. Tool: solve \(r(\theta)=0\) on \(0\le\theta\le\pi\).

Determine quadrant(s)

Goal: identify where the curve lives. Tool: \(x=r\cos\theta,\;y=r\sin\theta\) sign analysis.

Locate maximum radius

Goal: find where curve is farthest from origin. Tool: maximize simplified \(r(\theta)\) (derivative or range of sine).

Compute endpoint tangent slopes

Goal: label tangents at \(\theta=0\) and \(\theta=\pi\). Tool: polar slope formula \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{r'\sin\theta+r\cos\theta}{r'\cos\theta-r\sin\theta}\).

Sketch + label

Goal: draw the curve direction as \(\theta\) increases, annotate quadrants/points/tangents/max. Tool: key points + monotonic intuition.

Common pitfalls: (1) assuming \(0\to 2\pi\) instead of \(0\to\pi\), (2) ignoring that signs of \(x\) and \(y\) come from \(r\cos\theta\) and \(r\sin\theta\), (3) trying to get slope at the origin using naive \(\frac{dy/d\theta}{dx/d\theta}\) without care—polar slope formula is safer here.

PART 3 — Full Solution

Derivation + Final Labels
Intuition
The radius is a product of trig factors, so it vanishes at specific angles and reaches a smooth peak where the product is largest. Because we only allow \(0\le\theta\le\pi\), the curve traces only a half-turn, producing a right-side two-arc shape: one arc in Quadrant I and one in Quadrant IV, meeting at the origin.
Step A — Simplify \(r(\theta)\)
\[ r(\theta)=\sin\theta\,\sin\!\left(\theta+\frac{\pi}{2}\right) =\sin\theta\,\cos\theta =\frac12\sin(2\theta). \]
Step B — Endpoints and origin crossings
\[ r(0)=0 \Rightarrow (x,y)=(0,0),\qquad r(\pi)=0 \Rightarrow (x,y)=(0,0). \] Also, \[ r\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)=\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)=0 \Rightarrow (0,0). \]
So the curve starts at the origin, passes through the origin again at \(\theta=\pi/2\), and ends at the origin.
Step C — Quadrants (sign analysis)
Using \[ x=r\cos\theta,\quad y=r\sin\theta,\quad r=\sin\theta\cos\theta, \] we get \[ x(\theta)=\sin\theta\,\cos^2\theta \ge 0\quad\text{for }0\le\theta\le\pi \] because \(\sin\theta\ge0\) on \([0,\pi]\) and \(\cos^2\theta\ge0\) always. Next, \[ y(\theta)=\sin^2\theta\,\cos\theta. \] On \(0\le\theta\le\pi/2\), \(\cos\theta\ge0\Rightarrow y\ge0\) ⇒ Quadrant I. On \(\pi/2\le\theta\le\pi\), \(\cos\theta\le0\Rightarrow y\le0\) ⇒ Quadrant IV.
Step D — Maximum radius
Since \[ r(\theta)=\frac12\sin(2\theta), \] the maximum occurs when \(\sin(2\theta)=1\), i.e. \[ 2\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}\quad\Rightarrow\quad \theta=\frac{\pi}{4}. \] Then \[ r_{\max}=\frac12\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)=\frac12. \]
Step E — Slopes of endpoint tangents
Use the polar slope formula: \[ \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{r'\sin\theta+r\cos\theta}{r'\cos\theta-r\sin\theta}. \] First compute \(r'(\theta)\). With \(r=\sin\theta\cos\theta\), \[ r'(\theta)=\cos^2\theta-\sin^2\theta=\cos(2\theta). \]
At \(\theta=0\): \(r(0)=0\), \(r'(0)=1\), \(\sin0=0\), \(\cos0=1\)
\[ \left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{\theta=0} =\frac{(1)(0)+(0)(1)}{(1)(1)-(0)(0)}=0. \]
At \(\theta=\pi\): \(r(\pi)=0\), \(r'(\pi)=1\), \(\sin\pi=0\), \(\cos\pi=-1\)
\[ \left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{\theta=\pi} =\frac{(1)(0)+(0)(-1)}{(1)(-1)-(0)(0)}=0. \]
So both endpoint tangents at the origin are horizontal (slope \(0\)).

Final labeled results (put these on your sketch)

\(\displaystyle \boxed{r(\theta)=\sin\theta\cos\theta=\frac12\sin(2\theta),\quad 0\le\theta\le\pi}\)
\(\displaystyle \boxed{\text{Curve lies in Quadrant I and Quadrant IV (right half-plane), plus the origin.}}\)
\(\displaystyle \boxed{\theta=0\Rightarrow(0,0),\ \ \theta=\pi\Rightarrow(0,0)\ \ (\text{also } \theta=\pi/2\Rightarrow(0,0))}\)
\(\displaystyle \boxed{m(0)=0,\quad m(\pi)=0\ \ \text{(horizontal tangents at endpoints)}}\)
\(\displaystyle \boxed{\theta=\frac{\pi}{4}\ \text{gives}\ r_{\max}=\frac12}\)
Sanity checks (units • limits • interpretation)
  • Units: \(r\) is built from sines/cosines → dimensionless (consistent with a pure sketching problem).
  • Limits: near \(\theta=0\), \(r\approx\theta\) → starts smoothly at the origin; at \(\theta=\pi/2\), \(r=0\) → returns to origin mid-interval.
  • Interpretation: \(x(\theta)=\sin\theta\cos^2\theta\ge0\) ensures the curve stays in the right half-plane; sign of \(y\) follows \(\cos\theta\), splitting the trace into QI then QIV.
