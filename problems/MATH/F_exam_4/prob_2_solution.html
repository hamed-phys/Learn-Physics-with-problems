PART 1 Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Restate the problem

Form the region in the \(xy\)-plane bounded by \(y=0\), \(x=0\), \(x=a\), and \(y=\dfrac{ab}{x}-b\). Revolve this region about the \(y\)-axis and find the volume.

Given quantities

  • Constants \(a>0,\;b>0\)
  • Curve: \(y(x)=\dfrac{ab}{x}-b=b\left(\dfrac{a}{x}-1\right)\)
  • Bounds in \(x\): \(0\le x\le a\)
  • Lower boundary: \(y=0\)
  • Axis of rotation: \(y\)-axis

Unknowns / target

  • Unknown: volume \(V\)
  • Find: a closed form \(V(a,b)\)
Subtle point

As \(x\to 0^+\), \(y=\dfrac{ab}{x}-b\to \infty\). This looks “infinite,” but the volume can still be finite. We must check convergence properly.

Why shells / disks apply

The volume of a solid of revolution can be computed by integrating volumes of thin slices. Rotating around the \(y\)-axis, two standard models work: cylindrical shells (vertical slices, integrate \(dx\)) or disks/washers (horizontal slices, integrate \(dy\)).

Approaches comparison (shells vs disks vs centroid)
  • Shell method (integrate in \(x\)): uses the given function \(y(x)\) directly and the bounds are simply \(x\in[0,a]\).
  • Disk/washer method (integrate in \(y\)): requires solving \(x\) as a function of \(y\), and the upper \(y\)-limit becomes \(\infty\), making the algebra more involved.
  • Pappus (centroid theorem): elegant when the centroid is easy, but here the centroid of this region is not quick to compute.
Best choice

Choose shells. The region is naturally described by \(y(x)\) on \(0\le x\le a\), so the shell radius and height read off immediately. The “infinite” height near \(x=0\) is handled cleanly because the shell radius simultaneously goes to zero.

Common mistakes
  • Mixing up disk vs shell: for shells about the \(y\)-axis, the radius is \(x\), not \(y\).
  • Forgetting the factor \(2\pi\) from circumference.
  • Panicking at \(y\to\infty\) near \(x\to 0^+\) without checking the integrand.
Quick tips
  • Shell method: circumference \(\times\) height \(\times\) thickness.
  • Always simplify \(x\cdot y(x)\) before deciding whether the integral is improper.
  • At \(x=a\), the curve hits the \(x\)-axis (easy boundary check).

PART 3 Full Solution

Physical intuition

Think of the solid as built from thin cylindrical shells at position \(x\). Each shell wraps around the \(y\)-axis with circumference \(2\pi x\). The shell’s height is how far the region extends above the \(x\)-axis: \(h(x)=y(x)=\dfrac{ab}{x}-b\). Even though \(h(x)\to\infty\) as \(x\to 0^+\), the radius \(x\to 0\) simultaneously, and the combined volume contribution can remain finite.

Step-by-step derivation

\[ y(x)=\frac{ab}{x}-b=b\left(\frac{a}{x}-1\right) \]

1) Radius and height of a shell.
Rotating about the \(y\)-axis, a vertical strip at \(x\) forms a shell of radius \(\,r=x\). The region runs from \(y=0\) up to the curve, so the shell height is \(\,h(x)=y(x)=\dfrac{ab}{x}-b\).

\[ r(x)=x,\qquad h(x)=\frac{ab}{x}-b \]

2) Shell volume element.
The volume of a thin shell is: \[ dV = (2\pi r)\,h\,dx = 2\pi r(x)h(x)\,dx. \] Substitute \(r(x)=x\) and \(h(x)=\dfrac{ab}{x}-b\):

\[ dV = 2\pi\,x\left(\frac{ab}{x}-b\right)dx \]

3) Integrate across the region.
The region spans \(x\in[0,a]\), so:

\[ V=\int_{0}^{a}2\pi\,x\left(\frac{ab}{x}-b\right)\,dx \]

4) Handle the “infinite” behavior by simplifying the integrand.
Multiply inside: \[ x\left(\frac{ab}{x}-b\right)=ab-bx. \] This is finite at \(x=0\), so the integral converges.

\[ V=2\pi\int_{0}^{a}(ab-bx)\,dx \]

5) Evaluate the integral.
Factor out \(b\): \[ V=2\pi b\int_{0}^{a}(a-x)\,dx. \] Now compute: \[ \int_{0}^{a}(a-x)\,dx=\left[ax-\frac{x^{2}}{2}\right]_{0}^{a} =a^{2}-\frac{a^{2}}{2}=\frac{a^{2}}{2}. \]

\[ V=2\pi b\cdot\frac{a^{2}}{2}=\pi a^{2}b \]

Final Result

\[ \boxed{V=\pi a^{2}b} \]
Extra note: why “infinite height” can still give finite volume

Near \(x\to 0^+\), the height behaves like \(h(x)\sim \dfrac{ab}{x}\), which diverges. But the shell contribution involves \(2\pi x\cdot h(x)\sim 2\pi x\cdot \dfrac{ab}{x}=2\pi ab\), which stays finite. So the total volume remains finite.

CHECKS Sanity Checks

Units

\(a\) has units of length, \(b\) has units of length, so \(\pi a^2 b\) has units \(L^2\cdot L=L^3\), which is volume. ✅

Limiting cases

  • If \(b\to 0\): the curve collapses to \(y=0\), so \(V\to 0\). Our formula gives \(V\to 0\). ✅
  • If \(a\to 0\): the region width goes to 0, so \(V\to 0\). Our formula gives \(V\to 0\). ✅
Physical interpretation

The expression \(\pi a^2 b\) resembles “area of a disk of radius \(a\)” times “height \(b\)”. Even though the boundary curve is not a rectangle, the shell weighting makes the total volume equivalent to that simple-looking product.

Optional cross-check (disk method)

You can also solve with washers by expressing \(x\) in terms of \(y\) and integrating in \(y\), but the bounds involve \(y\to\infty\) and the algebra is longer. Shells are the cleanest here.
