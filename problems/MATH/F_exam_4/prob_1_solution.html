Volume of a Solid Formed by Revolving a Sine Arch

We revolve the region under \(y=b\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi x}{a}\right)\) from \(x=0\) to \(x=a\) about the \(x\)-axis. The result is a smooth “sine-shaped” solid whose cross-sections are circular disks.

Problem

A solid is formed by revolving about the \(x\)-axis the region bounded by the \(x\)-axis, the lines \(x=0\), \(x=a\), and the curve \[ y=b\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi x}{a}\right). \] Find the volume of the solid.

Given: \(a>0\), \(b>0\) Axis: \(x\)-axis Interval: \([0,a]\)

Key Idea

Rotating a nonnegative function \(y(x)\) about the \(x\)-axis gives circular cross-sections: \[ dV=\pi\,y(x)^2\,dx. \]

\[ V=\pi\int_{0}^{a}\Big[b\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi x}{a}\right)\Big]^2dx. \]

Hint: square introduces \(\sin^2\), so use \(\sin^2u=\dfrac{1-\cos(2u)}{2}\).

Analysis

Restatement

  • We consider the region under \(y=b\sin(\pi x/a)\) above \(y=0\), from \(x=0\) to \(x=a\).
  • We rotate that region around the \(x\)-axis to create a 3D solid.
  • We must compute the solid’s volume \(V\).

Given / Unknown

  • Given: \(y(x)=b\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi x}{a}\right)\), \(0\le x\le a\)
  • Axis: \(x\)-axis
  • Unknown: \(V\)
  • Goal: exact \(V(a,b)\)

Principles & Approaches

  • Disk/Washer method: rotation about \(x\)-axis makes circular disks of radius \(y(x)\), so area \(A(x)=\pi y(x)^2\).
  • Trig identity: \(\sin^2\) integrates cleanly using \(\sin^2u=\frac{1-\cos(2u)}{2}\).
  • Alternatives: average value of \(\sin^2\) (fast but needs justification), or Pappus (harder due to centroid).

Chosen method

Disk method + half-angle identity is the most direct, rigorous, and matches the hint. It uses one clean integral and standard trig simplification.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Roadmap

Express the volume as a sum of disk volumes.

Use Disk method: \(dV=\pi y(x)^2dx\), so \(V=\pi\int_0^a y(x)^2dx\).

Substitute the given curve into the integral.

Replace \(y(x)\) with \(b\sin(\pi x/a)\) to get an integral involving \(\sin^2\).

Convert \(\sin^2(\cdot)\) into a form you can integrate immediately.

Use \(\sin^2u=\dfrac{1-\cos(2u)}{2}\).

Integrate term-by-term on \([0,a]\).

Compute \(\int_0^a 1\,dx\) and \(\int_0^a \cos(2\pi x/a)\,dx\).

Evaluate the trig term at the bounds and simplify.

Use \(\sin(0)=0\), \(\sin(2\pi)=0\) to simplify quickly.

Check the result with units and limiting behavior.

Verify \(V\sim a\,b^2\) and that \(V\to0\) when \(a\to0\) or \(b\to0\).

Common mistakes: forgetting the factor \(\pi\); forgetting to square \(b\); using the wrong trig identity; integrating \(\cos(kx)\) but missing the \(1/k\) factor; evaluation errors at \(x=0,a\).

PART 3 — Full Solution

Derivation
Physical intuition Why disks?
When we rotate the region under the curve around the \(x\)-axis, a thin vertical slice at position \(x\) becomes a circular disk. The disk radius is the function value \(y(x)\), so its area is \(A(x)=\pi y(x)^2\). Adding all disks from \(0\) to \(a\) gives the volume. We also expect \(V\) to scale like \(a\) (length of the interval) and like \(b^2\) (area scales with radius squared).

Step 1 — Set up the disk-method integral

The radius of a typical disk at position \(x\) is \[ r(x)=y(x)=b\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi x}{a}\right). \] Hence the cross-sectional area is \[ A(x)=\pi r(x)^2=\pi b^2\sin^2\!\left(\frac{\pi x}{a}\right). \]

\[ V=\int_0^a A(x)\,dx =\pi b^2\int_0^a \sin^2\!\left(\frac{\pi x}{a}\right)\,dx. \]

Step 2 — Use a half-angle identity to integrate \(\sin^2\)

Use the identity \[ \sin^2 u=\frac{1-\cos(2u)}{2}. \] Let \(u=\dfrac{\pi x}{a}\). Then \[ \sin^2\!\left(\frac{\pi x}{a}\right)=\frac{1-\cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi x}{a}\right)}{2}. \]

\[ V =\pi b^2\int_0^a \frac{1-\cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi x}{a}\right)}{2}\,dx =\frac{\pi b^2}{2}\int_0^a \left[1-\cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi x}{a}\right)\right]dx. \]

Step 3 — Integrate and evaluate bounds

Split the integral: \[ \int_0^a \left[1-\cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi x}{a}\right)\right]dx =\int_0^a 1\,dx-\int_0^a \cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi x}{a}\right)dx. \] The first integral is \(a\).

For the second integral, use \(\int \cos(kx)\,dx=\frac{1}{k}\sin(kx)\) with \(k=\dfrac{2\pi}{a}\):

\[ \int_0^a \cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi x}{a}\right)dx =\left[\frac{a}{2\pi}\sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi x}{a}\right)\right]_0^a =\frac{a}{2\pi}\big(\sin(2\pi)-\sin(0)\big)=0. \]

Therefore the bracketed integral equals \(a\), so \[ V=\frac{\pi b^2}{2}\cdot a. \]

Final result

\[ \boxed{\,V=\frac{\pi a b^{2}}{2}\,} \]
Units

\(a\) has length, \(b\) has length. So \(ab^2\) has units \(L^3\), correct for volume.

Limiting cases

If \(b\to 0\), the radius goes to zero everywhere \(\Rightarrow V\to 0\). If \(a\to 0\), the interval collapses \(\Rightarrow V\to 0\).

Physical interpretation

\(\sin^2\) averages to \(1/2\) over this half-period, so the average disk area is \(\pi b^2/2\). Multiply by length \(a\) gives \(V=\frac{\pi b^2}{2}a\).

Optional: fast “average-value” shortcut (same result) Nice insight
Since \(\sin^2\) has average value \(1/2\) over a full period (and over symmetric half-periods here), \[ \int_0^a \sin^2\!\left(\frac{\pi x}{a}\right)\,dx=\frac{a}{2}. \] Then \(V=\pi b^2\cdot \frac{a}{2}\). This is correct, but the main solution above shows the full rigorous steps.
