CALC / METHODS • Problem 4 • Parameter \(a>0\)

Domain of \(\sqrt{2ax-x^2}\) & Its Antiderivative

We determine where the quadratic is nonnegative (so the square-root is real), then compute \(\displaystyle \int\sqrt{2ax-x^2}\,dx\) using completing-the-square and the standard \(\sqrt{R^2-u^2}\) integral.

① PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Understand • Model • Choose method

Problem statement (rewritten): Let \(a>0\). (a) Find all real \(x\) such that \(2ax-x^2\ge 0\). (b) On that \(x\)-range, compute the antiderivative \(\displaystyle \int \sqrt{2ax-x^2}\,dx\).

✓Given

\(a\in\mathbb{R}\), \(a>0\)

?Need to find

(a) domain where \(\,2ax-x^2\ge 0\)
(b) \(\,F(x)\) with \(F'(x)=\sqrt{2ax-x^2}\)

Relevant principles / tools (and why) ⌄
  • Quadratic sign analysis: the square root is real only when the inside is \(\ge 0\).
  • Completing the square: converts \(2ax-x^2\) into \(a^2-(x-a)^2\), a standard “circle” form.
  • Standard integral \(\int\sqrt{R^2-u^2}\,du\): produces an algebraic term plus an \(\arcsin\) term.
Candidate approaches (compare) ⌄
  • Factor + sign chart for (a), then complete square + standard formula for (b): fastest, least error-prone.
  • Trig substitution \(x-a=a\sin\theta\): systematic, but longer.
  • Geometry intuition (upper semicircle): great insight; still need calculus for a full antiderivative.

★Best method (chosen)

Use factoring/sign analysis to get the domain, then complete the square and apply the known \(\sqrt{R^2-u^2}\) antiderivative. It is clean, rigorous, and naturally enforces the “real integrand” range.

② PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Plan • Pitfalls • No algebra yet

  1. Make the square root real

    Solve \(2ax-x^2\ge 0\) to determine where the integrand exists in \(\mathbb{R}\). Tool: inequality / sign analysis

  2. Find the interval for \(x\)

    Factor \(2ax-x^2\) and use a sign chart to locate where it’s nonnegative. Tool: factoring

  3. Rewrite in circle form

    Complete the square to get \(a^2-(x-a)^2\), matching \(\sqrt{R^2-u^2}\). Tool: completing the square

  4. Reduce to a standard integral

    Substitute \(u=x-a\), turning the integral into \(\int\sqrt{a^2-u^2}\,du\). Tool: substitution

  5. Integrate using a known formula

    Apply the standard antiderivative for \(\int\sqrt{a^2-u^2}\,du\). Tool: standard result / trig sub

  6. Substitute back and state domain

    Replace \(u\to x-a\), keep the domain \(x\in[0,2a]\), and add \(+C\). Tool: back-substitution

  7. Sanity checks

    Verify units/scale, endpoint behavior, and differentiate mentally to confirm structure. Tool: checking

Common mistakes & quick tips ⌄
  • Domain oversight: you must enforce \(2ax-x^2\ge 0\) or \(\sqrt{\cdot}\) becomes imaginary.
  • Sign errors: remember \(2ax-x^2 = a^2-(x-a)^2\), not \((x-a)^2-a^2\).
  • Wrong inverse trig input: the arcsine argument must be \(\frac{u}{a}\) with \(u=x-a\).

③ PART 3 — Full Solution

Intuition • Derivation • Final + Checks
Physical / geometric intuition ⌄

Completing the square shows \[ 2ax-x^2 = a^2-(x-a)^2. \] Therefore \[ \sqrt{2ax-x^2}=\sqrt{a^2-(x-a)^2}, \] which is the upper semicircle of radius \(a\) centered at \(x=a\). That “circle geometry” is why the antiderivative contains an \(\arcsin\) term (circular segment area behavior).

(a) Domain where \(2ax-x^2\ge 0\) ⌄

Step 1: Start with the inequality ensuring the square root is real:

\[ 2ax-x^2\ge 0. \]

Step 2: Factor the quadratic:

\[ 2ax-x^2 = x(2a-x). \]

Step 3: A product is nonnegative when both factors have the same sign (or one is zero).

  • If \(x\ge 0\) and \(2a-x\ge 0\), then \(0\le x\le 2a\).
  • The alternative (\(x\le 0\) and \(2a-x\le 0\)) would force \(x\ge 2a\), impossible for \(a>0\).
Final (a) real integrand domain
\[ \boxed{\,2ax-x^2\ge 0 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad x\in[0,\,2a]\,} \]
(b) Compute \(\displaystyle \int\sqrt{2ax-x^2}\,dx\) on \(x\in[0,2a]\) ⌄

Step 1 (complete the square): Rewrite the quadratic in a circle form.

\[ 2ax-x^2 = -(x^2-2ax) = -(x^2-2ax+a^2)+a^2 = a^2-(x-a)^2. \]

So the integral becomes:

\[ \int\sqrt{2ax-x^2}\,dx = \int\sqrt{a^2-(x-a)^2}\,dx. \]

Step 2 (substitution): Let \(u=x-a\). Then \(du=dx\).

\[ \int\sqrt{a^2-(x-a)^2}\,dx =\int\sqrt{a^2-u^2}\,du. \]

Step 3 (standard integral): Use the known antiderivative

\[ \int\sqrt{a^2-u^2}\,du = \frac{u}{2}\sqrt{a^2-u^2} +\frac{a^2}{2}\arcsin\!\left(\frac{u}{a}\right)+C. \]

Step 4 (back-substitute): Replace \(u\to x-a\).

Final (b) antiderivative
\[ \boxed{ \int\sqrt{2ax-x^2}\,dx = \frac{x-a}{2}\sqrt{a^2-(x-a)^2} +\frac{a^2}{2}\arcsin\!\left(\frac{x-a}{a}\right)+C } \]

Since \(a^2-(x-a)^2 = 2ax-x^2\), an equivalent form is:

\[ \boxed{ \int\sqrt{2ax-x^2}\,dx = \frac{x-a}{2}\sqrt{2ax-x^2} +\frac{a^2}{2}\arcsin\!\left(\frac{x-a}{a}\right)+C, \quad x\in[0,2a]. } \]

✓Sanity check — units

If \(x\) has units of length, then \(a\) must also have length. Inside the root: \(2ax-x^2\sim L^2\), so the integrand \(\sim L\). Integrating over \(dx\sim L\) gives \(L^2\). Both terms in the result scale like \(a^2\) or \(x\sqrt{\cdot}\sim L^2\).

◎Sanity check — limiting/endpoints

On \(x\in[0,2a]\), the integrand vanishes at both endpoints: \(2a\cdot a - (2a)^2 = 0\) and \(0\) at \(x=0\). This matches the “upper semicircle” picture touching the axis at both ends.

◐Physical/geometric interpretation

\(\sqrt{a^2-(x-a)^2}\) describes the upper half of a circle of radius \(a\), centered at \(x=a\). The antiderivative accumulates area under that curve, hence the inverse-sine term from circular geometry.

Tip for your archive: This problem is a template: whenever you see \(\sqrt{\text{quadratic}}\), try completing the square first. If it becomes \(\sqrt{R^2-(x-h)^2}\), you’re in “circle mode.”
