Principles & why they apply

  • Improper rational integration: since \(\deg(x^3-1)>\deg(x^2-3x+2)\), divide first to rewrite as a polynomial plus a proper fraction.
  • Factoring: \(x^2-3x+2\) factors into linear terms, producing \(\frac{1}{x-a}\) structures that integrate to logarithms.
  • Linearity of the integral: integrate each term separately after simplification.

PART 3 ‚Äî Full Solution

Derivation

Physical intuition

For large \(|x|\), the integrand behaves like \(\dfrac{x^3}{x^2}\sim x\), so the antiderivative should grow like \(\sim \frac{x^2}{2}\). Also, because the denominator has real zeros, we expect logarithmic behavior near those points‚Äîtypical of integrating \(\frac{1}{x-a}\).

Step 1: Polynomial long division

Divide
We divide \(x^3-1\) by \(x^2-3x+2\) since the numerator has higher degree.
Goal: rewrite as ‚Äúpolynomial + proper fraction‚Äù.
1st term
\(\dfrac{x^3}{x^2}=x\). Multiply back: \[ x(x^2-3x+2)=x^3-3x^2+2x. \]
Subtract
\[ (x^3-1)-(x^3-3x^2+2x)=3x^2-2x-1. \]
2nd term
\(\dfrac{3x^2}{x^2}=3\). Multiply back: \[ 3(x^2-3x+2)=3x^2-9x+6. \]
Subtract
\[ (3x^2-2x-1)-(3x^2-9x+6)=7x-7. \] Therefore, \[ \frac{x^3-1}{x^2-3x+2}=x+3+\frac{7x-7}{x^2-3x+2}. \]

Step 2: Factor and simplify

Factor
\[ x^2-3x+2=(x-1)(x-2),\qquad 7x-7=7(x-1). \]
Cancel
\[ \frac{7x-7}{x^2-3x+2} =\frac{7(x-1)}{(x-1)(x-2)} =\frac{7}{x-2}. \]
Note: the original integrand is undefined at \(x=1\) and \(x=2\). Cancellation simplifies the algebra but does not remove the original domain restrictions.
Result
So the integrand becomes \[ \frac{x^3-1}{x^2-3x+2}=x+3+\frac{7}{x-2}. \]

Step 3: Integrate term-by-term

Split
\[ \int\left(x+3+\frac{7}{x-2}\right)\,dx =\int x\,dx+\int 3\,dx+\int \frac{7}{x-2}\,dx. \]
Basic
\[ \int x\,dx=\frac{x^2}{2},\qquad \int 3\,dx=3x. \]
Log
\[ \int \frac{7}{x-2}\,dx=7\ln|x-2|. \]

Final antiderivative

Sanity Checks (units ‚Ä¢ limits ‚Ä¢ interpretation)

Validation

Units

This is a pure-math integral with no explicit physical units given for \(x\). The result is dimensionally consistent in the mathematical sense: polynomials integrate to polynomials, and \(\frac{1}{x-a}\) integrates to \(\ln|x-a|\).

Limiting behavior

As \(|x|\to\infty\), \[ \frac{x^3-1}{x^2-3x+2}\sim x, \] so the antiderivative should scale like \(\sim \frac{x^2}{2}\). Our answer indeed has leading term \(\frac{x^2}{2}\).

Singular points & interpretation

The original integrand is undefined at \(x=1\) and \(x=2\). After simplification, we explicitly see a \(\frac{7}{x-2}\) term, so the antiderivative must contain a logarithmic divergence \(\ln|x-2|\) near \(x=2\), which it does.

In ‚Äúphysics language,‚Äù a \(\frac{1}{x-a}\) behavior in a rate-like function produces a logarithmic accumulation‚Äîcommon near resonance-like or pole-like structures.
