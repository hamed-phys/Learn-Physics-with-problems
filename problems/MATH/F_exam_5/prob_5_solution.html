Problem 5 — Derivatives of \(\sinh\), \(\sinh^{-1}\), and \(\sin(\tan^{-1}(2x))\)

A physics-tutor style solution in three parts: Problem Analysis, Strategy & Tips, and Full Solution with boxed answers and sanity checks (units, limits, interpretation).

PART 1 — Problem Analysis no solving yet

1) Restate the problem

Compute derivatives for the following functions:

\[ \text{(a)}\quad y=\sinh(t)=\frac{e^{t}-e^{-t}}{2}\quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{dy}{dt}\;? \] \[ \text{(b)}\quad y=\sinh^{-1}(x)=\ln\!\bigl(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}\bigr)\quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{dy}{dx}\;? \] \[ \text{(Extra)}\quad y=\sin\!\bigl(\tan^{-1}(2x)\bigr)\quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{dy}{dx}\;? \]
Notation note
The sheet shows \(dy/dx\) in (a), but the independent variable is \(t\). We interpret that as \(\frac{dy}{dt}\).

2) Given / Unknowns / Goal

  • Given: definitions of \(\sinh\) and \(\sinh^{-1}\) (as a log expression), plus a trig–inverse trig composition.
  • Unknowns: \(\frac{dy}{dt}\) for (a), \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) for (b), and \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) (simplified) for the extra credit.
  • Goal: produce correct derivatives with clear reasoning and final simplified results.

3) Relevant principles (and why)

  • Exponential derivatives because \(\sinh\) is built from \(e^{t}\) and \(e^{-t}\).
  • Linearity to differentiate sums and scalar multiples cleanly.
  • Chain rule for nested functions like \(\sin(\tan^{-1}(2x))\).
  • Log derivative rule and \(\sqrt{\cdot}\) chain rule (useful for the log form of \(\sinh^{-1}\)).
  • Hyperbolic identity \(\cosh^2y-\sinh^2y=1\) to rewrite \(\cosh(y)\) in terms of \(x\) when using implicit differentiation.

4) Candidate approaches

  • Direct differentiation from definitions: best for (a).
  • Differentiate the log form: works for (b) but can be algebra-heavy.
  • Implicit differentiation via inverse: often the cleanest for (b).
  • Triangle identity first: best for simplifying \(\sin(\arctan(\cdot))\) before differentiating (extra credit).

5) Best approach chosen

(a) Direct exponential differentiation. (b) Implicit inverse-function method + hyperbolic identity. (Extra) Convert \(\sin(\arctan(2x))\) to an algebraic form using a right triangle, then differentiate.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips roadmap only

Minimal roadmap (no algebra)

  1. (a) Expand \(\sinh(t)\) into exponentials
    Goal: set up a derivative you can do instantly. Tool: \(\sinh(t)=\frac{e^t-e^{-t}}{2}\).
  2. (a) Differentiate term-by-term
    Goal: compute \(\frac{dy}{dt}\). Tool: \((e^t)'=e^t\), \((e^{-t})'=-e^{-t}\), linearity.
  3. (b) Use inverse relationship
    Goal: avoid messy log algebra. Tool: \(y=\sinh^{-1}(x)\iff x=\sinh(y)\).
  4. (b) Differentiate implicitly
    Goal: isolate \(\frac{dy}{dx}\). Tool: chain rule; \(\frac{d}{dy}\sinh(y)=\cosh(y)\).
  5. (b) Eliminate \(y\) using an identity
    Goal: write everything in terms of \(x\). Tool: \(\cosh^2y-\sinh^2y=1\).
  6. (Extra) Convert \(\sin(\arctan(2x))\) to algebra
    Goal: simplest-term derivative. Tool: right-triangle identity \(\sin(\arctan u)=\frac{u}{\sqrt{1+u^2}}\).
  7. (Extra) Differentiate the algebraic form
    Goal: compute \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) cleanly. Tool: product/chain rule and factor simplification.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting the chain-rule minus sign in \(\frac{d}{dt}e^{-t}\).
  • Mixing variables: (a) is \(\frac{dy}{dt}\), not \(\frac{dy}{dx}\).
  • Not simplifying the extra-credit expression first (leads to messy derivatives).
Quick tips
  • \(\sinh\) differentiates into \(\cosh\) just like \(\sin\) differentiates into \(\cos\).
  • When you see \(\arctan(\cdot)\), consider triangle identities for simplification.
  • Always check limits: small input should match linear approximations.

PART 3 — Full Solution derivations + checks

Physical intuition (why these show up in physics)

Hyperbolic functions appear in many physics models (e.g., catenary shapes, relativistic rapidity, and solutions of ODEs like \(y''=y\)). Their derivatives “pair up”: \(\sinh \leftrightarrow \cosh\), analogous to \(\sin \leftrightarrow \cos\). Inverse hyperbolic functions often arise from integrals like \(\int dx/\sqrt{x^2+1}\). The extra-credit expression saturates to \(\pm 1\), so its derivative must decay at large \(|x|\).

(a) Differentiate \(y=\sinh(t)=\dfrac{e^{t}-e^{-t}}{2}\)

Start from the exponential definition and differentiate directly.

\[ y=\frac{e^{t}-e^{-t}}{2} \]

Differentiate term-by-term (linearity):

\[ \frac{dy}{dt} =\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{d}{dt}e^{t}-\frac{d}{dt}e^{-t}\right) \]

Compute each derivative: \(\frac{d}{dt}e^t=e^t\) and \(\frac{d}{dt}e^{-t}=-e^{-t}\).

\[ \frac{dy}{dt} =\frac{1}{2}\left(e^{t}-(-e^{-t})\right) =\frac{1}{2}\left(e^{t}+e^{-t}\right) \]

Recognize \(\cosh(t)=\frac{e^{t}+e^{-t}}{2}\).

Final (a)
\[ \boxed{\frac{dy}{dt}=\cosh(t)} \]
Sanity checks
  • Units: exponent arguments must be dimensionless; \(\sinh\) and \(\cosh\) are dimensionless.
  • Limit \(t\to 0\): \(\sinh(t)\approx t\Rightarrow dy/dt\approx 1\). Also \(\cosh(0)=1\).
  • Interpretation: for large \(t\), both \(\sinh\) and \(\cosh\) grow like \(\sim e^t/2\), consistent with “exponential-like” slope.

(b) Differentiate \(y=\sinh^{-1}(x)=\ln\!\bigl(x+\sqrt{x^2+1}\bigr)\)

Although the log form can be differentiated directly, the inverse-function method is cleaner: if \(y=\sinh^{-1}(x)\), then \(x=\sinh(y)\).

\[ y=\sinh^{-1}(x)\quad \Longleftrightarrow\quad x=\sinh(y) \]

Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\):

\[ \frac{d}{dx}(x)=\frac{d}{dx}\big(\sinh(y)\big) \]

Left side is \(1\). Right side uses chain rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}\sinh(y)=\cosh(y)\frac{dy}{dx}\).

\[ 1=\cosh(y)\frac{dy}{dx} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{1}{\cosh(y)} \]

Now eliminate \(y\) using \(\cosh^2y-\sinh^2y=1\). Since \(x=\sinh(y)\), we have \(\sinh^2(y)=x^2\).

\[ \cosh^2(y)=1+\sinh^2(y)=1+x^2 \] \[ \Rightarrow\quad \cosh(y)=\sqrt{1+x^2} \quad (\cosh(y)\ge 1 \text{ for real } y) \]
Final (b)
\[ \boxed{\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+x^2}}} \]
Sanity checks
  • Limit \(x\to 0\): \(\sinh^{-1}(x)\approx x\Rightarrow dy/dx\approx 1\). Formula gives \(1/\sqrt{1+0}=1\).
  • Large \(|x|\): \(\sinh^{-1}(x)\sim \ln(2|x|)\Rightarrow dy/dx\sim 1/|x|\). Formula behaves like \(1/|x|\).
  • Physical interpretation: inverse hyperbolic sine grows slowly (log-like) at large \(|x|\), so slope decays toward \(0\).

Extra credit: Differentiate \(y=\sin(\tan^{-1}(2x))\) (simplest terms)

The simplest route is to turn \(\sin(\arctan(\cdot))\) into an algebraic expression first. Let \(\theta=\tan^{-1}(2x)\Rightarrow \tan\theta=2x\).

\[ \theta=\tan^{-1}(2x)\quad\Rightarrow\quad \tan\theta=2x \]

Use a right triangle: opposite \(=2x\), adjacent \(=1\), hypotenuse \(=\sqrt{1+(2x)^2}=\sqrt{1+4x^2}\). Then \(\sin\theta=\dfrac{2x}{\sqrt{1+4x^2}}\).

\[ y=\sin(\theta)=\frac{2x}{\sqrt{1+4x^2}} =2x(1+4x^2)^{-1/2} \]

Differentiate using product rule:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{d}{dx}\Big(2x(1+4x^2)^{-1/2}\Big) =2(1+4x^2)^{-1/2} +2x\frac{d}{dx}(1+4x^2)^{-1/2} \]

Now chain rule on \((1+4x^2)^{-1/2}\):

\[ \frac{d}{dx}(1+4x^2)^{-1/2} =-\frac{1}{2}(1+4x^2)^{-3/2}\cdot (8x) =-4x(1+4x^2)^{-3/2} \]

Substitute back and simplify:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} =2(1+4x^2)^{-1/2}-8x^2(1+4x^2)^{-3/2} \] \[ =\frac{2(1+4x^2)-8x^2}{(1+4x^2)^{3/2}} =\frac{2}{(1+4x^2)^{3/2}} \]
Final (Extra)
\[ \boxed{\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{2}{\left(1+4x^2\right)^{3/2}}} \]
Sanity checks
  • Limit \(x\to 0\): \(\tan^{-1}(2x)\approx 2x\), \(\sin(2x)\approx 2x\Rightarrow dy/dx\approx 2\). Formula gives \(2\).
  • Large \(|x|\): \(y\to \pm 1\) so slope \(\to 0\). Formula decays like \(1/|x|^3\to 0\).
  • Interpretation: saturation (bounded output) forces diminishing slope at large input magnitude.
