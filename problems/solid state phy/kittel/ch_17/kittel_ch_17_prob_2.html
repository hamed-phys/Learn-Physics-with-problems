Surface Subbands in the Electric Quantum Limit: Variational Ground State of a Triangular Well

MOSFET inversion-layer physics via applied quantum mechanics: a conduction electron near an interface sees a one-sided, linearly rising potential (a “triangular quantum well”).

Model: V(x)=eEx, x>0 Barrier: V=∞, x<0 Method: Variational principle Result: ε ∝ (eE)^(2/3)

Quick Summary

  • We model a MOS interface as a half-line quantum problem (x>0) with an infinite wall at x=0.
  • The electron experiences a triangular potential V(x)=eEx caused by a strong electric field E.
  • Governing eigenproblem: 1D Schrödinger ODE on x>0 with boundary condition u(0)=0.
  • Trial wavefunction: u(x)=x e−αx (enforces u(0)=0 and decay for x→∞).
  • Variational energy: ε(α)= (ħ²/2m)α² + (3eE)/(2α).
  • Minimizer: αmin = (3eEm/(2ħ²))1/3.
  • Ground-state estimate: εmin = 1.89 (ħ²/2m)1/3 (3eE/2)2/3 (symbolic; physically sets “subband” energy).
  • Industrial meaning: ε scales as E2/3, so gate field shifts subband energies → field-effect control of a 2D channel.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols & SI units)

  • x — coordinate normal to the interface (m), domain: x > 0 in the semiconductor.
  • E — electric field magnitude normal to the interface (V/m).
  • e — elementary charge (C). Here eE has units N/C · C = N, so eEx is energy (J).
  • m — (effective) electron mass in the semiconductor (kg).
  • ħ — reduced Planck constant (J·s).
  • V(x) — potential energy (J). Model: V(x)=eEx for x>0; V=∞ for x<0.
  • ψ(x,y,z) — wavefunction (SI: m−3/2 for 3D normalization).
  • u(x) — x-dependent envelope (SI: m−1/2 if normalized in 1D).
  • ky, kz — in-plane wave numbers (m−1), free motion along the surface.
  • ε — energy eigenvalue associated with x-motion (“subband bottom”) (J).

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Infinite barrier at x<0 models an interface that the electron cannot penetrate (e.g., oxide).
  • Linear potential eEx is the energy cost of moving away from the interface in a uniform field.
  • The electron is trapped near x=0 by the barrier and the rising potential, forming a quasi-2D channel.
  • Motion in y,z is free: states carry surface current even though no net current flows in x.

Governing law & validity conditions

Effective-mass Schrödinger model (single-electron, envelope approximation): we treat the electron as a quantum particle of mass m moving in a prescribed electrostatic potential V(x).

  • Assumes single-particle picture and slowly varying envelope (relative to lattice spacing).
  • Assumes the field is approximately uniform over the extent of u(x).
  • Assumes we can neglect many-body corrections and detailed bandstructure beyond an effective mass (good first model for MOS inversion layers).

Mathematical model (PDE → ODE via separability)

In a 3D effective-mass model with a potential depending only on x, the time-independent Schrödinger equation is

Key equation (operator form) 
[ -(ħ²/2m) ∇² + V(x) ] ψ(x,y,z) = E_total ψ(x,y,z)

Because V depends only on x, the Hamiltonian is invariant under translations in y and z, so we use a plane-wave separation (a Fourier eigenbasis in the in-plane coordinates):

Separation ansatz 
ψ(x,y,z) = u(x) exp[i(k_y y + k_z z)]

Plugging this into ∇² = d²/dx² + d²/dy² + d²/dz² gives ∂²/∂y² → −ky² and ∂²/∂z² → −kz². Therefore the x-envelope satisfies a 1D eigenvalue problem:

1D eigenvalue problem for the surface subbands 
-(ħ²/2m) u''(x) + V(x) u(x) = ε u(x),   x > 0
Boundary: u(0)=0 (infinite wall), u(x)→0 as x→∞
Math Insight (what kind of mathematical object is this?)

This is a Sturm–Liouville eigenvalue problem on the half-line x>0 with a confining potential. The operator H = −(ħ²/2m) d²/dx² + V(x) is self-adjoint on a domain enforcing u(0)=0 and square-integrability. The spectrum is discrete for the bound “subband” states in x, and continuous in (ky,kz) for in-plane motion.

Common models/approximations & why

  • Triangular well (V(x)=eEx): captures strong-band-bending near a MOS interface with approximately constant field.
  • Infinite barrier at x=0: enforces vanishing wavefunction at the oxide (first approximation to large band offset).
  • Variational trial functions: avoid Airy-function algebra while capturing correct boundary behavior and length scale.

Mini intuition examples (no heavy algebra)

  • If E increases, V(x) rises faster, so u(x) is squeezed toward x=0 → higher kinetic energy → higher ε.
  • If m increases (heavier effective mass), kinetic energy cost of confinement drops → wavefunction can be tighter for the same energy scale.
  • The characteristic confinement length must come from balancing “kinetic” ~ ħ²/(2mL²) and “potential” ~ eE L.

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Boundary condition: the infinite wall implies u(0)=0 (not u'(0)=0).
  • Normalization: use ∫0∞|u|² dx, not 3D normalization.
  • Signs & units: ensure V(x)=+eEx is energy (J) and E is in V/m.
  • Variational principle: the computed ε is an upper bound to the true ground ε.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

The triangular-well “surface subband” model is a cornerstone of how engineers understand (and design) MOSFET channels. Even when real devices require more sophisticated simulations, this model provides scaling laws and intuition that guide process and design choices.

Where it shows up (3+ application areas)

  • CMOS MOSFETs: inversion layer quantization modifies threshold voltage and capacitance (especially in thin-oxide, high-field regimes).
  • HEMTs / III–V interfaces: 2D electron gases (2DEGs) form at heterointerfaces with strong built-in fields.
  • Quantum wells / sensors: field-tunable carrier confinement affects mobility and sensitivity (e.g., MOS sensors, Hall devices).
  • Power electronics: high fields near interfaces influence reliability phenomena (hot carriers, oxide stress).

Why engineers care

  • Performance: subband energies affect carrier density and mobility → on-current and transconductance.
  • Reliability: higher E tightens confinement and raises energies → impacts hot-carrier generation and interface scattering.
  • Cost & manufacturability: scaling laws (like ε ∝ E2/3) help prioritize which knobs matter most in process tuning.

How it’s validated in practice (brief)

  • C–V measurements (capacitance–voltage) to infer inversion charge and quantum-capacitance shifts.
  • Mobility extraction vs gate field (interface roughness scattering increases with stronger confinement).
  • Self-consistent Poisson–Schrödinger simulations in TCAD to refine beyond the constant-field triangular approximation.

Design trade-offs & non-idealities

  • Non-uniform E(x): real devices require Poisson coupling (charge modifies field).
  • Finite barrier: wavefunction penetrates slightly into oxide/insulator → changes boundary condition and energy.
  • Interface roughness & phonons: stronger confinement increases scattering → mobility can decrease even as density increases.
  • Valley anisotropy (Si): different effective masses along different crystallographic directions alter subbands.
From lab to product: how the ideal model is adapted

In product-grade MOSFET modeling, the triangular well is often used as a local approximation inside a larger Poisson–Schrödinger loop. Engineers replace the constant E with an effective field extracted from charge distribution, include anisotropic effective masses, and use finite barrier heights. Despite these refinements, the core scaling ε ∝ E2/3 remains a powerful guideline.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement (in my own words)

At a semiconductor–insulator interface (x=0), a conduction electron on the semiconductor side (x>0) feels a linear potential energy V(x)=eEx due to a strong electric field E pointing in +x. The insulator is modeled as an infinite barrier for x<0, so the wavefunction vanishes there and must satisfy u(0)=0. We separate the 3D wavefunction as ψ(x,y,z)=u(x)exp[i(kyy+kzz)], reducing the problem to a 1D eigenvalue equation for u(x).

Using the variational trial function u(x)=x e−αx, we must: (a) compute the expectation value ε(α) = (ħ²/2m)α² + (3eE)/(2α), (b) minimize it to find αmin = (3eEm/(2ħ²))1/3, (c) evaluate εmin = 1.89 (ħ²/2m)1/3(3eE/2)2/3.

Given quantities

  • Potential: V(x)=eEx for x>0; V(x)=∞ for x<0.
  • Effective mass m, field E, electron charge magnitude e, ħ.
  • Trial function: u(x)=x e−αx with α>0.

Unknowns

  • ε(α): variational estimate of the lowest x-direction eigenenergy (“lowest subband”).
  • αmin: parameter minimizing ε(α).
  • εmin: minimized energy estimate.

Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Separation of variables: V depends only on x, so y,z are translationally invariant; plane waves are eigenfunctions in y,z.
  • Variational principle: for a self-adjoint Hamiltonian, the Rayleigh quotient ⟨u|H|u⟩/⟨u|u⟩ provides an upper bound to the ground eigenvalue, minimized by the true ground state.
  • Boundary conditions: infinite barrier implies u(0)=0; confinement implies u is square integrable on (0,∞).

Assumptions stated explicitly

  • Single electron; neglects electron–electron interactions and exchange-correlation.
  • Constant field E in the relevant region; potential depends only on x.
  • Infinite barrier at x<0 (no penetration into insulator).
  • Effective-mass approximation with scalar mass m.
  • Stationary (time-independent) bound state in x.

Possible approaches (2–3) and comparison

  1. Exact Airy-function solution: Solve the ODE with V(x)=eEx, u(0)=0. Pros: exact eigenvalues. Cons: requires special-function zeros and more algebra.
  2. Variational method (given): Choose u(x)=x e−αx, compute integrals, minimize. Pros: fast, captures scaling and good numerical constant. Cons: approximate (upper bound).
  3. Scaling / dimensional analysis: Estimate energy scaling ε ~ (ħ²/2m)^{1/3}(eE)^{2/3}. Pros: immediate insight. Cons: cannot get numerical prefactor reliably.

Chosen approach: the variational method, because the problem explicitly requests it and it yields both the correct scaling and the requested numerical constant (1.89).

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Set up the Rayleigh quotient: ε(α)=⟨u|H|u⟩/⟨u|u⟩ with H=−(ħ²/2m)d²/dx² + eEx (for x>0).
  2. Compute normalization: N(α)=∫0∞|u(x)|² dx for u=x e−αx.
  3. Compute kinetic numerator: K(α)=∫0∞|u'(x)|² dx; then ⟨T⟩=(ħ²/2m)K/N.
  4. Compute potential numerator: P(α)=∫0∞x|u(x)|² dx; then ⟨V⟩=eE·P/N.
  5. Assemble ε(α): ε(α)=⟨T⟩+⟨V⟩; simplify.
  6. Minimize: solve dε/dα=0 for α>0; confirm it is a minimum using d²ε/dα²>0.
  7. Evaluate ε at αmin: simplify to the requested closed form and numerical constant.
  8. Sanity checks: units, limiting behavior as E→0 or E→∞, physical interpretation (confinement length).

Common mistakes (quick fixes)

  • Using u'' instead of |u'|²: for real u with vanishing boundary terms, ⟨T⟩ can be computed via ∫|u'|² dx, which avoids integration by parts errors.
  • Forgetting the half-line: all integrals are from 0 to ∞, not −∞ to ∞.
  • Dropping α powers: keep track of α dimensions (1/length). A wrong α exponent breaks units immediately.
  • Not checking α>0: decay requires α positive; the minimizer must respect that domain.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition before calculating

The electron is confined near x=0 because: (i) it cannot enter x<0 (infinite wall), and (ii) moving to larger x costs potential energy eEx. A tighter wavefunction lowers ⟨V⟩ but raises ⟨T⟩ due to stronger curvature. The ground state is found by balancing these. We expect a characteristic length L such that ħ²/(2mL²) ~ eE L ⇒ L ~ (ħ²/(2m eE))1/3, hence ε ~ eE L ~ (ħ²/2m)1/3(eE)2/3. The variational method will produce the same scaling plus a numerical prefactor.

Hamiltonian on the half-line: For x>0, H = −(ħ²/2m) d²/dx² + eE x, with u(0)=0 and u(x)→0 as x→∞.

Linearity & separability justify treating x-motion as an independent eigenproblem: translation symmetry in y,z yields plane-wave factors, leaving a 1D bound-state problem in x.

Step 1: Trial function and normalization

Choose the (real) trial function u(x)=x e−αx with α>0. It satisfies u(0)=0 automatically and decays as x→∞, so it belongs to the correct Hilbert space on (0,∞).

Trial function 
u(x) = x e^{-αx},  α > 0,  x ∈ (0, ∞)

Compute the norm:

Normalization integral 
N(α) = ∫_0^∞ |u(x)|^2 dx
     = ∫_0^∞ x^2 e^{-2αx} dx

Use the standard integral (valid for β>0): ∫0∞ xn e−βx dx = n! / βn+1. Here n=2 and β=2α, so:

Result for N(α) 
N(α) = 2! / (2α)^3 = 2 / (8 α^3) = 1 / (4 α^3)

Step 2: Kinetic energy expectation ⟨T⟩

For real u with u(0)=0 and u→0 at ∞, integration by parts shows ⟨T⟩ = (ħ²/2m) ∫ |u'|² dx / ∫|u|² dx. This is convenient because it avoids directly handling u'' and boundary terms.

Why is ⟨T⟩ = (ħ²/2m) ∫|u'|² / ∫|u|² valid here?

Starting from ⟨u|−u''⟩ = ∫_0^∞ u(−u'') dx, integration by parts gives ∫ u(−u'') dx = [−u u']_0^∞ + ∫ (u')² dx. The boundary term vanishes because u(0)=0 and u,u' decay at ∞, leaving ∫(u')² dx. Thus ⟨T⟩=(ħ²/2m) ∫(u')² / ∫u².

Differentiate u(x)=x e−αx:

Derivative 
u'(x) = d/dx [x e^{-αx}]
      = e^{-αx} + x(-α)e^{-αx}
      = e^{-αx}(1 - αx)

Then

Kinetic numerator K(α) 
K(α) = ∫_0^∞ |u'(x)|^2 dx
     = ∫_0^∞ e^{-2αx} (1 - αx)^2 dx
     = ∫_0^∞ e^{-2αx} (1 - 2αx + α^2 x^2) dx

Evaluate term-by-term using ∫ e−βx dx = 1/β, ∫ x e−βx dx = 1/β², ∫ x² e−βx dx = 2/β³ with β=2α:

Compute K(α) step-by-step 
Let β = 2α.

∫_0^∞ e^{-βx} dx = 1/β
∫_0^∞ x e^{-βx} dx = 1/β^2
∫_0^∞ x^2 e^{-βx} dx = 2/β^3

K(α) = 1/β - 2α (1/β^2) + α^2 (2/β^3)
     = 1/(2α) - 2α [1/(4α^2)] + α^2 [2/(8α^3)]
     = 1/(2α) - 1/(2α) + 1/(4α)
     = 1/(4α)

Therefore the kinetic expectation is

⟨T⟩ from K/N 
⟨T⟩ = (ħ²/2m) (K/N)
    = (ħ²/2m) [ (1/(4α)) / (1/(4α^3)) ]
    = (ħ²/2m) α^2

Step 3: Potential energy expectation ⟨V⟩

With V(x)=eEx for x>0,

Potential expectation setup 
⟨V⟩ = ( ∫_0^∞ u(x)^2 [eE x] dx ) / ( ∫_0^∞ u(x)^2 dx )
     = eE ( ∫_0^∞ x |u(x)|^2 dx ) / N(α)

Since u² = x² e−2αx, the numerator is ∫ x·x² e−2αx dx = ∫ x³ e−2αx dx. Using ∫ x³ e−βx dx = 3! / β⁴ = 6/β⁴ with β=2α:

Potential numerator and result 
P(α) = ∫_0^∞ x |u(x)|^2 dx
     = ∫_0^∞ x^3 e^{-2αx} dx
     = 3! / (2α)^4
     = 6 / (16 α^4)
     = 3 / (8 α^4)

⟨V⟩ = eE (P/N)
    = eE [ (3/(8α^4)) / (1/(4α^3)) ]
    = eE (3/2) (1/α)
    = (3 eE) / (2 α)

Step 4: Variational energy ε(α) and minimization

Add ⟨T⟩ and ⟨V⟩ to form the Rayleigh quotient (energy functional):

(a) Show ε(α) = (ħ²/2m)α² + (3eE)/(2α) 
ε(α) = ⟨T⟩ + ⟨V⟩
     = (ħ²/2m) α^2 + (3 eE)/(2 α)

Minimize for α>0. Differentiate:

dε/dα = 0 condition 
dε/dα = (ħ²/2m)·2α  + (3 eE)/2 · d/dα (α^{-1})
      = (ħ²/m) α  - (3 eE)/(2 α^2)

Set dε/dα = 0:
(ħ²/m) α = (3 eE)/(2 α^2)
⇒ (ħ²/m) α^3 = (3 eE)/2
⇒ α_min = ( (3 eE m) / (2 ħ²) )^{1/3}

Check it is a minimum:

Second derivative check 
d²ε/dα² = (ħ²/m) + (3 eE)/(α^3)  > 0 for α>0
So α_min gives a true minimum.

This completes part (b).

Step 5: Evaluate ε at αmin (part c)

Substitute αmin into ε(α). A clean way is to use the stationarity relation (ħ²/m)αmin³ = (3eE)/2 to reduce expressions. Start from: ε = (ħ²/2m)α² + (3eE)/(2α).

Let A=(ħ²/2m) and B=(3eE/2). Then ε(α)=Aα² + B/α and the minimizing α satisfies 2Aα³=B. This makes the algebra structured and avoids “magic jumps”.

Compute ε_min in structured steps 
Let A = ħ²/(2m),  B = 3eE/2.

ε(α) = A α^2 + B/α
Min condition: dε/dα=0 ⇒ 2A α^3 = B ⇒ α_min = (B/(2A))^{1/3}

Evaluate:
ε_min = A α_min^2 + B/α_min
      = A (B/(2A))^{2/3} + B (B/(2A))^{-1/3}
      = A^{1/3} B^{2/3} (1/2^{2/3}) + A^{1/3} B^{2/3} (2^{1/3})
      = A^{1/3} B^{2/3} [ 2^{-2/3} + 2^{1/3} ]
      = A^{1/3} B^{2/3} [ (1 + 2)/2^{2/3} ]
      = 3·2^{-2/3} A^{1/3} B^{2/3}
      = (3/2)·2^{1/3} A^{1/3} B^{2/3}

Now substitute back A and B: A1/3 = (ħ²/2m)1/3, and B2/3 = (3eE/2)2/3. The prefactor (3/2)21/3 ≈ 1.89.

Final result (boxed) 
ε(α) = (ħ²/2m) α² + (3eE)/(2α)

α_min = ( (3 eE m) / (2 ħ²) )^{1/3}

ε_min = (3/2)·2^{1/3} (ħ²/2m)^{1/3} (3eE/2)^{2/3}
      ≈ 1.89 (ħ²/2m)^{1/3} (3eE/2)^{2/3}

Interpretation: εmin is the approximate lowest “subband bottom” energy for motion normal to the surface. In a MOSFET, increasing gate field E raises ε and shifts the occupancy and charge distribution in the inversion layer, enabling field-effect control.

Sanity checks

1) Units / dimensional analysis

α has units 1/length. From αmin = (eEm/ħ²)1/3: eE has units of force (N = kg·m/s²), so eEm has units kg²·m/s². ħ² has units (J·s)² = (kg·m²/s)² = kg²·m⁴/s². Ratio → 1/m³, cube root → 1/m. Good.

ε scales as (ħ²/2m)1/3(eE)2/3: (ħ²/m) has units J·m²; (ħ²/m)1/3 ~ (J·m²)1/3. (eE)2/3 ~ (J/m)2/3. Multiply → J. Good.

2) Limiting behavior

  • E → 0: confinement weakens; αmin→0 and εmin→0 (bound state merges toward continuum). This matches the intuition that without a confining field the electron is not trapped by the rising potential.
  • E large: αmin grows, so u(x) becomes more localized near x=0; εmin increases as E2/3.

One simple static plot: the variational minimum (dimensionless)

Because the result is mostly symbolic, a useful plot is the dimensionless energy curve showing that the variational functional has a clear minimum at α=αmin. Define s = α/αmin. Then

Normalized energy curve 
Let s = α/α_min. Then ε(α)/ε_min = (s^3 + 2)/(3s) = (1/3)(s^2 + 2/s)
Minimum occurs at s=1 where ε/ε_min = 1.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the final formula

The optimized parameter αmin sets the confinement length scale L ~ 1/αmin:

Characteristic length scale 
L ~ 1/α_min = ( 2 ħ² / (3 eE m) )^{1/3}

Stronger field E shrinks L (tighter 2D channel), while heavier mass m also shrinks L for fixed ħ (lower kinetic penalty makes tighter confinement favorable).

Parameter dependence (qualitative & quantitative)

  • Electric field E: εmin ∝ E2/3. Increasing E raises subband energies and strengthens confinement.
  • Effective mass m: εmin ∝ m−1/3. Heavier mass lowers energy scale (easier confinement).
  • Charge e: εmin ∝ e2/3 because the potential slope is eE.
  • ħ: εmin ∝ ħ2/3 — quantumness directly sets confinement energy.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Scaling / nondimensionalization: Let x = L ξ. Then the Hamiltonian becomes H = (ħ²/2mL²)(-d²/dξ²) + (eE L) ξ. Choosing L so that ħ²/(2mL²) ~ eE L makes the dimensionless operator have O(1) coefficients. This yields L ~ (ħ²/(2m eE))1/3 and ε ~ eE L ~ (ħ²/2m)1/3(eE)2/3. The exact Airy solution refines only the numerical prefactor (your prompt notes 1.89 → 1.78).

Connecting to the exact Airy-function solution

With V(x)=eEx, the ODE maps to the Airy equation after shifting/scaling x. The boundary condition u(0)=0 quantizes ε via zeros of an Airy function. The variational result gives the correct E2/3 scaling and a close prefactor; the prompt notes the exact ground prefactor is about 1.78 instead of 1.89 (a typical level of accuracy for a simple one-parameter trial function).

Concept check (self-test, with answers)

  • Q: Why do we take ψ ∝ exp[i(kyy+kzz)] in y,z?
    A: Because V depends only on x, the Hamiltonian commutes with translations in y and z, so plane waves (Fourier modes) are eigenstates in those directions.
  • Q: What enforces u(0)=0?
    A: The infinite barrier at x<0 implies the wavefunction must vanish at the boundary to keep the energy finite and satisfy continuity conditions.
  • Q: Why does ε increase with E?
    A: Larger E steepens the potential, forcing stronger confinement (larger curvature), raising kinetic energy; overall ε scales as E2/3.
  • Q: In what sense is ε(α) “safe” as an estimate?
    A: Variational principle: ε(α) ≥ εtrue ground. Minimizing over α gives the best upper bound within the chosen trial family.

PART 5 — Optional Plot Guide

A plot is useful here because it visually confirms the variational logic: the energy functional has a unique minimum, and that minimum occurs at the α found by calculus.

What the plot shows

  • y-axis: y = ε/εmin (dimensionless).
  • x-axis: s = α/αmin (dimensionless).
  • Curve: y=(s³+2)/(3s), with minimum y=1 at s=1.

Features to notice

  • As s→0: y ~ (2)/(3s) → ∞ (too broad: potential energy dominates).
  • As s→∞: y ~ s²/3 → ∞ (too tight: kinetic energy dominates).
  • At s=1: perfect balance between confinement costs.
