Metallic “Optic Fibres”? Why Light in Sodium is Damped within ~0.1 μm at λ0=10 μm

Metals look “high-index,” but their free electrons make electromagnetic waves decay extremely fast. We’ll model sodium with the Drude free-electron response, derive the complex wavevector from Maxwell’s equations, and compute the damping length.

Quick Summary

  • ProblemEstimate how far a 10 μm vacuum-wavelength light wave penetrates/propagates inside sodium at room temperature.
  • Key ideaIn a conductor, the dielectric function is complex (Drude model), so the wavevector is complex and fields decay exponentially.
  • Governing equationsMaxwell + constitutive laws: ∇×E = −∂B/∂t, ∇×H = J + ∂D/∂t with J=σ(ω)E and D=ε0E.
  • Math objectComplex dispersion relation: k² = μ0ε(ω)ω², where ε(ω) is complex.
  • ResultNumeric estimate: damping length ℓ ≈ 0.1 μm (amplitude 1/e scale) at λ0=10 μm.
  • MeaningEnergy is converted to Joule heat over sub-micron distances (contrast: glass fibres ≈ 100 km).
  • Industrial relevanceExplains skin effect, IR shielding, plasmonic loss limits, and thermal loading in metal optics.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • E(r,t): electric field (V/m), H(r,t): magnetic field (A/m)
  • D=ε0E (C/m²), B=μ0H (T)
  • J: conduction current density (A/m²)
  • σ: conductivity (S/m), ρ=1/σ resistivity (Ω·m)
  • ω: angular frequency (rad/s), k: complex wavenumber (1/m)
  • λ0: vacuum wavelength (m), k0=ω/c=2π/λ0
  • ℓ: damping length (m), defined by |E| ∝ e^{−z/ℓ}

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Complex ε(ω) stores + dissipates energy (real + imaginary parts).
  • Complex k: k = k′ + iα ⇒ oscillation + exponential decay.
  • Skin depth ~ 1/α is the penetration scale in conductors.
  • Collisions of electrons → Joule heating → attenuation.

Key laws/principles & validity conditions

  • Linear response: J(ω)=σ(ω)E(ω) (small-signal regime).
  • Homogeneous, isotropic sodium; μ≈μ0 (non-magnetic).
  • Time-harmonic fields: e^{−iωt} so ∂/∂t→−iω.
  • Local model: σ depends on ω only (no spatial dispersion).

Common models/approximations and why

Drude model
  • Electrons accelerate under E and relax momentum over time τ.
  • Gives complex σ(ω) capturing phase lag + loss.
Good-conductor limit
  • If |σ(ω)| ≫ ε0 ω, conduction dominates displacement current.
  • Leads to simple √(1/ωσ) scaling for damping length.

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Amplitude vs intensity: I ∝ |E|², so intensity decays twice as fast in exponent.
  • Definition conventions: δ (skin depth) may refer to amplitude 1/e (δ=1/α) or power 1/e.
  • σ(ω) is not σ(0): Drude reduces |σ| when ωτ ≳ 1.

Math Insight (as a mathematical object)

Maxwell’s equations in a homogeneous medium reduce to a Helmholtz PDE: (∇² + k²)E = 0. In a conductor, k² is complex. This is an eigenvalue-like dispersion relation whose imaginary part produces exponential decay—analogous to damped modes in linear systems.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where it appears (applications)

  • RF/microwave conductors (skin effect losses in cables, waveguides)
  • IR/optical coatings (reflection + absorption → heating)
  • Plasmonics (tight confinement but strong ohmic loss)

Why engineers care

  • Attenuation sets signal budgets and device Q-factor
  • Ohmic heating impacts thermal design and reliability
  • Surface roughness/oxidation can dominate real losses

Measurement/validation

  • Ellipsometry / reflectometry → complex ε(ω)
  • Resistivity + Drude estimate → τ, σ0
  • Thermal imaging → dissipation hotspots

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Restate the problem

Show that in sodium at room temperature, a light wave with vacuum wavelength 10 μm is damped with a length of order 0.1 μm, due to the free-electron response of the metal.

Given

  • λ0 = 10 μm
  • Metal: sodium (room temperature)

Find

  • Damping length ℓ (amplitude 1/e)
  • Show ℓ ≈ 0.1 μm (order-of-magnitude)

Assumptions

  • Homogeneous, isotropic conductor; μ≈μ0
  • Linear response J=σ(ω)E
  • Good-conductor regime |σ(ω)|≫ε0ω

Approaches

A) Skin-depth from Maxwell + σ(ω)
  • Fast and directly yields ℓ
B) Full Drude ε(ω)=1−ωp²/(ω(ω+i/τ))
  • More complete; similar outcome
Choice: Use A with Drude σ(ω) to match the “free-electron term dominates” hint and get ℓ cleanly.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Write Maxwell curl equations and go to frequency domain (∂/∂t→−iω).
  2. Insert constitutive laws and Ohm’s law J=σ(ω)E.
  3. Derive Helmholtz equation (∇²+k²)E=0 and dispersion k²=μ0ε0ω²+iμ0σ(ω)ω.
  4. Apply good-conductor limit to simplify: k²≈iμ0σ(ω)ω.
  5. Take square root: k=(1+i)/√2 √(μ0ωσ(ω)).
  6. Identify attenuation α=Im(k) and ℓ=1/α.
  7. Use Drude |σ(ω)|=σ0/√(1+ω²τ²) and estimate |σ(ω)| in mid-IR.
  8. Compute ℓ numerically at λ0=10 μm and compare to glass.

Common mistakes

  • Using ω=c/λ0 instead of ω=2πc/λ0
  • Confusing amplitude length ℓ with intensity length
  • Forgetting σ is frequency-dependent

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition

The incident EM field drives conduction currents in sodium. Because electrons collide (finite τ), the induced current dissipates energy (J·E) as heat. Therefore the wave amplitude decays rapidly with depth.

Mathematical model

  • Half-space conductor z>0, fields vary as exp(i k z − i ω t).
  • Linear, homogeneous, isotropic medium; μ≈μ0.
  • Conduction: J(ω)=σ(ω)E(ω); displacement: D=ε0E.
  • We seek the complex k; damping length ℓ is from Im(k).

Step 1: Maxwell curl equations (frequency domain)

Assume time dependence e^(−iωt):

∇×E = i ω B
∇×H = J − i ω D

With B=μ0H, D=ε0E, and J=σ(ω)E:

∇×H = [σ(ω) − i ω ε0] E

Step 2: Derive the Helmholtz equation and dispersion relation

Take curl of Faraday’s law and use ∇×(∇×E)=∇(∇·E)−∇²E. In homogeneous bulk (no net free charge density oscillation), take ∇·E≈0 so ∇×(∇×E)=−∇²E. Then: 

∇×(∇×E) = i ω μ0 (∇×H)
−∇²E = i ω μ0 [σ(ω) − i ω ε0] E

⇒ ∇²E + k² E = 0

where  k² = μ0 ε0 ω² + i μ0 σ(ω) ω.

Step 3: Complex wavevector and definition of damping length

Let k = k′ + i α  (α>0).
Plane wave into +z:  E(z) = E0 e^(i k z) = E0 e^(i k′ z) e^(−α z)

Amplitude damping length:  ℓ ≡ 1/α  (so |E| falls by factor e over ℓ).

Step 4: Good-conductor approximation

If |σ(ω)| ≫ ε0 ω, then μ0ε0ω² is negligible compared to μ0σ(ω)ω, so: 

k² ≈ i μ0 σ(ω) ω.

Use i = e^{iπ/2} ⇒ √i = e^{iπ/4} = (1+i)/√2: 

k ≈ (1+i)/√2 · √( μ0 ω σ(ω) ).

Hence α = Im(k) ≈ √( μ0 ω |σ(ω)| / 2 )

⇒ ℓ = 1/α ≈ √( 2 / ( μ0 ω |σ(ω)| ) ).
Dimensional analysis

[μ0]=H/m, [σ]=S/m. Then μ0σ has units s/m²; multiply by ω (1/s) gives 1/m². So α has 1/m and ℓ has m. Consistent.

Step 5: Drude σ(ω) and magnitude reduction at optical frequencies

Drude momentum relaxation equation: 

dp/dt = −e E − p/τ

For E∝e^(−iωt), solve algebraically: 

p(ω) = (−e τ / (1 − i ω τ)) E(ω)

With J = −n e v and v=p/m: 

σ(ω) = σ0 / (1 − i ω τ),   σ0 = n e² τ / m

⇒ |σ(ω)| = σ0 / √(1 + ω² τ²).

In the mid-IR at ω≈10^14 s^−1, many metals have ωτ ≳ 1, so |σ(ω)| can be substantially smaller than DC σ0.

Step 6: Numerical estimate for λ0=10 μm

  • λ0 = 10 μm = 1.0×10^−5 m
  • ω = 2πc/λ0 ≈ 2π(3.0×10^8)/(1.0×10^−5) ≈ 1.88×10^14 s^−1
  • μ0 = 4π×10^−7 H/m
  • Use a Drude-reduced effective conductivity in this band of order |σ(ω)| ~ 10^6 S/m (order-of-magnitude, consistent with ωτ≫1 reduction).
ℓ ≈ √( 2 / ( μ0 ω |σ(ω)| ) )

Compute μ0 ω |σ|:
μ0 ω |σ| ≈ (4π×10^−7)(1.88×10^14)(10^6)
        ≈ 2.36×10^14  (1/m^2)

So:
ℓ ≈ √( 2 / 2.36×10^14 ) m
  ≈ √( 8.5×10^−15 ) m
  ≈ 9.2×10^−8 m
  ≈ 0.09 μm  ≈ 0.1 μm.

Final Answer (copyable)

In a good conductor, the complex wavevector satisfies:
  k² = μ0 ε0 ω² + i μ0 σ(ω) ω  ≈  i μ0 σ(ω) ω.

Thus:
  k ≈ (1+i)/√2 · √( μ0 ω σ(ω) )
  α = Im(k) ≈ √( μ0 ω |σ(ω)| / 2 )
  ℓ = 1/α ≈ √( 2 / ( μ0 ω |σ(ω)| ) ).

For λ0 = 10 μm ⇒ ω ≈ 1.88×10^14 s^−1, and sodium at room temperature has a Drude-reduced
mid-IR effective conductivity of order |σ(ω)| ~ 10^6 S/m, giving:

  ℓ ≈ 9×10^−8 m ≈ 0.1 μm.

So the wave amplitude decays by e over ~0.1 μm inside sodium (vs ~100 km in high-quality glass fibre).

Sanity checks

Units

μ0ωσ has units 1/m² ⇒ ℓ has meters.

Limiting behavior
  • Better conductor (larger σ) ⇒ smaller ℓ (more rapid damping).
  • Higher ω ⇒ smaller ℓ in the good-conductor regime.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Interpretation of terms

  • μ0: inductive coupling—stronger magnetic response shrinks ℓ.
  • ω: faster time variation increases induced currents and loss per depth.
  • |σ(ω)|: how strongly currents flow; higher |σ| increases shielding and damping.

Math Insight (diffusion-like viewpoint)

In the conduction-dominated (quasi-static) limit, Maxwell’s equations imply a diffusion-type PDE for magnetic fields: ∂B/∂t = (1/(μ0σ)) ∇²B. Harmonic solutions of this diffusion operator yield complex k and the same √(1/(ωσ)) penetration scaling.

Concept check (with answers)

  • Q: If |E| decays as e^{−z/ℓ}, how does intensity decay? A: I ∝ e^{−2z/ℓ}.
  • Q: What happens to ℓ if ω doubles? A: ℓ → ℓ/√2 (if σ fixed).
  • Q: Why does Drude reduce σ at high ω? A: electrons cannot follow quickly; current lags and magnitude drops as 1/ωτ.
