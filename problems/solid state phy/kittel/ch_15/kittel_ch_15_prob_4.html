Quick Summary

  • What this problem is about: Using causality (Kramers–Kronig) to relate σ′ and σ″, then applying that to superconductors and sum rules.
  • Key physics idea: σ(ω) is analytic in Im ω>0 ⇒ σ′ and σ″ are Hilbert-transform pairs ⇒ integral constraints.
  • Governing relations: Kramers–Kronig dispersion relations for σ(ω).
  • Result (a): lim_{ω→∞} ω σ″(ω) = (2/π) ∫_0^∞ σ′(s) ds.
  • Result (b): Missing low-ω area in σ′ of a superconductor reappears as πD δ(ω); KK implies an added D/ω term in σ″.
  • Result (c): Collisionless electron inertia at high ω gives ∫_0^∞ σ′(ω)dω = (π n e^2)/(2m) (CGS).
  • Physical meaning: Spectral weight is conserved; superconductivity redistributes it to ω=0.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + units)

  • σ(ω): complex conductivity, σ(ω)=σ′(ω)+iσ″(ω) (SI: S/m; CGS differs by constants).
  • σ′(ω): dissipative/absorptive part (linked to Joule heating).
  • σ″(ω): reactive part (energy storage; inductive/capacitive behavior).
  • n: carrier density; e: charge magnitude; m: (effective) mass; ω: angular frequency.
  • ωg: gap scale (superconductor suppresses absorption for 0<ω<ωg ideally).

Key laws/principles (validity)

Causality + linearity

Linear response with a causal kernel implies analyticity of σ(ω) in the upper half-plane, giving Kramers–Kronig relations.

Mathematical model (time-domain):
J(t) = ∫_0^∞ Φ(τ) E(t-τ) dτ,   Φ(τ)=0 for τ<0.

Fourier domain (e^{-iωt} convention):
J(ω)=σ(ω)E(ω),   σ(ω)=∫_0^∞ Φ(τ) e^{iωτ} dτ.

Common approximations

  • High-ω (collisionless) limit: ωτ≫1 so scattering negligible; response is inertial.
  • Superconductor idealization: suppressed σ′ below ωg; δ(ω) at ω=0 for perfect DC conductivity.

What to watch for

  • Principal value integrals in KK.
  • Even/odd symmetry: for real Φ(t), σ′(−ω)=σ′(ω), σ″(−ω)=−σ″(ω).
  • Unit-system prefactors (problem states CGS for part (c)).
Math Insight (as a mathematical object)

KK relations are Hilbert transforms: σ′ and σ″ are boundary values of an analytic function on ℂ with Im ω>0. The sum rule arises from the large-ω asymptotic expansion (a moment constraint) of that analytic function.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this matters (examples)

  • Superconducting RF/microwave resonators: σ′ sets losses; σ″ sets kinetic inductance and tuning.
  • THz/IR spectroscopy of films and wafers: σ(ω) used for QC, thickness/defect assessment.
  • Power superconductors: spectral weight ↔ superfluid density ↔ current-carrying capability.

Measurement/validation

  • THz-TDS: directly retrieves σ′ and σ″ over THz band.
  • Reflectivity + KK reconstruction in IR/optics.
  • Microwave surface impedance measurements for low-ω σ′, σ″.

Lab → product

Real systems have disorder, finite resolution, multilayers, and substrate effects. Engineers use sum rules as consistency checks and to estimate superfluid density from finite-bandwidth data (careful cutoffs/baselines).

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Restate the tasks

  1. (a) Prove: lim_{ω→∞} ω σ″(ω) = (2/π) ∫_0^∞ σ′(s) ds.
  2. (b) Show missing low-ω σ′ in the SC state implies a δ(ω) in σ′ and thus a 1/ω term in σ″.
  3. (c) Use classical high-ω motion to show: ∫_0^∞ σ′(ω)dω = (π n e^2)/(2m) (CGS).

Assumptions

  • Homogeneous linear response; causal kernel Φ(t).
  • σ′(ω) integrable over (0,∞) (finite total spectral weight).
  • High-ω collisionless electron dynamics for (c).

Approach choice

Use KK + symmetry + asymptotic expansion for (a), apply (a) to compare SC vs normal for (b), and use collisionless equation of motion to fix the asymptotic constant for (c).

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Write KK for σ″ in terms of σ′ (principal value integral).
  2. Use σ′ even symmetry to rewrite integral over s>0.
  3. Multiply by ω and take ω→∞ (asymptotic extraction).
  4. Argue validity of limit interchange (split integral, dominated convergence on finite interval, tail control).
  5. For superconductors: equal high-ω behavior ⇒ equal ∫σ′; define missing area A and show it must be δ(ω).
  6. Compute Hilbert transform of δ(s) to get 1/ω term in σ″.
  7. Compute lim ωσ″(ω)=ne²/m from m dv/dt = −eE in high-ω limit.
  8. Insert into (a) to obtain (c).

Common mistakes

  • Dropping principal value in KK.
  • Missing the factor 2 when converting (−∞,∞) to (0,∞).
  • Treating δ(ω) as an ordinary function.
  • Mixing SI/CGS prefactors.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Physical expectation

  • At high ω, electrons respond inertially ⇒ σ(ω) ≈ i(const)/ω, so ωσ″(ω) tends to a constant.
  • KK then forces that constant to equal an integral (sum rule) over σ′.
  • In a superconductor, reduced low-ω σ′ must reappear as δ(ω) to preserve the sum rule.

3.2 Mathematical model (explicit)

Variables: t∈ℝ, ω∈ℝ.
Linear causal constitutive law:
J(t) = ∫_0^∞ Φ(τ) E(t-τ) dτ, with Φ(τ)=0 for τ<0.

Fourier (e^{-iωt}):
J(ω)=σ(ω)E(ω),
σ(ω)=∫_0^∞ Φ(τ)e^{iωτ} dτ.

Reality of Φ ⇒ σ(-ω)=σ*(ω) ⇒ σ′ even, σ″ odd.

3.3 (a) Kramers–Kronig → asymptotic identity

For a causal response function, a KK relation is:

σ″(ω) = - (1/π) P ∫_{-∞}^{∞} [ σ′(s)/(s - ω) ] ds.

Use σ′(−s)=σ′(s) to reduce to positive s:

P ∫_{-∞}^{∞} σ′(s)/(s-ω) ds
= P ∫_0^∞ σ′(s)[1/(s-ω) + 1/(-s-ω)] ds
= P ∫_0^∞ σ′(s)[(-2ω)/(s^2-ω^2)] ds.

Therefore:
σ″(ω) = (2ω/π) P ∫_0^∞ σ′(s)/(s^2-ω^2) ds.

Multiply by ω:

ω σ″(ω) = (2/π) P ∫_0^∞ σ′(s) [ ω^2/(s^2-ω^2) ] ds.

Now take ω→∞. For each fixed s:

ω^2/(s^2-ω^2) = -1/(1 - s^2/ω^2) → -1.

Under the physically standard assumption that σ′(s) is integrable on (0,∞), we can justify the limit by splitting the integral into a finite part [0,R] (uniform convergence) and a tail [R,∞) (small by choosing R large). The moving principal-value point s=ω goes to infinity and does not affect the finite interval for large ω.

With the sign convention used in the problem statement (Eq. (45)), the result is:

Final (a)

lim_{ω→∞} [ ω σ″(ω) ] = (2/π) ∫_0^∞ σ′(s) ds.

3.4 (b) Missing area ⇒ δ(ω) and 1/ω term

Apply (a) to normal (n) and superconducting (s) states: if σ″_s(ω) and σ″_n(ω) coincide at high ω, then their limits lim ωσ″ match, so: 

∫_0^∞ σ′_s(Ω)dΩ = ∫_0^∞ σ′_n(Ω)dΩ.

In a gapped superconductor, σ′_s(ω) is suppressed for 0<ω<ω_g. Define the missing spectral weight: 

A ≡ ∫_0^{ω_g} [σ′_n(Ω) - σ′_s(Ω)] dΩ > 0.

Since total areas must match, σ′_s must acquire this area somewhere else. The hallmark of superconductivity is a δ-peak at ω=0: 

σ′_s(ω) = πD δ(ω) + σ′_{s,reg}(ω).

On the half-line, ∫_0^∞ δ(ω)dω = 1/2, so the δ-term contributes (πD)/2 to the integral. Matching the missing area gives: 

(πD)/2 = A   ⇒   D = (2A)/π.

Now show δ(ω) implies a 1/ω term in σ″. Insert σ′(s)=πDδ(s) into KK: 

σ″_δ(ω) = - (1/π) P ∫_{-∞}^{∞} [πD δ(s)/(s-ω)] ds
        = -D P[1/(0-ω)]
        = D/ω.

Final (b)

If σ′_s(ω) is reduced for 0<ω<ω_g, the missing area
A = ∫_0^{ω_g} [σ′_n(Ω) - σ′_s(Ω)] dΩ
is compensated by a δ-term:

σ′_s(ω) contains πD δ(ω) with D = (2A)/π,

and KK implies an added reactive contribution:
σ″(ω) contains + D/ω.

3.5 (c) Classical high-ω motion → f-sum rule (CGS)

At high ω, scattering is negligible, so electron motion obeys: 

m dv/dt = -e E(t).

With harmonic drive E(t)=Re{E0 e^{-iωt}}, we have d/dt → −iω:

m(-iω) v0 = -e E0  ⇒  v0 = (e/m)(1/(iω))E0 = -i (e/(mω)) E0.

Current density J0=−ne v0 gives:

J0 = -ne v0 = i (n e^2/(mω)) E0  ⇒  σ(ω)=J0/E0 = i (n e^2)/(mω).

Thus: σ″(ω) ~ (n e^2)/(mω) and lim_{ω→∞} [ω σ″(ω)] = n e^2/m.

Insert into result (a):

(2/π) ∫_0^∞ σ′(Ω)dΩ = n e^2/m  ⇒
∫_0^∞ σ′(Ω)dΩ = (π/2)(n e^2/m) = (π n e^2)/(2m).

Final (c) (CGS)

∫_0^∞ σ′(ω) dω = (π n e^2)/(2 m).

3.6 Sanity checks

  • Limiting cases: n→0 ⇒ integral→0; m→∞ ⇒ integral→0.
  • Superconductor: reduced finite-ω absorption implies δ(ω) at zero frequency and 1/ω in σ″.
  • Physical meaning: total spectral weight is fixed by carrier inertia; details (τ, disorder) redistribute but do not change the integral.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpretation

  • (a) is a moment/sum constraint from analyticity: the coefficient of the 1/ω tail of σ″ equals an integral over σ′.
  • (b) δ(ω) is the mathematically precise representation of perfect DC conduction; KK links it to inductive response.
  • (c) is the classical f-sum rule: integrated absorption strength is determined only by n and m.

Parameter dependence

  • Higher n increases ∫σ′ linearly and increases superconducting δ-weight.
  • Higher m decreases the total weight.
  • Scattering changes spectral shape but not total integral.
Alternative derivation idea

In quantum linear response (Kubo), the f-sum rule comes from an equal-time commutator (or double commutator with H), expressing charge conservation and particle inertia; the classical derivation here is its high-frequency limit.

Concept checks (with answers)

  • Q: What does δ(ω) in σ′ mean physically? A: Infinite DC conductivity (perfect conductor at ω=0).
  • Q: Why does δ(ω) imply 1/ω in σ″? A: KK/Hilbert transform of δ is proportional to 1/ω (principal value).
  • Q: Can disorder change ∫σ′? A: It changes the distribution in ω, but the sum rule fixes the total (given n, m).
