Quick Summary

  • What this is: A Frenkel exciton on a 1D ABAB… crystal has two atoms per primitive cell, so the exciton band splits into two bands (Davydov splitting).
  • Key physics idea: Excitation can hop between localized molecular/atomic sites; the two-sublattice structure makes a 2×2 Bloch Hamiltonian.
  • Governing equation: A Bloch eigenvalue problem for a 2×2 matrix H(k) built from transfer integrals T1, T2.
  • Result type: Fully symbolic dispersion: E±(k) = E0 ± √(T1² + T2² + 2T1T2 cos(ka)).
  • Davydov splitting at k=0: ΔD = E+(0) − E−(0) = 2|T1 + T2|.
  • Physical meaning: At k=0 the exciton states are cell-periodic “in-phase / out-of-phase” superpositions of A and B excitations; coupling lifts degeneracy.
  • Engineering relevance: Splitting and bandwidth set optical absorption peaks, exciton transport lengths, and sensitivity to disorder/strain in organic semiconductors and molecular crystals.
Math Insight

The core result comes from diagonalizing a Hermitian 2×2 Bloch matrix. Mathematically, it’s a dispersion relation obtained by solving det(H(k) − E I) = 0, i.e., an eigenvalue problem parameterized by the wavevector k.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions
  • Frenkel exciton: A localized electronic excitation on a site that can hop to neighboring sites (tight-binding picture).
  • Primitive cell: The smallest repeating unit; here it contains two sites (A and B).
  • Transfer integral T (energy units, e.g. joules or electronvolts): amplitude for excitation hopping between sites.
  • Wavevector k (1/m): labels Bloch states; in 1D, k ∈ [−π/a, π/a] for cell spacing a.
  • On-site excitation energy E0 (energy): excitation energy when localized on a single site (same for A and B in this problem).
Physical meaning & intuition
  • Why two bands? Two sites per cell means two “internal” basis states per cell (excitation on A or on B), yielding two eigenmodes per k.
  • Alternating couplings: If hopping strengths alternate (T1, T2), the chain resembles a “dimerized” tight-binding model: coupling inside the cell differs from coupling between cells.
  • Davydov splitting: At k=0, the in-phase and out-of-phase A/B superpositions split in energy by an amount set by T1 + T2.

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

  • Linearity & superposition: Single-exciton subspace is linear; the Hamiltonian acts as a linear operator on amplitudes.
  • Translation symmetry: Periodic AB cell implies Bloch’s theorem applies: eigenstates can be chosen with phase factor e^{ikna}.
  • Nearest-neighbor approximation: Only A↔B couplings between adjacent sites are retained (valid when farther dipole couplings are small or neglected).
  • Frenkel limit: Exciton is localized on sites; hopping is treated perturbatively as transfer integrals.

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • If T1 = T2: No dimerization; the splitting at k=0 becomes 2|2T| = 4|T|, and the two bands are symmetric around E0.
  • If T2 → 0: The chain breaks into isolated AB dimers; energies become nearly E0 ± |T1| (flat bands).
  • If T1 and T2 have opposite signs: Interference can reduce splitting at k=0 because it depends on T1 + T2.
What to watch for
  • Define the lattice constant carefully: here a is the cell spacing (A-to-next-A), not the A–B distance.
  • Phase factors: A↔B within a cell has no Bloch phase; hopping across cells picks up e^{±ika}.
  • Sign conventions: Some texts define transfer integrals with a minus sign. Your final form should be consistent and Hermitian.
  • “Davydov splitting” definition: here explicitly at k=0: ΔD = E+(0)−E−(0).

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this shows up (applications)
  • Organic semiconductors & OLEDs: Exciton band structure influences absorption/emission spectra and exciton diffusion.
  • Molecular crystals (photonics/optics): Davydov splitting produces multiple optical lines; used to infer crystal packing and coupling strengths.
  • Excitonic transport materials: Energy splitting and bandwidth relate to transport anisotropy and sensitivity to disorder.
  • 2D/1D engineered stacks: Dimerized couplings appear in layered heterostructures and superlattices (band engineering analogy).
Why engineers care
  • Performance: Splitting positions absorption peaks → affects color, efficiency, and spectral selectivity.
  • Reliability: Couplings depend on spacing/orientation; thermal expansion, strain, or aging can shift T1, T2.
  • Manufacturability: Processing affects crystal order; disorder broadens bands and can wash out Davydov splitting.
  • Cost/optimization: Knowing how packing controls T1, T2 guides materials design without brute-force iteration.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Optical absorption / reflectance: Splitting shows up as multiple peaks near the exciton resonance.
  • Photoluminescence (PL) & polarization-resolved spectra: Identifies symmetry of split states and coupling directionality.
  • Temperature/strain dependence: Tracks how T1, T2 change with lattice spacing and phonons.

Design trade-offs & real-world constraints

  • Disorder vs coupling: Larger |T| increases bandwidth but requires tight packing that may increase nonradiative losses.
  • Non-idealities: Site energies may differ (A≠B), longer-range couplings exist, and vibronic coupling renormalizes transfer integrals.
  • Scalability: Thin-film morphology can create domains with different effective T1, T2, causing inhomogeneous broadening.
From lab to product

The ideal model assumes perfect periodicity and identical site energies. In products, engineers treat E0, T1, T2 as effective parameters extracted from spectra, then add disorder broadening, temperature renormalization, and finite-size effects to predict device-level optical response.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement: A 1D crystal has repeating pattern …A B A B… with two atoms per primitive cell. The Frenkel exciton can hop with transfer integral T1 between an A and the adjacent B in the order AB, and with transfer integral T2 between a B and the adjacent A in the order BA. Find the two exciton bands as functions of wavevector k. The energy splitting between the bands at k=0 is the Davydov splitting.

Given

  • 1D periodic chain with two sites per cell: A and B.
  • Nearest-neighbor transfer integrals: T1 for AB, T2 for BA.
  • Implicit: identical on-site excitation energy E0 (as in standard Frenkel model unless stated otherwise).
  • Lattice periodicity: cell spacing a (A-to-next-A).

Unknowns / must find

  • Two dispersion relations E+(k) and E−(k).
  • Davydov splitting ΔD = E+(0) − E−(0).

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Bloch’s theorem: Translation by one cell leaves the Hamiltonian invariant, so exciton eigenstates can be labeled by k.
  • Tight-binding (Frenkel exciton) Hamiltonian: In the one-excitation manifold, the Hamiltonian is a linear operator on site amplitudes.
  • Hermiticity: Physical Hamiltonian must be Hermitian; hopping terms appear in conjugate pairs giving real energies.
Mathematical model (explicit)
  • Domain: 1D infinite periodic lattice indexed by cell n ∈ ℤ.
  • Basis states: |n,A⟩, |n,B⟩ (excitation localized on A or B in cell n).
  • State vector: |ψ⟩ = Σn (a_n |n,A⟩ + b_n |n,B⟩).
  • Operator form: Solve eigenvalue problem Ĥ|ψ⟩ = E|ψ⟩ with periodicity handled via Bloch ansatz.
  • Boundary condition: Bloch periodicity in k-space: amplitudes differ by phase e^{ik a} under translation by one cell.

Possible approaches (compare)

  • (A) Real-space difference equations → Bloch substitution: Write coupled recurrence relations for a_n, b_n, then insert e^{ikna}. Pros: very transparent physically. Cons: can be algebra-heavy if not organized.
  • (B) Build 2×2 Bloch Hamiltonian directly: Use basis (A,B) per cell and encode intercell hopping with phase factors. Pros: clean and general; yields immediate eigenvalues. Cons: requires careful bookkeeping of phases.
  • (C) Fourier transform the lattice operators: Second-quantized exciton creation operators and Fourier transform. Pros: scalable to higher dimensions. Cons: more formal than needed here.

Chosen approach: (B) 2×2 Bloch Hamiltonian, because the problem explicitly emphasizes two atoms per cell and band doubling.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Define geometry and indexing: choose a unit cell n with sites A_n and B_n; define cell spacing a.
  2. Write the Frenkel exciton Hamiltonian: on-site term E0 and hopping terms T1 (A_n↔B_n) and T2 (B_n↔A_{n+1}).
  3. Impose Bloch form: use amplitudes a_n = a e^{ikna}, b_n = b e^{ikna}.
  4. Construct the 2×2 Bloch matrix H(k): off-diagonal element becomes T1 + T2 e^{-ika}.
  5. Solve eigenvalue equation: compute det(H(k) − E I)=0 to get E±(k).
  6. Extract Davydov splitting: evaluate E± at k=0 and take the difference.
  7. Sanity checks: Hermiticity, dimensions, limiting cases (T2→0, T1=T2, etc.).
Common mistakes
  • Using the wrong phase factor (putting e^{ika} on the intra-cell hop).
  • Confusing site spacing with cell spacing; here a is the period of the AB motif.
  • Dropping the complex conjugate term and ending with a non-Hermitian H(k).
  • Forgetting that the splitting definition is specifically at k=0.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

With two sites per unit cell, for each wavevector k we expect two eigenmodes—one mostly “bonding” (A and B amplitudes in-phase) and one “antibonding” (out-of-phase). Therefore, one exciton band in a mono-atomic chain becomes two bands here. At k=0 the Bloch phase is unity, so both couplings T1 and T2 add constructively in the splitting.

Step 1 — Define the lattice and basis

Label unit cells by integer n. Each cell contains two sites: A_n and B_n. Let a be the translation period from cell n to cell n+1. Use localized one-exciton basis states |n,A⟩, |n,B⟩.

Step 2 — Write the Frenkel exciton Hamiltonian (operator form)

In the one-excitation manifold, the Hamiltonian is a linear operator with: (i) on-site excitation energy E0 and (ii) hopping (transfer) between nearest neighbors. We include two distinct nearest-neighbor couplings: T1 for A_n ↔ B_n (within the same cell), and T2 for B_n ↔ A_{n+1} (across the cell boundary).

Key equation: Frenkel-exciton tight-binding Hamiltonian 
Ĥ = E0 Σ_n ( |n,A⟩⟨n,A| + |n,B⟩⟨n,B| )
   + Σ_n [ T1 ( |n,A⟩⟨n,B| + |n,B⟩⟨n,A| )
         + T2 ( |n+1,A⟩⟨n,B| + |n,B⟩⟨n+1,A| ) ]

Why this is valid: we assume a single exciton (linear Hilbert space), translation invariance by one cell, and nearest-neighbor hopping only. Hermiticity is enforced by adding each hopping term with its adjoint.

Dimensional analysis check

E0, T1, T2 have units of energy. Therefore eigenvalues E±(k) also have units of energy. Phase factors e^{±ika} are dimensionless since k has units 1/length.

Step 3 — Apply Bloch’s theorem (Fourier/Bloch ansatz)

Because the system is periodic with period a, eigenstates can be taken as Bloch waves. Write a general one-exciton state:

Bloch form: amplitudes per cell 
|ψ⟩ = Σ_n ( a_n |n,A⟩ + b_n |n,B⟩ ),  with  a_n = a·e^{ikna},  b_n = b·e^{ikna}

Why this is valid: translation by one cell is a symmetry, so the translation operator commutes with Ĥ. Thus we can choose simultaneous eigenstates, giving the Bloch phase e^{ikna}.

Step 4 — Derive the 2×2 Bloch Hamiltonian H(k)

Act with Ĥ on |ψ⟩ and collect coefficients of |n,A⟩ and |n,B⟩. The on-site part contributes E0 a_n and E0 b_n. Hopping contributions:

  • Within cell: A_n ↔ B_n gives terms proportional to T1 b_n in the A equation and T1 a_n in the B equation.
  • Across cell boundary: B_n ↔ A_{n+1} gives T2 b_n contributing to A_{n+1}, which when rewritten at cell n picks up a Bloch phase.

Concretely, the coupled eigenvalue equations in real space are:

Coupled difference equations 
E a_n = E0 a_n + T1 b_n + T2 b_{n-1}
E b_n = E0 b_n + T1 a_n + T2 a_{n+1}

Why the indices look like this: A_n couples to B_n (T1) and to B_{n−1} via the intercell bond B_{n−1}↔A_n (T2). Similarly, B_n couples to A_n (T1) and to A_{n+1} via B_n↔A_{n+1} (T2).

Insert the Bloch forms a_n = a e^{ikna}, b_n = b e^{ikna}:

  • b_{n-1} = b e^{ik(n-1)a} = b e^{ikna} e^{-ika}
  • a_{n+1} = a e^{ik(n+1)a} = a e^{ikna} e^{+ika}

Factor out the common e^{ikna} and obtain a 2×2 linear system:

2×2 Bloch eigenvalue problem 
E [a] = [ E0         T1 + T2 e^{-ika} ] [a]
  [b]   [ T1 + T2 e^{+ika}    E0       ] [b]

This matrix is Hermitian because the off-diagonal entries are complex conjugates. Therefore its eigenvalues are real for all k.

Step 5 — Solve det(H(k) − E I) = 0

Let the off-diagonal element be f(k) = T1 + T2 e^{ika}. Then the matrix is H(k) = [[E0, f*(k)], [f(k), E0]]. The characteristic equation is:

Characteristic equation 
det(H − E I) = (E0 − E)^2 − |f(k)|^2 = 0

Hence (E − E0)^2 = |f(k)|^2, so E = E0 ± |f(k)|. Now compute |f(k)| explicitly:

Compute |f(k)| step-by-step 
f(k) = T1 + T2 e^{ika}
|f(k)|^2 = f(k) f*(k)
        = (T1 + T2 e^{ika})(T1 + T2 e^{-ika})
        = T1^2 + T2^2 + T1 T2 (e^{ika} + e^{-ika})
        = T1^2 + T2^2 + 2 T1 T2 cos(ka)
Final band energies (two exciton bands)

The dispersion relations are:

FINAL RESULT: E±(k) 
E±(k) = E0 ± √( T1^2 + T2^2 + 2 T1 T2 cos(ka) ),   with k ∈ [−π/a, π/a]

Equivalent compact form: E±(k) = E0 ± |T1 + T2 e^{ika}|.

Step 6 — Davydov splitting at k = 0

At k=0, cos(0)=1, so √(T1²+T2²+2T1T2)=|T1+T2|. Therefore:

Davydov splitting (k=0) 
ΔD ≡ E+(0) − E−(0) = 2 |T1 + T2|

Sanity checks

Limiting cases
  • T2 → 0: E±(k) → E0 ± |T1| (flat, isolated dimers AB).
  • T1 = T2 = T: E±(k) = E0 ± √(2T² + 2T² cos(ka)) = E0 ± 2|T|·|cos(ka/2)|.
  • k = π/a: E±(π/a) = E0 ± |T1 − T2| (maximal sensitivity to dimerization).
Sign / physical interpretation
  • The bands are symmetric about E0 because A and B have the same on-site energy here.
  • Band splitting is controlled by the magnitude of a complex hopping sum; interference via cos(ka) modulates it across the Brillouin zone.
  • If A and B had different site energies, symmetry would break and the eigenvalues would shift (not asked here).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the dispersion

The dispersion E±(k) = E0 ± √(T1² + T2² + 2T1T2 cos(ka)) is controlled by a single k-dependent “coupling magnitude” between the A/B sublattice amplitudes. Think of the vector (T1, T2) adding with a phase: T1 + T2 e^{ika}.

What each parameter controls
  • E0: sets the midline (average) energy of the two bands.
  • T1, T2 magnitudes: set the typical splitting scale and bandwidth; larger couplings → more dispersion.
  • Relative sign of T1 and T2: changes constructive/destructive interference at k=0 (splitting depends on |T1+T2|).
  • cos(ka): encodes how hopping across cells picks up a Bloch phase; it tunes the splitting continuously across k.

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Increase |T1| with fixed T2: both Davydov splitting and bandwidth generally increase.
  • Increase dimerization (|T1 − T2| larger): increases contrast between splitting at k=0 (∝|T1+T2|) and at k=π/a (∝|T1−T2|).
  • Small-k expansion (asymptotics): for small ka, use cos(ka) ≈ 1 − (ka)²/2. Then the square-root term behaves like: |T1+T2| minus a quadratic correction in k, indicating smooth band curvature near k=0.
Optional deeper note: small-k expansion (showing the curvature)

Let S = T1 + T2 and expand:

Small-k asymptotic (ka ≪ 1) 
√(T1^2 + T2^2 + 2T1T2 cos(ka))
= √( (T1+T2)^2 − T1T2 (ka)^2 + O((ka)^4) )
≈ |S| · √(1 − (T1T2/S^2)(ka)^2 )
≈ |S| − (T1T2/(2|S|)) (ka)^2 + O((ka)^4)

So near k=0: E±(k) ≈ E0 ± |S| ∓ (T1T2/(2|S|))(ka)². The curvature (effective mass analogy) depends on the product T1T2.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

You can derive the same result by Fourier transforming site amplitudes (or exciton creation operators) into k-space and writing the Hamiltonian as Ĥ = Σ_k (E0 a†_k a_k + E0 b†_k b_k + f(k) a†_k b_k + f*(k) b†_k a_k), then diagonalizing the resulting 2×2 form for each k. This is the same eigenproblem, expressed in operator language.

Concept check (self-test)

  • Q: Why are there two bands? A: Two basis states per cell (excitation on A or on B) → 2×2 Bloch Hamiltonian → two eigenvalues per k.
  • Q: What sets the Davydov splitting at k=0? A: The magnitude of the summed couplings with zero phase: ΔD = 2|T1+T2|.
  • Q: What happens if T2 = 0? A: Chain becomes disconnected dimers → no k-dispersion; energies E0 ± |T1|.
  • Q: Where does cos(ka) come from? A: From the sum of Bloch phase factors e^{ika}+e^{-ika} when computing |T1 + T2 e^{ika}|^2.

PART 5 — Optional Plot Guide

A plot is useful here because it visually emphasizes that a single exciton line becomes two dispersive bands, and that their separation changes with k through cos(ka). The plot included above uses example values only for visualization; the final derived formulas remain symbolic.

What the plot shows

  • x-axis: reduced wavevector k·a (dimensionless) from −π to π.
  • y-axis: energy E (eV in the example).
  • Two curves: E+(k) (upper) and E−(k) (lower).

Example values used (not part of the symbolic solution)

  • E0 = 2.00 eV, T1 = 0.08 eV, T2 = 0.04 eV, a = 1 (so axis is k·a).

Features to notice

  • At k·a = 0, the vertical gap is ΔD = 2|T1+T2| (largest here for same-sign T1,T2).
  • At k·a = π, the gap becomes 2|T1−T2|, revealing the effect of dimerization.
  • The bands are symmetric around E0.
