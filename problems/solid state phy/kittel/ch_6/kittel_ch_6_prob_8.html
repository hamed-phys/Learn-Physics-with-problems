Cohesive Energy of a Free-Electron Fermi Gas (Jellium Model)

Quick Summary

  • What this problem is about: compute the cohesive energy per electron of a uniform electron gas (jellium) at 0 K, as a function of the density parameter rs.
  • Key physics idea: at T = 0, electrons fill momentum states up to the Fermi wavevector kF; the total energy is kinetic (degeneracy pressure) + Coulomb (attraction/repulsion).
  • Governing equations: number density n fixes kF = (3π²n)^{1/3} and Ēkin = (3/5)EF; Coulomb energies come from integrals of ρ(r)φ(r) using symmetry.
  • Results (in Rydbergs):
    • Average kinetic energy per electron: Ēkin = 2.21 / rs² Ry.
    • Point +e with uniform electron cloud: E+− = −3 / rs Ry.
    • Self-energy of electron cloud: E−− = +6/(5rs) Ry.
    • Total Coulomb per electron: EC = −1.80 / rs Ry.
  • Total energy model: E(rs) = 2.21/rs² − 1.80/rs Ry.
  • Equilibrium density parameter: minimizing gives rs ≈ 2.45.
  • Stability vs separated H atoms: at the minimum, E ≈ −0.37 Ry, which is less bound than hydrogen (−1 Ry), so this idealized “metal” is not stable relative to isolated H atoms.
Scope note

This is a baseline jellium calculation (Hartree-level electrostatics + free-electron kinetic energy). Real metals need exchange-correlation, lattice/ion structure, and quantum chemistry to predict actual stability.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core Definitions (symbols + units)

  • n = electron number density [m−3].
  • r0 = radius of a sphere containing one electron on average: (4/3)π r0³ n = 1.
  • a0 = Bohr radius [m]: a0 = ħ²/(m e²) (Gaussian/atomic units convention).
  • rs = r0/a0 (dimensionless).
  • kF = Fermi wavevector [m−1].
  • Ry = Rydberg energy: 1 Ry = ħ²/(2m a0²) = m e⁴/(2ħ²).

Physical Meaning (intuition)

  • rs measures mean spacing in Bohr radii: small rs ⇒ high density.
  • Kinetic energy rises due to Pauli filling: ~ 1/rs².
  • Coulomb energy in this baseline model: ~ −1/rs.
  • These competing scalings create an energy minimum.
Math Insight

By symmetry and scaling, the full many-body problem collapses to a one-parameter minimization: E(rs) = A rs−2 − B rs−1. Competing homogeneities guarantee a minimum for rs > 0.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

  • Metals & alloys: baseline cohesion and density scaling informs stiffness/compressibility trends.
  • Degenerate semiconductors: heavy doping pushes carriers toward Fermi-gas behavior.
  • Plasma processing: dense electron fluids use related screening/energy-density ideas.

In practice this model is refined with DFT; jellium still underpins many exchange-correlation approximations used in engineering simulations.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Mathematical model
  • Domain Ω = {0 ≤ r ≤ r0} (Wigner–Seitz sphere), spherical coordinates.
  • Uniform electron charge density: ρe = −e/V inside Ω, V = (4/3)π r0³.
  • Electrostatics: ∇²φ = −4πρ (Gaussian units), spherical symmetry ⇒ ∇²φ = (1/r²) d/dr (r² dφ/dr).
  • T=0 Fermi filling: f(k)=1 for |k|≤kF, 0 otherwise.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Use (4/3)πr0³n=1 and r0=rsa0.
  2. Derive kF from k-space counting with spin factor 2.
  3. Compute Ēkin = (3/5)EF and express in Ry.
  4. Compute E+− = ∫ρe(e/r)dV.
  5. Compute E−− = (1/2)∫ρeφedV (solve Poisson).
  6. Sum Coulomb terms and minimize total E(rs).
  7. Compare with hydrogen: −1 Ry.

PART 3 — Full Solution (DETAILED + TEACHING)

Key unit identities
a0 = ħ^2/(m e^2)
1 Ry = m e^4/(2 ħ^2) = ħ^2/(2 m a0^2)
e^2/a0 = 2 Ry

(a) Average kinetic energy per electron

(4/3)π r0^3 n = 1  ⇒  n = 3/(4π r0^3),  r0 = rs a0
n = 2*(1/(2π)^3)*(4/3)π kF^3 = kF^3/(3π^2)
⇒ kF = (3π^2 n)^(1/3)
⇒ kF = (9π/4)^(1/3) * 1/r0
⇒ kF a0 = (9π/4)^(1/3) * 1/rs
Ēkin = (∫_{|k|≤kF} (ħ^2 k^2/(2m)) d^3k)/(∫_{|k|≤kF} d^3k)
= (ħ^2/(2m)) * (∫_0^{kF} 4π k^4 dk)/(∫_0^{kF} 4π k^2 dk)
= (ħ^2/(2m)) * ((kF^5/5)/(kF^3/3))
= (3/5)(ħ^2 kF^2/(2m)) = (3/5)EF
Ēkin/Ry = (3/5)(kF a0)^2
= (3/5)(9π/4)^(2/3) * 1/rs^2
≈ 2.21/rs^2
Result (a)
Ēkin = 2.21/rs^2  (Ry)

(b) Coulomb energy of point +e with uniform electron cloud

V=(4/3)π r0^3,  ρe = -e/V,  φ+(r)=e/r
E_{+−} = ∫_0^{r0} ρe (e/r) 4π r^2 dr
= 4π ρe e ∫_0^{r0} r dr
= 2π ρe e r0^2
= -(3/2) e^2/r0
= -(3/2) * (2 Ry)/rs
= -3/rs (Ry)
Result (b)
E_{+−} = -3/rs  (Ry)

(c) Coulomb self-energy of the uniform electron distribution

Poisson (spherical): ∇^2φ = (1/r^2) d/dr (r^2 dφ/dr) = -4πρe  (r≤r0)
Integrate:
d/dr(r^2 dφ/dr) = -4πρe r^2
r^2 dφ/dr = -(4πρe/3) r^3  (regular at r=0)
dφ/dr = -(4πρe/3) r
φ_in(r) = -(2πρe/3) r^2 + C2
Match φ_in(r0)=Q/r0 with Q=-e:
⇒ C2 = -e/r0 + (2πρe/3) r0^2
Self-energy (avoid double counting):
E_{−−} = (1/2)∫ ρe φ_in dV
= 3 e^2/(5 r0)
= 3/5 * e^2/(rs a0)
= 3/5 * (2 Ry)/rs
= 6/(5rs) (Ry)
Result (c)
E_{−−} = +6/(5 rs)  (Ry)

(d) Total Coulomb energy, minimization, and stability

E_C = E_{+−}+E_{−−} = (-3/rs) + (6/(5rs)) = -9/(5rs) = -1.80/rs (Ry)
Total: E(rs)=2.21/rs^2 - 1.80/rs (Ry)
dE/drs = -4.42/rs^3 + 1.80/rs^2 = 0  (rs>0) ⇒ rs = 4.42/1.80 ≈ 2.45
E(2.45) ≈ 2.21/2.45^2 - 1.80/2.45 ≈ -0.367 Ry
Compare: H atom ground state = -1 Ry ⇒ jellium state is higher ⇒ not stable vs separated H atoms.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (THEORY AROUND THE RESULT)

  • Why a minimum exists: rs−2 dominates as rs→0, rs−1 dominates as rs→∞.
  • Operator view: kinetic term comes from −(ħ²/2m)∇² eigenvalues; Coulomb from ∇²φ = −4πρ.
