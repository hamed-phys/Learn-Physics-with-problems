Dynamic Magnetoconductivity Tensor & Circular-Wave Dispersion in a Metal (B along z)

We derive the frequency-dependent conductivity tensor σ(ω) of free electrons in a static magnetic field B = B ẑ, then use Maxwell’s equations to obtain the dispersion relation for waves propagating along ẑ. The key outcome: the medium supports two circularly polarized eigenmodes with slightly different k(ω), causing Faraday rotation for linear polarization.

Quick Summary

  • What this is about: A metal’s free-electron gas in a static magnetic field B = Bẑ, driven by an AC electric field at frequency ω.
  • Key physics idea: Lorentz force couples x–y motion, producing a Hall-like off-diagonal conductivity even at finite frequency.
  • Governing model: Drude equation (with collisions) for drift velocity v(t): m dv/dt + (m/τ)v = -e( E + v×B/c )
  • Regime: High-frequency compared to cyclotron and collision rates: ω ≫ ωc, ω ≫ 1/τ, where ωc = eB/(mc).
  • Conductivity tensor result (symbolic): σxx=σyy = i ωp2/(4πω), σxy=-σyx = (ωcωp2)/(4πω2).
  • Wave result (symbolic): For propagation along ẑ, the eigenmodes are circular polarizations with dispersion c2k2 = ω2 - ωp2 ± (ωcωp2)/ω.
  • Physical meaning: Two circular modes have different k(ω), so a linear polarization (sum of the two) rotates as it propagates (Faraday rotation).
  • Engineering relevance: The same math underlies magneto-optic isolators, Hall/terahertz conductivity extraction, and polarization control in plasmas/metals.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + SI units; note on unit system)

The problem statement uses Gaussian/CGS electromagnetic conventions (e.g., factors of 4π and c). We will follow that to match the given answers.

  • n — free electron number density (cm-3 in CGS; m-3 in SI)
  • e — magnitude of electron charge (electron charge is -e)
  • m — electron (effective) mass
  • τ — collision/relaxation time (s)
  • B — static magnetic field magnitude; vector B = Bẑ
  • ω — angular frequency of applied AC fields (rad/s)
  • ωc — cyclotron frequency: ωc = eB/(mc) (rad/s)
  • ωp — plasma frequency: ωp2 = 4π n e2/m (rad/s)2
  • j — current density (statA/cm2 in CGS; A/m2 in SI)
  • σ — conductivity tensor relating j to E: j = σ E
  • ε — dielectric tensor; in CGS: ε = I + i(4π/ω)σ

0.2 Physical meaning: what each quantity represents

  • τ measures how quickly electron momentum relaxes due to scattering. In Drude theory, it is the only dissipation parameter.
  • ωc sets the rate at which velocity would rotate in the x–y plane under B if there were no E and no collisions.
  • ωp sets the natural “charge sloshing” frequency of the electron fluid relative to the ionic background; it also sets optical cutoffs in metals/plasmas.
  • The off-diagonal conductivity σxy encodes the magnetic-field-induced transverse current (Hall-like response) for AC driving.

0.3 Key laws/principles + validity conditions

Model assumptions (Drude + linear response):
  • Electrons are treated as a classical fluid of charge -e and mass m (no band-structure details unless m is an effective mass).
  • Collisions enter as a linear friction term -(m/τ)v (relaxation-time approximation).
  • Fields are spatially uniform at the scale of microscopic motion (local response).
  • Linear response: j is linear in E (small-signal regime).
  • Here we further assume ω ≫ 1/τ (weak damping over an oscillation) and ω ≫ ωc (magnetic coupling is a perturbation).

The electromagnetic part uses Maxwell equations in a homogeneous medium and seeks plane-wave solutions. For waves propagating along z, the x–y tensor structure determines polarization eigenmodes.

0.4 Common approximations and why they’re used

  • Harmonic ansatz: assume time dependence exp(-iωt), converting ODEs/PDEs into algebraic relations in frequency domain.
  • High-frequency expansion: treat (1/τ)/ω and ωc/ω as small parameters, enabling controlled asymptotics.
  • Propagation along B: with k parallel to B, the problem becomes an eigenvalue problem in the transverse (x–y) plane; circular polarizations diagonalize the antisymmetric Hall tensor.

0.5 Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • No B: electrons oscillate with a phase lag; at high ω they can’t follow E well, giving an almost purely imaginary conductivity (inertial response).
  • With B: the Lorentz term v×B/c deflects motion, creating a transverse component of current; in AC this shows up as σxy(ω).
  • For waves: if the medium responds differently to right/left circular polarizations, a linear polarization (sum of the two) rotates while propagating.

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Sign conventions: electron charge is -e, and time dependence exp(-iωt) makes d/dt → -iω.
  • Unit system: CGS relations include 4π and c in places where SI looks different.
  • Tensor algebra: σ is not diagonal in x–y when B ≠ 0; eigenvectors are circular, not linear x/y.
  • Approximations: keep track of which terms are dropped in the limits ω ≫ 1/τ and ω ≫ ωc.
Math Insight (core equation as a mathematical object):

The drift-velocity model is a linear vector ODE with constant coefficients: it is a forced, damped, gyroscopic system in &R;3. In frequency space it becomes a 3×3 complex linear algebra problem, and the wave problem becomes a transverse eigenvalue problem for the dielectric tensor, whose eigenvectors are circular polarizations.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Why engineers and industry care

Application areas (at least 3)
  • Magneto-optic isolators and circulators (Faraday rotators) in laser systems and fiber networks.
  • Terahertz/IR material characterization: extracting σ(ω,B) from transmission/reflection to infer carrier density n and scattering time τ.
  • Plasma diagnostics (fusion, space, ion thrusters): birefringence and polarization rotation reveal B and electron density.
  • Metrology & sensors: Hall/magneto-optic sensors, non-contact conductivity mapping, eddy-current testing in conductive manufacturing.
Design drivers
  • Performance: polarization purity, insertion loss, bandwidth, and isolation ratio.
  • Reliability: temperature dependence of n, m*, τ (and thus ωp, losses).
  • Cost/manufacturability: material choice (garnets, doped semiconductors), tolerances in B-field uniformity and thickness.
  • Safety: high-power lasers need robust isolation to prevent back-reflection damage.

How this is validated/measured in practice

  • Ellipsometry / magneto-ellipsometry: measures complex reflection coefficients to infer the dielectric tensor ε(ω,B).
  • THz time-domain spectroscopy (THz-TDS): gets amplitude + phase → complex conductivity tensor.
  • Faraday rotation measurements: track rotation angle vs thickness and B-field to validate circular birefringence.

Real-world constraints & model adaptation

  • Non-idealities: finite damping (keep 1/τ terms), spatial dispersion (k-dependent response), multiple bands (sum of Drude terms), anisotropic effective mass tensor.
  • Trade-offs: increasing n boosts ωp (stronger response) but often increases losses; stronger B increases splitting but requires heavier magnets/coils.
  • From lab to product: the ideal tensor becomes a fitted model ε(ω) with parameters (n, m*, τ, ωc) tuned to measured spectra; thermal drift and tolerances are engineered via feedback/control and robust design margins.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

1.1 Restate the problem (clear and complete)

A metal contains free electrons (density n, charge -e, mass m) in a static magnetic field B = Bẑ. An AC electric field in the x–y plane induces current density components jx = σxxEx + σxyEy, jy = σyxEx + σyyEy. Assume ω ≫ ωc and ω ≫ 1/τ.

(a) Solve the drift-velocity equation (Drude with B) to obtain the conductivity tensor components and show: σxx=σyy= iωp2/(4πω), σxy=-σyx= ωcωp2/(4πω2), where ωp2=4πne2/m.

(b) Using Maxwell’s relation ε = I + i(4π/ω)σ, consider a plane wave with wavevector k = kẑ. Show the dispersion relation: c2k2 = ω2 - ωp2 ± (ωcωp2)/ω.

1.2 Given quantities

  • n, e, m, B (so ωc = eB/(mc)), relaxation time τ
  • Frequency domain: angular frequency ω with ω ≫ ωc, ω ≫ 1/τ
  • Wave: k = kẑ; electromagnetic propagation along the magnetic field

1.3 Unknowns / what must be found

  • Conductivity tensor components σxx, σyy, σxy, σyx in the stated limit
  • Dispersion relation k(ω) for the two eigenmodes (and identify the physical mode types)

1.4 Mathematical physics model (variables, domain, coordinates, conditions)

Mathematical Model
  • Domain: homogeneous infinite medium (no boundaries needed for bulk dispersion).
  • Coordinates: Cartesian (x,y,z), with B = Bẑ and propagation k = kẑ.
  • Unknown fields: E(r,t), B(r,t) (wave fields), and drift velocity v(t) (electron response).
  • Constitutive law: j = σ(ω) E in frequency domain (local, linear).
  • Time dependence: assume harmonic fields exp[i(kz - ωt)] so that &partial;/&partial;t → -iω and ∇ → i k ẑ.
  • Initial/boundary conditions: not required for dispersion; we solve an eigenvalue condition for nontrivial plane waves (determinant = 0).

1.5 Relevant principles/laws (and why)

  • Drude-Lorentz equation for electron momentum (captures inertia + scattering + magnetic deflection).
  • Maxwell equations for wave propagation; for plane waves in homogeneous media they reduce to algebraic constraints.
  • Symmetry: With B along z, the x–y plane is isotropic except for a handedness induced by B, producing an antisymmetric Hall part (σ_xy = -σ_yx).

1.6 Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

Approach A: Solve v(t) ODE → σ tensor
  • Pros: Direct, transparent; shows where σ_xy comes from.
  • Cons: Requires careful complex algebra and expansions.
Approach B: Use circular basis (E±) early
  • Pros: Diagonalizes magnetic coupling in x–y; very clean for part (b).
  • Cons: Must map back to σ_xx, σ_xy for part (a) if asked explicitly.
Approach C: Start from dielectric tensor of magnetized plasma
  • Pros: Fast if you already know plasma dielectric tensor forms.
  • Cons: Less pedagogical; hides the requested drift-velocity derivation.

Best choice: Use Approach A for (a) to match the prompt (“solve the drift velocity equation”), then use Approach B (circular basis) for (b) because it turns the wave problem into two scalar dispersions.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Write the Drude-Lorentz ODE for v(t): inertia + damping + Lorentz force. (Tool: Newton’s law.)
  2. Assume harmonic time dependence v(t), E(t) ∝ exp(-iωt) so d/dt → -iω. (Tool: Fourier/phasor method; linearity.)
  3. Reduce to x–y linear system because B is along z and E is in the plane. (Tool: symmetry and cross product structure.)
  4. Solve the 2×2 system exactly for v_x, v_y in terms of E_x, E_y. (Tool: matrix inversion.)
  5. Convert velocity to current j = -ne v and read off σ_ij. (Tool: linear constitutive identification.)
  6. Apply high-frequency limits ω ≫ 1/τ and ω ≫ &omega_c to simplify (controlled asymptotic expansion).
  7. Build dielectric tensor ε = I + i(4π/ω)σ (CGS) and restrict to transverse x–y block for propagation along z.
  8. Use Maxwell plane-wave relations with ∇×∇×E = (ω²/c²) ε·E to get an eigenvalue condition in x–y.
  9. Diagonalize in circular basis E± = E_x ± iE_y to obtain two scalar dispersions (two modes).
  10. Interpret physically: two circular modes → Faraday rotation for linear polarization.
Common mistakes (quick tips)
  • Forgetting that electron charge is -e, so j = -ne v (sign matters for σ_xy orientation).
  • Mixing SI and CGS factors (4π and c). Stick to the problem’s convention.
  • Dropping 1/τ too early: do the algebra first, then take limits so you know what is being neglected.
  • Assuming x/y are eigenpolarizations: with B≠0 the eigenmodes are circular, not linear.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Qualitative expectation (before calculation)

  • At very high frequency, electrons mainly respond inertially, so the current is ~90° out of phase with E → conductivity mostly imaginary.
  • The magnetic field causes a small transverse deflection of velocity, so we expect an off-diagonal component σ_xy that is smaller by a factor ~(&omega_c/ω).
  • For wave propagation along B, circularly polarized waves should be eigenmodes because the magnetic field selects a handedness.

3.2 Part (a): Derive the magnetoconductivity tensor

Starting point: Drude-Lorentz drift-velocity equation 
m (dv/dt) + (m/τ) v = -e [ E + (v × B)/c ]

Validity: linear drag (relaxation-time), uniform B, linear response, classical drift dynamics.

Choose coordinates so B = Bẑ. Then for any velocity v = (v_x, v_y, v_z), the cross product is v × B = (v_y B, -v_x B, 0). Since the applied electric field is in the x–y plane (given by the j_x, j_y relations), we focus on x and y.

3.2.1 Frequency-domain (phasor) form

Assume harmonic time dependence (linear system ⇒ Fourier modes decouple): E(t) = Re{ E e^{-iωt} }, v(t) = Re{ v e^{-iωt} }. Then dv/dt → -iω v. Substituting into the ODE gives: 

m(-iω)v + (m/τ)v = -e [ E + (v × B)/c ]

Define the convenient complex “damping” parameter γ ≡ 1/τ - iω (units: s-1), and cyclotron frequency ωc = eB/(mc). Divide Eq. (eq2) by m: 

γ v = -(e/m) E - (e/mc)(v × B)
with γ = 1/τ - iω,  ωc = eB/(mc)

3.2.2 Component form in the x–y plane (explicit algebra)

Using v×B = (v_y B, -v_x B, 0), the x and y components of Eq. (eq3) are: 

γ v_x = -(e/m) E_x - ωc v_y
γ v_y = -(e/m) E_y + ωc v_x

This is a linear 2×2 system for (v_x, v_y). Write it in matrix form: 

[ γ   ωc ] [v_x] = -(e/m) [E_x]
[ -ωc γ  ] [v_y]           [E_y]

Invert the matrix. Its determinant is: det = γ^2 + ωc^2. The inverse is (1/(γ^2+ωc^2)) [[γ, -ωc],[ωc, γ]]. Therefore: 

v_x = -(e/m) ( γ E_x - ωc E_y ) / (γ^2 + ωc^2)
v_y = -(e/m) ( ωc E_x + γ E_y ) / (γ^2 + ωc^2)

3.2.3 From velocity to conductivity

Current density from electron drift is j = -n e v (because electron charge is -e). Substitute Eq. (eq6): 

j_x = (n e^2/m) ( γ E_x - ωc E_y ) / (γ^2 + ωc^2)
j_y = (n e^2/m) ( ωc E_x + γ E_y ) / (γ^2 + ωc^2)

By definition j_i = Σ_j σ_ij E_j, we read off the exact Drude magnetoconductivity (still in CGS conventions): 

σ_xx = σ_yy = (n e^2/m) * γ / (γ^2 + ωc^2)
σ_xy = -(n e^2/m) * ωc / (γ^2 + ωc^2)
σ_yx = +(n e^2/m) * ωc / (γ^2 + ωc^2)

The antisymmetry σ_xy = -σ_yx is forced by the handedness introduced by B and Onsager-type symmetry expectations (for a single carrier sign, the Hall part is antisymmetric).

3.2.4 Apply the problem’s high-frequency limits (controlled asymptotics)

We are told ω ≫ 1/τ and ω ≫ ωc. That means:

  • γ = 1/τ - iω ≈ -iω (dominant imaginary part)
  • γ2 = (1/τ - iω)2 = (1/τ2) - 2i(ω/τ) - ω2 ≈ -ω2 (dominant term)
  • γ2 + ωc2 ≈ -ω2 (since ωc2 ≪ ω2)
Optional math note: a clean nondimensionalization
Let ε = (1/τ)/ω and δ = ωc/ω, both ≪ 1. Then γ = ω(ε - i), and γ² + ωc² = ω²[(ε - i)² + δ²] = ω²[(-1 - 2iε + ε²) + δ²]. To leading order: γ/(γ²+ωc²) ≈ (ω(−i))/ (ω²(−1)) = i/ω, and ωc/(γ²+ωc²) ≈ ωc/(−ω²)= −ωc/ω².

Insert these leading-order approximations into Eq. (eq8):

  • σ_xx = (n e^2/m) * γ/(γ²+ωc²) ≈ (n e^2/m) * (−iω)/(−ω²) = i (n e^2/m) (1/ω)
  • σ_xy = −(n e^2/m) * ωc/(γ²+ωc²) ≈ −(n e^2/m) * ωc/(−ω²) = (n e^2/m) (ωc/ω²)

Now express (n e^2/m) in terms of the plasma frequency: ωp2 = 4π n e2/m ⇒ (n e^2/m) = ωp2/(4π). Therefore the requested results follow:

Final result for part (a): magnetoconductivity tensor (high-ω limit) 
σ_xx = σ_yy = i ωp^2 / (4π ω)
σ_xy = −σ_yx =  ωc ωp^2 / (4π ω^2)

where ωp^2 = 4π n e^2 / m,  ωc = eB/(mc).

3.2.5 Sanity checks (dimensions, limits, signs)

  • Dimensional check: In CGS, ωp² has units s-2; dividing by ω gives s-1, matching conductivity dimensions in this convention.
  • Limit B→0: ωc→0 ⇒ σ_xy→0 and σ_xx→ i ωp²/(4π ω), the familiar collisionless high-frequency Drude response (purely reactive/inertial).
  • Scaling: σ_xy/σ_xx ~ (ωc/ω)(1/i) ⇒ magnitude suppressed by ωc/ω, consistent with “small magnetic effect” at high frequency.
  • Sign structure: σ_xy = −σ_yx indicates an antisymmetric Hall-like response (rotation in x–y plane).

3.3 Part (b): Wave dispersion for k = kẑ using Maxwell equations

3.3.1 Maxwell operator form → wave eigenvalue equation

In frequency domain with exp[i(k·r − ωt)] convention, the differential operators become: ∇ → i k, &partial;/&partial;t → −iω. For a homogeneous medium with dielectric tensor ε and no free charges/currents beyond the constitutive response, the wave equation can be written compactly as: 

∇×(∇×E) = (ω^2/c^2) (ε · E)

Why valid: linear medium, harmonic time dependence, and eliminating B from Maxwell curl equations.

Using the vector identity (valid for sufficiently smooth fields): ∇×(∇×E) = ∇(∇·E) − ∇2E. For a plane wave with k = kẑ, it is more direct to use the algebraic form: 

k × (k × E) = -k^2 E + k (k·E)

For propagation along z in an isotropic background, the electromagnetic wave is transverse: k·E = 0 ⇒ E has only x and y components (E_z = 0). Then k×(k×E) = −k²E, and Eq. (eq9) becomes a 2D eigenvalue problem in the transverse plane: 

k^2 E⊥ = (ω^2/c^2) (ε⊥ · E⊥)   with E⊥ = (E_x, E_y),  k = k ẑ

3.3.2 Build the dielectric tensor from conductivity

The problem gives (CGS): ε = I + i(4π/ω)σ. Using part (a) results (high-ω), compute the transverse components: 

ε_xx = 1 + i(4π/ω) σ_xx = 1 + i(4π/ω) [ i ωp^2/(4π ω) ] = 1 − ωp^2/ω^2
ε_xy = i(4π/ω) σ_xy = i(4π/ω) [ ωc ωp^2/(4π ω^2) ] = i (ωc ωp^2)/ω^3
ε_yx = i(4π/ω) σ_yx = i(4π/ω) [ −ωc ωp^2/(4π ω^2) ] = −i (ωc ωp^2)/ω^3
ε_yy = ε_xx

So the transverse dielectric tensor is: 

ε⊥ = [ ε1   iε2 ]
     [−iε2  ε1  ]

where ε1 = 1 − ωp^2/ω^2,   ε2 = (ωc ωp^2)/ω^3.

3.3.3 Solve the eigenvalue problem (circular polarization diagonalizes ε⊥)

We need nontrivial E⊥ satisfying Eq. (eq11): k^2 E⊥ = (ω^2/c^2) (ε⊥·E⊥). This is an eigenvalue problem because ε⊥ is a linear operator on the 2D polarization space.

Observe that ε⊥ has the structure “scalar + i times a rotation generator”. The eigenvectors are the circular basis: 

E± = (1, ± i)   (right/left circular in x–y)

ε⊥ · E± = (ε1 ± ε2) E±

Why this step is valid: ε⊥ is a linear map; eigenvectors form a basis if eigenvalues are distinct. Here the antisymmetric part (±ε2) splits the degeneracy, selecting handed circular polarizations as eigenmodes.

Plugging into Eq. (eq11) gives: k^2 = (ω2/c2)(ε1 ± ε2). Multiply by c²: 

c^2 k^2 = ω^2 (ε1 ± ε2)
        = ω^2 (1 − ωp^2/ω^2) ± ω^2 (ωc ωp^2/ω^3)
        = (ω^2 − ωp^2) ± (ωc ωp^2)/ω
Final result for part (b): dispersion (two circular modes) 
c^2 k^2 = ω^2 − ωp^2 ± (ωc ωp^2)/ω

Two eigenmodes propagate: right/left circularly polarized waves (E±).

3.3.4 Physical interpretation + sanity checks

  • Cutoff: Without B (ωc=0) we recover c²k² = ω² − ωp², the standard plasma/metal dispersion: propagation requires ω>ωp.
  • Mode splitting: The ± term makes k different for the two circular polarizations → circular birefringence.
  • Faraday rotation: A linearly polarized wave is (E+ + E−)/2. Different k± cause a relative phase Δφ = (k+ − k−)z, rotating the polarization plane with z.
  • Scaling: splitting scales like ωc ωp²/ω, so it grows with B (via ωc) but decreases with frequency (∝ 1/ω for the correction to c²k²).
Math Insight (dispersion as an eigenvalue condition):

For k ‖ ẑ, the operator equation k^2 E⊥ = (ω^2/c^2) ε⊥·E⊥ is a 2×2 Hermitian-like eigenproblem when losses are neglected. The antisymmetric piece (∝ ε2) acts like a generator of rotations, so its eigenvectors are circular polarizations. The eigenvalues (ε1 ± ε2) directly produce the two branches of the dispersion relation.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpreting the final formulas

  • σ_xx = i ωp²/(4π ω): an inertial response. The current is largely reactive (energy stored in electron kinetic motion) when collisions are negligible over a period.
  • σ_xy ∝ ωc/ω²: transverse response from the Lorentz force; smaller at high ω because the velocity can’t rotate much within one optical cycle.
  • c²k² = ω² − ωp²: the medium behaves like a plasma with an effective refractive index squared n² = 1 − ωp²/ω².
  • ± (ωc ωp²)/ω term: a handedness-dependent correction → two different indices for RCP/LCP.

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Increasing n increases ωp, pushing the propagation threshold ω>ωp higher and strengthening the optical response.
  • Increasing B increases ωc, increasing the splitting between the two circular modes, strengthening Faraday rotation per unit length.
  • Increasing ω reduces σ_xy relative to σ_xx (since σ_xy/σ_xx ~ ωc/ω) and reduces the magnitude of the ± correction in c²k².
  • Including finite τ (not taken here) introduces absorption: ε becomes complex and waves attenuate as they propagate.
Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful): circular basis from the start
Define complex circular components: v± = v_x ± i v_y, E± = E_x ± i E_y. Then the Lorentz coupling becomes diagonal: from the component equations, one finds (γ ∓ iωc) v± = -(e/m) E±. So v± = -(e/m) E± /(γ ∓ iωc), and hence σ± = (ne²/m)/(γ ∓ iωc). In the high-ω limit, expand 1/(γ ∓ iωc) to recover σ_xx and σ_xy, and also see immediately why circular polarization is the eigenbasis.

4.3 Concept checks (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why is σ_xx mainly imaginary in this regime? A: Because collisions are negligible (ωτ ≫ 1), so the response is dominated by inertia, giving a 90° phase shift between v and E in harmonic motion.
  • Q: Why do circular polarizations appear as eigenmodes? A: The transverse dielectric tensor has an antisymmetric part (∝ iε2) corresponding to a rotation operator; its eigenvectors are (1, ±i).
  • Q: What happens if B = 0? A: ε2 = 0, the two modes become degenerate (same k), and no Faraday rotation occurs.
  • Q: What physical effect does finite 1/τ introduce? A: Dissipation/absorption: ε becomes complex, k becomes complex, and waves attenuate.

(a) From m dv/dt + (m/τ)v = -e [E + (v×B)/c] with harmonic time dependence e^{-iωt},
the high-ω magnetoconductivity tensor components are

c^2 k^2 = ω^2 − ωp^2 ± (ωc ωp^2)/ω.
