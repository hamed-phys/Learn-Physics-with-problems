Liquid He3 at 0 K: Fermi Energy & Fermi Temperature

We model liquid helium-3 as a degenerate, nearly free Fermi gas of He3 atoms (spin-1/2 fermions) and compute EF and TF from the measured mass density.

Given ρ = 0.081 g·cm−3 (at 0 K) Find EF, TF Model 3D ideal Fermi gas Spin s = 1/2 → g = 2

Quick Summary

  • About: Compute degeneracy scales of liquid He3 at absolute zero.
  • Key physics: Pauli exclusion fills quantum momentum states up to a Fermi sphere.
  • Governing eigenproblem: (−ħ²/2m)∇²ψ = Eψ in a volume V (periodic BC) → plane waves.
  • Counting states: N = g·V·(kF3)/(6π²) ⇒ kF = (3π²n)1/3.
  • Energy scale: EF = ħ²kF²/(2m) (numeric result).
  • Temperature scale: TF = EF/kB (in kelvin).
  • Result type: numeric from density + atom mass; interprets “how quantum” the liquid is.
  • Takeaway: For He3, TF is only a few kelvin → strong degeneracy even at sub-kelvin cryogenic conditions.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Stat mech + quantum

Core definitions (symbols & SI units)

  • ρ: mass density (kg·m−3).
  • m: particle mass (kg) — here, one He3 atom.
  • n: number density = N/V (m−3).
  • g: spin degeneracy = 2s+1; for s=1/2, g=2.
  • ħ: reduced Planck constant (J·s).
  • kB: Boltzmann constant (J·K−1).
  • k: wavevector (m−1).
  • kF: Fermi wavevector (m−1).
  • EF: Fermi energy (J or eV).
  • TF: Fermi temperature (K), defined by kBTF=EF.

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Pauli exclusion forces fermions to occupy distinct quantum states.
  • At T=0, all single-particle states are filled up to a maximum momentum pF=ħkF.
  • EF measures the “top” of the filled sea of states; it sets the scale for degeneracy pressure and low-T thermodynamics.
  • TF tells you when quantum statistics matters: if T ≪ TF, the system is strongly degenerate.

Key laws / principles & validity conditions

  • Nonrelativistic quantum mechanics: single-particle Hamiltonian H = −(ħ²/2m)∇² (kinetic-only) is a good first model if interactions mainly renormalize parameters weakly.
  • Thermodynamic limit: N,V → ∞ with n=N/V fixed; sums over states become integrals in k-space.
  • Degenerate Fermi gas at T=0: occupation f(E) = 1 for E<EF, and 0 for E>EF.
  • Assumption note for liquids: Liquid He3 is interacting, but its low-energy excitations behave like a Fermi liquid; using the ideal-gas counting gives a solid first estimate of EF and TF.

Common models / approximations (why used)

  • Periodic boundary conditions in a cube: avoids messy surfaces; makes eigenstates plane waves.
  • Free-particle dispersion E(k)=ħ²k²/(2m): simplest baseline; captures the main scaling with n and m.
  • Spin degeneracy g=2: accounts for two spin states per spatial k-state.
Optional: what interactions would change

In Landau Fermi-liquid theory, the counting of states (hence kF) remains tied to the density, but the dispersion near the Fermi surface can be written E ≈ EF + (ħ²kF/m*) (k−kF), introducing an effective mass m*. For a first-pass estimate from density alone, using m ≈ atomic mass is standard.

Mini intuition examples (low algebra)

  • If you increase density n, the Fermi sphere radius grows: kF ∝ n1/3, so EF ∝ n2/3.
  • If you use heavier particles, kinetic energy at the same k decreases: EF ∝ 1/m.
  • At T ≪ TF, only a thin shell of width ~kBT around EF participates in thermal processes (heat capacity is small).

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Unit conversions: g·cm−3 → kg·m−3 is a common trap.
  • Using electron mass instead of atomic mass (here the fermions are He3 atoms).
  • Forgetting the spin degeneracy factor g=2 in state counting.
  • Mixing ħ and h (the formulas here use ħ).

Math Insight: the core equation as a mathematical object

The heart of the derivation is an eigenvalue problem for the Laplacian: the operator −∇² on a bounded domain (a cube of volume V) with periodic boundary conditions has plane-wave eigenfunctions ψk(r)=V−1/2ei k·r and eigenvalues k². This turns the physics into counting lattice points in k-space, and in the thermodynamic limit the count becomes a volume integral over a sphere (the “Fermi sphere”).

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Engineering relevance

Where this appears in industry (concrete areas)

  • Dilution refrigerators (He-3/He-4 mixtures) used for quantum computing hardware and ultra-low-temperature research.
  • Cryogenic sensors: bolometers, SQUID environments, and low-noise readout chains where heat capacity and thermal conduction at mK matter.
  • Low-temperature fluid systems in space and scientific instruments (thermal management, calibration baths).

Why engineers care

  • Performance: TF sets how “quantum” thermodynamics is—affects heat capacity, viscosity trends, and transport at low T.
  • Reliability: accurate property models reduce risk in cryogenic operation (thermal runaway, cooldown time, stability margins).
  • Cost & supply: He-3 is scarce/expensive; predictive modeling supports minimizing inventory and optimizing cycle efficiency.

Typical measurement / validation methods

  • Density via volumetric methods or oscillating U-tube densitometry.
  • Heat capacity and spin susceptibility as signatures of Fermi-liquid behavior (sensitive to EF and effective mass).
  • Sound/second sound and transport measurements (viscosity, thermal conductivity) for model calibration.

Design trade-offs & constraints

  • Non-idealities: interactions shift effective mass and transport coefficients; container walls add boundary scattering.
  • Tolerances: small density changes (pressure/temperature) shift EF via n2/3.
  • Safety: cryogenic pressure management, leak prevention, and contamination control (especially at mK).
From lab to product: adapting the ideal model

Industrial design rarely uses a purely ideal-gas model for liquid He-3. Instead, engineers often: (i) keep the state-counting backbone (kF fixed by density), (ii) introduce an effective mass m* and interaction parameters (Landau parameters), (iii) fit to property tables and validated correlations over the operating range. The ideal derivation remains valuable because it gives the correct scaling and order-of-magnitude checks.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Set up

Problem (rewritten): Liquid He3 is a fermionic system (spin 1/2). Given its mass density ρ = 0.081 g·cm−3 at T = 0, estimate the Fermi energy EF and the corresponding Fermi temperature TF.

Given

  • ρ = 0.081 g·cm−3 (at 0 K)
  • He-3 atom spin s = 1/2 → degeneracy g = 2
  • Atomic mass (used in solution): m ≈ m(He-3)

Unknowns

  • EF (J; also convenient in eV or meV)
  • TF (K)

Mathematical model (explicit)

  • Domain: Ω = [0,L]³ with V=L³.
  • Coordinate system: Cartesian (x,y,z) on Ω; r = (x,y,z).
  • Boundary conditions: periodic in all directions.
  • Operator: H = −(ħ²/2m)∇².
  • Eigenproblem: Hφ = Eφ.
  • Filling rule: occupy lowest eigenvalues up to EF (T=0), max g per k.

Assumptions

  • Uniform bulk liquid; no external fields.
  • Nonrelativistic, nearly free-fermion estimate.
  • Thermodynamic limit so sums → integrals.

Approaches

  • k-space counting (chosen): shortest, most geometric.
  • Density of states: equivalent, a bit more integral calculus.
  • Thermo inversion: unnecessary here.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Derivation

Qualitative expectation: Heavy atoms + moderate density ⇒ EF small (Kelvin scale), so He-3 is quantum-degenerate at cryogenic temperatures.

Key equations (copyable)

n = ρ / m
k_F = (3π^2 n)^(1/3)
E_F = (ħ^2 k_F^2) / (2m)
T_F = E_F / k_B

Step 1 — Convert density and compute number density

ρ = 0.081 g·cm^-3
  = 0.081 × (10^-3 kg)/(10^-6 m^3)
  = 81 kg·m^-3

Use m(He-3) ≈ 3.0160293 u with 1 u = 1.66053906660×10−27 kg: 

m = 3.0160293 u
  = 3.0160293 × 1.66053906660×10^-27 kg
  ≈ 5.009×10^-27 kg
n = ρ/m
  ≈ 81 / (5.009×10^-27) m^-3
  ≈ 1.62×10^28 m^-3

Step 2 — Eigenvalue problem and dispersion

With H = −(ħ²/2m)∇² and periodic BC, plane waves are eigenfunctions. Since ∇²eik·r=−k²eik·r: 

E(k) = ħ^2 k^2 / (2m)

Step 3 — State counting (Fermi sphere)

Allowed k form a lattice with spacing Δk=2π/L, so volume per k-state is (2π)³/V. Number of k-states inside |k|≤kF is: 

(# k states) ≈ ((4/3)π k_F^3) / ((2π)^3/V)
            = V k_F^3 / (6π^2)

Multiply by spin degeneracy g=2 and divide by V:

n = N/V = g k_F^3/(6π^2)
⇒ k_F = (6π^2 n/g)^(1/3)
= (3π^2 n)^(1/3)

Step 4 — Compute kF, EF, TF

k_F ≈ (3π^2 × 1.62×10^28)^(1/3)
    ≈ 7.82×10^9 m^-1
E_F = ħ^2 k_F^2/(2m)
≈ (1.0546×10^-34)^2 (7.82×10^9)^2 / (2×5.009×10^-27)
≈ 6.80×10^-23 J
E_F ≈ 6.80×10^-23 J
    ≈ 4.24×10^-4 eV
    = 0.424 meV
T_F = E_F/k_B
   ≈ (6.80×10^-23)/(1.380649×10^-23) K
   ≈ 4.92 K

Final Answer (copyable)

For liquid He-3 at ρ = 0.081 g·cm^-3:
n ≈ 1.62×10^28 m^-3
k_F ≈ 7.82×10^9 m^-1
E_F ≈ 6.80×10^-23 J ≈ 4.24×10^-4 eV = 0.424 meV
T_F ≈ 4.92 K

Sanity checks

  • Units consistent ✅
  • EF on Kelvin scale (few K) ✅
  • Scaling: EF ∝ n2/3/m ✅

Math Insight (conservation/symmetry)

Rotational symmetry of E(k) makes the filled region a sphere in k-space. The step Σk → (V/(2π)³)∫d³k is the continuum limit of a Fourier-mode lattice, justified by the thermodynamic limit (Riemann sum convergence).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Interpretation

Re-interpretation

EF = (ħ²/2m)(3π²n)2/3 shows that density controls k-space filling (geometry), while mass controls kinetic-energy cost.

Parameter dependence

  • n → αn ⇒ TF scales as α2/3.
  • m → βm ⇒ TF scales as 1/β.

Alternative derivation (density of states)

Use n = ∫₀^{E_F} D(E)/V dE with 3D D(E) ∝ √E; the integral gives n ∝ E_F3/2, hence E_F ∝ n2/3, matching the k-space method.

Concept check (answers included)

  • Q: Why does g matter? A: More spin states per k reduces required kF for fixed n.
  • Q: When is the gas “degenerate”? A: When T ≪ TF.
  • Q: If density increases, does EF increase or decrease? A: Increase (∝ n2/3).
