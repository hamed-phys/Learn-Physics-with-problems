Static Magnetoconductivity Tensor (Drude Drift-Velocity Theory)

Derive the matrix form j = σ · E in a magnetic field, then take the high-field limit ωcτ ≫ 1 to obtain the Hall conductivity σxy → ±ne/B and the suppression of longitudinal conduction σxx → 0 (to order 1/(ωcτ)).

Quick Summary

  • What this problem is about: the static (DC) conductivity tensor of a Drude electron gas in a uniform magnetic field.
  • Key physics idea: Lorentz force bends carrier motion, coupling current components and producing the Hall effect.
  • Governing model: Drude equation of motion (ODE) with relaxation time: m dv/dt = q(E + v×B) − m v/τ, steady-state dv/dt = 0.
  • Mathematical structure: solve a linear 3×3 system for v(E), then use j = nq v (or j = −ne v for electrons).
  • Main result: j = (σ0/(1+(ωcτ)^2)) [[1, −ωcτ, 0],[ωcτ,1,0],[0,0,1+(ωcτ)^2]] E.
  • High-field limit: if β = ωcτ ≫ 1, σxy ≈ σ0/β = ne/B (magnitude; sign set by carrier charge), while σxx ≈ σ0/β^2 → 0.
  • Physical meaning: transverse current responds strongly (Hall), longitudinal response is suppressed by cyclotron motion.
  • Result type: symbolic tensor + asymptotics; one optional plot shows how σxx/σ0 and σxy/σ0 vary with ωcτ.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions & SI units

  • E electric field (V/m), B magnetic flux density (T).
  • v drift velocity (m/s), j current density (A/m²).
  • n carrier density (m⁻³), q charge (C). For electrons: q = −e.
  • m (effective) mass (kg), τ momentum-relaxation time (s).
  • ωc cyclotron frequency: ωc = |q|B/m (rad/s). For sign-aware form, use qB/m.
  • σ0 Drude conductivity at B=0: σ0 = n q^2 τ / m (S/m) (note: uses q^2, hence positive).

Validity conditions (what Drude assumes)

  • Classical transport: carriers behave like particles between scattering events.
  • Single relaxation time τ (linear friction term −m v/τ).
  • Uniform, steady fields (no time dependence, no spatial gradients in the bulk).
  • Low enough fields for linear response in E: current is linear in E.
  • Magnetic field enters via Lorentz force; quantum Landau quantization is ignored.

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Without B: carriers accelerate in E but scattering relaxes momentum → steady drift with v ∝ E.
  • With B: Lorentz force q v×B bends trajectories, mixing x and y motion. This produces an off-diagonal conductivity component (Hall).
  • Why a tensor: in a magnetic field, the response in one direction depends on fields in perpendicular directions: j_i = Σ_j σ_ij E_j.

Mini intuition examples (conceptual, minimal algebra)

  • If B = 0, symmetry says σ is isotropic: σ = σ0 I.
  • If B ≠ 0 along z, rotational symmetry about z remains → x and y couple, z stays independent.
  • When ωcτ ≫ 1, carriers complete many cyclotron rotations between collisions → longitudinal drift is strongly hindered, but transverse Hall response persists.

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Sign conventions: the sign of σxy depends on whether you use q (can be negative) or e>0 with electrons.
  • Matrix placement: j = σ E (conductivity) vs E = ρ j (resistivity). They are inverses, but not identical.
  • High-field limit: “σxx → 0” means “suppressed as 1/(ωcτ)^2,” not exactly zero.
  • Units check: ne/B has units of S/m (verify below!).

Math Insight (core equation as a mathematical object)

In steady state, the Drude model becomes a linear algebra problem: a linear operator acting on v equals a forcing vector proportional to E. The magnetic field contributes an antisymmetric operator (cross product with B), so the conductivity tensor naturally splits into a symmetric part (dissipative, ∝ τ) and an antisymmetric part (Hall, odd in B).

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears (real applications)

  • Semiconductor characterization: Hall measurements extract carrier type (n/p), density n, and mobility μ = |q|τ/m.
  • Magnetic field sensing: Hall sensors in automotive (wheel speed, current sensing), consumer electronics (compass modules), industrial automation (position sensing).
  • Metals and thin films: magnetotransport in conductors; quality control for thin-film stacks and interconnects (line width, scattering, reliability).
  • Power electronics & battery systems: non-contact current measurement via Hall effect improves safety/isolation and reduces insertion loss.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: mobility and scattering directly determine conductivity and switching losses.
  • Reliability: scattering mechanisms change with temperature, defects, and aging; Hall characterization can detect process drift.
  • Cost & manufacturability: choosing materials (doping, film quality) trades conductivity vs noise vs stability.

Typical measurement / validation methods

  • Hall bar / van der Pauw: measure transverse voltage under applied B and current to extract R_H, then infer n.
  • Magnetoresistance sweeps: measure σxx(B) or ρxx(B) to fit τ (or mobility).
  • Multi-carrier models: deviations from simple Drude reveal multiple bands, inhomogeneity, or quantum effects.

Design trade-offs & non-idealities

  • Temperature dependence: τ(T) changes due to phonons/impurities; sensor calibration is needed.
  • Geometry & contacts: edge roughness, misalignment, and nonuniform current paths bias Hall extraction.
  • High-field quantum regime: at low T / high B, Landau quantization breaks Drude assumptions (quantum Hall, Shubnikov–de Haas).

From lab to product

Real devices often replace the single τ with an effective mobility model μ(E,T), include parasitic resistances, and use calibration curves. But the Drude tensor is the “first-principles baseline” used to interpret data and sanity-check designs.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem rewritten

Using drift-velocity (Drude) theory, show that in a uniform magnetic field (take B along z) the DC current density can be written in matrix form j = σ(B) E with the stated tensor: 

(jx)          σ0           (  1    −ωcτ   0 ) (Ex)
(jy)   =  -----------  ·   ( ωcτ    1    0 ) (Ey)
(jz)     1+(ωcτ)^2         (  0      0  1+(ωcτ)^2 ) (Ez)

Then, in the high magnetic field limit ωcτ ≫ 1, show that σyx = ne c / B = −σxy (Gaussian-style form), and that σxx is zero to order 1/(ωcτ) (i.e., it is much smaller, scaling as 1/(ωcτ)^2).

Given quantities

  • Drift-velocity (Drude) model with relaxation time τ.
  • Uniform static fields: E and B.
  • Carrier density n, charge (electron) q = −e, mass m.

Unknowns / what to prove

  • Conductivity tensor σij such that ji = Σj σij Ej.
  • Asymptotic high-field forms for off-diagonal Hall terms and longitudinal suppression.

Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Newton + Lorentz force for carriers: gives the ODE for v(t).
  • Linear response in E: drift is proportional to E because the equation is linear in v and E.
  • Symmetry: with B∥ẑ, x–y plane is coupled; z is uncoupled.
  • Steady state: “static” means ∂/∂t = 0 for average drift velocity.

Mathematical model (explicit statement)

  • Domain: homogeneous bulk conductor/semiconductor, treated as a continuum (no spatial dependence of drift quantities in the bulk).
  • Coordinate system: choose Cartesian axes with B = (0,0,B).
  • Fields: E and B constant in space and time (so ∇E = 0, ∂E/∂t = 0, similarly for B).
  • Dynamical equation (ODE): for average drift velocity v(t), m dv/dt = q(E + v×B) − m v/τ.
  • Steady-state condition: dv/dt = 0 (time-independent drift under DC forcing).
  • Current closure: j = n q v (uniform density, single carrier type).
  • Boundary conditions: effectively “bulk” response; surfaces/contacts neglected (so no spatial PDE solve is required here). If one included spatial variation, charge conservation ∂ρ/∂t + ∇·j = 0 would constrain steady states via ∇·j = 0.

Possible approaches (compare)

  • Approach A — Solve the steady-state ODE as a 3×3 linear system (best here): direct, transparent, produces the matrix tensor immediately.
  • Approach B — Use complex notation in the x–y plane: define v+ = vx + i vy, E+ = Ex + i Ey; the magnetic coupling becomes multiplication by iωc. Fast and elegant, but less explicit for beginners.
  • Approach C — Derive resistivity tensor first then invert: useful experimentally (often measure ρ), but adds an extra inversion step.

Chosen approach: Approach A, because it matches the requested matrix form and lets us justify every step as linear algebra with clear physical interpretation.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Set geometry: take B = (0,0,B) so v×B couples x and y only.
  2. Write Drude ODE: m dv/dt = q(E + v×B) − m v/τ.
  3. Impose steady state: set dv/dt = 0 to obtain an algebraic vector equation.
  4. Expand components: write x, y, z equations explicitly (no skipped steps).
  5. Solve the 2×2 subsystem: invert the x–y linear system to get vx, vy in terms of Ex, Ey.
  6. Solve z directly: show vz = (qτ/m) Ez (no magnetic coupling).
  7. Convert to current: use j = nq v (or j = −ne v for electrons).
  8. Identify σ tensor: read off σij and factor out σ0.
  9. Nondimensionalize: define β = ωcτ and rewrite compactly.
  10. High-field asymptotics: expand for β ≫ 1 to show Hall limit and longitudinal suppression.
  11. Sanity checks: units, B→0, sign (electron vs hole), and symmetry (σxy = −σyx when B along z).

Common mistakes (quick tips)

  • For electrons, forgetting q = −e flips the sign of Hall terms.
  • Mixing ωc = eB/m (positive) with sign-aware qB/m without tracking signs.
  • Claiming σxx = 0 exactly at high B: it is small, scaling as 1/β^2.
  • Confusing conductivity tensor with resistivity tensor: ρ = σ^{-1}.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

  • At B=0: current flows along E: j ∥ E.
  • At B≠0 (along z): the Lorentz force pushes carriers sideways → a transverse current response proportional to the perpendicular field component (Hall effect).
  • At high B: cyclotron motion dominates between collisions, reducing net drift along E in the x–y plane, so σxx is strongly suppressed.

Step 1 — Start from the Drude equation (ODE) and specify operators

The drift-velocity theory models the average carrier momentum with a linear relaxation term. The dynamical equation is: 

m (d v/dt) = q ( E + v × B ) − (m/τ) v

This is a first-order linear ODE in time for the vector v(t). The cross product is a linear operator in v when B is fixed. “Static magnetoconductivity” means we want the steady solution where ∂/∂t = 0 for the average drift.

Step 2 — Impose steady state and rewrite as a linear algebra problem

Set dv/dt = 0. Then: 

0 = q ( E + v × B ) − (m/τ) v
(m/τ) v − q (v × B) = q E

This is valid because the equation is linear and time-independent under constant fields; steady state is the fixed point of the ODE. We now solve for v in terms of E.

Step 3 — Choose coordinates and expand the cross product

Take B = (0,0,B). For v = (vx, vy, vz), 

v × B = ( vy B,  −vx B,  0 )

(Check: determinant form gives v×B = (vyBz − vzBy, vzBx − vxBz, vxBy − vyBx). With B = (0,0,B), this reduces to (vyB, −vxB, 0).)

Step 4 — Write component equations (no jumps)

Insert v×B into (m/τ)v − q(v×B) = qE: 

x: (m/τ) vx − q (vy B) = q Ex
y: (m/τ) vy − q (−vx B) = q Ey   ⇒ (m/τ) vy + q (vx B) = q Ey
z: (m/τ) vz − q (0)     = q Ez

Step 5 — Solve the z-component (decoupled)

The z-equation is: 

(m/τ) vz = q Ez  ⇒  vz = (q τ / m) Ez

As expected, motion parallel to B is unaffected by the Lorentz force (since v×B has no z-component).

Step 6 — Solve the coupled x–y system via matrix inversion

Write the x–y equations in matrix form. Multiply both x and y equations by τ/m to simplify: 

vx − (qBτ/m) vy = (qτ/m) Ex
vy + (qBτ/m) vx = (qτ/m) Ey

Define the (sign-aware) cyclotron frequency: 

ωc := (qB/m)   (can be negative for electrons)

Then the system becomes: 

vx − (ωc τ) vy = (qτ/m) Ex
(ωc τ) vx + vy = (qτ/m) Ey

In matrix form: 

[ 1    −ωcτ ] [vx] = (qτ/m) [Ex]
[ ωcτ   1  ] [vy]           [Ey]

The determinant is: 

det = (1)(1) − (−ωcτ)(ωcτ) = 1 + (ωcτ)^2

Since 1+(ωcτ)^2 > 0, the matrix is invertible for all finite B and τ, so the solution exists and is unique. This is a rigorous linear-algebra justification (no hidden assumptions).

The inverse of [[a,b],[c,d]] is (1/(ad−bc)) [[d,−b],[−c,a]]. Applying that here: 

[vx] = (qτ/m) * 1/(1+(ωcτ)^2) * [ 1    ωcτ ] [Ex]
[vy]                              [−ωcτ  1 ] [Ey]

Multiply out explicitly (showing intermediate steps): 

vx = (qτ/m) * 1/(1+(ωcτ)^2) * ( Ex + (ωcτ) Ey )
vy = (qτ/m) * 1/(1+(ωcτ)^2) * (−(ωcτ) Ex + Ey )

Step 7 — Convert drift velocity to current density and identify σ

For a single carrier type with uniform density, the constitutive relation is: 

j = n q v

Therefore: 

jx = n q vx
jy = n q vy
jz = n q vz = n q (qτ/m) Ez

Define the zero-field (Drude) conductivity: 

σ0 := n q^2 τ / m   (always positive because q^2)

Then substituting vx, vy: 

jx = (σ0/(1+(ωcτ)^2)) ( Ex + (ωcτ) Ey )
jy = (σ0/(1+(ωcτ)^2)) (−(ωcτ) Ex + Ey )

And for z: 

jz = σ0 Ez

Note: Many texts rewrite the tensor so that the prefactor σ0/(1+(ωcτ)^2) multiplies a matrix whose zz entry is 1+(ωcτ)^2, making the z-response come out as σ0 automatically.

Step 8 — Assemble the full 3×3 conductivity tensor (matrix form)

Combine the components into j = σ E: 

(jx)          σ0           (  1    +ωcτ   0 ) (Ex)
(jy)   =  -----------  ·   ( −ωcτ   1    0 ) (Ey)
(jz)     1+(ωcτ)^2         (  0      0  1+(ωcτ)^2 ) (Ez)

The x–y sign pattern depends on whether you defined ωc = qB/m (sign-aware) or ωc = |q|B/m (positive). The problem statement’s matrix uses the common convention ωc = |q|B/m > 0 for electrons and builds the electron sign into the off-diagonal entries: σxy = −σ0 ωcτ/(1+(ωcτ)^2), σyx = +σ0 ωcτ/(1+(ωcτ)^2). 

Problem-statement form (ωc>0 for electrons):
(jx)          σ0           (  1    −ωcτ   0 ) (Ex)
(jy)   =  -----------  ·   ( ωcτ    1    0 ) (Ey)
(jz)     1+(ωcτ)^2         (  0      0  1+(ωcτ)^2 ) (Ez)

Step 9 — Nondimensionalization and scaling

Introduce the dimensionless magnetization parameter: 

β := ωc τ

Then (using the problem’s sign convention): 

σxx = σyy = σ0/(1+β^2)
σxy = −σ0 β/(1+β^2)
σyx = +σ0 β/(1+β^2)
σzz = σ0

Scaling: σxx ~ σ0 for β≪1, but σxx ~ σ0/β^2 for β≫1. Meanwhile |σxy| peaks near β≈1 and decays as σ0/β at high β.

Step 10 — High-field asymptotics (β = ωcτ ≫ 1)

Use the expansions (valid because 1/(1+β^2) is smooth and admits a series in 1/β for large β): 

1/(1+β^2) = (1/β^2) * 1/(1+1/β^2)
         = (1/β^2) * (1 − 1/β^2 + O(1/β^4))

Therefore: 

σxx = σ0/(1+β^2) = σ0/β^2 + O(σ0/β^4)
σxy = −σ0 β/(1+β^2) = −σ0*(1/β) + O(σ0/β^3)
σyx = +σ0*(1/β) + O(σ0/β^3)

Now insert σ0 = n q^2 τ/m and β = ωcτ. For the Hall term magnitude: 

|σxy| ≈ σ0/(ωcτ)
     = (n q^2 τ/m) / ( (|q|B/m) τ )          [using ωc = |q|B/m > 0]
     = n |q| / B
For electrons (q = −e):  σxy = −ne/B,  σyx = +ne/B.

In Gaussian/CGS units, many texts write ωc = |q|B/(mc), leading to |σxy| = nec/B. The problem’s Eq. (65) includes the factor c, consistent with that unit convention.

Final Answer (boxed)

Static magnetoconductivity tensor (B along z, Drude model):

j = σ E,  with

σ = (σ0/(1+(ωcτ)^2)) *
    [  1    −ωcτ    0
      ωcτ    1      0
       0     0   1+(ωcτ)^2  ]

where σ0 = n q^2 τ/m  and (problem convention) ωc = |q|B/m > 0.

High-field limit ωcτ ≫ 1:

σyx = +σ0(ωcτ)/(1+(ωcτ)^2) ≈ +σ0/(ωcτ) = n|q|/B   (→ nec/B in Gaussian units),
σxy = −σyx,
σxx = σ0/(1+(ωcτ)^2) ≈ σ0/(ωcτ)^2  = O(1/(ωcτ)^2)  (so “σxx ≈ 0 to order 1/(ωcτ)”).

Interpretation: the Hall conductivity becomes independent of τ at high B (set by n and B), while longitudinal conductivity in the x–y plane is strongly reduced.

Sanity checks

Dimensional analysis

  • σ0 = n q^2 τ/m has units: (m⁻³)(C²)(s)/kg = A/(V·m) = S/m (correct).
  • ne/B units: (C·m⁻³)/T. Since 1 T = kg/(C·s), we get (C·m⁻³)·(C·s/kg) = C² s/(kg m³) = S/m (correct).

Limiting cases & sign

  • B→0 ⇒ ωcτ→0 ⇒ σ→σ0 I (isotropic Ohm’s law).
  • Off-diagonal terms are odd in B (reverse B flips Hall sign): σxy(B) = −σxy(−B).
  • For electrons, Hall sign is negative in σxy as in the problem matrix.
Optional deeper math note: operator form of the cross product

For B = B ẑ, define the linear operator C acting on vectors in the x–y plane by C(vx,vy) = (vy, −vx). Then v×B = B C v (in x–y). The steady equation becomes (I − (qBτ/m) C) v = (qτ/m) E. Since C^2 = −I in the plane, the inverse is explicit: (I − αC)^{-1} = (1/(1+α^2))(I + αC). This algebraic identity is the hidden reason the denominator is 1+(ωcτ)^2.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the tensor: what each term controls

  • Diagonal (σxx, σyy): dissipative longitudinal conduction in the plane perpendicular to B, reduced by factor 1/(1+β^2).
  • Off-diagonal (σxy, σyx): antisymmetric Hall response; it rotates the current relative to E and is odd in B.
  • σzz: unaffected by B in this simple model because the Lorentz force is perpendicular to v.

Parameter dependence (qualitative + useful quantitative scaling)

  • Increasing B increases β=ωcτ: σxx decreases as ~1/B^2 at high B; |σxy| tends to ~ne/B.
  • Increasing τ (cleaner material, fewer scatterers) increases σ0 and β: at fixed B, it increases low-field conductivity but also pushes the system into the high-field regime more easily.
  • Increasing n scales all conductivities proportionally (including Hall magnitude).
  • Increasing m reduces σ0 and ωc; heavier effective mass lowers mobility and weakens magnetic deflection.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

A fast route is to solve the x–y equations using complex variables: let v+ = vx + i vy and E+ = Ex + i Ey. With B∥ẑ, the Lorentz coupling becomes multiplication by −iωc (sign depends on q), giving (1 − iωcτ) v+ = (qτ/m) E+. Taking real and imaginary parts reproduces the tensor elements immediately.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why is σxy = −σyx here? A: With B∥ẑ, the magnetic contribution is an antisymmetric operator (a planar rotation), producing an antisymmetric part of σ.
  • Q: Why doesn’t σzz depend on B in this model? A: Because v×B is perpendicular to v, so it cannot oppose or assist motion along B (no z-component).
  • Q: In high B, why does Hall conductivity lose dependence on τ? A: Because σxy ≈ σ0/(ωcτ) and σ0 ∝ τ, so τ cancels.
  • Q: What does “σxx = 0 to order 1/(ωcτ)” mean? A: σxx scales as 1/(ωcτ)^2, which is smaller than any term of order 1/(ωcτ).

PART 5 — Optional Plot Guide

A plot is useful here because it visually separates three regimes: (i) low-field β≪1 where σxx≈σ0, (ii) crossover near β≈1 where Hall response grows, and (iii) high-field β≫1 where σxx→0 but |σxy| decays slowly (~1/β).

  • x-axis: β = ωcτ (dimensionless, log-spaced for readability).
  • y-axis: normalized conductivity (dimensionless): σ/σ0.
  • What to notice:
    • σxx/σ0 starts at 1 and falls ~1/β^2.
    • |σxy|/σ0 rises ~β for small β, peaks near β=1 at 1/2, then falls ~1/β.
