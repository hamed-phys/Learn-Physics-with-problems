Open Orbits in a Brillouin Zone: k-Space Period and Real-Space Motion (Semiclassical Magnetodynamics)

We use the semiclassical electron equations of motion in a crystal under a magnetic field to estimate the characteristic time scale for an open k-space orbit spanning the Brillouin zone, then interpret what that implies for the electron’s real-space trajectory—an effect that matters directly for anisotropic magnetoresistance in metals.

Quick Summary

  • What this is about: Electron motion on an open constant-energy contour in k-space that connects opposite Brillouin-zone faces.
  • Key physics idea: In a magnetic field, the crystal momentum evolves via the semiclassical Lorentz-force law in k-space.
  • Governing equations: ṙ = v(k) = (1/ħ)∇kε(k) and ħ k̇ = −(e/c) v(k)×B (Gaussian-cgs form).
  • Given: separation between zone faces G = 2×108 cm−1, field B = 103 gauss, typical speed v ≈ 105 cm/s.
  • What we compute: an order-of-magnitude period T for traversing the open orbit in k-space (one “Brillouin-zone translation” by G).
  • Result type: numeric order-of-magnitude: T ≈ 10−7 s (about 0.1 microseconds).
  • Physical meaning: the velocity (and thus current response) varies with this time scale; in real space, the electron exhibits oscillatory motion plus a net drift (not a closed cyclotron circle).
  • Engineering relevance: open orbits cause strong anisotropic magnetoresistance and can dominate conduction along certain crystal directions.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols & units)
  • k (wavevector / crystal momentum): units 1/length (SI: m−1, here: cm−1).
  • ε(k) band energy: SI J (often eV in practice).
  • v(k) group velocity: cm/s (or m/s).
  • B magnetic field: gauss (cgs) or tesla (SI). Here: 103 gauss.
  • G reciprocal-lattice vector magnitude spanning zone faces: cm−1.
  • ħ reduced Planck constant: erg·s (cgs) or J·s (SI).
  • e elementary charge magnitude: statC (cgs) or C (SI). Electron charge is −e.
  • c speed of light: cm/s in cgs (appears in Lorentz force in Gaussian units).
Physical meaning & intuition
  • Constant-energy motion in k-space: in a purely magnetic field (no E), the electron moves along ε(k)=const. because magnetic forces do no work.
  • Open orbit: the intersection of the Fermi surface with the plane perpendicular to B is an open curve that crosses the Brillouin zone and reconnects via k → k + G.
  • Why “period” exists for open motion: even if the k-trajectory is not geometrically closed within one zone, the band structure is periodic in reciprocal space, so k(t) repeats modulo G, making v(t) periodic.

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

Semiclassical electron dynamics (magnetic field only) 
ṙ = v(k) = (1/ħ) ∇_k ε(k)
ħ k̇ = −(e/c) [ v(k) × B ]    (Gaussian cgs)
(Equivalent SI form: ħ k̇ = −e [ v(k) × B ])
Copied text is plain (no formatting).

Valid when the wavepacket is well-defined, fields vary slowly on the lattice scale, and scattering is not so strong that k becomes ill-defined on the timescale of interest. (We ignore Berry curvature and Zeeman effects here; they are usually smaller for simple order-of-magnitude timing.)

Mathematical model (variables, domains, coordinate system)

Let the open orbit lie in the kx–ky plane, and the magnetic field be B = B ẑ normal to this plane. The electron moves on the constant-energy manifold ε(kx,ky) = εF (Fermi energy for transport). The Brillouin zone is periodic: ε(k + G) = ε(k) for reciprocal lattice vectors G.

We will treat v(k) as having a typical magnitude v along the orbit, as the problem explicitly provides v ≈ 105 cm/s for an order-of-magnitude estimate.

Common models/approximations and why they’re used

  • Single-band semiclassics: captures magnetotransport timing and geometry without full quantum Landau quantization.
  • Constant |v| estimate: for order-of-magnitude periods, we approximate |v(k)| by a representative value.
  • Neglect scattering: the “period” is a dynamical timescale; scattering would limit how many cycles are coherent, but does not set the basic k̇ rate.

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • Closed orbit (usual cyclotron motion): k(t) traces a closed contour; r(t) is periodic (often circular/elliptic) with cyclotron frequency.
  • Open orbit: k(t) traverses across the zone and repeats modulo G; r(t) shows oscillations plus a net drift, producing strong directional conductivity.

What to watch for (pitfalls & misconceptions)

  • Unit system trap: in Gaussian-cgs the Lorentz term has a factor 1/c; in SI it does not. Keep consistent units.
  • “Open orbit means no period” (false): k is not closed within one zone, but the band is periodic, so v(t) can be periodic with a translation by G.
  • Mixing G as a vector vs magnitude: timing uses |Δk| ≈ |G|; drift direction uses the vector cross product B×G.
Math Insight: the core equation as a mathematical object

The equation ħ k̇ = −(e/c) v(k)×B is a first-order autonomous ODE on the constant-energy manifold. Because v(k) = (1/ħ)∇kε, we get dε/dt = (∇kε)·k̇ = ħ v·k̇ = −(e/c) v·(v×B) = 0, i.e., a conservation law: energy is constant along trajectories. Geometrically, the flow is tangent to ε(k)=const curves and is generated by a cross-product “rotation” with B.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Open orbits are not a niche curiosity: they are one of the clearest ways that Fermi-surface geometry controls macroscopic transport. Engineers often encounter the consequences even if they never draw a Brillouin zone.

Where this appears in industry (concrete areas)

  • Magnetic-field sensors & precision instrumentation: anisotropic magnetoresistance and field-dependent conductivity can be exploited or must be mitigated.
  • Cryogenic electronics and superconducting magnet environments: in high fields and low temperatures, long mean free paths allow open-orbit effects to dominate.
  • Metallurgy & alloy design: Fermi-surface topology changes with composition/strain; transport anisotropy can indicate phase, texture, or electronic reconstruction.
  • Reliability in high-field rotating machinery: conductive parts in strong B can exhibit directional heating due to anisotropic resistivity, affecting thermal margins.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: conductivity can become highly directional, altering current crowding and losses.
  • Safety/reliability: unexpected magnetoresistance can increase Joule heating and drift in sensor calibration.
  • Cost & manufacturability: controlling crystal texture (rolling, annealing) can tune anisotropy without exotic materials.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Angle-dependent magnetoresistance (ADMR): rotate B relative to crystal axes to detect open-orbit signatures.
  • de Haas–van Alphen / Shubnikov–de Haas oscillations: quantum oscillations reveal extremal orbit areas; disappearance/changes can indicate open-orbit regimes.
  • Hall measurements and tensor transport fits: extract anisotropic conductivity components under B.

Design trade-offs and constraints (non-idealities)

  • Scattering/defects: if the mean free time τ is shorter than the orbit timescale, open-orbit effects are suppressed.
  • Texture & grain boundaries: polycrystals average over orientations, reducing anisotropy unless textured.
  • Finite temperature: smears the Fermi surface and can wash out sharp geometric features.

From lab to product

Real devices incorporate multi-band effects, scattering, and microstructure. The idealized semiclassical model is adapted by using an effective velocity distribution, incorporating relaxation-time scattering, and averaging over the measured orientation distribution function (ODF) in textured metals. Still, the core timing and geometry from k̇ often correctly predicts when anisotropy becomes large.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem rewritten in plain words

An electron in a monovalent tetragonal metal has an open orbit on the Fermi surface in k-space. The open path connects opposite faces of the Brillouin zone that are separated by a reciprocal-space distance G = 2×108 cm−1. A magnetic field B = 103 gauss is applied normal to the plane of that open orbit. (a) Estimate the period (time scale) of the motion in k-space, given a typical speed v ≈ 105 cm/s. (b) Describe the corresponding real-space motion.

Given
  • Reciprocal-space separation: G = 2×108 cm−1
  • Magnetic field: B = 103 gauss = 10−1 tesla
  • Typical speed (group velocity): v = 105 cm/s
  • Field direction: B ⟂ (orbit plane)
Unknowns
  • (a) Order-of-magnitude k-space period T
  • (b) Qualitative real-space trajectory r(t)

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Semiclassical equations of motion: appropriate for electron wavepackets in weakly perturbed crystals; B bends trajectories in k-space.
  • Energy conservation under B: ensures k stays on ε(k)=const and just moves along the orbit.
  • Reciprocal periodicity: even “open” orbits repeat modulo G, enabling a meaningful “period” for v(t).

We do not need full quantum Landau-level quantization because the question asks only for an order-of-magnitude time scale and a qualitative real-space description.

Assumptions (explicit)
  • Single-band semiclassical dynamics; no electric field (E = 0).
  • Velocity magnitude along the orbit is of order the given v (treat as roughly constant for timing).
  • B is uniform and strictly perpendicular to the orbit plane, so v ⟂ B for motion confined to that plane.
  • We neglect scattering during one orbit when defining T; scattering only limits coherence, not the instantaneous k̇ law.

2–3 approaches and comparison

  • Approach A (direct k̇ estimate): use |k̇| ≈ (eB/ħc)|v| (cgs) and T ≈ G/|k̇|. Best for quick order-of-magnitude.
  • Approach B (cyclotron-frequency analogy): define an effective cyclotron frequency ω ~ eB/m* and infer a time scale. Needs m* and orbit size; not given → weaker.
  • Approach C (real-space displacement per G): integrate v = ṙ after relating v to k̇; gives geometric drift direction and magnitude. Best for part (b).

Choice: Use Approach A for (a) and Approach C for (b), because they use exactly the data given (G, B, v) and directly encode open-orbit periodicity.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Set coordinates: choose B = B ẑ and the orbit in kx–ky plane. (Geometry simplification.)
  2. Write semiclassical ODE: ħ k̇ = −(e/c) v×B. (Governing law.)
  3. Use perpendicularity: since v ⟂ B, |v×B| = vB, so |k̇| ≈ (eB/ħc) v. (Magnitude estimate.)
  4. Define the “period” for an open orbit: one cycle corresponds to k advancing by Δk ≈ G (translation across the zone). (Periodic modulo G.)
  5. Compute time scale: T ≈ G / |k̇| = ħcG/(eBv). (Main result for part a.)
  6. Dimensional check: verify T has units of seconds. (Sanity check.)
  7. Real-space motion relation: ṙ = v(k); relate average v to k̇ via cross-product identity and integrate over one “period” to find drift direction. (Part b.)
  8. Interpret qualitatively: oscillatory component + steady drift; drift direction set by B×G. (Engineering intuition.)
Common mistakes
  • Dropping the 1/c factor while using gauss (cgs) units.
  • Using Δk as the full orbit circumference (not given) instead of the zone-translation scale G (explicitly given).
  • Confusing the real-space cyclotron radius with the k-space span: this is a Bloch electron problem, not a free-particle circle.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition first (qualitative expectation)

A stronger magnetic field bends the k-space trajectory faster, so the k-space “period” should scale like 1/B. A larger reciprocal-space span G should take longer to traverse, so T ∝ G. A larger electron speed v means k evolves faster (because Lorentz deflection depends on velocity), so T ∝ 1/v. We therefore expect T ~ (const)·G/(Bv).

Step 1: State the governing equations (with symbols defined)

Let k(t) be the electron’s crystal wavevector, and ε(k) the band energy. The semiclassical group velocity is v(k) = ṙ = (1/ħ)∇kε(k). Under a magnetic field B (no electric field), the k-space equation of motion is 

ṙ = v(k) = (1/ħ) ∇_k ε(k)
ħ k̇ = −(e/c) [ v(k) × B ]      (Gaussian-cgs)
Use cgs consistently with gauss.

Step 2: Choose geometry and reduce to a magnitude estimate

The field is normal to the orbit plane, so choose coordinates such that B = B ẑ and the orbit is in the kx–ky plane. Then v is in-plane, hence v ⟂ B.

Take magnitudes of the k̇ equation: 

|ħ k̇| = (e/c) |v × B|
Since v ⟂ B:  |v × B| = v B
Therefore:     |k̇| = (e B / (ħ c)) v
No “magic jump”: used |a×b| = |a||b|sinθ with θ=90°.

Step 3: Define the “period” for an open orbit

The orbit is open in the first Brillouin zone, connecting opposite faces separated by a reciprocal vector magnitude G. Because the crystal is periodic in reciprocal space, ε(k+G) = ε(k), so when k advances by Δk ≈ G the local band velocity pattern repeats. Thus we define the characteristic period T by 

Δk over one cycle (modulo reciprocal periodicity):  Δk ≈ G
Time scale:   T ≈ Δk / |k̇|  ≈  G / |k̇|
This is the natural “repeat time” for v(t), not a geometrical closure in one zone.

Step 4: Combine results to get T (symbolic)

Substitute |k̇| from Step 2 into the period definition: 

T ≈ G / |k̇|
  = G / [ (eB/(ħc)) v ]
  = (ħ c G) / (e B v)
Scaling: T ∝ G/(Bv), as expected.

Step 5: Insert numbers (order-of-magnitude in cgs)

Use: ħ ≈ 1.055×10−27 erg·s, c ≈ 3.0×1010 cm/s, e ≈ 4.80×10−10 statC, G = 2×108 cm−1, B = 103 gauss, v = 105 cm/s. 

T ≈ (ħ c G)/(e B v)

Numerator: ħ c G ≈ (1.055×10^−27)(3.0×10^10)(2×10^8)
          ≈ 6.33×10^−9  (cgs-consistent)

Denominator: e B v ≈ (4.80×10^−10)(10^3)(10^5)
           ≈ 4.80×10^−2

So: T ≈ (6.33×10^−9)/(4.80×10^−2) ≈ 1.3×10^−7 s
Order-of-magnitude: ~10−7 s.
Final answer (boxed)
(a) k-space period (order of magnitude):
T ≈ (ħ c G)/(e B v)  ≈ 1×10^−7 s  (more precisely ~1.3×10^−7 s)

(b) Real-space motion:
Because the orbit is open in k-space (repeats only modulo G), r(t) is not a closed cyclotron circle.
The electron’s velocity v(k(t)) varies periodically with period T, but its position shows:
- an oscillatory component, and
- a net drift in a fixed in-plane direction set by B×G (perpendicular to both B and the zone-connecting reciprocal vector).

Over one k-space “cycle” (Δk = G), the net real-space displacement is:
Δr = (ħ c / (e B^2)) [ B × G ]   (directional drift across the lattice).
Includes both (a) and (b).

Sanity checks

  • Dimensional analysis: from T ≈ G/|k̇|, and k̇ has units 1/(length·time), so T has units time. ✔
  • Scaling: larger B → smaller T (faster traversal). Larger G → larger T. Larger v → smaller T. ✔
  • Limiting case: B → 0 implies T → ∞ (no magnetic bending in k-space). ✔
  • Physical interpretation: ~10−7 s is a slow (microelectronic) timescale compared with typical scattering times in good metals at room temperature (often much shorter), implying open-orbit periodicity is most visible when τ is sufficiently long (low T / high purity). ✔
Optional deeper note: why Δr depends only on ∫dk = G (geometry)

Starting from ħ k̇ = −(e/c) v×B, take B×(…) and use the vector identity B×(v×B) = B(B·v) − vB2. Since v ⟂ B in the orbit plane, B·v = 0, hence B×k̇ = (eB2/(ħc)) v. Therefore, 

v = ṙ = (ħ c / (e B^2)) [ B × k̇ ].

Integrate over one "cycle" where k changes by Δk = ∫ k̇ dt = G:

Δr = ∫ ṙ dt
   = (ħ c / (e B^2)) [ B × ∫ k̇ dt ]
   = (ħ c / (e B^2)) [ B × G ].
This reveals a topological/geometric flavor: only net Δk matters.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the final formulas

Timing (k-space): 
T ≈ (ħ c G)/(e B v)
Shows the controlling parameters cleanly.
  • ħ: quantum scale that converts band gradients into velocities and sets how quickly k responds to forces.
  • G: how far k must travel before the band structure repeats (open-orbit translation scale).
  • B: magnetic curvature strength; stronger B → faster k evolution.
  • v: the carrier speed; faster electrons bend through k-space faster.
Real-space drift per cycle: 
Δr = (ħ c / (e B^2)) [ B × G ]
Direction = B×G (in-plane, perpendicular to G).

This shows the motion is not a closed loop in real space: each k-space “repeat” shifts the electron by a finite Δr. This directional drift is one reason open orbits can produce extremely large, anisotropic magnetoresistance.

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Increase B: decreases T ~ 1/B; also decreases drift magnitude per cycle ~ 1/B (since |Δr| ~ (ħc/e)(G/B)).
  • Increase G: increases T linearly; increases |Δr| linearly (bigger zone-to-zone translation implies bigger drift per cycle).
  • Increase v: decreases T; does not appear explicitly in Δr (because Δr used only the net Δk = G). In real materials, v(k) variations still affect the detailed oscillatory part.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of using magnitudes, one can treat the k-space trajectory as a Hamiltonian-like flow on ε(k)=const, with B defining a symplectic structure. The “period” for open motion then emerges from reciprocal periodicity: k(t+T)=k(t)+G, implying v(t+T)=v(t) because ε and ∇kε are periodic in k.

Concept check (self-test Q&A)

  • Q: Why is ε(k) constant in time when only B is applied?
    A: dε/dt = (∇kε)·k̇ = ħ v·k̇ ∝ v·(v×B)=0.
  • Q: If the orbit is open, what repeats with period T?
    A: k repeats modulo G, so the band velocity v(k) (and thus current contribution) repeats.
  • Q: How does doubling B change the period?
    A: T halves (T ∝ 1/B).
  • Q: What sets the direction of net drift in real space?
    A: The vector cross product B×G (perpendicular to both B and the zone-connecting reciprocal vector).
  • Q: What experimental signature suggests open orbits?
    A: Strong angle-dependent magnetoresistance: large conductivity along a preferred direction for certain B orientations.
