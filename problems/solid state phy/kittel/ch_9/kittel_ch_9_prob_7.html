de Haas–van Alphen period of potassium (free-electron model) & real-space orbit area

We compute the oscillation period in 1/B (Onsager relation) for potassium treated as a free-electron metal, and then estimate the real-space cyclotron orbit area at B = 10 kG = 1 T. Same period applies to Shubnikov–de Haas oscillations in resistivity.

Model: 3D free-electron Key result: Δ(1/B) = 2πe/(ħAext) Field example: B = 1 T

Quick Summary

  • What this is about: Quantum oscillations of a metal’s magnetization (de Haas–van Alphen) are periodic in 1/B.
  • Key physics idea: Landau quantization makes the density of states oscillate as Landau levels pass through the Fermi energy.
  • Governing relation (Onsager): The oscillation “frequency” in 1/B is set by the extremal Fermi-surface cross-sectional area A_ext normal to B.
  • Core equation: F = (ħ/2πe) A_ext and the period is Δ(1/B) = 1/F = 2πe/(ħ A_ext).
  • Free-electron potassium: assume one conduction electron per atom and a spherical Fermi surface.
  • Result type: numeric (using standard material constants: density and molar mass) with clear physical meaning (how fast oscillations occur as you sweep 1/B).
  • Computed period: Δ(1/B) ≈ 5.66×10−5 T−1 (equivalently 5.66×10−6 kG−1).
  • Real-space orbit at B=1 T: cyclotron radius rc ≈ 4.82 μm, area πrc2 ≈ 7.30×10−11 m2.
  • Engineering tie-in: Same period governs Shubnikov–de Haas oscillations used for Fermi-surface mapping and mobility diagnostics.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (theory before solving)

Core definitions (symbols & SI units)

  • Magnetic field B [T]. Often quoted in gauss: 1 T = 10 kG.
  • Electron charge magnitude e [C]. (Electron charge is −e.)
  • Reduced Planck constant ħ [J·s].
  • Fermi wavevector k_F [m−1].
  • Extremal k-space area A_ext [m−2] of the Fermi surface cross-section ⟂ to B.
  • Onsager frequency F [T], defined by F = (ħ/2πe) A_ext.
  • Period in inverse field Δ(1/B) [T−1].

Physical meaning (intuition)

In a magnetic field, electronic motion transverse to B becomes quantized into Landau levels. As you sweep B, the spacing of these levels changes. Whenever a Landau level crosses the Fermi energy, thermodynamic quantities (like magnetization) and transport (like resistivity) oscillate.

  • Why periodic in 1/B? Because the Landau level index at the Fermi energy scales like n ∝ 1/B.
  • Why extremal area? Contributions from different orbits interfere; stationary-phase (extremal) orbits dominate.

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

  • Semiclassical quantization (Lifshitz–Onsager): quantizes k-space orbits in a magnetic field.
  • Validity: clean enough sample so Landau levels are not completely smeared (finite scattering time), and sufficiently low temperature.
  • Geometry: choose B direction; A_ext is the extremal cross-section ⟂ B.

Mathematical model (variables, domain, coordinates)

We model conduction electrons in potassium as a 3D free-electron gas in a uniform magnetic field. Choose Cartesian coordinates with B = B ẑ.

Quantum model: minimal coupling Hamiltonian (operator form) 
H = (1/2m) ( -iħ∇ + eA(r) )^2
with  B = ∇×A,  and  B = B z-hat  (uniform)

The uniform-field problem leads to Landau levels for motion in the (x,y)-plane and free motion along z. The de Haas–van Alphen period is governed by the semiclassical quantization of closed k-space orbits.

Common models/approximations (and why)

  • Free-electron dispersion: ε(k) = ħ²k²/(2m). Good for alkali metals as a first approximation.
  • Spherical Fermi surface: then A_ext = πk_F² for B along any direction.
  • One conduction electron per atom: potassium is monovalent → electron density n equals atomic density.

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • If the Fermi surface cross-section doubles, the oscillations become twice as fast in 1/B (period halves).
  • Rotating B in an anisotropic metal changes A_ext(θ) → angle-dependent oscillation frequency (Fermi-surface mapping).

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Units: A_ext is in k-space (m−2), not a real-space area.
  • Extremal means max/min cross-section along k_z; for a sphere it’s the equatorial section.
  • Field units conversion: 10 kG = 1 T and Δ(1/B) rescales accordingly.

Math Insight

The key relation F = (ħ/2πe)A_ext is a geometric spectral rule: it links a measurable oscillation frequency to the area of a level set in momentum space. Mathematically, it arises from semiclassical quantization of closed orbits (an action integral) and is closely related to a Bohr–Sommerfeld condition on a periodic trajectory.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where it appears in industry (examples)

  • Semiconductor & metal characterization: quantum oscillations to extract effective masses and Fermi-surface geometry.
  • Cryogenic electronics & sensors: magnetoresistance oscillations can affect precision components in high fields.
  • Superconducting magnet systems: understanding normal-metal components (stabilizers, leads) under strong fields and low T.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: mobility and scattering time influence oscillation visibility → indicates material purity/defect density.
  • Reliability: transport anomalies at low T/high B can matter for stable operation of cryogenic systems.
  • Cost & manufacturability: high-quality crystals and processing control are required; oscillations are a sensitive QA tool.

Measurement / validation in practice

  • de Haas–van Alphen: torque magnetometry or pickup coils measure oscillatory magnetization vs B.
  • Shubnikov–de Haas: 4-probe resistivity vs B at low T; Fourier analysis in 1/B yields F.
  • Cross-checks: compare extracted A_ext and effective mass to band-structure calculations.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Non-idealities: impurity scattering broadens Landau levels (Dingle damping) and reduces oscillation amplitude.
  • Temperature: thermal smearing around the Fermi level suppresses oscillations; cryogenic operation is often needed.
  • Real materials: potassium is reactive; sample prep/encapsulation impacts achievable purity and surface quality.
From lab to product: adapting the ideal model

In products, you rarely have an ideal free-electron sphere. Engineers incorporate: (i) anisotropic effective mass tensors, (ii) multiband Fermi surfaces, (iii) finite-thickness and surface scattering, and (iv) temperature- and disorder-dependent damping. Yet the same Onsager area rule remains the backbone for interpreting frequencies, while amplitude models handle the “messy” parts.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Problem restatement (in plain words)

(a) Using the free-electron model for potassium, compute the expected period of de Haas–van Alphen oscillations in inverse magnetic field, Δ(1/B). (b) For a magnetic field B = 10 kG = 1 T, estimate the real-space area enclosed by the extremal cyclotron orbit.

Given / implied

  • Metal: potassium (monovalent) → one conduction electron per atom.
  • Model: 3D free-electron gas; spherical Fermi surface.
  • Field in (b): B = 10 kG = 1 T.
  • Material constants needed for a numeric value (standard): mass density ρ, molar mass M, Avogadro N_A.

Unknowns

  • (a) Δ(1/B) (period in inverse field).
  • (b) Real-space orbit area A_real = π r_c^2 at B=1 T.

Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Onsager relation: directly connects oscillation frequency to extremal Fermi-surface area; applies to dHvA and SdH.
  • Free-electron Fermi sphere: gives A_ext = πk_F^2 once k_F is known.
  • Semiclassical cyclotron radius: for free electrons, r_c = v_F/ω_c with ω_c = eB/m, or equivalently r_c = ħk_F/(eB).

Assumptions (explicit)

  • Single-band, isotropic free-electron dispersion ε(k)=ħ²k²/(2m).
  • One conduction electron per potassium atom.
  • Ignoring band-structure distortions and spin-splitting for the period (these mainly affect amplitude/phase).
  • Uniform magnetic field; semiclassical orbit picture valid (many Landau levels occupied).

2–3 possible approaches (compare)

  1. Onsager + density method (best here): compute electron density n from ρ, M, then k_F=(3π²n)^{1/3}, then A_ext, then Δ(1/B). Pros: direct, standard, minimal extra data. Cons: relies on tabulated density.
  2. Use known Fermi energy for K: if E_F is provided elsewhere, infer k_F from E_F=ħ²k_F²/(2m). Pros: quick if E_F known. Cons: needs that extra input.
  3. Use lattice constant: from bcc structure and lattice parameter, compute atomic density then n. Pros: crystallographic. Cons: needs lattice parameter.

Chosen approach: (1) Onsager + density method, because it is self-contained with standard constants and aligns with the free-electron model requested.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Find electron density n: use n = (ρ/M) N_A. Meaning: number of conduction electrons per unit volume.
  2. Compute Fermi wavevector k_F: use k_F = (3π²n)^{1/3}. Meaning: radius of the Fermi sphere in k-space.
  3. Get extremal area A_ext: for a sphere, A_ext = πk_F². Meaning: largest cross-section ⟂ B.
  4. Use Onsager relation: Δ(1/B) = 2πe/(ħA_ext). Meaning: spacing of oscillations in inverse field.
  5. Real-space orbit at B=1 T: use r_c = ħk_F/(eB) then A_real = πr_c². Meaning: physical size of electron’s cyclotron path.
  6. Sanity checks: dimensions, order-of-magnitude, and scaling with n and B.

Common mistakes

  • Mixing k-space area A_ext (m−2) with real-space orbit area (m2).
  • Forgetting that the period is in 1/B, not in B.
  • Unit slip: using gauss/kG without consistent conversion (1 T = 10 kG).
  • Dropping the factor 2π in the Onsager relation.

PART 3 — Full Solution (detailed + teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

Potassium has a relatively low electron density compared with typical transition metals, so its Fermi wavevector is moderate. A spherical Fermi surface means a single dominant oscillation frequency. Because F ∝ A_ext ∝ k_F² ∝ n^{2/3}, we expect Δ(1/B) ∝ 1/F ∝ n^{-2/3}.

Step 1: Electron density n from mass density

For a monovalent metal, conduction electron density equals atomic number density: n = n_atoms. Using mass density ρ and molar mass M, the molar density is ρ/M [mol/m³], and multiplying by N_A gives atoms per m³.

Electron density 
n = (ρ / M) N_A   [m^-3]   (monovalent: 1 electron per atom)

Using standard values for potassium: ρ ≈ 0.862 g/cm³ = 862 kg/m³, M ≈ 39.10 g/mol = 3.910×10^-2 kg/mol, N_A = 6.022×10^23 mol^-1.

Compute:

Numeric evaluation of n 
n = (862 / 3.910e-2) * 6.022e23
  ≈ 1.33×10^28  m^-3

Why this step is valid: It is a conservation/counting argument (mass → moles → atoms → electrons). Linearity of counting means proportionality is exact under the monovalent assumption.

Step 2: Fermi wavevector kF for a 3D free-electron gas

In k-space, each allowed quantum state occupies volume (2π)^3/V. Filling all states inside a sphere of radius k_F (two spin states) gives:

3D free-electron density–kF relation 
n = (k_F^3) / (3π^2)   ⇒   k_F = (3π^2 n)^(1/3)

Substitute n ≈ 1.33×10^28 m^-3:

Numeric kF 
k_F = (3π^2 n)^(1/3)
    ≈ (3π^2 · 1.33×10^28)^(1/3)
    ≈ 7.33×10^9  m^-1
Dimensional analysis check (why units work)

In k_F = (3π^2 n)^(1/3), n has units m−3. Taking the cube root gives m−1, as required for a wavevector.

Step 3: Extremal cross-sectional area Aext in k-space

With B along z, the semiclassical cyclotron motion corresponds to a closed orbit in the (k_x,k_y) plane at fixed k_z. For a spherical Fermi surface, the maximal (extremal) cross-section is at k_z=0, a circle of radius k_F.

Extremal area for a sphere 
A_ext = π k_F^2

Numerically:

Numeric Aext 
A_ext = π (7.33×10^9)^2
     ≈ 1.69×10^20  m^-2

Step 4: de Haas–van Alphen period Δ(1/B) from Onsager relation

The Lifshitz–Onsager quantization condition (semiclassical, large Landau index) implies the oscillation frequency in inverse field is set by the k-space area:

Onsager relation & period 
F = (ħ / 2πe) A_ext        [T]
Δ(1/B) = 1/F = (2πe) / (ħ A_ext)   [T^-1]

Substitute ħ = 1.0546×10^-34 J·s, e = 1.6022×10^-19 C, and A_ext ≈ 1.69×10^20 m^-2:

Numeric period 
Δ(1/B) = (2πe)/(ħ A_ext)
      ≈ (2π·1.6022×10^-19) / (1.0546×10^-34 · 1.69×10^20)
      ≈ 5.66×10^-5  T^-1
Unit check for Δ(1/B)

ħ A_ext has units (J·s)·(m−2). Using 1 T = 1 kg/(C·s) and J = kg·m²/s², one can show (2πe)/(ħA_ext) reduces to T−1. Practically: Onsager’s formula is constructed to yield F in tesla.

Answer (a): period in inverse field

Δ(1/B) ≈ 5.66×10−5 T−1.
Since 1 T = 10 kG, in kG units: Δ(1/B) ≈ 5.66×10−6 kG−1.

Step 5: Real-space extremal orbit area at B = 10 kG = 1 T

Now we translate the k-space orbit into a real-space cyclotron orbit size. For a free electron, v = (1/ħ)∇_k ε = ħk/m. The semiclassical equations of motion are:

Semiclassical dynamics (operator/field form) 
ε(k) = ħ^2 k^2 / (2m)
v(k) = (1/ħ) ∇_k ε = ħ k / m
ħ (dk/dt) = -e (v × B)

For B = B ẑ, the motion is circular in the plane perpendicular to z with cyclotron frequency ω_c = eB/m (magnitude). The real-space cyclotron radius is:

Cyclotron radius (free electron) 
r_c = v_⊥ / ω_c
    = (ħ k_⊥ / m) / (eB / m)
    = ħ k_⊥ / (eB)

For the extremal orbit of a spherical Fermi surface, k_⊥ = k_F. Therefore the orbit area in real space is:

Real-space orbit area 
A_real = π r_c^2 = π (ħ k_F / (eB))^2

Insert k_F ≈ 7.33×10^9 m^-1 and B = 1 T:

Numeric r_c and A_real for B=1 T 
r_c = ħ k_F / (eB)
    ≈ (1.0546×10^-34 · 7.33×10^9) / (1.6022×10^-19 · 1)
    ≈ 4.82×10^-6  m  = 4.82 μm

A_real = π r_c^2 ≈ π (4.82×10^-6)^2
      ≈ 7.30×10^-11  m^2

Answer (b): real-space area at B = 10 kG = 1 T

rc ≈ 4.82 μm, hence the extremal orbit area is
Areal = πrc2 ≈ 7.30×10−11 m2.

Sanity checks

  • Scaling: Δ(1/B) ∝ 1/A_ext → larger Fermi surface gives smaller period (more rapid oscillations).
  • B dependence (real-space): r_c ∝ 1/B and A_real ∝ 1/B², consistent with tighter orbits at higher field.
  • Magnitude: micron-scale cyclotron radius at 1 T is plausible for v_F ~ 10^6 m/s.

Final answer (copy-friendly)

Final numeric results 
de Haas–van Alphen (and Shubnikov–de Haas) period for potassium (free-electron model):
Δ(1/B) = 2πe/(ħA_ext),  A_ext = πk_F^2,  k_F=(3π^2 n)^(1/3),  n=(ρ/M)N_A

Using ρ≈862 kg/m^3, M≈3.910×10^-2 kg/mol:
n≈1.33×10^28 m^-3,  k_F≈7.33×10^9 m^-1,  A_ext≈1.69×10^20 m^-2
Δ(1/B)≈5.66×10^-5 T^-1  (≈5.66×10^-6 kG^-1)

Real-space extremal orbit at B=10 kG=1 T:
r_c = ħk_F/(eB) ≈ 4.82 μm
A_real = πr_c^2 ≈ 7.30×10^-11 m^2

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (theory around the result)

Re-interpreting the final formula

The period Δ(1/B) = 2πe/(ħA_ext) says the oscillations are a direct geometric probe of the Fermi surface: measuring Δ(1/B) gives A_ext, and thus k_F if the shape is known.

  • Increase electron density n: k_F ∝ n^(1/3) → A_ext ∝ n^(2/3) → Δ(1/B) ∝ n^(−2/3) decreases.
  • Rotate B in anisotropic metals: A_ext = A_ext(θ) changes → oscillation frequency maps the Fermi surface shape.
  • Real-space orbit: r_c = ħk_F/(eB) shows how k-space size translates into actual micron-scale paths at tesla fields.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Landau-level counting viewpoint

In 2D, each Landau level has degeneracy per area eB/h. In 3D, you can think of stacking 2D slices at different k_z. Oscillations occur when the number of filled Landau levels changes by one at the Fermi energy. The condition for successive crossings leads to periodicity in 1/B and reproduces Onsager’s result, with the dominant contribution coming from extremal A(k_z) by stationary phase.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why does the oscillation depend on A_ext and not the full Fermi-surface volume?
    A: Landau quantization concerns closed cyclotron orbits ⟂ B; the quantized action depends on the cross-sectional area, and extremal slices dominate the signal.
  • Q: If A_ext doubles, what happens to Δ(1/B)?
    A: It halves: Δ(1/B) ∝ 1/A_ext.
  • Q: Does changing B change the period?
    A: The period in 1/B is constant (for fixed A_ext), although amplitudes can vary with B.
  • Q: Why do SdH and dHvA share the same period?
    A: Both originate from the same Landau-level quantization and the same geometric quantization condition on k-space orbits.
