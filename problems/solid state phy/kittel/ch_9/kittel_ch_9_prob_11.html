Landau Levels (Landau Gauge): Turning a 3D Electron into a 1D Harmonic Oscillator

A uniform magnetic field quantizes the transverse motion of a free electron into discrete energy levels. In the Landau gauge, the Schrödinger eigenproblem separates cleanly and reduces to a shifted 1D harmonic oscillator.

Quick Summary

  • What this problem is about: Solve the stationary Schrödinger eigenvalue problem for a free electron in a uniform magnetic field using the Landau gauge.
  • Key physics idea: A magnetic field bends trajectories into cyclotron orbits; quantum mechanically this becomes harmonic-oscillator quantization in the transverse direction.
  • Governing model: Minimal coupling p → p − (e/c)A inside H = (1/2m)(p − (e/c)A)^2, yielding a PDE eigenproblem Hψ = εψ.
  • Separation ansatz: Choose plane waves along the translationally invariant directions: ψ(x,y,z)=χ(y) exp[i(k_x x + k_z z)].
  • Main reduction: The PDE reduces to a 1D ODE: (ħ²/2m) χ''(y) + [ε − ħ²k_z²/2m − (1/2)mω_c²(y − y_0)²] χ(y)=0.
  • Interpretation: This is exactly the stationary Schrödinger equation of a 1D harmonic oscillator of frequency ω_c = eB/(mc) centered at y_0 = (cħ k_x)/(eB).
  • Final energy spectrum (symbolic): ε_n = (n + 1/2) ħω_c + ħ²k_z²/(2m), with n = 0,1,2,....
  • Physical meaning: Transverse motion is quantized (Landau levels), while motion along z remains free (continuous k_z).

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • B: magnetic flux density (tesla, T). Here uniform and along z: B = B ẑ.
  • A: vector potential (T·m in SI; often used with e/c in Gaussian-cgs). Here Landau gauge: A = −B y x̂.
  • e: elementary charge magnitude (C). Electron charge is q = −e.
  • m: electron mass (kg).
  • ħ: reduced Planck constant (J·s).
  • ω_c: cyclotron frequency (s⁻¹). In the problem’s convention: ω_c = eB/(mc).
  • ψ: wavefunction (complex amplitude).
  • k_x, k_z: wave numbers (m⁻¹) along invariant directions.

Physical meaning of key quantities

  • Minimal coupling: The canonical momentum operator p = −iħ∇ becomes p − (q/c)A. For an electron q = −e, so it is p + (e/c)A.
  • Landau gauge intuition: Choosing A = −By x̂ keeps translation symmetry in x and z, enabling plane waves there.
  • Landau level center y₀: The transverse oscillator is centered at y = y₀, set by k_x. This encodes the huge degeneracy of Landau levels.
  • Free motion along z: Since the field is along z, motion parallel to the field remains free: kinetic energy ħ²k_z²/(2m).

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

Assumptions / regime

When this model applies

  • Single nonrelativistic electron (Schrödinger equation), no spin (no Zeeman term).
  • Uniform static magnetic field, no electric field.
  • “Free” electron: no lattice potential, no disorder, no boundaries (unless later added for degeneracy counting).
  • Gauge choice affects form of wavefunctions but not energies.
Key principle

Translation symmetry → separability

In the Landau gauge, the Hamiltonian is independent of x and z. Therefore, the momentum components p_x and p_z commute with H, and eigenstates can be chosen with definite k_x, k_z.

Common models/approximations used and why

  • Neglect spin: avoids adding the Zeeman term −(gμ_B/ħ) S·B, focusing purely on orbital quantization.
  • Effective mass (engineering context): in solids, replace m → m* to model band curvature; the math is unchanged.
  • 2D electron gas (2DEG): set k_z = 0 (or confine in z) to get the familiar Landau ladder used in quantum Hall physics.

Mini intuition examples (no heavy algebra)

  • Classical cyclotron motion: circular orbit frequency ω_c. Quantum: oscillator spacing Δε = ħω_c.
  • B → 0: level spacing goes to zero, recovering continuous free-particle energies in the transverse plane.
  • Stronger B: larger ω_c means wider spacing, making quantization visible at higher temperatures or with more disorder tolerance.

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Charge sign: electron has q = −e; many sign confusions come from mixing conventions.
  • Gauge dependence: A changes wavefunctions; energies must be gauge-invariant.
  • Units system: the problem uses c (Gaussian-like). In strict SI, ω_c = eB/m. Keep consistent with the given Hamiltonian.
  • k_x degeneracy: energy does not depend on k_x (only shifts y_0), leading to macroscopic degeneracy in finite samples.
Math Insight

Core equation as a mathematical object

After separation, the transverse equation becomes a Sturm–Liouville eigenvalue problem equivalent to the 1D harmonic oscillator. Normalizable solutions exist only for a discrete set of eigenvalues, which is why the Landau spectrum is quantized.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (concrete areas)

  • Magnetotransport & Hall sensors: classical and quantum Hall effects underpin precision magnetic sensing and metrology; Landau quantization is the quantum backbone.
  • Semiconductor 2DEGs (GaAs/AlGaAs, graphene, Si MOSFETs): device physics under high fields (mobility, Shubnikov–de Haas oscillations) is explained by Landau levels.
  • Materials characterization: extracting effective mass m*, scattering times, and Fermi surface info from oscillatory magnetoresistance relies on Landau quantization.
  • Cryogenic electronics & quantum tech: high-mobility channels and edge states (quantum Hall) enable low-dissipation conduction paths.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: Landau quantization modifies density of states, affecting conductivity and noise.
  • Reliability: strong-field operation can induce heating and breakdown; quantization helps predict regimes where transport becomes non-classical.
  • Cost/efficiency: knowing when quantum oscillations appear informs temperature and field specs for sensors and characterization tools.
  • Manufacturability: disorder broadening smears Landau levels; fabrication quality directly impacts whether quantization is observable.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Magnetoresistance vs. B and temperature (Shubnikov–de Haas oscillations).
  • Hall voltage and quantized Hall plateaus (for high mobility and low temperature).
  • Cyclotron resonance (optical/THz absorption peak at ω=ω_c).

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Disorder & finite temperature: broadening ~ ħ/τ competes with spacing ħω_c; need ω_c τ ≫ 1 to resolve levels.
  • Finite size & edges: boundaries break perfect degeneracy and create edge states—essential in real devices.
  • Effective mass & band nonparabolicity: in semiconductors, m* depends on energy; the ideal ladder is modified at higher energies.
From lab to product: how the ideal model is adapted

In real materials, one replaces m by m*, adds scattering (finite τ) to model Landau level broadening, includes confinement potentials (quantum wells) and edges, and may add spin (Zeeman splitting) and interactions. The clean harmonic-oscillator mapping remains the mathematical “core,” while engineering adds perturbations and boundary conditions.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement

A free electron (no spin) moves in a uniform magnetic field B = B ẑ. Using the Landau gauge A = −B y x̂, the Hamiltonian is given (in the problem’s convention) as H = −(ħ²/2m)(∂²/∂y² + ∂²/∂z²) + (1/2m)[ −iħ ∂/∂x − (eBy/c) ]². We seek stationary states Hψ = εψ and:

  1. (a) Show that with ψ = χ(y) exp[i(k_x x + k_z z)], the function χ(y) satisfies a shifted-oscillator ODE.
  2. (b) Show that this ODE is the harmonic oscillator equation with frequency ω_c and obtain energies ε_n.

Given

  • Uniform magnetic field B = B ẑ.
  • Vector potential (Landau gauge) A = −B y x̂.
  • Electron mass m, charge magnitude e, speed of light c, Planck constant ħ.
  • Hamiltonian operator H as provided.

Unknowns / what to prove

  • The reduced ODE for χ(y), including the shift y_0.
  • The identification with a harmonic oscillator of frequency ω_c.
  • The energy spectrum ε_n.

Mathematical model (explicit)

Mathematical physics model

Eigenvalue problem with operators, domain, and symmetry

  • Domain: idealized infinite space (x,y,z) ∈ ℝ³ (later one can impose periodic boundary conditions in x, z for degeneracy counting).
  • Unknown: stationary wavefunction ψ(x,y,z) in Hilbert space L²(ℝ³).
  • Operator form: with canonical momentum p = −iħ∇, the minimal-coupling Hamiltonian is H = (1/2m) [ p − (q/c)A ]², here effectively encoded in the provided expression.
  • Eigenproblem: find ε ∈ ℝ and nonzero ψ such that Hψ = εψ.
  • Boundary/normalization: physical states require ∫|ψ|² d³r < ∞. This will enforce discrete oscillator indices n in y.
  • Symmetry: translation invariance in x and z implies separability using plane waves (eigenfunctions of −i∂/∂x and −i∂/∂z).

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Linearity: Schrödinger equation is linear; separation of variables is valid when coefficients are independent of some coordinates.
  • Gauge choice: selecting Landau gauge simplifies operator dependence, enabling constants of motion p_x, p_z.
  • Normalizability: only specific eigenvalues yield square-integrable χ(y), producing quantization.

Assumptions (explicit)

Assumptions

What is idealized and what is neglected

  • No electric field; static uniform B.
  • No spin (no Zeeman splitting).
  • No lattice or disorder potential (free particle).
  • Infinite extent (or periodic BCs) so k_x, k_z are good quantum numbers.

Possible approaches (compare)

  1. Direct separation in Landau gauge (best here): exploit translational symmetry in x, z to reduce to 1D HO. Pros: shortest, matches given hint. Cons: gauge-specific wavefunctions.
  2. Operator (ladder) method: define kinetic momenta and show commutators form HO algebra. Pros: elegant, gauge-invariant energies. Cons: more abstract; must still connect to coordinates.
  3. Symmetric gauge solution: yields radial equation and angular momentum states. Pros: good for rotational symmetry. Cons: longer for this prompt; less aligned with given Hamiltonian.

Chosen approach: direct separation in Landau gauge, because the Hamiltonian is already written for it and the problem explicitly proposes the separable form.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Identify symmetries: notice H has no explicit x or z dependence → use plane waves in those directions.
  2. Insert ansatz: ψ=χ(y)e^{i(k_x x+k_z z)} into Hψ=εψ.
  3. Evaluate derivatives: compute ∂/∂x and ∂/∂z acting on the ansatz to replace operators by constants k_x, k_z.
  4. Reduce to ODE: isolate a differential equation in y only.
  5. Complete the square: rewrite the y-dependent potential term as (1/2)mω_c²(y−y_0)² to identify the HO centered at y_0.
  6. Read off spectrum: use known HO eigenvalues: E_osc=(n+1/2)ħω_c.
  7. Add longitudinal kinetic energy: include ħ²k_z²/(2m) from free motion along z.
  8. Sanity checks: units, limits B→0, physical meaning of y_0.
Common mistakes

Quick tips to avoid errors

  • When squaring [−iħ∂/∂x − (eBy/c)], remember y commutes with ∂/∂x, so it behaves like a number under ∂/∂x.
  • Do not lose the minus sign when using −(ħ²/2m)∂²/∂z² on a plane wave: it becomes +(ħ²k_z²)/(2m) in the energy balance.
  • Keep the problem’s convention for ω_c = eB/(mc) (includes c).
  • y_0 depends on k_x; energy does not — that’s not a bug, it’s degeneracy.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition first (what to expect)

Classically, a charged particle in a uniform magnetic field executes circular cyclotron motion in the plane perpendicular to B with angular frequency ω_c, while moving freely along the field direction. Quantum mechanically, “circular motion” becomes quantized, producing discrete transverse energies spaced by ħω_c, while the longitudinal energy stays continuous.

Step 1: Start from the given Hamiltonian operator

The problem provides the Hamiltonian (Landau gauge A = −By x̂) as:

Given Hamiltonian (operator form in coordinates)
H = -(ħ^2/2m)(∂^2/∂y^2 + ∂^2/∂z^2) + (1/2m)[ -iħ ∂/∂x - (eB y / c) ]^2

This is a linear partial differential operator acting on ψ(x,y,z). Note: x and z appear only through derivatives, not explicitly, which signals translational symmetry.

Step 2: Use separability from translation symmetry in x and z

Because H is invariant under x → x + const and z → z + const, we choose eigenstates of the commuting operators −i∂/∂x and −i∂/∂z. This motivates the standard plane-wave ansatz:

Separation ansatz
ψ(x,y,z) = χ(y) exp[i(k_x x + k_z z)]

Here k_x and k_z are real constants (wave numbers), and χ(y) is to be determined. This step is valid because the PDE is linear and its coefficients are independent of x and z.

Step 3: Apply the derivatives explicitly (no magic jumps)

Compute the needed derivatives:

Derivative actions on the ansatz
∂ψ/∂x = i k_x ψ
∂^2ψ/∂z^2 = -k_z^2 ψ
∂ψ/∂y = χ'(y) e^{i(k_x x + k_z z)}
∂^2ψ/∂y^2 = χ''(y) e^{i(k_x x + k_z z)}

Therefore: −(ħ²/2m)∂²ψ/∂z² = (ħ²k_z²/2m) ψ, a positive kinetic-energy contribution.

Step 4: Evaluate the squared bracket operator

Consider the operator inside the square: Q ≡ [ −iħ ∂/∂x − (eBy/c) ]. Acting on our ansatz:

Action of the x-momentum-like operator
Q ψ = [ -iħ (i k_x) - (eB y / c) ] ψ
    = [ ħ k_x - (eB y / c) ] ψ

Since y commutes with ∂/∂x, we can square this result directly when acting on ψ:

Squared operator term
Q^2 ψ = [ ħ k_x - (eB y / c) ]^2 ψ

Step 5: Insert into Hψ = εψ and reduce to an ODE

Assemble all contributions. Using ∂²ψ/∂y² = χ'' e^{i(...)} and dividing out the nonzero factor e^{i(k_x x + k_z z)}:

Reduced equation before completing the square
-(ħ^2/2m) χ''(y) + (ħ^2 k_z^2 / 2m) χ(y) + (1/2m)[ ħ k_x - (eB y / c) ]^2 χ(y) = ε χ(y)

Rearrange to the standard “kinetic + potential = energy” ODE form:

ODE in y (energy-balance form)
(ħ^2/2m) χ''(y) + [ ε - (ħ^2 k_z^2 / 2m) - (1/2m)(ħ k_x - (eB y / c))^2 ] χ(y) = 0

This already matches the requested structure: a second-order linear ODE with a quadratic (in y) term. Next we rewrite the quadratic term as a shifted parabola.

Step 6: Complete the square and identify ωc and y0

Expand the square:

Quadratic term expansion
(ħ k_x - (eB y / c))^2
= (eB/c)^2 ( y - (cħ k_x)/(eB) )^2

The identity above is a direct algebraic completion of the square: factor out (eB/c) and define the shift y_0 ≡ (cħ k_x)/(eB).

Definitions of the shift and cyclotron frequency
y_0 = (c ħ k_x)/(e B)
ω_c = e B/(m c)

Now rewrite the potential-like term:

Quadratic term as (1/2)mωc²(y−y0)²
(1/2m)(ħ k_x - (eB y / c))^2
= (1/2m) (eB/c)^2 (y - y_0)^2
= (1/2) m ω_c^2 (y - y_0)^2

The last equality uses ω_c = eB/(mc): mω_c = eB/c so (eB/c)^2 / m = mω_c^2.

Step 7: Arrive at the requested harmonic-oscillator ODE

Substitute the completed-square form into the ODE:

Requested ODE (part a)
(ħ^2/2m) d^2χ/dy^2 + [ ε - (ħ^2 k_z^2 / 2m) - (1/2) m ω_c^2 (y - y_0)^2 ] χ(y) = 0

This matches the problem statement: a second-order ODE with a quadratic confining term centered at y_0.

Step 8: Recognize the harmonic oscillator and read off the spectrum (part b)

The standard 1D harmonic oscillator eigenproblem (centered at y_0) is:

Standard 1D harmonic oscillator (stationary Schrödinger form)
-(ħ^2/2m) d^2χ/dy^2 + (1/2) m ω^2 (y - y_0)^2 χ = E_osc χ

Multiply our ODE (eq11) by −1 and compare term-by-term:

Identification of the oscillator energy
E_osc = ε - ħ^2 k_z^2/(2m)
ω = ω_c

Since the harmonic oscillator has discrete eigenvalues E_osc,n = (n + 1/2) ħ ω_c with n = 0, 1, 2, ..., we conclude:

Landau level energies (final spectrum)
ε_n = (n + 1/2) ħ ω_c + ħ^2 k_z^2/(2m),   n = 0,1,2,...
Final Answer

Boxed result (what you must show)

(a) With ψ = χ(y) e^{i(k_x x + k_z z)}, χ(y) satisfies (ħ^2/2m) χ'' + [ ε − ħ^2 k_z^2/(2m) − (1/2)mω_c^2 (y − y_0)^2 ] χ = 0, where ω_c = eB/(mc) and y_0 = (cħ k_x)/(eB).
(b) This is the 1D harmonic oscillator equation ⇒ ε_n = (n+1/2)ħω_c + ħ^2 k_z^2/(2m).

Final answer (plain text, ready to copy)
(a) For ψ(x,y,z)=χ(y)exp[i(kx x + kz z)],
(ħ^2/2m) d^2χ/dy^2 + [ ε − ħ^2 kz^2/(2m) − (1/2) m ωc^2 (y − y0)^2 ] χ(y) = 0,
with ωc = eB/(mc) and y0 = (c ħ kx)/(eB).

(b) This is the 1D harmonic oscillator Schrödinger equation (center y0, frequency ωc),
so εn = (n + 1/2) ħ ωc + ħ^2 kz^2/(2m),  n = 0,1,2,...

Sanity checks

Units / dimensional analysis

Check key dimensions

  • ω_c = eB/(mc) has units of 1/time (given the problem’s convention).
  • ħω_c has units of energy, so level spacing is energy.
  • y_0 = (cħk_x)/(eB) has units of length (since ħk_x is momentum).
Limiting cases

Does it reduce correctly?

  • B → 0: ω_c → 0 so spacing ħω_c → 0; discrete levels merge into a continuum, consistent with a free particle.
  • k_z → 0: energies reduce to pure Landau ladder ε_n = (n+1/2)ħω_c, relevant for 2D motion.
Optional deeper math: nondimensionalization (shows HO structure cleanly)

Define magnetic length (in the problem’s convention) by ℓ_B ≡ √(ħc/(eB)). Then set η = (y − y_0)/ℓ_B. Using d/dy = (1/ℓ_B)d/dη and mω_cℓ_B² = ħ, the ODE becomes:

Dimensionless HO form
d^2χ/dη^2 + [ 2(ε - ħ^2 kz^2/(2m))/(ħωc) - η^2 ] χ = 0

Normalizable solutions require 2(ε - ħ^2 kz^2/(2m))/(ħωc) = 2n+1, giving the same spectrum.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the final formula

The energy splits into two physically distinct parts:

  • Quantized transverse energy: (n+1/2)ħω_c comes from motion in the x–y plane, which becomes an oscillator due to the magnetic field.
  • Free longitudinal energy: ħ²k_z²/(2m) is the usual kinetic energy for motion parallel to B.

Parameter dependence (qualitative + quantitative)

  • Magnetic field B: ω_c ∝ B ⇒ Landau level spacing Δε = ħω_c ∝ B increases linearly with field.
  • Mass m (or effective mass m*): ω_c ∝ 1/m ⇒ heavier carriers have tighter spacing; light effective mass makes quantization easier to observe.
  • k_z: shifts energies continuously upward by ħ²k_z²/(2m); does not affect level spacing.
  • k_x: does not appear in ε_n; it only sets the orbit center y_0 (degeneracy).

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Define the kinetic (gauge-covariant) momenta Π = p − (q/c)A. In a uniform B, the components satisfy commutation relations like [Π_x, Π_y] = iħ (qB/c), which is mathematically the same algebra as [a, a†]=1 after rescaling. Then H = (Π_x² + Π_y² + p_z²)/(2m) becomes a harmonic oscillator in the transverse kinetic momenta, yielding the same ladder spectrum without committing to a specific gauge.

Concept check (with answers)

Self-test

  • Q: Why can we assume a plane wave in x and z?
    A: Because H is independent of x and z, so those directions have translation symmetry and conserved momenta.
  • Q: What does y_0 represent physically?
    A: The guiding-center (orbit-center) position of the cyclotron motion in y; changing k_x slides the orbit without changing energy.
  • Q: What sets the Landau level spacing?
    A: Δε = ħω_c, proportional to B and inversely proportional to m.
  • Q: If we included spin, what changes?
    A: Add Zeeman splitting ±(1/2)gμ_B B to the energies, further splitting each Landau level.
