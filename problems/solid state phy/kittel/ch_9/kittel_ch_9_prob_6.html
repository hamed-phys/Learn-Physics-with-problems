Quick Summary

  • What the problem is about: Determine the binding energy of a bound electron in a 1D finite square well (single well), using an even (symmetric) wavefunction.
  • Key physics idea: Oscillatory solutions inside the well must match exponentially decaying solutions outside via boundary continuity—this quantizes energy.
  • Governing equation(s): The eigenvalue ODE from the Hamiltonian operator Ĥ = −(ħ²/2m) d²/dx² + V(x): Ĥψ = Eψ.
  • Dimensionless reduction: With z = ka/2, y = κa/2, ρ² = m|U0|a²/(2ħ²), the even-state condition becomes z tan z = y and z² + y² = ρ².
  • Symbolic result type: Binding energy is given implicitly because z is found from a transcendental equation.
  • Numeric result (special case): For |U0| = 2ħ²/(ma²) ⇒ ρ=1, the even ground state yields Eb ≈ 0.45375 |U0| and E ≈ −0.45375 |U0|.
  • Physical meaning: In the “widely separated wells” limit, tunneling is negligible, the band width → 0, and the band energy equals the isolated-well eigenenergy (k-independent).

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • x (m): 1D coordinate.
  • ψ(x) (m−1/2): wavefunction, normalized ∫|ψ|² dx = 1.
  • V(x) (J): potential energy.
  • E (J): energy eigenvalue.
  • m (kg): mass (often effective mass m* in solids).
  • ħ (J·s): reduced Planck constant.
  • a (m): well width.
  • U0 (J): well parameter; we use |U0| for depth magnitude.

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Bound state: localized state with energy below outside reference (V=0 outside), so ψ decays outside.
  • Inside the well: ψ oscillates (like a standing wave) because kinetic energy is positive there.
  • Boundary matching: continuity forces only certain “fits” → quantized energies.
  • Binding energy: Eb = −E (positive) measures “how tightly” the state is bound below the continuum edge.

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

Schrödinger eigenvalue problem + continuity

The time-independent Schrödinger equation −(ħ²/2m)ψ'' + Vψ = Eψ is linear; solutions in each region superpose. For finite jumps in V(x), integrating across a boundary shows ψ and ψ' must be continuous (no delta-function forcing term). Normalizability selects decaying exponentials outside for E<0.

Common models/approximations used and why

  • Square-well approximation: captures confinement with solvable piecewise-constant regions.
  • Effective mass: replace m by m* for electrons in semiconductors; keeps same math, better physical fidelity.
  • 1D reduction: captures essential quantization and matching; used for intuition and first-pass design checks.

Mini intuition examples (1–3 quick)

  • Deeper well: stronger localization → larger κ (faster decay) → larger Eb.
  • Wider well: lowers kinetic energy cost of localization → easier binding.
  • Very shallow well: only the lowest even state may exist; excited states require stronger confinement.
What to watch for (pitfalls)
  • Define V(x) explicitly to avoid sign confusion (depth vs potential value).
  • Don’t confuse eigenenergy E (negative) with binding energy Eb (positive).
  • Even state uses cos inside; odd state uses sin inside.
  • Expect a transcendental equation—numerical root needed.
Math Insight: Sturm–Liouville eigenvalue problem

The ODE −(ħ²/2m)ψ'' + V(x)ψ = Eψ is a linear eigenproblem. For piecewise-constant V, solutions are exponentials or sines/cosines; matching conditions create a nonlinear eigenvalue equation (transcendental quantization condition). This is a classic “spectral” problem for a self-adjoint operator.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry

  • Quantum wells in optoelectronics: lasers/LEDs where confinement sets subband energies and optical transitions.
  • Infrared detectors (QWIPs/quantum cascade concepts): bound-state energies determine absorption wavelengths and selection rules.
  • Superlattices & minibands: arrays of wells form bands; “isolated well → band” is tight-binding physics.
  • Thin-film electronics: quantization in ultrathin layers influences carrier distribution and transport.

Why engineers care (IE-style considerations)

  • Performance: Eb controls confinement, leakage, and optical energies.
  • Reliability & yield: Eb sensitivity to thickness a means process variation translates to spectral/transport variation.
  • Cost/throughput: achieving deeper wells may require more complex materials or epitaxy steps.

Validation methods

  • Spectroscopy: photoluminescence/absorption to infer subband gaps.
  • Transport: Hall and magnetotransport to extract subband occupancy/effective mass.
  • Electrical profiling: C–V to estimate confinement and carrier distribution.

Trade-offs and non-idealities

  • Real wells are not perfectly square (band bending, interface roughness, asymmetry).
  • Effective mass can be position-dependent; barriers may have different m* than wells.
  • Coupling between wells depends exponentially on barrier thickness: helps conduction, hurts localization.
From lab to product: adapting the ideal model

Product-grade modeling replaces the square profile with realistic band edges, includes strain and temperature dependence, and solves the same eigenproblem numerically (finite difference, transfer matrix, FEM). The square-well solution remains a fast “back-of-the-envelope” scaling tool and a sanity check for simulations.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Rewrite the problem clearly

Consider a 1D finite square well centered at x=0 of width a and depth U0 (attractive). (a) Derive the binding energy condition for an even (symmetric) bound state of an electron in this well. (b) For the special depth magnitude |U0| = 2ħ²/(ma²), compute a numerical binding energy in units of |U0| and interpret the result in the limit of widely separated wells (bandwidth → 0).

Given

  • a, |U0|, m, ħ
  • Even (symmetric) solution about x=0

Find

  • Implicit equation for Eb (or E)
  • Numeric Eb/|U0| for |U0| = 2ħ²/(ma²)
  • Interpretation: isolated-well limit of a band (k-independence)

Principles used (and why)

  • Schrödinger equation: single-particle quantum eigenproblem.
  • Parity symmetry: V(x)=V(−x) ⇒ eigenfunctions can be even/odd; ground state is even.
  • Continuity of ψ and ψ': finite jump in V(x) implies continuous ψ and derivative across boundary.
Assumptions
  • 1D, time-independent, nonrelativistic.
  • Perfect square well: V constant in regions.
  • No interactions (single electron).
  • Outside potential reference is V=0.

Approaches (pros/cons) and choice

Direct boundary matching

  • Pros: clearest, exact transcendental quantization condition.
  • Cons: requires numerical root for Eb.

Transfer matrix

  • Pros: extends naturally to multilayers/superlattices.
  • Cons: extra machinery for a single well.

We choose: direct matching + parity symmetry.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Define V(x) precisely and specify E<0 for bound states.
  2. Write Schrödinger ODE in each region (inside/outside).
  3. Solve: oscillatory inside; decaying exponential outside.
  4. Use even parity to keep only cos(kx) inside.
  5. Match ψ and ψ' at x=a/2 to obtain k tan(ka/2)=κ.
  6. Nondimensionalize: z=ka/2, y=κa/2, ρ²=m|U0|a²/(2ħ²).
  7. Express Eb via y: Eb=(2ħ²/(ma²))y², with y²=ρ²−z².
  8. For ρ=1, solve z tan z = √(1−z²) numerically; compute Eb/|U0|.
  9. Interpret isolated-well limit: band width→0 ⇒ energy independent of Bloch k.
Common mistakes
  • Using sin for even state (wrong parity).
  • Forgetting ψ' continuity.
  • Confusing κ with k (decay vs oscillation).
  • Skipping nondimensionalization (makes algebra harder and obscures physics).

PART 3 — Full Solution (DETAILED + TEACHING)

Physical intuition: what to expect

Inside the well the electron can form a standing wave; outside it must decay. Matching these behaviors at the edges restricts the allowed energies. The shallowest bound state is even and has no nodes. Increasing depth or width strengthens binding.

Step 1 — Mathematical model (ODE eigenproblem)

Coordinate: x ∈ ℝ, well centered at 0, edges at x = ±a/2.

Potential:
V(x) = -|U0|   for |x| < a/2
V(x) =  0      for |x| ≥ a/2

Time-independent Schrödinger equation:
[-(ħ^2/2m) d^2/dx^2 + V(x)] ψ(x) = E ψ(x)

Bound-state conditions:
E < 0, and ψ(x) → 0 as |x| → ∞.
Why matching works (mathematical justification)

Since V(x) has finite steps, Schrödinger’s equation has no delta-like forcing at x=±a/2. Integrating the ODE across an infinitesimal interval around the boundary implies ψ is continuous. The second derivative term implies ψ' is also continuous (otherwise ψ'' would contain a delta distribution).

Step 2 — Outside solution (evanescent)

For |x|≥a/2, V=0. For a bound state, define the binding energy Eb>0 via E = −Eb. 

Outside: V=0, E=-Eb (Eb>0)

Schrödinger:
-(ħ^2/2m) ψ'' = E ψ = -Eb ψ
=> ψ'' = (2m Eb/ħ^2) ψ ≡ κ^2 ψ

Define κ = sqrt(2m Eb)/ħ > 0.

Normalizable solution (written to match at x=±a/2):
ψ_out(x) = A e^{-κ(|x| - a/2)}.

Step 3 — Inside solution (oscillatory)

For |x|<a/2, V=−|U0|. With E=−Eb: 

Inside: V = -|U0|, E = -Eb

Schrödinger:
-(ħ^2/2m) ψ'' - |U0| ψ = -Eb ψ
=> -(ħ^2/2m) ψ'' = (|U0| - Eb) ψ

Define k = sqrt(2m(|U0| - Eb))/ħ.

Then:
ψ'' + k^2 ψ = 0
=> ψ_in(x) = C cos(kx) + D sin(kx).

Step 4 — Impose symmetry (even parity)

The well is symmetric: V(x)=V(−x). Even solutions satisfy ψ(x)=ψ(−x), so the sine term must vanish (odd function). Thus D=0: 

Even parity:
ψ_in(x) = C cos(kx).

Step 5 — Match ψ and ψ' at x=a/2 (derive the quantization condition)

Compute ψ and ψ' at x=a/2: 

At x = a/2:

Inside:
ψ_in(a/2)  = C cos(ka/2)
ψ'_in(a/2) = -C k sin(ka/2)

Outside (x ≥ a/2):
ψ_out(x)   = A e^{-κ(x-a/2)}
ψ_out(a/2) = A
ψ'_out(a/2)= -A κ

Apply continuity: 

Continuity at x=a/2:

(1) ψ_in(a/2) = ψ_out(a/2)  =>  C cos(ka/2) = A
(2) ψ'_in(a/2)= ψ'_out(a/2) => -C k sin(ka/2) = -A κ

Divide (2) by (1) (valid if cos(ka/2)≠0; this selects the even branch):
[-C k sin(ka/2)]/[C cos(ka/2)] = [-A κ]/[A]
=> -k tan(ka/2) = -κ
=> k tan(ka/2) = κ   (even bound-state condition)
Key even-parity quantization condition

k tan(ka/2) = κ, where k = √(2m(|U0|−Eb))/ħ and κ = √(2mEb)/ħ.

Step 6 — Nondimensionalization + binding-energy expression

Introduce dimensionless variables at the natural length scale a: 

Define:
z = (k a/2),   y = (κ a/2)

Then:
k tan(ka/2) = κ  becomes  z tan z = y.

Also:
k^2 + κ^2 = (2m/ħ^2)(|U0|-Eb) + (2m/ħ^2)Eb = 2m|U0|/ħ^2

Multiply by (a/2)^2:
z^2 + y^2 = (a^2/4)(2m|U0|/ħ^2) = m|U0|a^2/(2ħ^2) ≡ ρ^2.

Now write Eb explicitly in terms of y: κ = 2y/a, so: 

Binding energy:
Eb = (ħ^2 κ^2)/(2m) = (ħ^2/(2m)) (4y^2/a^2)
=> Eb = (2ħ^2/(m a^2)) y^2.

Using y^2 = ρ^2 - z^2:
Eb = (2ħ^2/(m a^2)) (ρ^2 - z^2).

And z is determined implicitly by:
z tan z = sqrt(ρ^2 - z^2)   (even states).
Final symbolic result (Part a)
Let ρ^2 = m|U0|a^2/(2ħ^2), z = ka/2, y = κa/2.

Even bound states satisfy:
z tan z = √(ρ^2 − z^2)

Binding energy:
Eb = (2ħ^2/(m a^2)) (ρ^2 − z^2)

Eigenenergy:
E = −Eb.

Step 7 — Special numeric case |U0| = 2ħ²/(ma²)

Compute ρ: 

Given |U0| = 2ħ^2/(m a^2):

ρ^2 = m|U0|a^2/(2ħ^2)
    = m*(2ħ^2/(m a^2))*a^2/(2ħ^2)
    = 1
So ρ = 1.

The even condition becomes: z tan z = √(1−z²) for 0<z<π/2 (ground even state). The numerical root is: 

Root for ρ=1 (ground even):
z ≈ 0.7390851332

Then:
y^2 = 1 - z^2 ≈ 0.4537531659

Since Eb = |U0| y^2 (because |U0| = 2ħ^2/(m a^2)):
Eb ≈ 0.4537531659 |U0|
E  ≈ -0.4537531659 |U0|
Final answer (Part b)
For |U0| = 2ħ^2/(m a^2) (⇒ ρ=1):

Eb ≈ 0.45375 |U0|
E  ≈ -0.45375 |U0|.

Equivalently:
Eb ≈ 0.90751 (ħ^2/(m a^2)).

Sanity checks

Dimensional analysis

  • ħ²/(m a²) has units of energy. ✅
  • z,y,ρ are dimensionless. ✅

Limits & interpretation

  • E must be negative for bound states with outside V=0; we found E=−Eb. ✅
  • Wells widely separated ⇒ tunneling → 0 ⇒ bandwidth → 0 ⇒ energy does not depend on Bloch k. ✅
“Compare with appropriate limit of Fig. 20” (conceptual connection)

In a periodic array of wells, each isolated-well level becomes a band. In the limit of widely separated wells, inter-well coupling is exponentially small, so the band becomes flat: E(k) ≈ constant. The lowest band approaches the single-well ground energy computed above. Higher bands approach excited single-well levels.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (THEORY AROUND THE RESULT)

What controls the binding?

  • ρ (well strength): ρ² = m|U0|a²/(2ħ²) captures depth × width² compared to quantum kinetic scale.
  • z (interior phase): determines how the cosine fits inside; larger z means more oscillation inside.
  • y (exterior decay): directly sets Eb via Eb = (2ħ²/(ma²)) y².

Parameter dependence

  • Increase |U0|: increases ρ ⇒ generally increases y ⇒ increases Eb.
  • Increase a: increases ρ (easier confinement) and changes the energy scale ħ²/(ma²) (smaller for larger a); net effect typically favors binding and more states.
  • Increase m (effective mass): increases ρ and reduces ħ²/(ma²) scale; confinement energies shift—important in semiconductor design.
Alternative derivation (log-derivative method)

Define L(x)=ψ'(x)/ψ(x). For the even inside solution ψ=C cos(kx), L(a/2)=−k tan(ka/2). For the outside decaying solution, L(a/2)=−κ. Matching L gives −k tan(ka/2)=−κ ⇒ k tan(ka/2)=κ directly, bypassing coefficient elimination.

Concept check (Q&A)

  • Q: Why does the outside solution become exponential? A: Because E<0 makes the Schrödinger ODE have positive coefficient ψ''=κ²ψ ⇒ exponentials.
  • Q: Why is the ground state even? A: Lowest eigenfunction has no nodes; odd parity forces a node at x=0, raising energy.
  • Q: What does a larger κ imply? A: Faster decay, stronger localization, larger Eb.
  • Q: In widely separated wells, why does the band flatten? A: Tunneling amplitude (overlap) → 0, eliminating k-dependent splitting.
