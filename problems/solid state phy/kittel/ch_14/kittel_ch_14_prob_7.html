Quick Summary

  • What this problem is about: the photon-like polariton branch at small wavevector K in a polar crystal.
  • Key physics idea: electromagnetic waves in a crystal obey c²K² = ω² ε(ω); at long wavelengths the field is slow enough to polarize the lattice, so ε(ω→0)=ε(0).
  • Governing equation(s): Maxwell in matter + a dispersive dielectric model (Lorentz oscillator for the optical phonon).
  • Dielectric function form used: ε(ω) = ε(∞) (ω_L² − ω²)/(ω_T² − ω²), with ε(0)=ε(∞) ω_L²/ω_T² (Lyddane–Sachs–Teller).
  • Part (a) result type: symbolic modification of the polariton dispersion (the “(56)” root formula) when ε(∞) is retained.
  • Part (b) result type: a small-K asymptotic: ω ≈ cK / √ε(0) (photon in a medium of refractive index n²=ε(0)).
  • Physical meaning: low-frequency photons “see” the fully relaxed polarization (electronic + ionic), not just the fast electronic part.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (THEORY BEFORE SOLVING)

Core definitions & symbols (with SI units)
  • ω — angular frequency (rad/s)
  • K = |k| — wavevector magnitude (1/m)
  • c — speed of light in vacuum (m/s)
  • E, D — electric field (V/m) and displacement field (C/m²)
  • ε(ω) — relative permittivity (dimensionless), dispersive in frequency
  • ε(∞) — “high-frequency” dielectric constant (electronic polarization only)
  • ε(0) — “static” dielectric constant (electronic + ionic polarization)
  • ω_T, ω_L — transverse/longitudinal optical phonon frequencies at K→0 (rad/s)

Physical meaning (intuition)

In a polar (ionic) crystal, the optical phonon mode couples strongly to the electromagnetic field. The coupled eigenmodes are polaritons. Because polarization needs time to respond, the dielectric constant depends on frequency: ε(ω).

At high frequencies (faster than ionic motion), ions cannot follow: the medium responds only electronically, giving ε(∞). At low frequencies, ions also follow, yielding the larger static value ε(0).

Key laws/principles & validity conditions

  • Linear response: D(ω)=ε0 ε(ω) E(ω) (small fields; no strong nonlinear optics).
  • Homogeneous, isotropic (or one principal direction): treat ε as a scalar.
  • Lossless model (here): ignore damping; thus ε(ω) is real.
  • Plane-wave ansatz: fields ∝ exp[i(K·r − ω t)] in an infinite medium (no boundaries).

Common model used here: Lorentz oscillator dielectric

A standard polar-crystal dielectric function near an optical phonon resonance is 

ε(ω) = ε(∞) (ω_L^2 − ω^2) / (ω_T^2 − ω^2)

This compact form encodes the longitudinal–transverse splitting and implies the Lyddane–Sachs–Teller relation: ε(0)/ε(∞)=ω_L^2/ω_T^2.

Mini intuition examples (conceptual, minimal algebra)

  • Very low ω: the medium fully polarizes ⇒ waves slow down by √ε(0).
  • Very high ω (but below electronic absorption): only electrons polarize ⇒ speed set by √ε(∞).
  • Near ω≈ω_T: resonance causes strong dispersion and mode mixing (polariton anti-crossing behavior).
What to watch for (pitfalls)
  • Do not replace ε(ω) by a constant unless you justify the frequency regime (ω→0 or ω→∞).
  • Be careful: the “photon branch at low K” means the solution with ω→0 as K→0 (not the phonon-like one).
  • When taking limits, keep track of which root (±) you are following in the quadratic for ω^2.
Math Insight (core equation as a mathematical object)

The dispersion relation is an eigenvalue condition arising from a linear PDE system (Maxwell’s equations) with a frequency-dependent constitutive law. In Fourier space, the PDEs reduce to an algebraic system; non-trivial solutions require the determinant to vanish, producing a polynomial (here quadratic) equation in ω^2.

Mathematical physics structure (PDE → algebraic eigenproblem)

In a source-free, homogeneous medium: 

∇ × E = −∂B/∂t
∇ × H =  ∂D/∂t
D = ε0 ε(ω) E,   B = μ0 H  (μ≈1 assumed)

Take Fourier components with E(r,t)=E0 e^{i(K·r−ωt)}. Then ∇→iK, ∂/∂t→−iω. Eliminating H gives the wave equation operator: 

(K × (K × E0)) = (ω^2/c^2) ε(ω) E0

For transverse waves (K·E0=0), K×(K×E0)=K^2 E0, yielding c^2 K^2 = ω^2 ε(ω).

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Polaritons and frequency-dependent permittivity are not just “textbook elegance”—they directly shape how real devices transmit, confine, and dissipate electromagnetic energy.

Where this appears in industry (examples)
  • Infrared optics & sensing: Reststrahlen bands in polar crystals (strong reflection near phonon resonances).
  • THz components: waveguides, filters, and emitters where lattice response matters.
  • Semiconductor manufacturing metrology: ellipsometry / FTIR to extract ε(ω) and film thickness.
  • Photonics & nanophotonics: phonon-polariton confinement in polar dielectrics (sub-diffraction IR guiding).
Why engineers care
  • Performance: group velocity and impedance depend on ε(ω) → affects bandwidth and delay.
  • Reliability: absorption (not modeled here) heats materials; dispersion determines where hot spots form.
  • Cost & manufacturability: selecting substrates/coatings relies on accurate ε(0), ε(∞), and resonance frequencies.
  • Safety: high-field THz/IR systems require knowing where strong reflection or field enhancement occurs.
How it’s measured/validated (brief)
  • Dielectric spectroscopy / impedance: low-frequency access to ε(0).
  • FTIR / Raman / reflectivity: locate ω_T, ω_L and dispersion features.
  • Ellipsometry: broadband extraction of complex ε(ω) (including loss).
From lab to product: adapting the ideal model
  • Add damping: ω_T^2 − ω^2 → ω_T^2 − ω^2 − iγω to capture loss and finite linewidths.
  • Account for anisotropy: replace scalar ε with tensor ε̿ in crystals like sapphire or hBN.
  • Include boundaries/finite size: solve Maxwell with interface conditions; modes become guided or surface polaritons.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Problem restatement

You are given a polariton dispersion relation (labeled as equation (55) in your source) and a derived expression (equation (56)). (a) Modify (56) when the high-frequency dielectric constant ε(∞) is included. (b) Prove that one solution of (55) behaves at small wavevector as ω = cK/√ε(0), matching the expected photon dispersion in a dielectric of refractive index n² = ε.

Given / Unknowns / Targets
Given
A polar-crystal dielectric model and the polariton dispersion framework
Unknown
Explicit photon-branch behavior at low K with ε(∞) retained
Find (a)
Updated (56): the two ω(K) branches including ε(∞)
Show (b)
As K→0, one branch satisfies ω ≈ cK/√ε(0)

Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Maxwell + constitutive law: linear homogeneous media give a plane-wave dispersion c²K² = ω² ε(ω) for transverse waves.
  • Frequency-dependent ε(ω): optical phonons create strong dispersion; a Lorentz-type model yields a rational function in ω².
  • Asymptotics: low wavevector implies low photon frequency on the photon-like branch, enabling the limit ε(ω)→ε(0).
Assumptions (explicit)
  • Lossless medium (real ε(ω)), no damping.
  • Non-magnetic: μ≈1.
  • Infinite homogeneous crystal (no boundaries; plane waves).
  • Isotropic response (scalar ε) or propagation along a principal axis so the scalar form applies.

Possible approaches (compare)

  • Approach A (direct algebra): insert ε(ω) into c²K² = ω² ε(ω), obtain a quadratic in ω², solve explicitly. Best for part (a).
  • Approach B (low-ω asymptotic): for the photon branch at low K, use ε(ω)≈ε(0) directly to get ω≈cK/√ε(0). Fast for part (b) and physically transparent.
  • Approach C (series expansion of the exact root): pick the correct (±) root and expand in small K. Most rigorous link between (a) and (b).

We will use A + C to produce the modified root formula (a), then extract the low-K photon branch (b) both by a quick physical limit and by an explicit small-K expansion of the exact solution.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Write the wave dispersion: for transverse waves, reduce Maxwell to c²K² = ω² ε(ω). (Fourier-space eigencondition.)
  2. Insert the polar-crystal ε(ω): use ε(ω)=ε(∞)(ω_L²−ω²)/(ω_T²−ω²).
  3. Clear denominators: multiply both sides by (ω_T²−ω²) to obtain a polynomial equation.
  4. Convert to x = ω²: show it becomes a quadratic in x.
  5. Solve the quadratic: get ω²(K) with the two branches. This is the “(56) with ε(∞) included.”
  6. Identify the photon branch: choose the root that goes to ω→0 as K→0.
  7. Low-K expansion: expand that root for small K to show ω² ≈ c²K²/ε(0).
  8. Sanity checks: dimensional analysis; limits K→0, and consistency with ε(0)=ε(∞)ω_L²/ω_T².
Common mistakes
  • Using ε(∞) instead of ε(0) in the K→0 photon slope (that would correspond to fast polarization only).
  • Picking the wrong quadratic root for the photon branch.
  • Forgetting that ε(0)=ε(ω→0), not ε(ω_T).
  • Mixing k and K or dropping factors of c (unit errors).

PART 3 — Full Solution (DETAILED + TEACHING)

Physical intuition (before equations)

On the photon-like branch at low wavevector, the wavelength is huge and the frequency is low. The ionic lattice has enough time to respond, so the polarization is fully relaxed. Therefore the wave should propagate like a normal photon in a dielectric with refractive index n = √ε(0), i.e. ω ≈ (c/n)K = cK/√ε(0).

Mathematical model (variables, domain, coordinate system)
  • Domain: infinite homogeneous crystal, r ∈ ℝ³, time t ∈ ℝ.
  • Fields: E(r,t), H(r,t); assume harmonic plane waves ∝ e^{i(K·r−ωt)}.
  • Constitutive law (linear, isotropic): D(ω)=ε0 ε(ω)E(ω), B=μ0H.
  • Boundary/initial conditions: none needed for bulk dispersion; we seek non-trivial plane-wave eigenmodes.

Step 1 — Maxwell → transverse wave dispersion

In a source-free, homogeneous medium, Fourier-transforming Maxwell’s equations turns spatial derivatives into algebraic factors: ∇→iK and time derivatives into ∂/∂t→−iω. Eliminating H yields the vector wave equation 

K × (K × E0) = (ω^2/c^2) ε(ω) E0

For a transverse electromagnetic wave, K·E0 = 0, and the vector identity K×(K×E0)=K(K·E0)−K^2 E0 = −K^2 E0 reduces magnitude-wise to 

c^2 K^2 = ω^2 ε(ω)

This is valid because the system is linear, homogeneous, and we seek non-trivial eigenvectors E0 (determinant = 0 condition).

Step 2 — Insert the polar-crystal dielectric function (including ε(∞))

Use the standard lossless polar-crystal form: 

ε(ω) = ε(∞) (ω_L^2 − ω^2) / (ω_T^2 − ω^2)

Substitute into c²K² = ω² ε(ω): 

c^2 K^2 = ω^2 ε(∞) (ω_L^2 − ω^2) / (ω_T^2 − ω^2)

Step 3 — Clear denominators to obtain a polynomial equation

Multiply both sides by (ω_T² − ω²) (valid provided we are not exactly at the pole; the resulting equation still captures the modes away from the singular point): 

c^2 K^2 (ω_T^2 − ω^2) = ω^2 ε(∞) (ω_L^2 − ω^2)

Step 4 — Reduce to a quadratic in x = ω²

Let x = ω². Expand both sides:

Left:  c^2 K^2 ω_T^2 − c^2 K^2 x
Right: ε(∞) x ω_L^2 − ε(∞) x^2

Bring all terms to one side:

ε(∞) x^2 − (ε(∞) ω_L^2 + c^2 K^2) x + c^2 K^2 ω_T^2 = 0
Dimensional check
  • x has units of s⁻². Each term is ε(∞)×(s⁻⁴) or (m²/s²)(1/m²)(s⁻²)=s⁻⁴ times dimensionless factors → consistent.

Step 5 — Solve for ω²(K): the modified “(56)” including ε(∞) (Part a)

Solve the quadratic for x: 

Let x = ω^2.

x = 1/2 * [ (ω_L^2 + c^2 K^2 / ε(∞))
           ± sqrt( (ω_L^2 + c^2 K^2 / ε(∞))^2
                   − 4 (c^2 K^2 ω_T^2)/ε(∞) ) ]

Therefore, the two polariton branches are ω_±(K) = √x_±. This is exactly what the “(56) becomes” when ε(∞) is retained: all occurrences of c²K² effectively appear as c²K²/ε(∞) inside the bracket and discriminant, and the constant term is divided by ε(∞).

Why the structure changes this way (quick rationale)

Since ε(∞) multiplies the entire dielectric function, the dispersion equation c²K² = ω² ε(∞) f(ω²) can be rewritten as c²K²/ε(∞) = ω² f(ω²). Algebraically, this rescales the “photon” stiffness term.

Step 6 — Identify the photon-like solution and take the low-K limit (Part b)

The photon branch at low wavevector is the solution that satisfies ω→0 as K→0. Look at the quadratic: 

ε(∞) x^2 − (ε(∞) ω_L^2 + c^2 K^2) x + c^2 K^2 ω_T^2 = 0

For very small K, we expect x to be small on the photon branch. In that case, the ε(∞) x² term is higher order (small squared), so to leading order we keep only the linear-in-x balance: 

− (ε(∞) ω_L^2 + c^2 K^2) x + c^2 K^2 ω_T^2 ≈ 0

As K→0, the term c²K² in the parentheses is negligible compared to ε(∞)ω_L², giving 

x ≈ (c^2 K^2 ω_T^2) / (ε(∞) ω_L^2)

Now use the static dielectric constant implied by the model: 

ε(0) = ε(ω→0) = ε(∞) (ω_L^2)/(ω_T^2)

Rearranging ε(0)=ε(∞) ω_L²/ω_T² gives ω_T²/(ε(∞)ω_L²)=1/ε(0). Substitute into the small-K expression: 

x = ω^2 ≈ c^2 K^2 / ε(0)
Final Answer (Parts a & b)

(a) Including ε(∞), the “(56)” branch formula becomes

ω_±^2(K) = 1/2 * [ (ω_L^2 + c^2 K^2 / ε(∞))
                 ± sqrt( (ω_L^2 + c^2 K^2 / ε(∞))^2
                         − 4 (c^2 K^2 ω_T^2)/ε(∞) ) ]

(b) The photon-like solution at low wavevector satisfies

ω(K) ≈ cK / √ε(0)     as K → 0,   with   ε(0)=ε(∞) ω_L^2/ω_T^2.

Sanity checks

Checks
  • Units: cK has units of s⁻¹, and ε is dimensionless ⇒ ω has correct units.
  • Limiting case K→0: photon branch gives ω→0; the other branch tends to ω→ω_L (from the ± root), consistent with a longitudinal-like high-frequency limit of the coupled system.
  • Physical interpretation: long-wavelength EM fields allow ionic displacement to contribute to polarization ⇒ use ε(0), not ε(∞).
Alternate “one-line” proof of part (b) (very physical)

Start from the general transverse-wave relation c²K² = ω² ε(ω). For the photon branch at low K, the mode frequency is also low, so ε(ω)≈ε(0). Then immediately: 

c^2 K^2 = ω^2 ε(0)  ⇒  ω = cK / √ε(0).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (THEORY AROUND THE RESULT)

Re-interpreting the final formula

The key outcome ω ≈ cK/√ε(0) says: the slope of the photon branch near the origin is controlled by the static dielectric constant. Mathematically, it is the leading term in the small-K expansion of the small root of a quadratic in ω².

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Increasing ε(0) (stronger ionic polarizability) reduces the photon branch group velocity v_g≈c/√ε(0).
  • A larger separation between ω_L and ω_T increases ε(0)/ε(∞)=ω_L²/ω_T², typically slowing low-frequency propagation more.
  • ε(∞) matters most at higher frequencies (where ions cannot follow), effectively setting the “fast” refractive index.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

You can derive the same dispersion by writing coupled equations for the lattice polarization P (a driven harmonic oscillator ODE) and Maxwell’s equations, then Fourier transforming: 

(d^2P/dt^2) + ω_T^2 P = α E    (lossless oscillator model)
D = ε0 E + P

Eliminating P in frequency space gives D(ω)=ε0 ε(ω)E(ω), recovering the same ε(ω) and hence the same polariton dispersion. This highlights the linear systems viewpoint: a driven oscillator (lattice) coupled to a wave equation (EM field).

Concept check (self-test)
  • Q: Why does the low-K photon branch use ε(0) instead of ε(∞)?
    A: Low K on the photon branch implies low ω; ions can follow the field, contributing to polarization → ε(ω→0)=ε(0).
  • Q: What mathematical step creates the two branches?
    A: Substituting dispersive ε(ω) turns the dispersion into a quadratic equation in ω², producing two roots (two eigenmodes).
  • Q: If damping were included, what changes first?
    A: ε(ω) becomes complex, and ω(K) can acquire an imaginary part (attenuation) or K becomes complex for driven waves.
  • Q: What is the engineering meaning of v_g≈c/√ε(0)?
    A: It sets signal/energy transport speed at long wavelengths in that crystal (important for timing, delays, and impedance matching in IR/THz components).
