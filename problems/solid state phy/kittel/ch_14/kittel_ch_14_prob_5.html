Quick Summary

  • What this problem is about: the natural oscillation frequency of a uniform electron displacement inside a conducting sphere (a “plasmon mode”).
  • Key physics idea: a displaced electron gas creates a restoring depolarization field inside the sphere that pulls electrons back.
  • Core constitutive relation: polarization from displacement P = −ne r (Gaussian/cgs), where r(t) is the mean electron displacement.
  • Geometry-specific field: uniform polarization inside a sphere produces a uniform internal field E = −(4π/3) P.
  • Governing equation: Newton’s law gives a harmonic-oscillator ODE m r¨ + (4πne²/3) r = 0.
  • Result (symbolic): ω₀² = 4πne²/(3m) (Gaussian/cgs) — a collective mode because all electrons oscillate coherently.
  • Engineering SI translation: ω₀² = ne²/(3ε₀ m) (SI) for the same idealized model.
  • Physical meaning: higher electron density n increases the “spring constant,” raising the resonance as ω₀ ∝ √n.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

ΣKey quantities
  • n — electron number density (SI: m−3)
  • e — elementary charge magnitude (SI: C)
  • m — electron mass (SI: kg)
  • r(t) — mean displacement of electron fluid relative to ions (SI: m)
  • P — polarization (dipole moment per volume). SI: C·m/m3 = C/m2
  • E — electric field (SI: V/m)
  • ω₀ — resonance (angular) frequency (SI: rad/s)
⚠Units convention in this problem

The statement uses the classical electrostatics result for a uniformly polarized sphere: E = −(4π/3)P, which is written in Gaussian/cgs units.

In SI, the same geometric result is: E = −P/(3ε₀).

Same physics; different unit conventions.

0.2 Physical meaning and intuition

A metal (or electron gas) can be modeled as a positive “jellium” background (ions) plus a mobile electron fluid. If the electrons are displaced uniformly by a small vector r relative to the ions, the sphere develops opposite surface charges on the two sides. Those charges generate a uniform internal field that points opposite the polarization, acting like a restoring force.

0.3 Key laws/principles + validity conditions

  • Electrostatic (quasi-static) approximation: sphere size ≪ electromagnetic wavelength at ω (so retardation and radiation are neglected).
  • Linear response: small displacements so polarization is linear in r.
  • Rigid electron-fluid motion: all electrons move together (uniform mode), so the displacement field is spatially constant inside the sphere.
  • No damping: collisions and losses are ignored (ideal resonance frequency).

0.4 Common models/approximations used here

MModel stack (what we keep / neglect)
  • Keep: charge separation → depolarization field → restoring acceleration.
  • Neglect: spatial dispersion, pressure term (Thomas–Fermi), surface roughness, interband effects, damping (γ), radiation reaction.
  • Why it’s used: gives a clean analytic eigenfrequency capturing the dominant geometry factor (1/3 for a sphere).

0.5 Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • Higher density, stiffer “spring”: more electrons per volume means more charge separation for the same displacement → stronger restoring field → larger ω.
  • Shape matters: a sphere has depolarization factor 1/3. A needle-like particle has a smaller depolarization factor along its long axis → lower plasmon frequency.
  • Collective vs single-particle: frequency depends on n, not on temperature (in this ideal model) — it’s a bulk charge-density oscillation.

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Sign conventions: electrons carry −e, so P = −ne r. Missing the minus sign flips the restoring force.
  • Unit system mixing: don’t combine E = −(4π/3)P with SI forces; use consistent units or translate carefully.
  • Uniform mode assumption: this is the dipolar (l=1) uniform displacement mode; higher multipoles have different fields and resonances.
  • Quasi-static limit: for larger spheres, retardation shifts and broadens resonance.
✓Math Insight

The entire plasmon-mode derivation reduces to an eigenfrequency of a linear ODE: a harmonic oscillator of the form r¨ + ω₀² r = 0. Mathematically, this is the simplest Sturm–Liouville-type eigenvalue situation (constant coefficients), where ω₀² plays the role of the eigenvalue set by geometry (depolarization factor) and material parameters (n, e, m).

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this shows up (concrete application areas)

  • Plasmonic sensing & biosensors: nanoparticles used for refractive-index sensing (LSPR shifts with environment).
  • Photothermal therapy & heating: plasmon resonance enhances absorption → localized heating (medical and materials processing).
  • Metamaterials & antennas at optical/IR: engineered resonant inclusions behave like tiny “LC” elements at high frequencies.
  • Color engineering in coatings: nanoparticle plasmon resonances produce vivid, tunable colors (e.g., decorative films, security inks).

Why engineers care

  • Performance: resonance frequency sets what wavelength is absorbed/scattered strongly.
  • Efficiency: determines field enhancement → affects sensing limit-of-detection and nonlinear response.
  • Reliability: heating at resonance can cause sintering/shape changes; understanding ω helps avoid damaging regimes.
  • Cost & manufacturability: spherical particles are easier to produce reproducibly; the sphere’s simple factor 1/3 is a benchmark for QA.

Measurement/validation methods (brief)

  • Optical spectroscopy: extinction/absorption/scattering spectra; resonance peak gives ω (or λ).
  • Electron energy-loss spectroscopy (EELS): maps plasmon modes at nanoscale.
  • Ellipsometry / dielectric function extraction: validates the underlying ε(ω) used in more complete models.

Design trade-offs and constraints (non-idealities)

  • Damping: collisions (Drude γ), surface scattering, and interband transitions broaden and shift the peak.
  • Shape & tolerances: deviations from a sphere change depolarization factors → resonance variability across a batch.
  • Environment: embedding dielectric constant changes the resonance condition in full electrodynamics (Mie/LSPR models).
  • Size effects: for larger particles, radiation damping and retardation reduce the accuracy of the quasi-static formula.
From lab to product: how the ideal model is adapted

In real devices, the “ω₀” here becomes a starting point. Engineers typically replace the lossless electron-gas model by a Drude dielectric function ε(ω) = ε∞ − ωp²/(ω² + iγω), include an embedding medium εm, and then apply a geometry factor (depolarization). Manufacturing specs then focus on controlling particle size distribution, shape anisotropy, and surface chemistry because these dominate resonance spread and stability.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No solving yet)

1.1 Restate the problem in plain language

A sphere contains an electron gas of number density n. If the electrons are displaced uniformly by an average vector r(t) relative to the positive background, the polarization is P = −ne r. For a uniformly polarized sphere, the internal depolarization field is E = −(4π/3)P. Using the electron equation of motion, show that the resonance (uniform plasmon) frequency satisfies: ω₀² = (4πne²)/(3m).

1.2 Given, unknowns, and what must be shown

GGiven
  • Uniform depolarization field (sphere): E = −(4π/3)P
  • Polarization from displacement: P = −ne r
  • Electron mass m, charge magnitude e, density n
UUnknown / To prove
  • Derive the equation of motion for r(t)
  • Identify the resonance frequency ω₀
  • Show: ω₀² = 4πne²/(3m) (Gaussian/cgs)

1.3 Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Electrostatics of polarized media: for uniform polarization in an ellipsoid, internal field is uniform (by symmetry and linearity).
  • Newton’s second law: electrons accelerate due to electric force F = qE with q = −e.
  • Linear ODE theory: constant-coefficient second-order ODE yields harmonic motion and defines ω.

1.4 Assumptions (explicit)

AAssumptions box
  • Uniform displacement mode: r(t) is spatially constant inside the sphere.
  • Quasi-static: no magnetic induction, no radiation, no retardation (∇×E = 0, electrostatic field).
  • Lossless and collisionless: no damping term mγ r˙.
  • Positive background fixed (ion inertia neglected).

1.5 Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

  1. Direct polarization + depolarization field (given): quickest, uses known internal field of a polarized sphere. Best for this problem.
  2. Surface charge method: compute bound surface charge density σb = P·n̂, then compute E inside the sphere from that surface charge distribution. More labor, but reveals where the factor 1/3 comes from.
  3. Energy method: compute electrostatic energy stored due to displacement, interpret it as (1/2)k r², then use ω² = k/m. Elegant and physically transparent, but requires careful energy bookkeeping.

We will choose Approach 1 because the problem explicitly provides the depolarization field and polarization relation, letting us focus on the physics-to-ODE mapping cleanly and rigorously.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Define the model variables (r(t), P(t), E(t)) and the geometry (sphere, uniform polarization).
  2. Use the polarization-displacement relation P = −ne r to express P in terms of r.
  3. Use the depolarization field relation E = −(4π/3)P to express E in terms of r.
  4. Write the electron equation of motion using Newton’s law: m r¨ = qE with q = −e.
  5. Substitute E(r) into the equation of motion to obtain a linear ODE for r(t).
  6. Identify harmonic-oscillator form r¨ + ω₀² r = 0 and read off ω₀².
  7. Sanity checks: signs (restoring), dimensions, scaling with n, and compare with bulk plasma frequency.
!Common mistakes
  • Using m r¨ = +eE instead of m r¨ = −eE (electron charge is negative).
  • Dropping the minus sign in P = −ne r.
  • Mixing SI and Gaussian formulas (watch for ε₀ vs 4π factors).
  • Forgetting that “uniform mode” means E is uniform inside (no ∇ dependence here).

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

If electrons shift by r, the sphere polarizes. A polarized sphere produces an internal field opposing the polarization. Since electrons are negatively charged, that field exerts a force that pulls them back toward equilibrium. So we expect r(t) to obey a restoring-force equation: a harmonic oscillation with some ω₀ that grows with density n.

3.2 Mathematical physics model (variables, domain, operators, conditions)

ℳMathematical model statement
  • Domain: solid sphere of radius R, interior region Ω = {x ∈ ℝ³ : |x| < R}.
  • Unknown dynamical variable: mean electron displacement r(t) ∈ ℝ³ (uniform in space).
  • Fields: electrostatic field E(x,t) satisfying ∇×E = 0 (quasi-static).
  • Constitutive relations: P(t) = −ne r(t) and (for a sphere) E(t) = −(4π/3)P(t), uniform in Ω.
  • Equation of motion (ODE): Newton’s law for electrons: m r¨(t) = q E(t) with q = −e.
  • Initial conditions: r(0) = r₀, r˙(0) = v₀ (arbitrary small perturbation).

3.3 Step-by-step derivation

Step 1 — Polarization from a uniform displacement.

If each electron (charge −e) is displaced by the same vector r relative to the fixed positive background, the dipole moment per electron is p = (−e) r. With n electrons per unit volume, polarization (dipole moment density) is P = n p = n(−e r) = −ne r.

Key equation (given / definition) 
P = - n e r

Why this step is valid: it’s a linear superposition statement—polarization is the sum (per volume) of identical dipoles. Linearity holds for small displacements.

Step 2 — Depolarization field inside a uniformly polarized sphere.

A uniformly polarized sphere produces a uniform internal electric field opposing P:

Geometry law for a sphere (given) 
E = - (4π/3) P      (Gaussian/cgs)

Why this step is valid: by symmetry, uniform P in a sphere implies a field that must be uniform inside (no preferred interior point). The factor 1/3 is the sphere’s depolarization factor (a geometric eigenvalue of the electrostatic boundary-value problem).

Step 3 — Express E directly in terms of r.

Substitute P = −ne r into E = −(4π/3)P:

Algebraic substitution 
E = - (4π/3) P
  = - (4π/3) (-n e r)
  = (4π n e / 3) r

So the internal field is parallel to r (same direction). That might look surprising until you remember the electron charge is negative: the force on electrons is F = qE = (−e)E, which will be opposite to r (restoring).

Step 4 — Newton’s second law for the electron fluid.

Treat the electron gas as a rigid fluid element. The mean displacement obeys

Equation of motion 
m r¨(t) = q E(t) = - e E(t)

Why this step is valid: the uniform mode has no internal deformation, so the electron fluid element behaves like a single coordinate r(t). The dynamics reduce from a PDE to an ODE (a symmetry reduction).

Step 5 — Substitute E(r) to get a closed ODE for r(t).

Insert E = (4π n e / 3) r into m r¨ = −eE:

Derivation of the oscillator form 
m r¨ = - e E
    = - e (4π n e / 3) r
    = - (4π n e^2 / 3) r

Divide both sides by m (allowed since m ≠ 0) and move terms to the left:

Standard form 
r¨ + (4π n e^2 / 3m) r = 0

This is exactly the harmonic oscillator equation r¨ + ω₀² r = 0. Therefore we identify

★Final Result (Gaussian/cgs)
Resonance frequency of the uniform plasmon mode (sphere) 
ω0^2 = (4π n e^2) / (3 m)
Plain-text final answer (copy) 
Uniform plasmon mode of a sphere:
ω0^2 = (4π n e^2)/(3 m)   (Gaussian/cgs)

SI translation:
ω0^2 = (n e^2)/(3 ε0 m)

3.4 Sanity checks (dimensions, limits, signs, interpretation)

✓Dimensional analysis check (SI version)

In SI, ω₀² = ne²/(3ε₀ m). Units: n is m−3, e² is C², ε₀ is C²/(N·m²), so e²/ε₀ has units N·m². Then n(e²/ε₀) has units N/m = kg/s². Divide by m (kg) → 1/s². Correct for ω².

✓Sign / restoring-force check

We found E ∥ r, but electrons feel force F = −eE, so F ∥ −r. Therefore equilibrium is stable and oscillations occur.

✓Limiting and comparison checks
  • As n → 0: ω₀ → 0. With no electrons, no collective oscillation. Good.
  • Scaling: ω₀ ∝ √n. Doubling density increases ω₀ by √2. Reasonable for “spring constant” ∝ n.
  • Relation to bulk plasma frequency: in Gaussian/cgs, bulk ωp² = 4πne²/m. Our sphere mode gives ω₀ = ωp/√3, reflecting the sphere depolarization factor 1/3.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpret the formula (what controls what)

  • n (density): sets how much charge is displaced per unit volume → stiffness of restoring field → ω increases as √n.
  • e (charge): stronger coupling to E → larger restoring force → ω increases with |e|.
  • m (mass): inertia; larger m slows motion → ω decreases as 1/√m.
  • Geometry (sphere): the factor 1/3 is the depolarization factor for a sphere; other shapes replace 1/3 by a different factor L.

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative + simple quantitative)

∂Sensitivity snapshot
  • ω₀(n): d ln ω₀ / d ln n = 1/2 (a 10% increase in n → ≈ 5% increase in ω₀).
  • ω₀(m): d ln ω₀ / d ln m = −1/2.

4.3 Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Energy method sketch (how ω emerges as an eigenvalue)

The displaced electron gas creates an internal field E (uniform for the sphere). The electrostatic energy density is (in SI) u = (1/2)ε₀E². Since E ∝ r, the total energy U ∝ r², i.e., U = (1/2)k r² for an effective spring constant k. Then the collective coordinate r behaves like a harmonic oscillator: m r¨ = −k r → ω² = k/m. This method highlights that the plasmon is the normal mode minimizing energy subject to the geometry-imposed field pattern.

4.4 Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why is this called a “collective” excitation?
    A: Because the restoring field arises from the macroscopic polarization produced by the coherent motion of many electrons; the mode involves the electron gas as a whole.
  • Q: What changes if the particle is not a sphere?
    A: The depolarization factor changes. The internal field becomes E = −4π L P (Gaussian) or E = −(L/ε₀)P (SI), so ω depends on L.
  • Q: What is the relationship to the bulk plasma frequency ωp?
    A: For a sphere in this ideal model, ω₀ = ωp/√3.
  • Q: What physics makes real resonances broader and shifted?
    A: Damping (electron scattering), interband transitions, surface roughness, radiation damping, and nonlocal response.
