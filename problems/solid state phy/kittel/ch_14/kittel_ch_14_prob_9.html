Bulk Modulus of the Fermi Gas (T = 0): Kinetic-Energy Contribution

We derive the bulk modulus of a degenerate (zero-temperature) electron gas from first principles and connect it to the speed of sound in a compressible medium—an idea that shows up in metals, plasmas, and materials engineering.

Quick Summary

  • What: Compute the bulk modulus B of a zero-temperature (degenerate) electron gas due to kinetic energy.
  • Key physics idea: Degeneracy pressure arises because the Pauli principle fills momentum states up to the Fermi momentum even at T=0.
  • Governing thermodynamic relation: B = -V (∂P/∂V) at fixed entropy/temperature; equivalently B = n (∂P/∂n).
  • Equation of state at T=0: P ∝ n^(5/3) for a 3D ideal Fermi gas (nonrelativistic).
  • Main result (symbolic): B = (1/3) n m v_F^2 = (2/3) n ε_F, where ε_F = (1/2) m v_F^2.
  • Sound speed link: For a compressible medium of mass density ρ, v_s = sqrt(B/ρ).
  • For metals: If inertia is mostly ionic (ρ ≈ n M), then v_s = v_F sqrt(m/(3M)), matching the prompt.
  • Meaning: Even at absolute zero, the electron gas is “stiff” to compression because changing volume shifts the filled Fermi sea.
Key Equations (copyable)
B = -V (∂P/∂V) = n (∂P/∂n)
P = (2/5) n ε_F
ε_F = (ħ^2/2m) (3π^2 n)^(2/3) = (1/2) m v_F^2
⇒ B = (5/3) P = (2/3) n ε_F = (1/3) n m v_F^2

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

Mechanical / thermodynamic quantities
  • Number density n [m−3]: particles per volume.
  • Volume V [m3], particle number N [dimensionless], with n = N/V.
  • Pressure P [Pa = N/m2].
  • Bulk modulus B [Pa]: stiffness under uniform compression.
  • Mass density ρ [kg/m3]: inertia of the compressible medium.
Quantum / Fermi-gas quantities
  • Electron mass m [kg].
  • Planck constant ħ [J·s].
  • Fermi wavevector k_F [m−1].
  • Fermi energy ε_F [J].
  • Fermi velocity v_F [m/s].

0.2 Physical meaning (intuition)

  • B answers: “How much does pressure rise if I compress the material a little?” Large B means hard to compress.
  • A classical ideal gas has pressure from thermal motion; at T=0 it would have P=0.
  • A Fermi gas has pressure even at T=0, because fermions must occupy distinct momentum states up to k_F. Compressing increases n, which increases k_F, raising kinetic energy and thus pressure.

0.3 Key laws/principles and validity conditions

Assumptions behind the “ideal” T=0 Fermi gas
  • Nonrelativistic dispersion: ε(k)=ħ²k²/(2m) (good when v_F ≪ c).
  • 3D, uniform electron gas with spin degeneracy 2.
  • No attractive interactions (as the prompt notes). We keep only kinetic-energy (degeneracy) effects.
  • Zero temperature: step-function occupancy up to k_F.

0.4 Common models/approximations (and why)

  • Continuum thermodynamics: treat P(n) as an equation of state; bulk modulus is a derivative.
  • Local density approximation idea: even in slowly varying situations, one uses the uniform-gas formula locally.
  • Linear acoustics: for small-amplitude sound, v_s^2 = (∂P/∂ρ); for uniform n this becomes v_s = sqrt(B/ρ).

0.5 Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • Compression increases “Fermi sea depth”: higher density forces electrons into higher momentum states, raising pressure.
  • Power-law stiffness: because k_F ∝ n^{1/3}, energies scale like ε_F ∝ k_F^2 ∝ n^{2/3}, and pressure scales like P ∝ n ε_F ∝ n^{5/3}.

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Bulk modulus definition: B = -V(∂P/∂V) (sign matters!).
  • Hold the right variable fixed: for T=0 the isothermal and adiabatic derivatives coincide, but in general they differ.
  • Don’t confuse electron mass density (n m) with lattice/ionic mass density (n M) used for sound speed in solids.
  • Use consistent 3D DOS factors: the factor 3/5 in energy density is specific to 3D nonrelativistic fermions.
Math Insight

The bulk modulus here is the tangent stiffness of an equation of state: B = n (dP/dn). Since the Fermi-gas equation of state is a power law P(n) ∝ n^(5/3), the derivative is structurally simple: B = (5/3) P. Mathematically, this is the hallmark of polytropic relations used widely in fluid mechanics and astrophysics.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Why engineers care

  • Mechanical stiffness & acoustic response: Electron degeneracy contributes to compressibility and can influence elastic and acoustic properties in conductors (especially in simplified models and scaling estimates).
  • High-pressure materials: In extreme compression (shock loading, diamond-anvil experiments), electron degeneracy pressure becomes a key part of the equation of state.
  • Plasmas & dense matter: In inertial confinement fusion targets and warm dense matter, degeneracy alters sound speed and compressibility benchmarks.

Where it appears (3+ concrete application areas)

  • Metallurgy & NDE: Ultrasonic testing depends on sound speeds; simple relations link elastic moduli, density, and wave speeds.
  • Microelectronics / thin films: Electron density and effective mass influence transport; compressibility enters screening and collective modes (e.g., plasmons) that affect high-frequency behavior.
  • High-pressure engineering: EOS modeling for impact, detonation physics (in inert materials), and planetary materials uses Fermi-gas-like scaling as a baseline term.
  • Cryogenic systems: At very low temperatures, electronic contributions to thermodynamics and response functions become more visible relative to lattice thermal contributions.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Ultrasonic pulse-echo to measure wave speeds, then infer moduli using continuum relations.
  • Brillouin scattering (optical phonons/acoustic modes) to access sound velocities in materials.
  • High-pressure EOS from shock Hugoniot data and diamond-anvil cell measurements.

Design trade-offs and constraints (real materials)

  • Interactions & band structure: Real electrons live in bands with effective masses, and Coulomb interactions modify compressibility.
  • Ionic lattice dominates inertia: For sound in solids, mass density is mostly ionic, so the electron-derived stiffness must be paired with ionic density when estimating acoustic speeds.
  • Manufacturability & variability: Alloying changes electron density and effective mass; microstructure changes elastic constants far more than the ideal-gas estimate in many regimes.
From lab to product: how the ideal model gets adapted
  • Replace m with an effective mass m* when band curvature matters.
  • Add interaction corrections using Fermi-liquid parameters or density-functional compressibility.
  • Use measured elastic constants (Young’s modulus, shear modulus) and convert to bulk modulus with solid-mechanics relations; treat the Fermi-gas result as a baseline scaling check.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement (in plain words)

For a 3D ideal electron Fermi gas at absolute zero, show that the kinetic-energy (degeneracy) contribution to the bulk modulus is B = (1/3) n m v_F^2. Then use the standard relation between bulk modulus and sound speed in a compressible medium to obtain v_s = v_F sqrt(m/(3M)) when the mass density is dominated by ions of mass M per electron.

Given / known

  • Degenerate (T=0) electron gas, nonrelativistic dispersion ε = ħ²k²/(2m).
  • Fermi velocity v_F and density n.
  • Sound speed relation (as stated): v_s = sqrt(B/ρ).

Unknowns / what must be proved

  • Prove: B = (1/3) n m v_F^2 (kinetic contribution at T=0).
  • Deduce: v_s = v_F sqrt(m/(3M)) given ρ ≈ nM.

Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Thermodynamic definition of bulk modulus: compression response of a uniform system.
  • Equation of state of an ideal Fermi gas at T=0: gives P(n) from quantum statistics.
  • Linear acoustics: relates wave speed to elastic stiffness and inertia.
Assumptions stated explicitly
  • Uniform, isotropic 3D gas; no external fields; equilibrium.
  • T = 0 so occupancy is a step function at k_F.
  • Neglect attractive interactions (as prompt says); we keep kinetic contribution only.
  • Small compressions (linear response): bulk modulus is a local derivative.

2–3 possible approaches (compare)

Approach A: Use known EOS P(n)
  • Pros: Fast, clean; highlights power-law scaling.
  • Cons: Requires remembering/deriving P = (2/5) n ε_F.
Approach B: Start from total energy E(V)
  • Pros: Directly shows how compression changes kinetic energy.
  • Cons: Needs careful derivative: P = -∂E/∂V at fixed N.
Chosen approach

We’ll do both in a consistent way: (i) derive the EOS at T=0 from state counting, then (ii) compute B = n (dP/dn). This is mathematically transparent and easiest to check dimensionally.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Set the model: uniform 3D ideal Fermi gas at T=0; define n, k_F, ε_F, v_F.
  2. State counting: compute n in terms of k_F using the volume of a Fermi sphere in k-space.
  3. Energy density: integrate ε(k) over occupied states to get U/V.
  4. Pressure from energy: use P = - (∂E/∂V)_N or the known identity P = (2/3)(U/V) for nonrelativistic free particles (derive briefly).
  5. Equation of state: show P = (2/5) n ε_F and thus P ∝ n^(5/3).
  6. Bulk modulus: use B = n (∂P/∂n) to get B = (5/3)P.
  7. Express in desired form: rewrite via ε_F = (1/2) m v_F^2 to obtain B = (1/3) n m v_F^2.
  8. Sound speed: use v_s = sqrt(B/ρ); set ρ ≈ nM to get the stated estimate.
  9. Sanity checks: dimensions, scaling with density, and physical interpretation.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting N is fixed when differentiating E(V).
  • Dropping the minus sign in P = - (∂E/∂V)_N.
  • Using electron mass density nm in sound speed when the problem intends ionic inertia nM.
  • Mixing 2D and 3D formulas (the 3π² factor is 3D-specific).

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Physical intuition (before calculating)

If we compress the electron gas, the density n rises. The Pauli principle forces electrons into higher momentum states, increasing the maximum occupied momentum p_F = ħ k_F. That raises the average kinetic energy, producing a pressure that grows rapidly with density. Therefore we expect a positive bulk modulus, and a power-law scaling B ∝ n^(5/3).

3.2 Mathematical model (variables, domain, conditions)

Model statement (mathematical physics)
  • System: N noninteracting fermions (electrons) in a cubic box of volume V with periodic boundary conditions (standard ideal-gas quantization).
  • State variable: uniform number density n = N/V (scalar field constant in space).
  • Dispersion: ε(k)=ħ²k²/(2m), k ∈ ℝ³.
  • Occupation at T=0: f(k)=1 for |k| ≤ k_F, and f(k)=0 for |k| > k_F.
  • Bulk modulus definition: for uniform compression, B = -V (∂P/∂V) (at fixed entropy/temperature; at T=0 both coincide).
  • Linear acoustics (wave context): small perturbations satisfy a conservation-law linearization leading to v_s^2 = (∂P/∂ρ), and for uniform scaling this becomes v_s = sqrt(B/ρ).

3.3 Step 1: Relate density n to k_F (state counting)

In k-space, allowed states have density V/(2π)^3 per spin, and there are 2 spin states for electrons. Occupied states form a sphere of radius k_F. 

Number of states with |k| ≤ k_F:
N = 2 * (V/(2π)^3) * ∫_{|k|≤k_F} d^3k
  = 2 * (V/(2π)^3) * (4π/3) k_F^3.

Divide by V:
n = N/V = (1/3π^2) k_F^3
⇒ k_F = (3π^2 n)^(1/3).

3.4 Step 2: Fermi energy and Fermi velocity

The highest occupied single-particle energy at T=0 is the Fermi energy: 

ε_F = ε(k_F) = ħ^2 k_F^2 / (2m) = (ħ^2/2m) (3π^2 n)^(2/3).
Define v_F via p_F = ħ k_F = m v_F  ⇒ v_F = ħ k_F / m.
Then ε_F = (1/2) m v_F^2.

3.5 Step 3: Compute the kinetic energy density U/V

The total internal energy U at T=0 is the sum of occupied single-particle energies. Replace the sum by an integral over occupied k-states (valid in the thermodynamic limit). 

U = 2 * (V/(2π)^3) * ∫_{|k|≤k_F} ε(k) d^3k
  = 2 * (V/(2π)^3) * ∫_{0}^{k_F} [ħ^2 k^2/(2m)] * 4π k^2 dk
  = 2 * (V/(2π)^3) * (4π) * (ħ^2/(2m)) * ∫_{0}^{k_F} k^4 dk
  = 2 * (V/(2π)^3) * (4π) * (ħ^2/(2m)) * (k_F^5/5).

Simplify constants:
2*(4π) = 8π, and (2π)^3 = 8π^3
⇒ U = V * (ħ^2/(2m)) * (1/π^2) * (k_F^5/5).

Therefore energy density:
U/V = (ħ^2 k_F^5)/(10 m π^2).

Now express U/V in terms of n and ε_F. From state counting, n = k_F^3/(3π^2), so k_F^5/(π^2) = k_F^2 * k_F^3/(π^2) = k_F^2 * 3n. Also ε_F = ħ^2 k_F^2/(2m). 

U/V = (ħ^2/(10m)) * (k_F^5/π^2)
    = (ħ^2/(10m)) * (k_F^2 * 3n)
    = (3n/5) * (ħ^2 k_F^2/(2m))
    = (3/5) n ε_F.

3.6 Step 4: Pressure P at T=0

We can obtain pressure from the thermodynamic identity (fixed particle number N): P = - (∂U/∂V)_N. Since n = N/V, any U expressed as U = V·u(n) implies: P = n u'(n) - u(n). Here, it’s equivalent (and standard) to use the known nonrelativistic relation P = (2/3)(U/V), which follows from isotropic momentum flux/virial reasoning for quadratic dispersion.

Optional derivation sketch: why P = (2/3)(U/V) for ε ∝ k²

For a uniform isotropic gas with single-particle energy ε(p)=p²/(2m), the pressure equals one-third the momentum flux. Averaging over directions gives P = (1/3)⟨n p v⟩. Since v = p/m, we get p·v = p²/m = 2ε, so the energy density U/V = ⟨n ε⟩ implies P = (2/3)(U/V). 

Using U/V = (3/5) n ε_F:
P = (2/3)(U/V) = (2/3) * (3/5) n ε_F = (2/5) n ε_F.

3.7 Step 5: Bulk modulus B

Bulk modulus is defined by uniform compression: B = -V (∂P/∂V). Since n = N/V, we rewrite the derivative in terms of n. This is a clean chain-rule step. 

Because n = N/V with N fixed:
dn/dV = d(N/V)/dV = -N/V^2 = -n/V.

Now chain rule:
(∂P/∂V)_N = (dP/dn) * (dn/dV) = (dP/dn) * (-n/V).

Therefore:
B = -V (∂P/∂V)_N = -V * [ (dP/dn)(-n/V) ] = n (dP/dn).

Next we need P(n). From above, P = (2/5) n ε_F, and ε_F ∝ n^(2/3), so P ∝ n * n^(2/3) = n^(5/3). Differentiate explicitly: 

Let ε_F(n) = C n^(2/3), where C = (ħ^2/2m) (3π^2)^(2/3).
Then P(n) = (2/5) n ε_F = (2/5) n * C n^(2/3) = (2/5) C n^(5/3).

Differentiate:
dP/dn = (2/5) C * (5/3) n^(2/3) = (2/3) C n^(2/3) = (2/3) ε_F.

Hence:
B = n (dP/dn) = n * (2/3) ε_F = (2/3) n ε_F.

Finally, convert to the form requested using ε_F = (1/2) m v_F^2: 

B = (2/3) n ε_F = (2/3) n * (1/2) m v_F^2 = (1/3) n m v_F^2.
Final Answer (boxed)
Kinetic (degeneracy) contribution to the bulk modulus of a 3D ideal electron Fermi gas at T = 0:

B = (2/3) n ε_F = (1/3) n m v_F^2.

Using v_s = sqrt(B/ρ) and ρ ≈ n M (ionic inertia):
v_s = v_F * sqrt(m/(3M)).

3.8 Sanity checks

Dimensional analysis

n m v_F^2 has units: (m−3)(kg)(m2/s2) = kg/(m·s2) = Pa. Multiplying by 1/3 keeps Pa. Good.

Scaling / limiting behavior

Since v_F ∝ n^(1/3), we get B ∝ n * n^(2/3) = n^(5/3). Compressing raises stiffness rapidly—consistent with degeneracy pressure intuition.

Physical interpretation (plain language)

Even at absolute zero, electrons “push back” against compression because the allowed quantum states get squeezed: the Fermi surface moves outward in momentum space, increasing kinetic energy and pressure. The bulk modulus quantifies that stiffness as a local derivative of the EOS.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpret the final formula

  • B = (2/3) n ε_F: stiffness is proportional to “energy per particle” (ε_F) times “how many particles per volume” (n).
  • B = (1/3) n m v_F^2: stiffness looks like a kinetic-energy density scale (~ n m v^2), with a geometric factor 1/3 from isotropy.

4.2 Parameter dependence

How changing parameters affects B
  • Increase density n ⇒ k_F increases ⇒ v_F increases ⇒ B increases strongly: B ∝ n^(5/3).
  • Increase effective mass (or use heavier carriers) ⇒ ε_F decreases at fixed n ⇒ B decreases (since ε_F ∝ 1/m for fixed n).
  • Include interactions ⇒ compressibility can increase or decrease depending on exchange-correlation (beyond this ideal estimate).

4.3 Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of using P = (2/3)(U/V), you can compute P directly from P = - (∂U/∂V)_N with U(V) = V·(3/5) n ε_F(n) and n=N/V. The chain rule produces the same P = (2/5) n ε_F and hence the same bulk modulus. This emphasizes thermodynamic conjugacy between P and V.

4.4 Concept checks (self-test + answers)

  • Q: Why is P nonzero at T=0 for fermions?
    A: Pauli exclusion forces occupation up to k_F, giving finite kinetic energy and pressure.
  • Q: If P ∝ n^(5/3), what is B in terms of P?
    A: B = n(dP/dn) = (5/3)P.
  • Q: What changes in 2D?
    A: Density of states differs; the power law changes, so P(n) and B(n) scale differently.
  • Q: Why does the sound speed in a metal use ρ ≈ nM rather than nm?
    A: Lattice ions carry most mass/inertia for acoustic waves; electrons provide pressure response but not the dominant inertia.
