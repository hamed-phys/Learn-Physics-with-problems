Response of an Electron Gas to a Suddenly Applied Electric Field Copper sheet • damping included

We debunk the common misconception that the static conductivity “sets the response frequency.” Using a self-consistent Drude–plasma model, we compute the relevant timescales for copper and solve the step-response problem for a field applied normal to a sheet.

Quick Summary

  • What this is about: How a metal’s electron gas responds in time when a DC electric field is turned on suddenly.
  • Key physics idea: For fast transients, the electrons have inertia and the field is self-consistently screened by charge separation; the natural timescale is set by the plasma frequency and scattering rate, not the DC conductivity.
  • Governing model: 1D damped plasma oscillator for electron displacement x(t): 
    x¨ + (1/τ) x˙ + ω_p^2 x = -(e/m) E_ext(t)
  • Self-consistency: Total internal field is E(t)=E_ext(t) - 4π n e x(t) (Gaussian), so screening emerges dynamically.
  • Order-of-magnitude frequencies for copper (room T): 1/τ ≈ 4×10^13 s^-1, ω_p ≈ 1.6×10^16 s^-1 (rad/s), while the “conductivity frequency” σ/ε0 in SI is ~10^19 s^-1—not physically controlling the transient.
  • Result type: Symbolic step-response (damped oscillations) plus numerical scales for copper.
  • Physical meaning: Turning on a normal field makes electrons slosh (plasma oscillations) while damping removes energy; the internal field decays toward zero (electrostatic screening).

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • n — conduction electron density m−3
  • e — elementary charge magnitude C (electron charge is −e)
  • m — electron mass kg
  • τ — momentum-relaxation time (scattering time) s
  • σ — conductivity: SI S/m; Gaussian-cgs s−1 (same symbol, different units)
  • ρ — resistivity Ω·m, with σ = 1/ρ in SI
  • ω_p — plasma angular frequency rad/s
  • E — electric field V/m (SI) or statV/cm (Gaussian)
  • j — current density A/m2

Physical meaning of key quantities

  • DC conductivity σ(0): how much steady current flows long after transients die out (low-frequency/steady transport).
  • Relaxation rate 1/τ: how quickly momentum is lost to phonons/impurities—this is the damping scale in time-domain dynamics.
  • Plasma frequency ω_p: the natural “spring” frequency of collective electron motion against the positively charged lattice background; it sets how fast charge separation restores neutrality (screening dynamics).

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

Maxwell + charge continuity

The electromagnetic field and charges obey, in operator form, ∇·E = ρ/ε0, ∇×B = μ0 j + μ0ε0 ∂E/∂t, and ∂ρ/∂t + ∇·j = 0. In a spatially uniform 1D normal geometry, these reduce to an ODE for the collective motion.

Drude equation of motion (with damping)

For the average electron velocity v(t): m dv/dt + (m/τ) v = -e E(t). This is linear, time-invariant (LTI), so step inputs produce exponential/oscillatory responses.

Common models/approximations (and why)

  • Linear response: fields small enough that drift velocity ≪ Fermi velocity, so response is proportional to E.
  • Uniform electron gas + rigid positive background: lets us treat collective motion as a single displacement x(t).
  • Single relaxation time τ: captures damping without microscopic complexity; good for order-of-magnitude and many engineering calculations.
  • 1D normal geometry: field applied normal to a sheet; symmetry kills lateral variation (no ∂/∂x, ∂/∂y), only time dependence remains.

Mini intuition examples (no heavy algebra)

  1. Slowly varying field (ωτ ≪ 1): electrons track the field almost instantly in a drift sense; you measure DC conductivity.
  2. Very fast field (ωτ ≫ 1): collisions can’t keep up; inertia dominates and the current lags the field—conductivity becomes complex and decreases in magnitude.
  3. Normal field to a conductor: steady-state internal field tends toward zero because charges rearrange to screen; the time to do so is tied to collective charge motion (plasma dynamics) plus damping.

What to watch for (pitfalls & misconceptions)

Pitfall: “σ has units of frequency, so it measures response frequency.”

In Gaussian units σ indeed carries s^-1 dimensions, but a dimensional resemblance is not a dynamical law. The real transient can be limited by ω_p (collective restoring force) and 1/τ (damping), and by electromagnetic propagation/geometry.

Math Insight (core equation as a mathematical object)

The step-response here reduces to a linear damped harmonic oscillator ODE with forcing: x¨ + (1/τ) x˙ + ω_p^2 x = constant·Θ(t). As an LTI system, its solution is the sum of a particular (steady) solution plus a homogeneous transient. The transient is exponentially decaying oscillation when ω_p > 1/(2τ) (underdamped), which is true for good metals.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (concrete areas)

  • High-speed interconnects & packaging: sub-ps/ps transients, EMC, and signal integrity depend on how quickly conductors screen fields.
  • RF & microwave components: complex conductivity σ(ω), skin depth, and reflection depend on τ and ω_p at high frequencies.
  • Ultrafast lasers & pump–probe metrology: femtosecond excitation drives electron gases far from equilibrium; plasma oscillations and damping show up in optical response.
  • Electrostatic discharge (ESD) & transient robustness: fast field changes can couple into metal structures; understanding the correct timescales matters for protection design.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: transient screening affects impedance, crosstalk, and rise-time fidelity.
  • Reliability: fast transients can concentrate currents/fields at edges (field enhancement), stressing materials.
  • Cost & manufacturability: selecting copper vs. alloys, plating thickness, and surface treatments depends on conductivity and high-frequency behavior.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Four-point probe for DC ρ (gives σ(0)).
  • THz time-domain spectroscopy (THz-TDS) to extract σ(ω), τ, and sometimes signatures related to ω_p.
  • Ellipsometry/reflectometry in optics: plasma frequency influences reflectance cutoffs.

Design trade-offs & constraints

  • Real materials: τ is reduced by impurities, grain boundaries, roughness, and temperature—especially in thin films.
  • Geometry: “sheet” vs. bulk changes boundary conditions and field penetration; edges break the ideal 1D symmetry.
  • Frequency regime: DC specs don’t predict ultrafast response; for GHz–THz you need σ(ω) and the correct electrodynamic model.
From lab to product

In product design, the ideal Drude/plasma model becomes: (i) frequency-dependent material data (σ(ω) from measurements), (ii) geometry-resolved Maxwell solvers (skin effect, edges), and (iii) temperature/process variability for robust tolerancing.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement (in plain words)

Some books say “the static conductivity σ (Gaussian units: frequency) measures the response frequency of a metal.” For copper at room temperature, evaluate the relevant characteristic frequencies: the conductivity frequency scale, the plasma frequency ω_p, and the scattering rate 1/τ. Then set up and solve the time response x(t) of the electron gas when an electric field is switched on suddenly, applied normal to a copper sheet: E(t<0)=0, E_ext(t>0)=1 (a step). Include damping.

Given quantities

  • Resistivity: ρ = 1 μΩ·cm
  • Electron concentration: n = 8×10^22 cm^-3
  • Mean free path: ℓ = 400 Å
  • Fermi velocity: v_F = 1.6×10^8 cm/s

Unknowns / outputs

  • Order-of-magnitude of three frequency scales: “conductivity frequency,” ω_p, 1/τ.
  • Mathematical model and solution for x(t) (electron displacement) and related current j(t) after a step field is applied.
  • Critique: why DC conductivity does not generally set the response frequency.

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Drude dynamics applies because we are modeling average electron motion with damping (scattering).
  • Self-consistent electrostatics applies because a normal field produces charge separation and a restoring field (plasma oscillations).
  • Linearity applies (small-signal), so superposition and standard ODE solution methods are valid.

Assumptions (explicit)

Assumptions
  • Uniform, isotropic copper; electron gas described by a single displacement x(t) along the field normal.
  • Positive lattice background fixed (ions immobile on these ultrafast timescales).
  • Field applied uniformly over a large sheet → effectively 1D normal problem (no edge effects).
  • Single relaxation time τ (phenomenological damping), constant during the transient.
  • Nonrelativistic; ignore magnetic induction details beyond the 1D electrostatic restoring field (appropriate for the normal-charge-separation mode).

Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

  1. Self-consistent displacement model (plasma oscillator): set up x(t) with restoring field from charge separation; yields a damped oscillator ODE. Best for this geometry.
  2. Frequency-domain conductivity σ(ω) + inverse transform: use Drude σ(ω) and solve for fields/currents via Laplace/Fourier. Powerful but more algebra and boundary-condition heavy.
  3. Maxwell + continuity PDE reduction: start from ∂ρ/∂t + ∇·j=0 and ∇·E=ρ/ε0, eliminate variables to get an ODE. Equivalent insight, more “field-theory” style.

Chosen approach

We use the self-consistent displacement (plasma oscillator) approach because the field is normal to the sheet, so screening is driven by a collective displacement and the mathematics becomes a clean, solvable ODE with clear physical interpretation.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Convert given data to SI (or consistently use Gaussian) so each frequency can be compared on the same scale.
  2. Estimate τ = ℓ / v_F (ballistic between collisions), hence 1/τ.
  3. Compute plasma frequency ω_p = sqrt(n e^2/(m ε0)) (SI) (or ω_p^2=4π n e^2/m in Gaussian).
  4. Interpret “conductivity as frequency” via Maxwell: compare conduction term to displacement term → characteristic scale σ/ε0 in SI (or 4πσ in Gaussian).
  5. Build the 1D model: electron displacement x(t), velocity v=x˙, current j=-n e v.
  6. Self-consistent field: total field E = E_ext - E_restoring, where E_restoring ∝ x.
  7. Derive the ODE: insert E into Drude equation → damped, driven harmonic oscillator.
  8. Solve the ODE for a step input using elementary methods (homogeneous + particular, apply initial conditions).
  9. Extract current and screening behavior and interpret limits (t→0+, t→∞, weak/strong damping).
Common mistakes
  • Mixing SI and Gaussian formulas for ω_p and the restoring field without converting.
  • Thinking “units of frequency” means “controls time response.” Dimensional analysis suggests candidates; dynamics selects which one actually appears in the governing ODE.
  • Forgetting that a normal field leads to charge buildup and a restoring field (you can’t treat E as externally fixed everywhere inside the conductor).
  • Dropping inertia (m dv/dt)—that erases plasma oscillations and mispredicts ultrafast response.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

When the field is switched on normal to the copper sheet, electrons accelerate briefly, but they quickly pile up at the surface, creating an opposing electric field that screens the interior. Because electrons have inertia, they don’t stop exactly at the screened state; they “overshoot” and oscillate collectively at about the plasma frequency ω_p. Scattering (finite τ) damps these oscillations.

A. Order-of-magnitude frequency scales for copper

A1) Scattering rate 1/τ from mean free path

The relaxation time is estimated by ballistic travel between collisions: τ ≈ ℓ / v_F. Convert: ℓ = 400 Å = 400×10^-10 m = 4.0×10^-8 m, v_F = 1.6×10^8 cm/s = 1.6×10^6 m/s. 

τ ≈ ℓ / v_F = (4.0×10^-8 m) / (1.6×10^6 m/s) ≈ 2.5×10^-14 s
1/τ ≈ 4×10^13 s^-1

Dimensional check: length / (length/time) = time. Good.

A2) Plasma frequency ω_p

In SI, the electron plasma frequency is ω_p = sqrt(n e^2/(m ε0)). Convert density: n = 8×10^22 cm^-3 = 8×10^28 m^-3. 

ω_p = sqrt(n e^2/(m ε0))
n = 8×10^28 m^-3
⇒ ω_p ≈ 1.6×10^16 s^-1 (rad/s)

This corresponds to a timescale ω_p^-1 ~ 6×10^-17 s and frequency f_p = ω_p/(2π) ≈ 2.5×10^15 Hz.

A3) The “conductivity frequency” and why it misleads

In Gaussian units, σ indeed has dimensions of s^-1 because it appears in Maxwell’s equation as ∇×B = (1/c) ∂E/∂t + (4π/c) j with j = σ E, so the conduction term is (4πσ/c)E(1/c)∂E/∂t ~ (ω/c)E. This suggests a crossover scale ω ~ 4πσ (Gaussian).

In SI, the analogous comparison in ∇×B = μ0 j + μ0ε0 ∂E/∂t yields a crossover μ0 σ E vs μ0 ε0 ω E, i.e. ω ~ σ/ε0.

From ρ = 1 μΩ·cm = 10^-8 Ω·m, we get σ = 1/ρ = 10^8 S/m. 

σ ≈ 10^8 S/m
σ/ε0 ≈ (10^8) / (8.85×10^-12) ≈ 1.1×10^19 s^-1
Critique (core point)

The number σ/ε0 ~ 10^19 s^-1 is far larger than the physically relevant microscopic rates in copper, namely ω_p ~ 10^16 s^-1 and 1/τ ~ 10^13 s^-1. The time response cannot be faster than the system’s natural collective dynamics (plasma oscillations) and damping; therefore, the “σ-as-response-frequency” claim is misleading for fast transients. DC conductivity describes the low-frequency limit of transport, not the ultrafast screening transient.

A4) Compare the three scales (numerically)

Copper (room T, order of magnitude):
1/τ  ≈ 4×10^13 s^-1
ω_p  ≈ 1.6×10^16 s^-1   (rad/s)
σ/ε0 ≈ 1.1×10^19 s^-1   (SI "crossover" scale)

Since ω_p ≫ 1/τ, copper is strongly underdamped on the plasma timescale: ω_p τ ~ 1.6×10^16 × 2.5×10^-14 ≈ 400 ≫ 1.

B. Mathematical model for the step-response normal to a sheet

B1) Variables, domain, coordinates, and boundary/initial conditions

Mathematical model (explicit)
  • Coordinate: 1D normal direction z. By symmetry, all quantities depend only on t.
  • Unknown: electron displacement x(t) along +z (relative to fixed ionic background).
  • Velocity: v(t) = x˙(t).
  • Current density: j(t) = -n e v(t) = -n e x˙(t).
  • External field (step): E_ext(t) = E0 Θ(t) with E0 = 1 (given).
  • Initial conditions: system at rest for t<0: x(0)=0, x˙(0)=0.

B2) Self-consistent restoring field (screening)

A displacement x creates a charge separation: electrons shift relative to ions, producing polarization P = -n e x (dipole moment per volume). In Gaussian electrostatics, the depolarization (restoring) field for a uniform displacement is proportional to x: 

E_restoring(t) = 4π n e x(t)   (Gaussian; direction opposes electron shift)
E_total(t) = E_ext(t) - E_restoring(t)

The key point is linearity: for small displacements, the restoring field is linear in x. This is the mathematical origin of harmonic-oscillator behavior.

Why this step is valid (linearity & symmetry)

Because the sheet is large and the field is applied normal, the problem is translationally invariant in the plane. Therefore ∂/∂x = ∂/∂y = 0 and only the normal displacement matters. For small perturbations around neutrality, Gauss’s law makes the electric field proportional to the separated charge density, which is proportional to displacement. Hence the restoring force is linear in x.

B3) Drude equation with damping (ODE)

The average electron (charge −e) obeys the damped equation of motion: 

m x¨(t) + (m/τ) x˙(t) = -e E_total(t)
E_total(t) = E_ext(t) - 4π n e x(t)

Substitute E_total into the equation (algebra shown explicitly): 

m x¨ + (m/τ) x˙ = -e [E_ext - 4π n e x]
= -e E_ext + 4π n e^2 x

Bring all x-terms to the left and divide by m:
x¨ + (1/τ) x˙ + (4π n e^2 / m) x = -(e/m) E_ext(t)

Define ω_p^2 ≡ 4π n e^2 / m  (Gaussian)
⇒ x¨ + (1/τ) x˙ + ω_p^2 x = -(e/m) E_ext(t)

Dimensional check: x¨ has units m/s^2, (1/τ)x˙ has (1/s)(m/s)=m/s^2, and ω_p^2 x has (1/s^2)m=m/s^2. The forcing term (e/m)E has units of acceleration. Consistent.

B4) Nondimensionalization (scaling argument)

Let t̃ = ω_p t and γ = 1/(2τ). Then the ODE becomes x''(t̃) + 2(γ/ω_p) x'(t̃) + x(t̃) = -(e/m)(E_ext/ω_p^2), showing the single key dimensionless damping parameter γ/ω_p = 1/(2ω_p τ). For copper, ω_p τ ≈ 400 so γ/ω_p ≈ 1/800: very weak damping on the plasma period.

C. Solve the ODE for a step field (elementary method)

C1) Specify forcing and initial conditions

Take E_ext(t) = E0 Θ(t) with E0=1 for the given step. For t>0, the forcing is constant, so we solve x¨ + (1/τ) x˙ + ω_p^2 x = -(e/m) E0, with x(0)=0, x˙(0)=0.

C2) Particular (steady) solution

Seek a constant particular solution x_p = constant. Then x˙=x¨=0, giving ω_p^2 x_p = -(e/m)E0, so 

x_p = -(e E0)/(m ω_p^2)
Using ω_p^2 = 4π n e^2/m ⇒ x_p = -E0/(4π n e)

This is precisely the displacement needed so that E_restoring = 4π n e x cancels the external field: as t→∞, E_total → E0 - 4π n e x_p = 0 (perfect screening in the ideal model).

C3) Homogeneous solution (transient)

Solve x¨ + (1/τ) x˙ + ω_p^2 x = 0. Characteristic equation: r^2 + (1/τ) r + ω_p^2 = 0. Let γ = 1/(2τ). Then r = -γ ± i ω_d, with ω_d = sqrt(ω_p^2 - γ^2). 

γ = 1/(2τ)
ω_d = sqrt(ω_p^2 - γ^2)   (underdamped if ω_p > γ)

x_h(t) = e^{-γ t} [A cos(ω_d t) + B sin(ω_d t)]

C4) Apply initial conditions (show steps)

The full solution for t>0 is x(t)=x_p + x_h(t). Use x(0)=0: 

x(0)= x_p + A = 0  ⇒ A = -x_p

Now use x˙(0)=0. Differentiate: 

x_h(t)= e^{-γ t}[A cos(ω_d t)+B sin(ω_d t)]

x˙_h(t)= e^{-γ t}[
 -γ(A cos(ω_d t)+B sin(ω_d t))
 + (-A ω_d sin(ω_d t) + B ω_d cos(ω_d t))
]

At t=0:
x˙_h(0)= -γ A + B ω_d

Since x_p is constant, x˙(0)=x˙_h(0)=0 gives -γ A + B ω_d = 0 so B = (γ/ω_d)A. With A=-x_p: 

B = (γ/ω_d) A = -(γ/ω_d) x_p

C5) Final closed-form displacement and current

Substitute A and B: 

x(t) = x_p + e^{-γ t}[A cos(ω_d t)+B sin(ω_d t)]
     = x_p + e^{-γ t}[-x_p cos(ω_d t) -(γ/ω_d) x_p sin(ω_d t)]

⇒ x(t) = x_p [ 1 - e^{-γ t}( cos(ω_d t) + (γ/ω_d) sin(ω_d t) ) ]

The current density (along the normal) is j(t) = -n e x˙(t). Differentiate the compact form (one clean way is to differentiate the bracketed term): 

Let F(t)= e^{-γ t}( cos(ω_d t) + (γ/ω_d) sin(ω_d t) )
x(t)= x_p[1 - F(t)]
⇒ x˙(t)= -x_p F˙(t)

Compute F˙(t):
F˙ = e^{-γ t}[ -γ( cos + (γ/ω_d) sin ) + ( -ω_d sin + (γ/ω_d) ω_d cos ) ]
   = e^{-γ t}[ -γ cos -(γ^2/ω_d) sin -ω_d sin + γ cos ]
   = -e^{-γ t}[ (ω_d + γ^2/ω_d) sin(ω_d t) ]
   = -e^{-γ t}[ (ω_p^2/ω_d) sin(ω_d t) ]   (since ω_d^2=ω_p^2-γ^2)

Thus:
x˙(t)= -x_p(-e^{-γ t})(ω_p^2/ω_d) sin(ω_d t)
      = x_p (ω_p^2/ω_d) e^{-γ t} sin(ω_d t)

With x_p = -(eE0)/(m ω_p^2):
x˙(t)= -(eE0)/(m ω_d) e^{-γ t} sin(ω_d t)

Finally:
j(t)= -n e x˙(t) = (n e^2 E0)/(m ω_d) e^{-γ t} sin(ω_d t)
Final Answer (boxed)
For t>0 with E_ext(t)=E0 Θ(t), define γ=1/(2τ), ω_d = sqrt(ω_p^2 - γ^2), and ω_p^2 = 4π n e^2/m (Gaussian).

Displacement:
x(t) = x_p [ 1 - e^{-γ t} ( cos(ω_d t) + (γ/ω_d) sin(ω_d t) ) ],
where x_p = -(eE0)/(m ω_p^2) = -E0/(4π n e).

Current density (normal):
j(t) = (n e^2 E0)/(m ω_d) e^{-γ t} sin(ω_d t).

Copper scales (room T, using given data):
τ ≈ 2.5×10^-14 s  ⇒ 1/τ ≈ 4×10^13 s^-1
ω_p ≈ 1.6×10^16 s^-1 (rad/s)
σ/ε0 ≈ 1.1×10^19 s^-1  (does NOT set the transient timescale).

D. Sanity checks

  • Units: j has units (in SI form) consistent with n e^2 E / (m ω) → A/m² (up to unit-system conventions). The time dependence is dimensionless.
  • Initial behavior: For small t, sin(ω_d t) ≈ ω_d t, so j(t) ≈ (n e^2 E0/m) t (linear ramp) — electrons initially accelerate almost freely.
  • Long-time limit: e^{-γ t}→0 so j(t)→0 and x(t)→x_p. That means charges have rearranged to screen the interior (E_total→0), so no steady conduction current flows in the normal direction.
  • Damping regime: For copper, ω_p ≫ γ, so ω_d ≈ ω_p and decay envelope is e^{-t/(2τ)}.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the final formula (what controls what)

  • ω_p sets the oscillation timescale: how fast electrons collectively “spring back” when displaced. Higher n → larger ω_p → faster screening oscillations.
  • τ sets damping: the envelope e^{-t/(2τ)} comes from momentum relaxation. Smaller τ (dirtier metal, higher T, thinner film) → faster decay of oscillations.
  • Step forcing sets final displacement: x_p = -E0/(4π n e) is exactly what’s needed to cancel the applied field internally (ideal screening).

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • If n increases: ω_p ∝ √n increases, so oscillations are faster; x_p ∝ 1/n decreases (less displacement needed to build the same screening field).
  • If τ decreases: damping rate γ=1/(2τ) increases → oscillations die sooner; in the overdamped limit γ ≥ ω_p, you lose oscillations entirely (monotone relaxation).
  • If field is not normal: then steady current can persist (tangential fields drive conduction), and σ(0) becomes central for long-time response.
Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Start from Maxwell + continuity in 1D: ∂ρ/∂t + ∂j/∂z = 0, with j=σ(ω)E (Drude) and ∂E/∂z=ρ/ε0. Eliminating ρ yields a dynamical equation for E(t) inside the conductor that contains ω_p and τ. In Laplace space, the step input produces poles at -γ ± iω_d, the same damped-plasma modes found above.

Concept check (self-test)

  • Q: Why does the normal current j(t) go to zero at long times even though the applied field is constant?
    A: Surface charge builds until the internal field is screened (E_total→0), so there is no field to sustain bulk drift in the normal direction.
  • Q: What sets the fastest timescale in the model?
    A: The plasma period ~1/ω_p (collective restoring force), not σ.
  • Q: Where does damping enter mathematically?
    A: Through the linear friction term (1/τ)x˙, giving exponential decay envelope e^{-t/(2τ)}.
  • Q: When would “conductivity-like” scales matter more directly?
    A: In tangential-field conduction (steady transport), or in electromagnetic diffusion/skin-effect problems where geometry and Maxwell coupling make σ appear in field-penetration time constants.
