Quick Summary

  • What this is about: connecting an optical measurement (plasma frequency ω_p and relaxation time τ) to DC electrical conductivity σ, then extracting the effective mass m*.
  • Key physics idea: in the Drude model, the same carrier dynamics govern both optical response and low-frequency transport.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols & SI units)

  • n — carrier (electron) concentration [m−3]
  • e — elementary charge [C]
  • m* — effective mass [kg]
  • τ — momentum relaxation time [s]
  • σ — electrical conductivity [S/m = (Ω·m)−1]
  • ε0 — vacuum permittivity [F/m]
  • ω_p — (angular) plasma frequency [s−1]
  • ε(∞) — high-frequency dielectric constant (background) [dimensionless]

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • τ: average time between momentum-randomizing events (phonons, impurities, defects).
  • ω_p: how strongly the electron fluid “pushes back” against an electric field (collective inertia + density).
  • σ: how much current density J you get for a given electric field E in the linear regime: J = σE.

Mathematical model (Drude transport as an ODE)

Model statement: Treat electrons as a continuum of charge carriers whose average drift velocity v(t) obeys a linear, forced, first-order ODE under an applied electric field E(t). 
Drude equation of motion (average drift):
m* dv/dt + (m*/τ) v = -e E(t)

Current density:
J(t) = -n e v(t)

(Linear constitutive law in frequency domain)
J(ω) = σ(ω) E(ω)

Why each step is valid (math): The ODE is linear in v and E, so Fourier transforms apply. The damping term (m*/τ)v represents a linear relaxation (a standard linear-response approximation).

Key Drude results

Drude AC conductivity:
σ(ω) = (n e^2 τ / m*) * 1/(1 - i ω τ)

DC limit (ω→0):
σ0 = n e^2 τ / m*

Plasma frequency (ε∞ = 1):
ωp^2 = n e^2 /(ε0 m*)
Math Insight: what kind of equation is Drude transport?

The drift ODE m* dv/dt + (m*/τ)v = -eE(t) is a linear time-invariant (LTI) system. Mathematically it is a forced, damped first-order system (like an RC circuit, or a low-pass filter): its Green’s function is an exponential kernel ~ exp(-t/τ), and Fourier transform gives the rational response 1/(1 - iωτ). The DC conductivity is simply the zero-frequency gain.

Validity conditions (assumptions & limitations)

  • Linear response: fields small enough that J ∝ E and τ is field-independent.
  • Single relaxation time: scattering summarized by one effective τ (real materials may have multiple channels).
  • Local response: ignore spatial dispersion (no k-dependence), appropriate for long wavelengths.
  • Background dielectric: setting ε(∞)=1 ignores bound-charge polarization; acceptable if specified.

Mini intuition examples (conceptual)

  • If τ doubles (cleaner material), then σ0 doubles: carriers keep momentum longer.
  • If ω_p increases (more/lighter carriers), then σ0 ∝ ω_p^2 rises strongly.
  • At high frequency ωτ ≫ 1, carriers cannot follow the field; conductivity becomes mostly imaginary (inertial response).
What to watch for (pitfalls):
  • Using ω_p vs f_p: plasma frequency here is angular (rad/s), not Hz.
  • Unit conversions: cm⁻³ → m⁻³, and S/m → (Ω·cm)⁻¹.
  • Background dielectric: if ε(∞) ≠ 1, plasma frequency relation becomes ω_p^2 = n e^2 /(ε0 ε(∞) m*) (depending on convention); here the problem explicitly says take ε(∞)=1.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears (real applications)

  • Organic electronics: conductive polymers and molecular crystals in flexible circuits and electrodes.
  • Transparent conductors & coatings: balancing optical properties (reflectivity/plasma edge) with sheet resistance.
  • EMI/ESD materials: static dissipation and shielding films where conductivity targets are specified.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: conductivity controls losses, RC time constants, and power dissipation.
  • Reliability: scattering (hence τ) is sensitive to defects, humidity, aging, and processing.
  • Cost & manufacturability: optical measurement can be faster/non-contact compared with four-probe transport in production.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Optical reflectivity/ellipsometry: fit Drude parameters ω_p and τ (or scattering rate 1/τ).
  • Four-probe conductivity: direct DC transport measurement to validate σ0.
  • Hall effect: separate n and mobility μ = eτ/m* (when feasible).

Design trade-offs and non-idealities

  • Anisotropy: organic crystals can have tensor conductivity σ_ij; optical fits may probe specific directions.
  • Multiple bands / localization: Drude is a simplification; hopping transport may dominate at low T or high disorder.
  • Interface/contact effects: DC measurements can be contact-limited; optics can bypass contacts.
From lab to product: In real production, engineers often use Drude fits as a parameterized surrogate model: (ω_p, τ) become quality-control metrics. Acceptance windows are set so the derived σ0 (and sheet resistance) meets system-level targets under realistic variability (thickness tolerances, humidity, thermal cycling).

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restated

An organic conductor has optical Drude parameters: plasma frequency ω_p = 1.80×10^15 s⁻¹ and electron relaxation time τ = 2.83×10⁻15 s. (a) Use these to compute the DC electrical conductivity σ0 assuming ε(∞)=1, and express it in (Ω·cm)⁻¹. (b) Given the conduction electron concentration n = 4.7×10^21 cm⁻3, infer the effective mass m*.

Given

  • ω_p = 1.80×10^15 s⁻1
  • τ = 2.83×10⁻15 s
  • ε(∞) = 1
  • n = 4.7×10^21 cm⁻3 (for part b)
  • Constants: ε0, e, m_e (for comparison)

Unknowns

  • (a) σ0 (DC conductivity)
  • (b) m* (effective mass)

Relevant principles & why they apply

  • Drude transport: optical studies reported ω_p and τ, which are Drude parameters, so using Drude relations is the intended model.
  • Linear response: conductivity is defined via J = σE in the small-field regime.
  • Plasma frequency relation: links ω_p to n and m*, allowing extraction of m*.
Key modeling assumptions:
  • Single-carrier Drude response, isotropic scalar σ.
  • Spatially uniform medium (no boundary-value complications needed for DC).
  • ε(∞)=1 (background polarization neglected per instruction).

Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

  • Approach A (best): Use σ0 = ε0 ω_p^2 τ by eliminating n and m*. Pros: simplest, mass not needed. Cons: relies on correct plasma-frequency convention.
  • Approach B: Compute mobility μ = eτ/m* then σ0 = neμ. Pros: physically transparent. Cons: needs m* or n first.
  • Approach C: Start from dielectric function ε(ω) and take ω→0 limit with Maxwell relations. Pros: connects optics to EM rigor. Cons: longer; unnecessary for this task.

Chosen approach: Approach A for part (a), then plasma-frequency formula for part (b). It is minimal, rigorous, and uses exactly the provided optical parameters.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Goal: connect optical parameters to DC transport.
    Tool: Drude model relations.
  2. Goal: write DC conductivity formula.
    Equation: σ0 = ne^2τ/m*.
  3. Goal: write plasma frequency in terms of n and m* (with ε(∞)=1).
    Equation: ω_p^2 = ne^2/(ε0 m*).
  4. Goal: eliminate n and m* to get σ0 purely from (ω_p, τ).
    Result: σ0 = ε0 ω_p^2 τ.
  5. Goal: compute σ0 numerically and convert units to (Ω·cm)⁻¹.
  6. Goal: use given n to solve for m* from plasma-frequency relation.
  7. Goal: sanity-check units, scaling, and plausibility vs m_e.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting that 1 S/m = 0.01 S/cm = 0.01 (Ω·cm)⁻¹.
  • Using n in cm⁻3 inside SI formulas (must convert to m⁻3).
  • Dropping the square on ω_p.
  • Confusing ε0 with ε(∞) (here ε(∞)=1 multiplies ε0 if included).

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition before calculating

  • Short τ means frequent scattering, reducing σ0.
  • Large ω_p usually indicates high carrier density and/or small effective mass, increasing σ0.
  • Since σ0 ∝ ω_p^2 τ, even modest changes in ω_p strongly affect conductivity (quadratic dependence).

(a) Conductivity from ω_p and τ

Start from the Drude DC conductivity: σ0 = n e^2 τ / m*. The problem says the carrier mass is unknown and not needed, so we eliminate it using the plasma frequency.

With ε(∞)=1, the (angular) plasma frequency is ω_p^2 = n e^2 / (ε0 m*).

Why elimination is valid (math): Both equations are linear-response identities in the same Drude model and share the factor n e^2 / m*. Since the model is consistent, substitution preserves correctness.

Solve the plasma relation for n e^2 / m*: 

From ωp^2 = (n e^2)/(ε0 m*),
(n e^2)/m* = ε0 ωp^2.

Substitute into σ0 = (n e^2 τ)/m*:
σ0 = (ε0 ωp^2) τ = ε0 ωp^2 τ.

Numerical evaluation

Define constants and plug in values:

  • ε0 = 8.854×10⁻12 F/m
  • ω_p = 1.80×10^15 s⁻1 ⇒ ω_p^2 = (1.80×10^15)² = 3.24×10^30 s⁻2
  • τ = 2.83×10⁻15 s

Compute step-by-step (no jumps):

ωp^2 = (1.80×10^15 s^-1)^2 = 3.24×10^30 s^-2

ε0 ωp^2 = (8.854×10^-12 F/m)(3.24×10^30 s^-2)
        = 2.869×10^19 (F·s^-2)/m

σ0 = ε0 ωp^2 τ
   = (2.869×10^19)(2.83×10^-15)
   = 8.12×10^4 S/m (since F/s = S).
Unit/dimension check (sanity):
  • [ε0] = F/m, [ω_p^2] = s⁻2, [τ]=s ⇒ [ε0 ω_p^2 τ] = (F/m)(1/s) = (S·s/m)(1/s) = S/m. Correct.

Convert to (Ω·cm)⁻¹

Because 1 S/m = 0.01 S/cm and S/cm is the same as (Ω·cm)⁻¹, we have: 

σ0 = 8.12×10^4 S/m
   = 8.12×10^4 × 0.01 S/cm
   = 8.12×10^2 S/cm
   = 8.12×10^2 (Ω·cm)^-1.

(b) Effective mass from n and ω_p

Now use the plasma frequency relation (with ε(∞)=1) and solve for m*: 

ωp^2 = n e^2 /(ε0 m*)  ⇒  m* = n e^2 /(ε0 ωp^2).

Convert carrier concentration to SI:

  • n = 4.7×10^21 cm⁻3 = 4.7×10^21 × 10^6 m⁻3 = 4.7×10^27 m⁻3

Insert numbers carefully:

  • e = 1.602×10⁻19 C ⇒ e² = 2.566×10⁻38 C²
  • ε0 ω_p² = (8.854×10⁻12)(3.24×10^30) = 2.869×10^19 (same as above)
n e^2 = (4.7×10^27)(2.566×10^-38) = 1.206×10^-10

m* = (n e^2)/(ε0 ωp^2)
   = (1.206×10^-10)/(2.869×10^19)
   = 4.20×10^-30 kg.

Relative to electron mass me = 9.109×10^-31 kg:
m*/me = (4.20×10^-30)/(9.109×10^-31) = 4.61.
Physical interpretation: The effective mass is a few times the free electron mass. In organic conductors, this can reflect band-structure effects and/or that the Drude “effective” mass can fold in interaction/renormalization and multi-band physics into one parameter.
Limiting-case check:
  • If τ → 0, then σ0 = ε0 ωp^2 τ → 0 (strong scattering kills DC conduction).
  • If ωp → 0, then σ0 → 0 (no mobile carriers / very heavy carriers).

Final boxed results

(a) σ0 = ε0 ωp^2 τ
    = 8.12×10^4 S/m
    = 8.12×10^2 (Ω·cm)^-1

(b) m* = n e^2 /(ε0 ωp^2)
    = 4.20×10^-30 kg
    = 4.61 me

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the final formulas

  • σ0 = ε0 ωp^2 τ: conductivity is the product of (i) an “optical strength” ε0 ωp^2 and (ii) a memory time τ.
  • ωp^2 ∝ n/m*: the plasma frequency measures how much “charge per inertia” the carrier fluid has.
  • μ = eτ/m* (mobility): combining gives σ0 = neμ, so you can translate between optical fits and mobility targets.

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Increase τ (cleaner crystal, fewer defects) → σ0 increases linearly.
  • Increase n (doping, gating) → ωp increases as √n and thus σ0 increases linearly with n if τ and m* stay fixed.
  • Increase m* (flatter bands) → ωp decreases as 1/√m* and σ0 decreases as 1/m* (again if τ fixed).
Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Start from Maxwell–Ampère in matter: ∇×H = J_free + ∂D/∂t, with D = ε0 E + P. In frequency domain, one can fold the Drude current into an effective complex permittivity: ε(ω) = ε(∞) + i σ(ω)/(ε0 ω). Matching the pole/zero structure of ε(ω) yields the same identities for σ(ω) and ωp. This is the optics-first route often used in ellipsometry fitting pipelines.

Math Insight (core equation as an object): The relation σ0 = ε0 ωp^2 τ is a parameter-elimination identity arising because both σ0 and ωp depend on the shared combination n/m*. Mathematically, it is a consequence of the Drude ODE being an LTI system where the DC gain and the inertial pole strength share the same coefficients.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: If τ is halved but ωp stays the same, what happens to σ0?
    A: It halves, since σ0 ∝ τ.
  • Q: If n quadruples (and m*, τ fixed), how does ωp change?
    A: ωp → 2 ωp, because ωp ∝ √n.
  • Q: Why didn’t we need m* in part (a)?
    A: Because ωp already contains n/m*, allowing elimination to get σ0 directly.
  • Q: What physical process does 1/τ represent?
    A: The momentum scattering rate (how quickly drift momentum relaxes).
