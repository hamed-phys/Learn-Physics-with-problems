Quick Summary

  • What this is: Identify which crystallographic directions contain the densest “string” of atoms (closest packing) in FCC and BCC.
  • Key physics idea: In a hard-sphere picture, “closest-packed” lines are the lines along which nearest-neighbor atoms touch (minimum center-to-center distance).
  • Mathematical model: A periodic point set in ℝ3 defined by a cubic Bravais lattice (parameter a [m]) plus a basis (FCC or BCC).
  • Governing object: A distance minimization on a discrete set: find shortest nonzero lattice/basis displacement vectors Δr between atoms.
  • Core computation: Compare distances along candidate directions: cube edge ⟨100⟩, face diagonal ⟨110⟩, body diagonal ⟨111⟩.
  • FCC result: The shortest interatomic vectors lie along face diagonals → direction family ⟨110⟩.
  • BCC result: The shortest interatomic vectors lie along body diagonals → direction family ⟨111⟩.
  • Physical meaning: These lines are where atoms are most “crowded,” affecting slip, diffusion, and mechanical anisotropy.
Key takeaway: Closest-packed directions are exactly the directions of nearest-neighbor vectors: FCC → ⟨110⟩, BCC → ⟨111⟩.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • a — cubic lattice parameter (unit cell edge), [m]
  • r — position vector in Euclidean space ℝ3, [m]
  • Δr — interatomic displacement vector, [m]
  • |Δr| — center-to-center distance, [m]
  • [uvw] — a crystallographic direction (integer triplet)
  • ⟨uvw⟩ — family of symmetry-equivalent directions
  • Bravais lattice: set {R = n1a1 + n2a2 + n3a3 | ni ∈ ℤ}
  • Basis: finite set of points within a cell; the crystal is {R + bj}
  • Nearest neighbors: atoms at minimal nonzero distance from a given atom
  • Closest-packed line: line direction along which atoms appear at the smallest repeat spacing (and, in hard-sphere view, touch)

0.2 Physical meaning & intuition

Imagine the atoms as equal hard spheres arranged periodically. “Closest packing along a line” means: if you walk along some straight direction through the crystal, you meet atoms most frequently and at the smallest center-to-center spacing along that direction. In cubic lattices, the natural candidates are: along an edge, a face diagonal, or a body diagonal.

0.3 Key principles and validity conditions

Hard-sphere + lattice geometry principle:
  • Atomic centers form a periodic discrete set in ℝ3.
  • Closest-packed directions align with the shortest nonzero displacement vectors between atoms (nearest neighbors).
  • This is a geometric statement; it does not require forces/energies, only the point set and distances.
Assumes ideal crystal Ignores defects/thermal expansion Geometry-only argument

0.4 Common models/approximations and why

  • Ideal periodic lattice: Real crystals have defects, but the direction families are robust to small disorder.
  • Hard-sphere contact picture: Useful for “packing” language; mathematically it translates to “nearest-neighbor distance is minimal.”
  • Cubic symmetry: Allows us to compare only a few representative directions; symmetry generates the full ⟨uvw⟩ families.

0.5 Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • FCC: There are atoms at face centers. So along a face diagonal you pass through a corner atom → face-center atom → next corner atom: very tight spacing.
  • BCC: There is an atom at the body center. Along a body diagonal you go corner atom → body-center atom → opposite corner: very tight spacing.

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Directions vs planes: ⟨uvw⟩ are directions; {hkl} are planes. Closest-packed lines are directions.
  • Don’t confuse “densest plane” with “densest line”: FCC has densest planes {111}, but densest lines are ⟨110⟩.
  • Need the basis: FCC and BCC are not “simple cubic”; their extra basis atoms change which direction hits nearest neighbors.
  • Use nearest-neighbor vectors: The shortest Δr is the rigorous criterion for “closest packing along a line.”
Math Insight (core equation as a mathematical object):

This problem is a discrete optimization on a periodic point set: find argmin_{Δr ≠ 0} |Δr|. The minimizing displacement vectors define a symmetry family of directions (a “shell” in the lattice’s pair-correlation geometry).

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Why engineers care (real-world impact)

  • Plastic deformation & forming: Slip tends to occur along close-packed directions within favorable planes; this controls yield strength, anisotropy, and formability (sheet metal stamping, rolling).
  • Diffusion and fast pathways: Migration barriers and short hop distances often correlate with nearest-neighbor geometry; close-packed directions can influence directional diffusion in crystals and thin films.
  • Fracture, fatigue, and texture: Grain orientation (texture) affects which close-packed directions align with load, impacting crack initiation and fatigue life.

Concrete application areas (at least 3)

1) Manufacturing & metallurgical processing
  • Rolling/extrusion: texture evolution in FCC metals (Al, Cu, Ni).
  • Heat treatment: recrystallization and grain growth depend on crystallography.
2) Mechanical design of structural alloys
  • BCC steels: temperature-dependent slip systems; close-packed directions matter for ductile-to-brittle transition behavior.
  • FCC superalloys: creep resistance tied to crystallographic slip behavior.
3) Microelectronics & thin films
  • Texture in Cu interconnects: electromigration and stress voiding influenced by atomic pathways.
  • Deposition processes: preferred orientation affects mechanical and electrical properties.
4) Quality control & materials characterization
  • EBSD (electron backscatter diffraction) maps orientations to infer active slip directions.
  • XRD texture measurements relate to close-packed features.

Measurement/validation methods (brief)

  • X-ray diffraction (XRD): reveals lattice type and orientation; confirms FCC/BCC and texture.
  • EBSD: maps grain orientations and correlates deformation with crystallographic directions.
  • Transmission electron microscopy (TEM): observes dislocations; their Burgers vectors often align with close-packed directions.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Non-idealities: impurities, vacancies, and dislocations alter local packing but not the underlying direction families.
  • Temperature: thermal expansion changes a but not which direction minimizes distance (the geometry scales).
  • Manufacturability: controlling texture can improve deep drawability (FCC) or strength (BCC steels) but may introduce anisotropy.
From lab to product: adapting the ideal model

In real components, grains are finite and misoriented. Engineers use the ideal FCC/BCC geometry as a “local rule” within each grain, then average over grain orientations (polycrystal models: Taylor model, crystal plasticity FEM). The closest-packed directions enter via slip system definitions, which in turn drive stress–strain predictions used in forming simulations.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

1.1 Restate the problem

We must prove—using crystal geometry—that:

  • In an FCC crystal, the lines (directions) of closest atomic packing are the family ⟨110⟩.
  • In a BCC crystal, the lines (directions) of closest atomic packing are the family ⟨111⟩.

1.2 Given quantities

  • Cubic lattice parameter a (unit cell edge length).
  • Lattice type: FCC or BCC (i.e., same cubic Bravais lattice symmetry but different bases).

1.3 Unknowns / what must be found

  • Which crystallographic directions contain the nearest-neighbor “touching” chain of atoms (closest-packed line).
  • Equivalently: determine the direction(s) parallel to the shortest nonzero interatomic displacement vectors Δr.

1.4 Relevant principles and why they apply

Principle: In a periodic lattice, closest-packed directions are defined by nearest-neighbor bonds.
  • We compare candidate distances along high-symmetry directions (edge, face diagonal, body diagonal).
  • Because cubic symmetry is high, checking these suffices: any other direction is symmetry-related or longer for the nearest-neighbor shell.

Other physics (forces, quantum bonding, elasticity PDEs) is not required here because the question is purely geometric: it asks about packing, i.e., point spacing.

1.5 Assumptions (explicit)

  • Ideal infinite crystal (periodic boundary conditions implicitly).
  • Atoms treated as points (or identical hard spheres for intuition).
  • We identify “closest packing” with smallest center-to-center spacing between consecutive atoms on a straight line.

1.6 Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

Approach A: Nearest-neighbor vector enumeration (most rigorous)
  • Write lattice + basis coordinates.
  • Compute all shortest Δr vectors.
  • Read off their direction indices.
Pro: exact Con: a bit algebraic
Approach B: Compare distances along ⟨100⟩, ⟨110⟩, ⟨111⟩
  • Use unit-cell geometry to see which direction hits an intermediate basis atom.
  • Compute spacing between successive atoms on that line.
Pro: intuitive Con: must justify completeness
Approach C: Reciprocal-space / structure factor reasoning
  • Use selection rules and real-space neighbor shells.
  • Infer shortest bonds from allowed reflections.
Pro: connects to XRD Con: overkill here
Chosen method

We’ll use A + B: explicitly compute nearest-neighbor vectors (rigorous), while interpreting them as edge/face/body diagonal chains (intuitive).

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Model the crystal: represent atomic positions as a periodic set in ℝ3 using a cubic cell of edge a.
  2. Choose a reference atom at the origin; all neighbor vectors are displacements Δr to other atomic sites.
  3. FCC basis: list basis points; compute candidate Δr to nearby atoms; find the minimal nonzero |Δr|.
  4. Identify direction indices of the minimizing Δr vectors (normalize to integer triplets [u v w]).
  5. Repeat for BCC: compute its nearest-neighbor Δr, minimize |Δr|, identify direction indices.
  6. Interpret physically: show the minimizing Δr are along face diagonals (FCC) and body diagonals (BCC), hence ⟨110⟩ and ⟨111⟩.
  7. Sanity checks: dimensional check (distances scale with a), symmetry check (families ⟨uvw⟩), and neighbor-count consistency (FCC has 12 NN; BCC has 8 NN).
Common mistakes (quick tips)
  • Mixing up {hkl} planes with ⟨uvw⟩ directions.
  • Using simple-cubic neighbor distances (wrong basis!) for FCC/BCC.
  • Claiming “densest plane” implies “densest line” (not the same statement).
  • Not checking that the candidate Δr is truly minimal among all nearby basis-lattice combinations.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Qualitative expectation before calculating

In FCC, atoms sit at face centers, so directions crossing a face center (face diagonals) should be crowded. In BCC, an atom sits at the body center, so directions crossing the body center (body diagonals) should be crowded. We now prove this rigorously by finding the nearest-neighbor displacement vectors.

3.2 Mathematical model (variables, domain, coordinate system)

Let ℝ3 be equipped with Cartesian coordinates (x,y,z). A cubic conventional cell has side length a. Define the simple cubic translation lattice: 

Bravais translations:  R = a (n1, n2, n3),  with n1,n2,n3 ∈ ℤ.

A crystal structure is the periodic point set: S = { R + b_j } where b_j are basis vectors inside the cell. The interatomic displacement set from a chosen origin site is: 

Δr = (R + b_j) - (0 + b_0)  for all R ∈ lattice, all basis sites b_j.
Nearest neighbors minimize |Δr| over all Δr ≠ 0.
Why this step is valid (mathematical justification): The structure is periodic (translation invariance), so choosing any atom as the origin loses no generality. Nearest-neighbor directions are translation-invariant and therefore define global “closest-packed” directions.

3.3 FCC: compute the shortest nonzero interatomic vectors

For FCC (face-centered cubic), one convenient basis (in units of a) is: 

FCC basis (conventional cubic cell):
b0 = (0, 0, 0)
b1 = (0, 1/2, 1/2)
b2 = (1/2, 0, 1/2)
b3 = (1/2, 1/2, 0)
(All multiplied by a to get meters.)

Choose the atom at b0 as the reference (origin). Candidate nearest neighbors must lie in the same or adjacent cells (because farther cells give larger |Δr|). Consider displacements to the other basis atoms within the same cell first: 

Δr01 = a(0, 1/2, 1/2)   ⇒ |Δr01| = a √[(0)^2 + (1/2)^2 + (1/2)^2] = a √(1/2) = a/√2
Δr02 = a(1/2, 0, 1/2)   ⇒ |Δr02| = a/√2
Δr03 = a(1/2, 1/2, 0)   ⇒ |Δr03| = a/√2

So we have found interatomic separations a/√2. We must still verify no shorter separation exists. The next obvious candidates are simple-cubic corner-to-corner along an edge: 

Edge displacement to a translated corner: Δr_edge = a(1,0,0) (or permutations)
⇒ |Δr_edge| = a  >  a/√2

What about a body diagonal to the opposite corner? That displacement is: 

Body diagonal corner-to-corner: Δr_body = a(1,1,1)
⇒ |Δr_body| = a√3  >  a/√2

Any displacement involving translations by integers (ni) will include at least one component of magnitude ≥ a/2 (often ≥ a), and the shortest ones are precisely those we already found: vectors of type a(0, ±1/2, ±1/2) and permutations.

FCC nearest-neighbor vectors (full symmetry family):

All nearest neighbors from a corner site are obtained by taking Δr = a(0, ±1/2, ±1/2), a(±1/2, 0, ±1/2), a(±1/2, ±1/2, 0). There are 12 such vectors (matching the known FCC coordination number 12).

Direction indices: why this implies ⟨110⟩

The direction of Δr = a(0, 1/2, 1/2) is the same as the direction of the integer vector (0,1,1), because scaling a direction vector by any nonzero constant does not change its direction: 

If Δr = c·v with c ≠ 0, then Δr ∥ v (same direction).
So a(0, 1/2, 1/2) ∥ (0,1,1).

Therefore the nearest-neighbor directions in FCC are of type [0 1 1] and permutations/sign changes, i.e. the family ⟨110⟩ (because [0 1 1] is a representative of ⟨110⟩).

FCC conclusion: Closest-packed lines are along face diagonals ⇒ ⟨110⟩.

3.4 BCC: compute the shortest nonzero interatomic vectors

For BCC (body-centered cubic), use basis (in units of a): 

BCC basis (conventional cubic cell):
b0 = (0, 0, 0)
b1 = (1/2, 1/2, 1/2)
(All multiplied by a to get meters.)

Choose the corner atom at b0 as the origin. The displacement to the body-center atom in the same cell is: 

Δr01 = a(1/2, 1/2, 1/2)
⇒ |Δr01| = a √[(1/2)^2 + (1/2)^2 + (1/2)^2]
        = a √(3/4)
        = (a√3)/2

Compare with the edge distance to a neighboring corner (translation by a(1,0,0)): 

Corner-to-corner along an edge: |Δr_edge| = a
Since (a√3)/2 ≈ 0.866a < a, the body-center is closer than any edge neighbor.

Next, check that nothing is shorter: the only way to get components smaller than 1/2 in magnitude would be to use the same half/half/half pattern, since translations are integers. Thus the nearest-neighbor vectors are those to body centers of the eight cubes that share the corner. They have signs ±(1/2,1/2,1/2), giving 8 nearest neighbors (matching BCC coordination number 8).

BCC nearest-neighbor vectors:

Δr = a(±1/2, ±1/2, ±1/2), all sign combinations. Each has magnitude (a√3)/2.

Direction indices: why this implies ⟨111⟩

The direction of a(1/2,1/2,1/2) is parallel to (1,1,1). Therefore the nearest-neighbor directions in BCC are of type [1 1 1] with sign changes, i.e. the family ⟨111⟩.

BCC conclusion: Closest-packed lines are along body diagonals ⇒ ⟨111⟩.

3.5 Boxed final result

FINAL RESULT (closest-packed directions = nearest-neighbor directions)

FCC:
Nearest-neighbor vectors: a(0, ±1/2, ±1/2) and permutations
⇒ directions ∥ (0,1,1) etc.
⇒ closest-packed direction family: ⟨110⟩ (face diagonals)

BCC:
Nearest-neighbor vectors: a(±1/2, ±1/2, ±1/2)
⇒ directions ∥ (1,1,1) etc.
⇒ closest-packed direction family: ⟨111⟩ (body diagonals)

3.6 Sanity checks

Dimensional check

All distances are multiples of a: FCC gives a/√2, BCC gives (a√3)/2. Units remain [m].

Limiting/scaling check

If the lattice expands (a → λa), all distances scale linearly, but the direction families ⟨110⟩ and ⟨111⟩ are unchanged.

Symmetry check

Cubic symmetry produces equivalent directions by permutations and sign changes, matching the ⟨uvw⟩ notation.

Neighbor-count check

FCC has 12 nearest neighbors (matches 12 vectors of the ⟨110⟩ type). BCC has 8 nearest neighbors (matches 8 vectors of the ⟨111⟩ type).

Operator notation note (why ∇, Laplacian etc. are not central here)

This is a static, discrete geometry problem: no fields, waves, or PDEs are required. In mathematical-physics terms, we are working with a point set in ℝ3 and the Euclidean norm |·|. If one wanted a “continuous” analogue, one could view the crystal as a periodic density ρ(r) = Σsites δ(r − ri) and study correlation functions, but the closest-packed directions still come from the shortest Δr in the support of ρ.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpretation: what controls the result?

The result is controlled by where the basis atoms sit inside the cubic cell:

  • FCC: adding atoms at face centers creates shorter bonds along face diagonals (⟨110⟩).
  • BCC: adding an atom at the body center creates shorter bonds along body diagonals (⟨111⟩).

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Changing a scales all distances equally; it does not change which directions are closest-packed.
  • Changing structure (FCC ↔ BCC) changes the basis and thus changes which displacement vectors are shortest.

4.3 Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Alternative viewpoint: “which straight lines pass through the most lattice sites?”

A direction is close-packed if a line parallel to it intersects lattice sites at the smallest repeat spacing. In FCC, a face diagonal line hits corner–face-center–corner repeatedly, giving spacing a/√2. In BCC, a body diagonal line hits corner–body-center–corner, giving spacing (a√3)/2. Since these are the minimal repeat spacings, they define the close-packed direction families.

4.4 Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why isn’t ⟨111⟩ the closest-packed direction in FCC?
    A: Along a body diagonal in FCC there is no atom at the cube center, so you jump corner-to-corner with length a√3, which is larger than a/√2 along ⟨110⟩.
  • Q: Why isn’t ⟨100⟩ closest-packed in BCC?
    A: Along an edge you go corner-to-corner with distance a, but along ⟨111⟩ you reach a body-center at distance (a√3)/2 < a.
  • Q: Does thermal expansion change ⟨110⟩ vs ⟨111⟩?
    A: No—expansion rescales distances but preserves the ordering of nearest-neighbor shells in an ideal lattice.
  • Q: How does this connect to deformation?
    A: Dislocation Burgers vectors often align with close-packed directions; thus FCC slip commonly involves ⟨110⟩ directions, while BCC commonly involves ⟨111⟩ directions (within the relevant slip planes).
