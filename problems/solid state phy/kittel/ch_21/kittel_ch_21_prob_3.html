Force on a Dislocation from Energy Balance

Quick Summary

  • We study the mechanical force acting on an edge dislocation under applied shear stress.
  • Key idea: Use energy balance — work done by external stress equals mechanical work on the dislocation.
  • Governing principle: Elastic strain energy density u = ½σijεij.
  • Geometry: Cube of side L, Burgers vector b.
  • Result: Force per unit length = F = bσ.
  • Physical meaning: Shear stress drives dislocation glide linearly.

PART 0 — Concept Primer

Core Definitions

  • Shear stress σ (Pa)
  • Burgers vector b (m)
  • Dislocation line (m)
  • Strain energy density u = ½σijεij (J/m³)

Physical Meaning

An edge dislocation represents a line defect producing lattice mismatch equal to the Burgers vector.

Key Assumptions:
  • Linear elasticity
  • Uniform applied shear stress
  • Quasi-static motion
  • Small strain approximation

Mathematical Structure

Elastic equilibrium satisfies:

∇·σ = 0

Strain tensor:

εij = ½(∂ui/∂xj + ∂uj/∂xi)

PART 1 — Problem Analysis

Given:

  • Cube side L
  • Shear stress σ
  • Burgers vector b

Find:

  • Force per unit length on dislocation

Relevant principle: Mechanical work equals change in elastic energy.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips

  1. Calculate work done by applied stress
  2. Relate displacement to Burgers vector
  3. Equate energy change to F × displacement
  4. Solve for F
Common Mistakes:
  • Forgetting per-unit-length normalization
  • Confusing total force with force density

PART 3 — Full Solution

Step 1 — Work Done by Shear Stress

Shear strain increment:

γ = Δx / L

Work per volume:

δW/V = σ dγ

Total work:

δW = σ L² b

Step 2 — Relate to Force

If dislocation moves distance b:

δW = F L b
Equate:
F L b = σ L² b ⇒ F = σ b
Final Answer (per unit length):
F = bσ

Sanity Checks

  • Units: (Pa)(m) = N/m ✔
  • Linear in σ ✔
  • Zero stress ⇒ zero force ✔

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding

The result shows a linear driving force proportional to Burgers vector magnitude.

Math Insight:

This is a direct energy-conjugate relationship: stress and Burgers vector act as dual variables.

  • Increasing σ doubles force
  • L cancels — geometry independent

Concept Checks

  • Q: Does sign matter?
    A: Yes — direction of σ relative to b determines glide direction.
  • Q: Why linear?
    A: Because of linear elasticity assumption.

No plot is necessary because the relationship is purely linear and symbolic.
