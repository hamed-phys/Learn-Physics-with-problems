Superlattice lines in Cu₃Au: which new X-ray reflections appear when the alloy orders?

Cu₃Au (75% Cu, 25% Au) undergoes an order–disorder transition (L1₂ ordering). The key physics is that ordering makes previously “forbidden” fcc reflections become weakly allowed, producing superlattice lines.

Quick Summary

  • What this is about: When Cu₃Au orders (Au on cube corners, Cu on face centers), new weak diffraction peaks (“superlattice lines”) appear.
  • Key physics idea: X-ray peak intensities are set by the structure factor, i.e., the Fourier coefficient of the unit-cell electron density.
  • Governing equation: For reciprocal vector G = (2π/a)(h, k, l), the unit-cell structure factor is F(hkl) = Σ_j f_j e^{i G·r_j}.
  • Ordered Cu₃Au (L1₂ basis): Au at (0,0,0); Cu at (1/2,1/2,0), (1/2,0,1/2), (0,1/2,1/2).
  • Main result (selection rule):
    • If h,k,l are all even or all odd → “fundamental” peaks, strong: F = f_Au + 3 f_Cu.
    • If h,k,l have mixed parity → new superlattice peaks: F = f_Au − f_Cu (weak if scattering factors are close).
  • Final list (new reflections, indices ≤ 2): (100), (110), (210), (211), (221) (include all symmetry-equivalent permutations/signs).
Big picture: Ordering changes the basis (chemical identity at lattice sites), not the geometric lattice constant. In reciprocal space this creates additional Fourier components at reflections that were extinct in the disordered fcc average.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (THEORY BEFORE SOLVING)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • Direct lattice constant a (m): cubic unit-cell edge length.
  • Direct position r (m): point in the crystal.
  • Reciprocal vector G (m⁻¹): Bragg reflection vector; for cubic indexing G = (2π/a)(h,k,l).
  • Atomic scattering factor f (dimensionless): X-ray scattering amplitude of an atom.
  • Structure factor F(hkl) (dimensionless): coherent sum over atoms in the unit cell.
  • Intensity I(hkl) ∝ |F(hkl)|² (arb. units): measured peak strength.

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Diffraction = Fourier analysis of electron density.
  • Extinctions happen when phase factors cancel.
  • Ordering makes basis atoms inequivalent (Au vs Cu), so some cancellations break → new weak peaks.
Why the math works: Scattered amplitude is linear in charge density → contributions add (superposition).

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

  • Kinematic diffraction (single-scattering) approximation.
  • Bragg condition: constructive interference when momentum transfer equals a reciprocal lattice vector: Δk = G.
  • Fourier-series view: F(G) = ∫_cell ρ(r) e^{iG·r} dr is a Fourier coefficient.

Common models/approximations

  • Point-atom model: ρ(r) ≈ Σ_j f_j δ(r − r_j) (good for selection rules).
  • Disordered alloy average: replace each site by f̄ = c_Cu f_Cu + c_Au f_Au.
  • Ignore temperature factors for allowed/forbidden decisions.
What to watch for (pitfalls):
  • Confusing fcc “systematic absences” with ordered-basis effects.
  • Forgetting that “≤ 2” includes (210), (221), etc.
  • Not treating (hkl) as a cubic family (permutations/signs).
Math Insight: F(G) is a Fourier coefficient of a periodic function; extinctions are zeros due to symmetry (roots of unity cancellation).

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this shows up in industry

  • Heat treatment & phase control (ordering affects strength and diffusion).
  • Thin films / microelectronics (ordering can change resistivity and stability).
  • High-temperature alloys (order strengthening and lifetime).

Why engineers care

  • Performance: ordering raises yield strength via APB energy barriers.
  • Reliability: ordering changes diffusion and thermal stability.
  • QC: superlattice peaks provide a non-destructive ordering fingerprint.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • XRD: appearance/intensity of superlattice lines tracks degree of order.
  • TEM/electron diffraction: superlattice spots are often very clear.
  • DSC: detects transition energetics but not specific reflections.
From lab to product: Production often monitors an intensity ratio like I_super/I_fund to validate heat-treatment recipes robustly.

Problem rewritten clearly

Determine which X-ray reflections appear only in the ordered Cu₃Au (L1₂) phase, not in the disordered fcc average, and list all new reflections with Miller indices ≤ 2.

Given

  • Ordered basis: Au at (0,0,0); Cu at (1/2,1/2,0), (1/2,0,1/2), (0,1/2,1/2).
  • Disordered state: random occupancy → average scattering factor per site.

Unknowns

  • List of “new” (hkl) reflections ≤ 2.

Approaches

  • Direct structure factor (best here): compute F(hkl) and identify mixed-parity reflections.
  • Symmetry/translation: ordering reduces translation symmetry → extra reflections.
  • Order-parameter Fourier: connects to intensity scaling, more advanced than needed.

PART 3 — Full Solution (DETAILED + TEACHING)

Mathematical physics setup (Fourier coefficient of periodic density)

Model the unit-cell electron density as a sum of point contributions: ρ(r) ≈ Σ_j f_j δ(r − r_j). The scattered amplitude at reciprocal vector G is the Fourier coefficient: F(G)=∫_cell ρ(r)e^{iG·r}dr = Σ_j f_j e^{iG·r_j}. This is valid by linearity of the integral and superposition of scattering amplitudes.

F(hkl) = Σ_j f_j exp(i G·r_j), G = (2π/a)(h,k,l).

Ordered Cu₃Au (L1₂) basis and phase factors

Use fractional coordinates r_j = a(u_j,v_j,w_j), so G·r_j = 2π(hu_j + kv_j + lw_j).

  • Au: (u,v,w)=(0,0,0) ⇒ exp(iG·r₀)=1.
  • Cu: (1/2,1/2,0) ⇒ exp(iG·r₁)=exp(iπ(h+k))=(-1)^(h+k).
  • Cu: (1/2,0,1/2) ⇒ exp(iG·r₂)=(-1)^(h+l).
  • Cu: (0,1/2,1/2) ⇒ exp(iG·r₃)=(-1)^(k+l).

Ordered structure factor

F_ordered(hkl) = f_Au + f_Cu[ (-1)^(h+k) + (-1)^(h+l) + (-1)^(k+l) ].

Selection rule via parity

Each factor (-1)^(h+k) is +1 if (h+k) is even, and −1 if (h+k) is odd. The parity of sums is determined by whether h,k,l are even/odd.

Case 1: h,k,l all even OR all odd (fundamentals)
Then (h+k), (h+l), (k+l) are all even, so the bracket is 3:
F_ordered = f_Au + 3 f_Cu.
Case 2: mixed parity (superlattice reflections)
In any mixed-parity triple, exactly one of (h+k),(h+l),(k+l) is even and two are odd, giving:
(-1)^(even) + (-1)^(odd) + (-1)^(odd) = 1 − 1 − 1 = −1, so F_ordered = f_Au − f_Cu.
Why “exactly one even pair” in mixed parity?

Work mod 2: even=0, odd=1. Mixed parity means the triple isn’t (0,0,0) or (1,1,1), so two entries match and one differs. The sum of the matching pair is 0 mod 2 (even), while sums involving the differing entry are 1 mod 2 (odd).

Why these are “new” compared to disordered fcc

Disordered Cu₃Au is modeled as fcc with identical average scattering f̄ at each site: F_dis(hkl) = f̄[1 + (-1)^(h+k) + (-1)^(h+l) + (-1)^(k+l)]. For mixed parity the bracket equals 0, so those reflections are absent in the disordered state. In the ordered state they have amplitude f_Au − f_Cu, hence they appear as superlattice peaks.

Intensity scaling (useful engineering intuition):
Fundamentals: I_fund ∝ |f_Au + 3 f_Cu|² (strong).
Superlattice: I_sup ∝ |f_Au − f_Cu|² (weak).
Therefore superlattice peaks are excellent order indicators but may require longer counting time / better background control.

Enumerate new reflections with indices ≤ 2

“Indices ≤ 2” means h,k,l ∈ {0,1,2}, excluding (000). New peaks are exactly the mixed-parity sets, grouped into unique cubic families.

New family representative Mixed-parity type Includes by cubic symmetry Ordered amplitude
(100) odd-even-even (100),(010),(001) plus sign changes F = f_Au − f_Cu
(110) odd-odd-even (110),(101),(011) plus sign changes F = f_Au − f_Cu
(210) even-odd-even all permutations of 2,1,0 plus sign changes F = f_Au − f_Cu
(211) even-odd-odd all permutations of 2,1,1 plus sign changes F = f_Au − f_Cu
(221) even-even-odd all permutations of 2,2,1 plus sign changes F = f_Au − f_Cu
FINAL ANSWER (new superlattice reflections, indices ≤ 2): Ordering in Cu₃Au (Au at 000; Cu at 1/2,1/2,0 etc.) makes mixed-parity reflections become allowed. For mixed parity (not all even, not all odd): F(hkl) = f_Au − f_Cu ≠ 0 (superlattice). With h,k,l ∈ {0,1,2} (excluding 000), the new reflection families are: (100), (110), (210), (211), (221) including all symmetry-equivalent permutations and sign changes. All-even/all-odd reflections (e.g., (111), (200), (220), (222)) are fundamental and are not “new”.

Sanity checks

  • Dimensional: sums of scattering factors → intensity ∝ |F|² is consistent.
  • Contrast limit: if f_Au = f_Cu, superlattice peaks vanish (ordering invisible to XRD).
  • Consistency with fcc: mixed parity extinct in disordered fcc average, present in ordered L1₂.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (THEORY AROUND THE RESULT)

Interpretation: what controls what

  • Fundamental peaks measure the “average” cell scattering (large baseline).
  • Superlattice peaks measure the chemical contrast between sublattices (order fingerprint).

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • More long-range order → stronger superlattice intensity (in refined models, scales with order parameter squared).
  • Approaching the disorder temperature → superlattice peaks fade and vanish.
  • Form-factor dependence on angle/wavelength can change relative visibility but not the allowed/forbidden rule.

Alternative derivation idea

Symmetry view: ordering reduces translation symmetry from fcc equivalence to a simple-cubic chemical lattice, so the reciprocal lattice gains additional allowed points. The structure factor is the explicit Fourier-amplitude calculation of that symmetry change.

Concept check (Q → A)
  • Q: Why is (100) a hallmark of ordering? A: It is mixed parity and extinct in disordered fcc, but becomes allowed with amplitude f_Au − f_Cu.
  • Q: Are (200) or (111) new? A: No; they’re all-even or all-odd and already fcc-allowed.
  • Q: Why are superlattice peaks weak? A: Their amplitude is a difference of form factors, usually much smaller than the sum for fundamentals.
