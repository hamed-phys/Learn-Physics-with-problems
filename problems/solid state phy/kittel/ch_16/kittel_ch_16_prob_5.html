Polarization of a Dielectric Sphere in a Uniform External Electric Field (CGS)

We solve the classic electrostatics boundary-value problem: a linear dielectric sphere placed in a uniform applied field E0. The goal is to find the (volume-averaged) internal field and the induced polarization, with a mathematically rigorous derivation using Laplace’s equation and boundary conditions.

Quick Summary

  • What this is: A dielectric sphere (dielectric constant ε) placed in a uniform external field E0.
  • Key physics idea: The applied field polarizes the sphere; bound charges appear on the surface and create a reaction field that changes the internal field.
  • Governing equations: Electrostatics: ∇×E=0, ∇·D=0 (no free charge), with D=εE inside and D=E outside (CGS).
  • Mathematical core: Solve ∇²φ=0 inside/outside with spherical symmetry of the forcing (only the l=1 Legendre mode survives).
  • Main result (symbolic): The electric field inside the sphere is uniform: E_in = (3/(ε+2)) E0.
  • Volume average: Because E is uniform inside, ⟨E⟩ = E_in.
  • Polarization (CGS): P = χ_e E_in with χ_e=(ε−1)/(4π), so P = χ_e E0 / (1 + (4π/3)χ_e).
  • Physical meaning: The sphere’s own depolarization field reduces the internal field; stronger dielectrics (larger ε) polarize more but also self-screen more.
Final results (copy-ready)
E_in = ⟨E⟩ = (3/(ε+2)) E0  = E0 / (1 + (4π/3) χ_e)

P = χ_e E_in = χ_e E0 / (1 + (4π/3) χ_e)

χ_e = (ε − 1)/(4π)   (CGS)

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (CGS/Gaussian)

  • Electric field E (statvolt/cm): force per unit charge.
  • Electric potential φ: E = −∇φ (valid because ∇×E=0 in electrostatics).
  • Displacement field D: in CGS, D = E + 4πP.
  • Polarization P (dipole moment per unit volume): linear isotropic dielectric model P = χ_e E.
  • Dielectric constant ε (relative permittivity in this CGS convention): D = εE. Therefore, ε = 1 + 4πχ_e.

Key laws and validity conditions

Electrostatics:
∇×E = 0  ⇒  E = −∇φ
Gauss (free charge):
∇·D = 4π ρ_free

Constitutive relations (linear, isotropic):
Inside sphere:  D_in = ε E_in
Outside (vacuum/air): D_out = E_out
  • Steady-state: no time variation; no induction effects.
  • Linear dielectric: P ∝ E; valid for moderate fields (below strong nonlinear polarization regimes).
  • Homogeneous & isotropic: ε is constant scalar inside the sphere.
  • No free charge at interface: only bound (polarization) charge appears on the surface.

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • The applied uniform field E0 tries to align molecular dipoles → induces polarization P.
  • Polarization produces bound surface charge density σ_b = P·n̂ (in CGS), which creates a field opposing the applied field inside (a depolarizing field).
  • For a sphere, symmetry forces the internal field to be uniform (a powerful simplification).

Common models/approximations and why

  • Continuum polarization: Replace discrete dipoles by smooth P(x) to use PDEs (Laplace/Poisson).
  • Linear constitutive law: P=χ_e E reduces material response to a single parameter.
  • Boundary-value formulation: Use Laplace’s equation because there is no volume charge in each region (only surface effects).

Mini intuition examples (no heavy algebra)

  • If ε = 1 (sphere is just vacuum): nothing changes, so E_in = E0, P=0.
  • If ε → ∞ (perfect conductor-like polarization response): the sphere almost cancels internal field → E_in → 0.
  • Shape matters: a needle-like object has a different depolarizing factor than a sphere; spheres are “mild” depolarizers.
What to watch for (pitfalls)
  • Do not assume the external field changes when inserting the sphere if the source is fixed charges far away (as the hint states).
  • Keep CGS relations straight: D=E+4πP and ε=1+4πχ_e.
  • At the boundary, use the correct conditions: tangential E continuous; normal D continuous when no free surface charge.
  • “Average field” inside equals the pointwise field only because the solution is uniform inside a sphere; this is not true for arbitrary shapes.
Math Insight

This is a harmonic boundary-value problem: in each region the potential satisfies ∇²φ=0. The uniform field at infinity corresponds to the l=1 spherical harmonic (a dipolar mode). Linearity + symmetry imply the solution is a superposition of that mode and a decaying dipole term. Determining coefficients is a clean example of matching solutions across an interface using boundary conditions—an archetype of PDE-based modeling in engineering physics.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (concrete examples)

  • Composites & polymer fillers: dielectric particles dispersed in polymers (cables, encapsulants, insulating coatings). The local field inside fillers affects breakdown and loss.
  • Capacitor dielectrics: ceramic inclusions and engineered microstructures; local depolarization fields influence effective permittivity and reliability.
  • Sensors & actuators: electrostatic MEMS and dielectric elastomers; internal field determines polarization stress and coupling.
  • High-voltage insulation: voids/particles in insulating oils/solids; field enhancement or reduction drives partial discharge inception.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: effective permittivity and energy storage depend on how inclusions polarize.
  • Reliability & safety: local field hot-spots accelerate dielectric breakdown and aging (electrical treeing, partial discharge).
  • Manufacturability & cost: choosing particle material (ε), size, and loading fraction trades cost vs. dielectric performance.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Dielectric spectroscopy (frequency-dependent permittivity and loss) to infer polarization response and interfacial effects.
  • Electrostatic probing / E-field mapping in controlled geometries; comparison with finite-element electrostatics simulations.
  • Breakdown tests (AC/DC) to correlate microstructure-driven local fields with failure probability.

Design trade-offs & constraints (non-idealities)

  • Real particles are not perfectly spherical; shape changes depolarization factors and thus internal fields.
  • Interfaces may carry free charge (contamination, ionic conduction), altering boundary conditions.
  • At high fields, nonlinear polarization and temperature dependence can invalidate the linear model.
  • At high frequencies, displacement currents and losses matter; full Maxwell equations replace pure Laplace modeling.
From lab to product: adapting the ideal model

In product design, the “single sphere in uniform field” result is often used as a building block: (i) compute local fields in inclusions, (ii) feed those into homogenization models (Maxwell-Garnett, Bruggeman), and (iii) validate against finite-element simulations of realistic particle packs. Engineers also introduce safety factors for tolerances (particle size distribution, clustering) that can amplify local fields beyond the ideal prediction.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement (in plain words)

A sphere of radius a made of a linear dielectric with dielectric constant ε is placed in an otherwise uniform, externally applied electric field E0 (produced by far-away charges/plates so it remains fixed). (a) Find the volume-averaged electric field inside the sphere. (b) Show the polarization inside the sphere is P = χ_e E0 / (1 + (4π/3)χ_e) with χ_e=(ε−1)/(4π).

Given / unknowns

  • Given: dielectric constant ε (dimensionless), sphere radius a, applied uniform field E0.
  • Outside medium: vacuum/air (ε_out = 1 in CGS).
  • No free charge anywhere (in the idealized setup), only bound charge on the interface.
  • Unknown (a): ⟨E⟩ inside the sphere.
  • Unknown (b): P inside the sphere, expressed in terms of E0 and χ_e.
  • Intermediate: the potential φ in and out, and matching coefficients.

Relevant principles (and why)

  • Electrostatics: fields are static; therefore ∇×E=0 and we can use a scalar potential.
  • No free charge in volumes: ∇·D=0 inside and outside, which with constant ε in each region implies ∇²φ=0 (Laplace).
  • Boundary conditions at r=a: tangential E continuous; normal D continuous since no free surface charge.
  • Symmetry: uniform E0 picks a single axis → axial symmetry → only P1(cosθ)=cosθ dependence appears.

Assumptions (explicit)

Assumptions
  • Linear, isotropic dielectric: D=εE inside; D=E outside.
  • Quasi-static, no time dependence: ∂/∂t = 0.
  • No free charges in space or on the interface: ρ_free=0, σ_free=0.
  • Applied field E0 is fixed by far-away sources (as in the hint), so boundary condition at infinity is unchanged by inserting the sphere.

Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

  • (A) Boundary-value PDE (Laplace) method: Solve ∇²φ=0 in spherical coordinates with axial symmetry.
    Pros: rigorous, transparent, generalizes to many shapes/modes. Cons: requires comfort with spherical harmonics.
  • (B) Depolarization-field argument: Use known result that a uniformly polarized sphere creates a uniform internal field E_dep=-(4π/3)P, then self-consistency with P=χ_e E.
    Pros: fast and physically insightful. Cons: relies on a known theorem about polarized spheres.
  • (C) Energy / variational viewpoint: Minimize electrostatic energy subject to constitutive law; yields same self-consistency.
    Pros: connects to optimization. Cons: more abstract for a first pass.

We choose (A) as the main derivation for mathematical rigor, then show how (B) reproduces the polarization formula cleanly.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Set coordinates: Use spherical coordinates (r,θ,φ) with the applied field along +z. Tool: symmetry → no dependence on azimuthal angle φ.
  2. Introduce potential: Because ∇×E=0, write E=−∇φ. Meaning: reduces vector field to scalar PDE.
  3. Write PDE in each region: No free charge and constant ε in each region → ∇²φ=0 inside and outside. Meaning: potential is harmonic.
  4. Choose general axial solutions: Only l=1 mode needed: inside φ_in = −A r cosθ; outside φ_out = −E0 r cosθ + (B cosθ)/r². Meaning: uniform field + induced dipole field.
  5. Apply boundary conditions at r=a: (i) continuity of φ (equivalently tangential E continuity), (ii) continuity of normal D: ε E_in,n = E_out,n. Meaning: no free surface charge.
  6. Solve for A: Extract E_in = A (uniform) and express in terms of E0, ε.
  7. Compute volume average: Because the field inside is uniform, ⟨E⟩=E_in.
  8. Compute polarization: Use P=χ_e E_in and ε=1+4πχ_e to get the requested form.
  9. Sanity checks: Test limits ε→1 and ε→∞; confirm directions/signs.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting that D_out = E_out (since outside is vacuum/air) in CGS.
  • Mixing SI and CGS: in SI the factors of 4π disappear and ε means something different.
  • Using the wrong boundary condition: it’s D_n that is continuous (if no free charge), not E_n.
  • Assuming “average field” needs integration—here it doesn’t because the internal field is uniform.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

The applied field E0 polarizes the dielectric. Bound surface charge appears with opposite signs on the “north/south” hemispheres. The field from these bound charges points against the applied field inside, so we expect: |E_in| < |E0| for ε > 1. Also, the symmetry of a sphere suggests the internal field is uniform and aligned with E0.

Mathematical model (variables, domains, coordinate system, BCs)

Unknown: potential φ(r,θ) and field E = −∇φ.

Coordinates: spherical (r,θ,ϕ) with E0 along +z ⇒ dependence only on r,θ (axial symmetry).

Domains:
Region I (inside sphere): 0 ≤ r < a, permittivity ε (constant).
Region II (outside): r > a, permittivity 1 (vacuum/air in CGS).

Governing PDE (no free charge, electrostatics):
∇·D = 0,   D = εE (inside), D = E (outside), and E = −∇φ.
With constant ε in each region ⇒ ∇²φ = 0 in each region.

Boundary conditions:
(1) Far-field (source-fixed uniform field): as r→∞,  E → E0  ⇒  φ_out → −E0 r cosθ.
(2) Interface r=a:
    (i) φ_in = φ_out  (continuity; also enforces E_t continuous)
    (ii) D_n continuous (σ_free=0):  ε E_in,n = E_out,n .

Step 1: Write the general harmonic solutions using symmetry

Because the far-field forcing is proportional to cosθ (uniform field along z), only the l=1 Legendre mode is required. The general axisymmetric harmonic solutions are: inside: regular at r=0 so only r^1 term; outside: must match uniform field plus a decaying dipole term r^(−2). 

Ansatz (l = 1 only):

φ_in(r,θ)  = −A r cosθ                      (finite at r=0)

φ_out(r,θ) = −E0 r cosθ + (B cosθ)/r^2      (→ −E0 r cosθ as r→∞)

Why this step is valid: Laplace’s equation is linear, and spherical harmonics form a complete basis for angular dependence. The boundary condition at infinity is purely l=1, so no other l can be generated by linear matching on a spherical surface.

Step 2: Compute fields from the potential

Using E = −∇φ in spherical coordinates, we need the radial component at the interface: 

E_r = −∂φ/∂r

For φ_in = −A r cosθ:
∂φ_in/∂r = −A cosθ   ⇒   (E_in)_r = A cosθ

For φ_out = −E0 r cosθ + B cosθ r^(−2):
∂φ_out/∂r = −E0 cosθ − 2B cosθ r^(−3)
⇒ (E_out)_r = E0 cosθ + 2B cosθ / r^3

Step 3: Apply boundary condition (i) — potential continuity at r=a

Set φ_in(a,θ) = φ_out(a,θ): 

At r=a:

−A a cosθ = −E0 a cosθ + (B cosθ)/a^2

Divide by cosθ (valid except at θ=π/2 where both sides are zero, and continuity holds by limit):
−A a = −E0 a + B/a^2

⇒ B = a^3 (E0 − A)

Step 4: Apply boundary condition (ii) — normal D continuity at r=a

With no free surface charge, D_n is continuous: inside D_in = εE_in, outside D_out = E_out. The normal direction is radial, so: 

At r=a:

(D_in)_r = (D_out)_r
ε (E_in)_r = (E_out)_r

Using (E_in)_r = A cosθ
and (E_out)_r at r=a:  E0 cosθ + 2B cosθ / a^3

ε A cosθ = E0 cosθ + 2B cosθ / a^3

Divide by cosθ:
ε A = E0 + 2B/a^3

Substitute B = a^3(E0 − A): 

ε A = E0 + 2(E0 − A)
ε A = E0 + 2E0 − 2A
ε A + 2A = 3E0
A(ε + 2) = 3E0
⇒ A = 3E0/(ε + 2)

Step 5: Identify the internal field and its volume average

Inside, φ_in = −A r cosθ corresponds to a uniform field: E_in = A ẑ (since −∇(−A z)=A ẑ). Therefore:

Result (a): volume-average field
E_in = (3/(ε+2)) E0

Because E is uniform inside a sphere in this setup:
⟨E⟩_volume = E_in = (3/(ε+2)) E0

Why “average” needs no integral: if E(r)=constant for all r in the sphere, then ⟨E⟩ = (1/V)∫_V E dV = (1/V) E ∫_V dV = E.

Step 6: Compute polarization P and show the requested form

In a linear dielectric (CGS), P = χ_e E and ε = 1 + 4πχ_e. Using E = E_in inside: 

P = χ_e E_in = χ_e (3/(ε+2)) E0

Rewrite factor in terms of χ_e:
ε + 2 = (1 + 4πχ_e) + 2 = 3 + 4πχ_e

So:
E_in = 3E0/(3 + 4πχ_e) = E0 / (1 + (4π/3)χ_e)

Therefore:
P = χ_e E0 / (1 + (4π/3)χ_e)
Final Answer (boxed)
⟨E⟩ = E_in = (3/(ε+2)) E0  = E0 / (1 + (4π/3)χ_e)

P = χ_e E_in = χ_e E0 / (1 + (4π/3)χ_e)

where χ_e = (ε − 1)/(4π)  (CGS)

Sanity checks

  • Limiting case ε → 1: E_in → (3/3)E0 = E0, and χ_e→0 ⇒ P→0. Correct.
  • Limiting case ε → ∞: E_in → 0. Strong polarization screens interior field. Correct trend.
  • Direction/sign: For ε>1, E_in is parallel to E0 but reduced in magnitude; P=χ_eE_in aligns with E0.
  • Dimensional check (CGS): ε and χ_e are dimensionless; thus the prefactors are dimensionless and E_in has units of field, consistent.
Optional deeper note: Why Laplace’s equation appears

Starting from ∇·D=4πρ_free and D=εE with constant ε in a region, we have ∇·(εE)=ε∇·E=0 because ρ_free=0. With E=−∇φ, this becomes ∇·(−∇φ)=−∇²φ=0, i.e., ∇²φ=0.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpreting the formula

The internal field reduction factor 3/(ε+2) is a geometry + material effect. The “+2” is purely geometric for a sphere and is closely tied to the sphere’s depolarization factor N=1/3. In CGS language, a uniformly polarized sphere produces an internal field E_dep = −(4π/3)P, which is why the combination (4π/3)χ_e appears.

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Increasing ε decreases E_in monotonically: E_in/E0 = 3/(ε+2).
  • Polarization magnitude: P = χ_e E0 / (1 + (4π/3)χ_e) increases with χ_e but saturates because the denominator grows too (self-screening).
  • Sphere size a does not affect E_in or P in this ideal electrostatic, homogeneous model; size affects only the induced dipole moment (∝ a^3) in the outside field.

Alternative derivation idea (self-consistency via depolarization field)

Sketch (brief but meaningful)

Known theorem (can be proven by the same Laplace method): a uniformly polarized sphere produces a uniform internal field E_dep = −(4π/3)P. The internal field is then 

E_in = E0 + E_dep = E0 − (4π/3)P.

With P = χ_e E_in, substitute: E_in = E0 − (4π/3)χ_e E_in, so (1 + (4π/3)χ_e)E_in = E0, yielding E_in = E0/(1 + (4π/3)χ_e) and hence P = χ_e E0/(1 + (4π/3)χ_e).

Concept checks (self-test)

  • Q: Why is E uniform inside the sphere? A: Symmetry + the fact that only the l=1 harmonic is needed; φ_in ∝ r cosθ gives a uniform gradient.
  • Q: If σ_free ≠ 0 at the interface, which boundary condition changes? A: Normal D jumps: D_out,n − D_in,n = 4πσ_free (CGS).
  • Q: Does making the sphere bigger increase E_in? A: No, not in this ideal model; E_in depends only on ε.
  • Q: For ε>1, is the induced dipole moment aligned with E0? A: Yes; the sphere polarizes along the applied field.
