Polarizability of a Conducting Sphere

We prove that a perfectly conducting metallic sphere of radius a placed in a uniform external electric field has induced dipole moment p proportional to E₀ with polarizability α: α = a³ (Gaussian) and α = 4π ε₀ a³ (SI). Along the way, we connect the “depolarization factor” method to the underlying Laplace boundary-value problem.

Quick Summary

  • Goal: Find the polarizability α of a perfectly conducting sphere (radius a) in a uniform applied field E₀.
  • Key physics idea: In electrostatic equilibrium a conductor has zero electric field inside (E = 0 for r < a).
  • Mathematical model: Outside the sphere, the potential satisfies Laplace’s equation ∇²φ = 0 with boundary condition “constant potential on the surface”.
  • Fast method used here: Treat surface charges as equivalent to a uniformly polarized sphere and use the depolarization field.
  • Governing relation (Gaussian): Inside a uniformly polarized sphere, E_dep = -(4π/3) P.
  • Governing relation (SI): Inside a uniformly polarized sphere, E_dep = -P/(3ε₀).
  • Result (symbolic): Induced dipole moment p = α E₀ with α = a³ (Gaussian) and α = 4π ε₀ a³ (SI).
  • Physical meaning: The “size” of induced dipole scales like volume (∝ a³) because the conductor rearranges charge over its surface to cancel the internal field.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • External (applied) field E₀ [V/m]: uniform electric field far from the object.
  • Dipole moment p [C·m]: leading multipole moment of the induced charge distribution.
  • Polarizability α:
    • SI: p = α E₀ with α having units [C·m²/V] = [F·m²].
    • Gaussian: p = α E₀ with α having units of volume [cm³] (often “geometric”).
  • Polarization P [C/m²] (SI): dipole moment per unit volume. For a uniformly polarized object, p = P V.
  • Permittivity ε₀ [F/m]: vacuum permittivity (SI).

Physical meaning (intuition)

A conductor in electrostatic equilibrium cannot sustain an internal electric field: mobile charges move until the net field inside is zero. This rearrangement of surface charge makes the exterior field look like the superposition of the applied uniform field plus the field of an induced dipole.

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

Electrostatic conductor conditions

  • ∇×E = 0 (electrostatics) so E = -∇φ.
  • Charge resides on the surface; inside the conductor bulk, E = 0.
  • The conductor surface is an equipotential: φ(r=a,θ)=const.

Assumes static (or quasi-static), perfect conductivity, and a sphere isolated in a uniform field (no nearby boundaries).

Common models/approximations and why they’re used

  • Perfect conductor: simplifies boundary conditions to “equipotential + zero interior field”.
  • Multipole expansion: far-field induced response dominated by the dipole term.
  • Uniform polarization equivalence: a conductor sphere’s induced surface charge produces the same dipole moment as a uniformly polarized sphere; then we can use the known depolarization field.

Mini intuition examples (quick)

  • Bigger sphere → bigger dipole: doubling a increases α by a factor 2³ = 8.
  • No applied field: if E₀ = 0, symmetry demands p = 0.
  • Direction: induced p aligns with E₀ (it enhances the field on one side and reduces on the other, while keeping inside zero).

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Unit system trap: the numerical prefactor differs between Gaussian and SI; the geometric scaling ∝ a³ is universal.
  • Definition of α: always check whether p = α E₀ uses SI or Gaussian conventions.
  • Depolarization factor: for a sphere it is 1/3 (SI form) or 4π/3 (Gaussian form) depending on how the formula is written.
  • Uniform polarization statement: the sphere is not “filled with dipoles” physically; it’s a mathematical equivalence for the field produced by a surface charge distribution.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

The polarizability of conducting particles is a foundational “building block” in applied electromagnetics and materials engineering: it tells you how strongly a particle couples to an electric field, which drives forces, torques, effective permittivity, and signal response.

Where it shows up (3+ application areas)

  • EMI/EMC & shielding: metal inclusions and beads polarize and reshape near-fields in enclosures and cables.
  • Dielectrophoresis (DEP) & microfluidics: conductive beads experience field-gradient forces ∝ polarizability.
  • Composite materials: effective permittivity of metal-filled polymers depends on particle polarizability and concentration.
  • Sensing & metrology: capacitive probes, near-field sensors, and scanning techniques rely on induced dipoles.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: coupling strength scales as a³; particle size distributions matter a lot.
  • Reliability: field enhancement near conductors can trigger breakdown or ESD in tight geometries.
  • Cost & manufacturability: smaller particles reduce coupling but may be harder to disperse or sinter uniformly.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Impedance/capacitance measurements: infer effective permittivity changes due to polarizable inclusions.
  • Microscopy + field mapping: Kelvin probe, electrostatic force microscopy (EFM) for local response (quasi-static regime).
  • RF scattering (small-particle limit): induced dipole dominates scattering/absorption when particle is electrically small.

Design trade-offs and non-idealities

  • Finite conductivity & frequency: at higher frequencies, currents lag; “perfect conductor” becomes an approximation.
  • Surface roughness & coatings: oxide layers or dielectric shells modify boundary conditions and effective α.
  • Nearby boundaries: substrates, enclosures, or neighboring particles change the field and invalidate “isolated sphere” formulas.

From lab to product

In product design, the idealized α ∝ a³ serves as a first-order scaling law. Engineers then correct it using: (i) effective-medium models for many particles, (ii) numerical electrostatics/EM solvers for complex geometries, and (iii) frequency-dependent material models when the quasi-static assumption fails.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No solving yet)

Restatement in plain words

A metallic (perfectly conducting) sphere of radius a is placed in an externally applied uniform electric field E₀. Because the sphere is a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium, the total electric field inside must be zero. Use the known “depolarization field” of a uniformly polarized sphere to relate the induced polarization (or dipole moment) to E₀, and show that the polarizability scales as a³ (with the correct unit-system prefactor).

Given, unknowns, and what must be shown

  • Given: conducting sphere radius a; applied uniform field E₀ (take along +z).
  • Unknown: induced dipole moment p (vector along z), and polarizability α defined by p = α E₀.
  • To prove: α = a³ (Gaussian). In SI, α = 4π ε₀ a³.

Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Electrostatic equilibrium of conductors: ensures E = 0 inside the metal and the surface is an equipotential.
  • Linearity: electrostatics in linear media allows superposition (applied field + induced field).
  • Known field of uniformly polarized sphere: gives a shortcut to compute the internal induced field due to surface charge.

Assumptions (explicit)

Assumptions

  • Perfect conductor (infinite conductivity), static fields (no time dependence).
  • Sphere is isolated in an infinite medium (vacuum/air) with uniform applied field at infinity.
  • No net free charge on the sphere (only induced surface charge from polarization).
  • Quasi-static limit: geometry is “electrically small” if you later think about AC; here strictly DC.

Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

  • (A) Depolarization-factor shortcut (chosen): Use E_dep inside a uniformly polarized sphere, impose E_total = 0. Fast, minimal algebra, highlights physical cancellation.
  • (B) Solve Laplace boundary-value problem: Solve ∇²φ = 0 outside with φ(a,θ)=const and φ→-E₀ r cosθ at infinity. More “first principles”; shows multipole structure explicitly.
  • (C) Image/Green’s function viewpoint: Use spherical harmonics / Green’s function for Dirichlet boundary on a sphere. Powerful, generalizable, but heavier mathematically.

We choose (A) because the problem statement explicitly points to the depolarization factor and because it cleanly connects the conductor condition E=0 to the induced dipole moment.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap only)

  1. Set geometry & direction: take E₀ = E₀ ẑ; sphere of radius a.
  2. Use conductor condition: inside the sphere, total field must satisfy E_in = 0.
  3. Model induced charges as polarization: replace the induced surface charge distribution by an equivalent uniform polarization P that produces the same dipole moment.
  4. Insert depolarization field: for a sphere, internal field from uniform P is E_dep = -(4π/3)P (Gaussian) or E_dep = -P/(3ε₀) (SI).
  5. Enforce cancellation: E₀ + E_dep = 0 ⇒ solve for P in terms of E₀.
  6. Convert polarization to dipole: p = P V with V = 4π a³/3.
  7. Read off polarizability: compare with p = α E₀.
  8. Sanity checks: dimensions, scaling with a, direction.

Common mistakes

  • Mixing SI and Gaussian prefactors (especially 4π factors).
  • Forgetting that p = P V (for uniform polarization) and using area instead of volume.
  • Sign error: the depolarization field points opposite to P.
  • Assuming the internal field equals the applied field (true for vacuum, not for conductors).

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition first

A conductor must have E = 0 inside. If we apply a uniform field E₀, charges shift: negative charge accumulates on the “upfield” side and positive charge on the “downfield” side (or vice versa depending on sign convention), creating an induced dipole that produces an internal field that exactly cancels E₀. Because the object size sets the “lever arm” and the charge distribution spreads over the surface, we expect the dipole strength to scale with the sphere’s volume, i.e. p ∝ a³ E₀.

Mathematical model (explicit)

Model statement (electrostatics boundary-value problem)

  • Domain: conductor interior r < a and exterior vacuum r > a in spherical coordinates (r,θ,φ).
  • Unknown: electrostatic potential φ(r,θ) (axisymmetric about z-axis).
  • Governing equation (exterior): ∇²φ = 0 for r > a (no free charge in vacuum).
  • Conductor boundary condition: φ(a,θ) = const (equipotential), and E = -∇φ = 0 for r < a.
  • Far-field condition: φ(r,θ) → -E₀ r cosθ as r → ∞.

We will not fully solve this PDE here (we sketch it later as an alternative derivation), but we will use a known equivalent-field result for a uniformly polarized sphere to get the dipole moment directly.

Step 1: Superposition inside the sphere

Let the applied field be uniform: E₀ = E₀ ẑ. The conductor develops an induced surface charge that produces an additional electric field E₁. By linearity (Maxwell’s equations in electrostatics are linear in φ and E), the total field is

Superposition (inside) 
E_in = E0 + E1

Electrostatic equilibrium in a perfect conductor requires

Conductor condition 
E_in = 0  (for r < a)

Therefore, inside the sphere:

Cancellation requirement 
E0 + E1 = 0  ⇒  E1 = -E0

Step 2: Replace induced surface charge by an equivalent uniform polarization

A uniformly polarized sphere (constant P throughout its volume) produces a particular internal field called the depolarization field. Importantly, the field produced by its bound surface charge has the same symmetry as the induced surface charge of a conductor in a uniform field: it is proportional to cosθ and leads to a uniform internal field. This equivalence is valid here because:

  • Symmetry: the problem is axisymmetric and linear; the dominant response is dipolar (ℓ=1 spherical harmonic).
  • Uniqueness theorem: the electrostatic solution for given boundary conditions is unique; matching the correct dipole moment yields the same exterior potential up to a constant.

Step 3: Use the known depolarization field of a sphere

For a uniformly polarized sphere, the internal field due to its polarization is uniform and opposite to P. The problem statement provides the appropriate “depolarization factor” form.

Gaussian (cgs) depolarization field 
E1 = E_dep = -(4π/3) P

Here P has Gaussian polarization units; the prefactor 4π/3 is the sphere’s depolarization factor in this convention.

SI depolarization field 
E1 = E_dep = -P/(3 ε0)

This is the same physics expressed with SI units; the “factor” is 1/3 in the form E = -(N/ε0) P with N=1/3.

Step 4: Enforce the conductor cancellation to solve for P

Gaussian units

Inside the conductor, E0 + E1 = 0. Substitute E1 = -(4π/3)P:

Solve for P (Gaussian) 
E0 + (-(4π/3) P) = 0
⇒ E0 = (4π/3) P
⇒ P = (3/(4π)) E0

SI units

Use E1 = -P/(3ε0):

Solve for P (SI) 
E0 + ( -P/(3 ε0) ) = 0
⇒ E0 = P/(3 ε0)
⇒ P = 3 ε0 E0

Step 5: Convert polarization to dipole moment

For a uniformly polarized body, the dipole moment is

Dipole moment from uniform polarization 
p = P V,  where V = (4π/3) a^3

Gaussian units: compute p and α

Insert P = (3/(4π)) E0:

Gaussian: p(E0) 
p = P V
  = ( (3/(4π)) E0 ) * ( (4π/3) a^3 )
  = E0 a^3

By definition p = α E0, so

Final (Gaussian) 
α = a^3

SI units: compute p and α

Insert P = 3 ε0 E0:

SI: p(E0) 
p = P V
  = (3 ε0 E0) * ( (4π/3) a^3 )
  = 4π ε0 a^3 E0

Hence

Final (SI) 
α = 4π ε0 a^3

Final Answer (plain text)

Copy all 
Gaussian (cgs):   p = a^3 E0   ⇒   α = a^3
SI:               p = 4π ε0 a^3 E0   ⇒   α = 4π ε0 a^3

Sanity checks

Dimensional analysis

  • Gaussian: α has dimensions of volume. a³ matches.
  • SI: α should scale as ε0 × (length)³. The result 4π ε0 a³ matches.

Limiting & scaling behavior

  • a → 0 ⇒ α → 0 (tiny sphere barely polarizes).
  • Doubling a multiplies α by 8.
  • Direction: p is parallel to E₀ (positive polarizability).

Math Insight

The core mathematical object behind this result is a harmonic function solution of Laplace’s equation (∇²φ=0) in the exterior, with a Dirichlet boundary condition on the sphere. The induced response is the ℓ=1 spherical-harmonic (dipole) mode, whose coefficient is fixed by the boundary condition “equipotential surface.” The depolarization-factor shortcut works because the internal field of that ℓ=1 mode is uniform and exactly cancels the applied uniform field.

Optional deeper note: how Laplace’s equation leads to the same α (sketch)

Because ∇×E=0, write E=-∇φ. Outside the sphere, ∇²φ=0. Axisymmetry implies a Legendre expansion:

General exterior harmonic form (axisymmetric) 
φ(r,θ) = -E0 r cosθ + (A cosθ)/r^2   (keep only ℓ=1 term)

The first term is the applied field potential; the second is the dipole term. The boundary condition φ(a,θ)=const forces the coefficient of cosθ to vanish at r=a:

Determine A 
At r=a:  -E0 a cosθ + (A cosθ)/a^2 = const
⇒ -E0 a + A/a^2 = 0
⇒ A = E0 a^3

The dipole potential is φ_dip = (p cosθ)/(4π ε0 r^2) in SI, hence A = p/(4π ε0). With A = E0 a^3 we get p = 4π ε0 a^3 E0, i.e. α = 4π ε0 a^3. In Gaussian units, the corresponding dipole potential has no ε0 and yields p = a^3 E0.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the final formula

The polarizability measures how much dipole moment is induced per unit applied field. For a sphere, the geometry is maximally symmetric, and the response is governed by a single length scale a. Therefore α must scale like a³, the only volume-like quantity you can form. The SI factor 4π ε0 converts the geometric volume scaling into SI dipole units.

Parameter dependence

  • Radius a: α ∝ a³. Size is the dominant lever (cube law).
  • Environment: in a medium of permittivity ε (instead of vacuum), the SI result generalizes to α ∝ 4π ε a³ for an ideal conductor in that medium (same geometry, different permittivity scale).
  • Frequency / finite conductivity: at high enough frequency, the induced charges/currents cannot perfectly cancel the interior field; effective α(ω) becomes complex and smaller in magnitude.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

The Laplace-equation method (sketched above) is the most “first-principles” approach: it treats the conductor as a Dirichlet boundary (φ=const) and uses separation of variables in spherical coordinates. The induced dipole emerges as the ℓ=1 coefficient needed to satisfy the boundary condition, and uniqueness guarantees this is the physical solution.

Concept check (with answers)

  • Q: Why must the internal field of a perfect conductor be zero in electrostatics?
    A: Any nonzero E would drive charge motion; equilibrium requires charges rearrange until E=0.
  • Q: Why is the induced response dipolar (not quadrupolar, etc.) for a uniform applied field?
    A: A uniform field corresponds to the ℓ=1 spherical harmonic (cosθ); linearity means the leading induced mode is also ℓ=1.
  • Q: If a doubles, how does p change (for fixed E₀)?
    A: p increases by a factor of 8.
  • Q: In SI, what part of α = 4π ε₀ a³ depends on material constants?
    A: Only ε₀ (or ε in a medium); the rest is purely geometric.
