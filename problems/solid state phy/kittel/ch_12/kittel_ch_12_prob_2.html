Heat Capacity of Magnons in a 3D Ferromagnet (Low Temperature)

We derive the leading low-T magnon contribution to the heat capacity per unit volume for an isotropic three-dimensional ferromagnet when the spin-wave dispersion is approximately ω = A k². The result scales as T3/2 and features the Riemann zeta value ζ(5/2).

Quick Summary

Back to top ↑
  • Goal: Find the leading low-temperature magnon heat capacity in a 3D ferromagnet with dispersion ω = A k².
  • Key physics idea: Magnons are bosonic quasiparticles; at low T they form a dilute Bose gas of spin-wave excitations.
  • Governing statistical formula: Energy density from Bose occupation nB(x)=1/(ex-1) integrated over k-space.
  • Mathematical heart: Convert the k-integral using the substitution x = βℏA k², then use ∫0∞ xs-1/(ex-1) dx = Γ(s)ζ(s).
  • Result type: Closed-form symbolic constant involving ζ(5/2), plus a numerical coefficient 0.113.
  • Main result (per unit volume): C/V = 0.113 kB(kBT/ℏA)3/2.
  • Physical meaning: Magnons contribute a T3/2 heat capacity at low T (distinct from phonons’ T3 in 3D).
  • Validity: Low-T, long-wavelength limit; magnons noninteracting; isotropic continuum; negligible magnon gap; temperatures well below exchange scale J.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Back to top ↑

What are magnons?

In an ordered ferromagnet, spins are aligned in the ground state. Small deviations (spin waves) propagate as collective modes. Quantizing these modes produces magnons, bosonic quasiparticles carrying one unit of spin angular momentum (typically ℏ).

Definitions Core symbols & units

  • k — wavevector magnitude [m-1]
  • ω(k) — magnon angular frequency [s-1]
  • A — dispersion coefficient in ω = A k² [m2/s] (since ω has units s-1)
  • ε(k) = ℏω(k) — magnon energy [J]
  • T — temperature [K], kB — Boltzmann constant [J/K]
  • u — energy density [J/m3], C/V — heat capacity per volume [J/(K·m3)]

Validity Assumptions you must check

  • Low T: kBT ≪ J (exchange scale) so only long-wavelength magnons matter.
  • Gapless: ignore anisotropy/field gaps; otherwise C is exponentially suppressed at very low T.
  • Dilute bosons: magnon–magnon interactions negligible to leading order.
  • Continuum: treat k as continuous (system size » lattice spacing; T low enough that k is small).

Why is ω proportional to k²?

For an isotropic ferromagnet, the long-wavelength spin-wave stiffness produces a quadratic dispersion: ω = A k². Mathematically, the long-wavelength mode amplitude behaves like a field whose energetic cost contains gradients, so in Fourier space gradients contribute factors of |k|. A quadratic spectrum is consistent with rotational symmetry and the Goldstone mode of broken spin-rotation symmetry.

Math Insight Dispersion as a mathematical object

The relation ω(k)=A k² is a dispersion relation linking eigenfrequency to eigenwavenumber. It is the Fourier-space signature of a real-space operator dominated by a Laplacian: spatial variations contribute an energy density proportional to |∇m|², so the linearized equations lead to eigenvalues proportional to k². In spectral terms, you are integrating a Bose occupation against a density of states generated by the Laplacian’s spectrum in 3D.

Mini intuition examples (quick)

  • Compare to phonons: acoustic phonons have ω ∝ k in 3D, giving C ∝ T3 (Debye law). Quadratic magnons instead give C ∝ T3/2.
  • Effect of a gap: if &epsilon(k)=Δ+ℏA k², then at kBT ≪ Δ you get C ∝ e-Δ/(kBT), not a power law.
  • Dimensional “smell test”: if only scale is (kBT)/(ħA) with dimension 1/m2, then a density must scale like (kBT/ħA)3/2 ∼ 1/m3.

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Confusing ω with energy: use &epsilon = ℏω.
  • Forgetting the k-space measure (d3k)/(2π)3 and the 4πk2 factor in 3D spherical coordinates.
  • Dropping a factor when substituting x = βℏA k².
  • Using the wrong zeta value: the relevant one here is ζ(5/2) (not ζ(3/2)).

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Back to top ↑

Low-temperature magnetic heat capacity matters because it controls thermal budgets, cooldown times, and noise / stability in devices that operate with magnetic materials at cryogenic or sub-Kelvin temperatures. Even when magnons are not the dominant heat reservoir, they can be the design-limiting contribution in precision systems.

Where it shows up Application areas

  • Spintronics & magnonics: magnon transport, spin caloritronics, thermal spin currents.
  • Cryogenic instrumentation: magnetic shielding, sensors, readout components in dilution refrigerators.
  • Quantum tech packaging: magnetic materials near qubits: unwanted heat capacity can slow thermalization.
  • Magnetic refrigeration & calorimetry: characterizing magnetic contributions to C(T) in candidate materials.

Why engineers care Practical consequences

  • Performance: extra C increases thermal time constant τ = C/G (slower response).
  • Reliability: thermal gradients and cycling can induce stress; knowing C(T) helps predict transients.
  • Cost & efficiency: cooldown power and time are expensive; reducing parasitic heat capacities helps.
  • Manufacturability: impurities and anisotropy can open gaps, changing low-T behavior (diagnostic tool).

Measurement How it’s validated in practice

  • Low-T calorimetry (relaxation or AC calorimetry): measures C(T) and fits power laws.
  • Inelastic neutron scattering / Brillouin light scattering: probes ω(k) and extracts stiffness A.
  • Magnetometry: detects gaps/anisotropy that would modify the low-T asymptotics.

From lab to product Adapting the idealized model

Real devices include anisotropy, finite size, and interfaces. These can introduce an energy gap (Δ), quantize k, or add damping. Engineers often start with the clean continuum prediction C/V ∝ T3/2 as a baseline, then incorporate (i) a gap term, (ii) dimensional crossover (thin films → 2D), and (iii) additional heat-capacity channels (electrons, phonons, nuclear spins).

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Back to top ↑

Restated problem: Consider a three-dimensional ferromagnet at low temperatures (kBT ≪ J). Magnons have approximate dispersion ω = A k². Find the leading term in the heat capacity per unit volume. The expected coefficient is 0.113 in (C/V) = 0.113 kB(kBT/ℏA)3/2.

Given Inputs

  • 3D ferromagnet; low-T regime kBT ≪ J.
  • Magnon dispersion ω(k)=A k² (isotropic).
  • Magnons are bosons with energy &epsilon(k)=ℏA k².

Unknown Output

  • Leading low-T contribution to heat capacity per unit volume, C/V.
  • Show the T3/2 scaling and compute the prefactor.

Relevant principles (and why)

  • Bose–Einstein statistics: magnons have no conserved number (chemical potential μ = 0), so occupations are nB(β&epsilon).
  • Continuum k-space integration: at low T, only small k contribute, so replace lattice sum by integral.
  • Asymptotic analysis: leading term comes from a universal integral giving Γ and ζ functions.

Assumptions Explicit idealizations

  • Infinite or very large volume V with periodic boundary conditions (so k is quasi-continuous).
  • Isotropic dispersion; no gap (Δ = 0); no external field splitting.
  • Noninteracting magnons to leading order (interactions give higher-order T corrections).

Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

  • Direct k-space energy integral (best here): compute u(T) = ∫ (d3k/(2π)3) &epsilon(k) nB, then differentiate. Pros: clean, systematic, yields exact prefactor with ζ(5/2). Cons: requires careful substitution.
  • Density of states method: derive g(ε) for quadratic dispersion in 3D and integrate over energies. Pros: conceptual; highlights scaling. Cons: DOS derivation can introduce algebra slips.
  • Dimensional analysis only: infer C/V ∝ kB(kBT/ℏA)3/2. Pros: fast. Cons: cannot get the 0.113 coefficient.

Chosen approach: the direct k-space integral, because it produces the coefficient cleanly and transparently.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

Back to top ↑
  1. Model the excitations: set magnon energy &epsilon(k)=ℏA k² and occupation nB(β&epsilon).
  2. Write energy density: u(T) = ∫ (d3k/(2π)3) &epsilon(k) nB.
  3. Use isotropy: convert to spherical k coordinates: d3k = 4πk2dk.
  4. Non-dimensionalize: substitute x = βℏA k² to factor out T-dependence.
  5. Evaluate the universal integral: reduce to ∫ x3/2/(ex-1) dx and apply the Γζ identity.
  6. Differentiate: compute C/V = (du/dT).
  7. Sanity checks: dimensional analysis, limiting behavior, and coefficient evaluation to match 0.113.

Common mistakes Quick fixes

  • Missing factors: keep track of (2π)-3 and 4π from angular integration.
  • Wrong power of x: after substitution, verify the exponent by checking units.
  • Wrong special function: for energy you need ζ(5/2); for number density it would be ζ(3/2).
  • Confusing C and u: C/V = du/dT, not u/T.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Back to top ↑

Physical intuition first

At low temperatures the system excites only long-wavelength magnons (small k). Because magnons are bosons and their dispersion is quadratic, the phase-space available at energy ~kBT grows like k3 while the energy per excitation grows like k2. This combination yields an energy density scaling like T5/2 and thus a heat capacity scaling like T3/2.

Mathematical model Variables, domain, and conditions

  • Domain: 3D bulk ferromagnet of volume V, large enough that k is continuous (periodic boundary conditions used to quantize k, then V→∞).
  • Coordinates: k-space in spherical coordinates (k, θ, φ) due to isotropy.
  • Dispersion: ω(k)=A k², so ε(k)=ℏA k².
  • Statistics: Bose occupation with chemical potential μ=0: nB(ε)=1/(eβε-1), where β = 1/(kBT).
  • Goal observable: heat capacity per unit volume C/V = (∂u/∂T), where u is energy density.

Operator note: in long-wavelength spin-wave theory, the dominant spatial operator is Laplacian-like (∇²), whose Fourier eigenvalues are -k². That spectral structure underlies the quadratic dispersion and the power law.

Step 1 — Write the energy density integral

In the continuum limit, the sum over modes becomes an integral:

Key equation: energy density of bosonic modes 
u(T) = ∫ [d^3k/(2π)^3]  ε(k) nB(β ε(k)),
nB(x) = 1/(e^x − 1),   ε(k) = ħ A k^2,   β = 1/(kB T).

Why this is valid: linearity of expectation values in statistical mechanics plus independent harmonic modes in spin-wave theory. At low T magnons are dilute, so the noninteracting Bose gas expression gives the leading term.

Step 2 — Use isotropy to reduce to a 1D integral

Because ε(k) depends only on k = |k|, we write in spherical coordinates: d3k = 4πk2dk. Then

Reduced 1D integral (3D isotropic) 
u(T) = ∫0^∞ [4π k^2 dk /(2π)^3] (ħ A k^2) / (exp(β ħ A k^2) − 1)
     = (ħ A)/(2π^2) ∫0^∞ dk  k^4 / (exp(β ħ A k^2) − 1).

Check the prefactor: (4π)/(2π)3 = 1/(2π2).

Step 3 — Nondimensionalize: x = βħA k²

Define the dimensionless variable x = βℏA k². Then k² = x/(βħA), k = (x/(βħA))1/2, and dk = (1/2)(βħA)-1/2 x-1/2 dx.

Substitute into k4dk:

Substitution algebra (no magic steps) 
k^4 dk = (k^2)^2 dk
       = [x/(βħA)]^2 * (1/2)(βħA)^(-1/2) x^(-1/2) dx
       = (1/2) (βħA)^(-5/2) x^(3/2) dx.

Therefore the energy density becomes

Energy density in universal integral form 
u(T) = (ħA)/(2π^2) * ∫0^∞ [ (1/2)(βħA)^(-5/2) x^(3/2) /(e^x − 1) ] dx
     = (1/(4π^2)) (β)^(-5/2) (ħA)^(-3/2) ∫0^∞ x^(3/2)/(e^x − 1) dx.

Why the power (−3/2) of ħA? We had one factor (ħA) in front and (βħA)−5/2 from k4dk, giving (ħA)1−5/2 = (ħA)−3/2.

Step 4 — Evaluate the integral using Γ and ζ

Use the standard identity (valid for Re(s)>1):

Special-function identity 
∫0^∞ x^(s−1)/(e^x − 1) dx = Γ(s) ζ(s).

Here we have x3/2 = x(5/2)−1, so s = 5/2:

Integral value for s = 5/2 
∫0^∞ x^(3/2)/(e^x − 1) dx = Γ(5/2) ζ(5/2),
Γ(5/2) = (3/2)(1/2)√π = (3√π)/4.

Plugging in:

Energy density u(T) 
u(T) = (1/(4π^2)) (kB T)^(5/2) (ħA)^(-3/2) Γ(5/2) ζ(5/2)
     = (1/(4π^2)) (kB T)^(5/2) (ħA)^(-3/2) * (3√π/4) ζ(5/2)
     = [3 ζ(5/2)] / [16 π^(3/2)] * (kB T)^(5/2) / (ħA)^(3/2).

It is often written equivalently using (4π)3/2: since (4π)3/2 = 8π3/2, we have 3/(16π3/2) = (3/2)/(4π)3/2. Thus

Same u(T), alternative compact form 
u(T) = (3/2) * ζ(5/2)/(4π)^(3/2) * (kB T)^(5/2)/(ħA)^(3/2).

Step 5 — Differentiate to get heat capacity per volume

By definition at fixed volume, C/V = (∂u/∂T). Since u(T) ∝ T5/2, du/dT = (5/2) u/T. Compute explicitly:

Differentiate u(T) 
C/V = d/dT [ (3/2) ζ(5/2)/(4π)^(3/2) * (kB T)^(5/2)/(ħA)^(3/2) ]
    = (3/2) ζ(5/2)/(4π)^(3/2) * (5/2) (kB)^(5/2) T^(3/2)/(ħA)^(3/2)
    = (15/4) ζ(5/2)/(4π)^(3/2) * kB * (kB T/(ħA))^(3/2).

In the last step we factor out one kB to make the result look like “kB times a number density scale”.

Final answer (per unit volume) 
C/V = (15/4) * ζ(5/2)/(4π)^(3/2) * kB * (kB T/(ħA))^(3/2)
    ≈ 0.11293 * kB * (kB T/(ħA))^(3/2).

Leading low-T magnon heat capacity in 3D with ω = A k^2:
C/V ≈ 0.113 kB (kB T/(ħA))^(3/2).

Sanity checks

Units Dimensional analysis

ω = A k² ⇒ A has units m²/s, so ℏA has units (J·s)(m²/s)=J·m². Therefore (kBT/ℏA) has units 1/m², and (kBT/ℏA)3/2 has units 1/m³. Multiplying by kB gives J/(K·m³), correct for C/V.

Limiting behavior Low-T asymptotics

As T→0, C/V → 0 like T3/2, consistent with vanishing thermal population of excitations. If a gap Δ existed, the true asymptotic would cross over to exponential suppression at sufficiently low T.

Optional deeper note: why μ = 0 for magnons (and why that matters)

In equilibrium without external pumping, magnons are not conserved: the system can create/annihilate magnons while conserving energy and total angular momentum with the lattice and other reservoirs. For nonconserved bosons, the chemical potential in the Bose distribution is μ = 0, giving nB(ε)=1/(eβε−1). If magnons are pumped (nonequilibrium magnon condensates), an effective nonzero μ can appear, changing thermodynamics.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Back to top ↑

Re-interpreting the final formula

The result C/V = const · kB(kBT/ℏA)3/2 can be read as:

  • kB sets the energy per Kelvin per excitation.
  • (kBT/ℏA)3/2 is an effective thermal density of accessible k-modes in 3D for a quadratic dispersion.
  • A (spin-wave stiffness) controls how costly it is to excite short-wavelength magnons: larger A → fewer excitations at a given T → smaller C.
  • ζ(5/2) encodes the universal Bose integral; it is the same mathematical structure behind many bosonic thermodynamic power laws.

Parameter dependence What changes what?

  • Increase T → C/V increases as T3/2.
  • Increase A (stiffer spin waves) → C/V decreases as A-3/2.
  • Introduce a gap Δ → power law replaced by an exponential factor at low enough T.
  • Change dimensionality:
    • 2D with ω ∝ k² gives C/Aarea ∝ T (different DOS power).
    • 1D with ω ∝ k² gives C/L ∝ T1/2.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of integrating over k directly, derive the density of states in energy. With ε=ℏA k² in 3D, the number of states with |k|<k is N(k) = V·(4πk3/3)/(2π)3. Convert k to ε and differentiate to get g(ε) ∝ ε1/2. Then compute u = ∫ dε g(ε)ε/(eβε−1), which reduces to the same Γζ integral.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why is the temperature exponent 3/2, not 3? A: Because magnons here have ε∝k² (quadratic), changing the DOS and hence the power law; phonons have ε∝k (linear).
  • Q: Which zeta value appears in C/V? A: ζ(5/2), because energy involves an extra factor of ε relative to number density.
  • Q: What would happen if an anisotropy gap exists? A: At sufficiently low T, C becomes exponentially small, roughly ∝ e-Δ/(kBT).
  • Q: How does increasing A affect heat capacity? A: It decreases C/V as A−3/2 (excitations become more energetic).
