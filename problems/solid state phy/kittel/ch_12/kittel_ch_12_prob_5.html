Coercive Force of a Small Single-Domain Ferromagnetic Particle

We derive the reverse field required to destroy metastability of a uniaxial single-domain particle (Stoner–Wohlfarth switching along the easy axis), then estimate the single-domain size threshold by balancing magnetostatic energy against domain-wall energy—framed with applied-physics and industrial relevance.

Model: single-domain macrospin
Key result: Bc = 2K/M
Scaling: Emag ~ Ms2d3

Quick Summary

  • What this is about: switching (reversal) of a small single-domain uniaxial ferromagnetic particle under a reverse field along its easy axis.
  • Key physics idea: reversal happens when the energy minimum corresponding to the “wrong” orientation becomes unstable (a local minimum disappears).
  • Governing energy (macrospin): U(θ)=K sin²θ − Ba M cosθ (anisotropy + Zeeman interaction).
  • Mathematical criterion: stability of a stationary point requires d²U/dθ² > 0; loss of stability occurs when d²U/dθ² = 0 at the metastable angle.
  • Coercive (reverse) field along easy axis: Bc = 2K/M (CGS; same form as Stoner–Wohlfarth axial switching field).
  • Magnetostatic energy scaling of a saturated sphere: Emag ~ Ms² d³ (precise CGS factor for a sphere: (π²/9) Ms² d³).
  • Domain-wall energy scaling: Ew ≈ ωw(π d²/4) for one planar wall across the sphere.
  • Single-domain critical size (order estimate): dc ~ ωw/Ms²; more specifically (sphere, CGS) dc ≈ 9 ωw/(4π Ms²).
  • Physical meaning: smaller particles favor single-domain (domain walls too “expensive”); larger particles split into domains to reduce demagnetizing energy.
Math Insight: The switching problem is a local bifurcation in a 1D energy landscape U(θ): a metastable minimum vanishes when its curvature changes sign. In dynamical terms (Landau–Lifshitz–Gilbert with damping), the stable equilibrium becomes unstable when the Hessian (here just d²U/dθ²) crosses zero.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (THEORY BEFORE SOLVING)

Core definitions (symbols + typical units)

  • M: magnetization magnitude of the particle (CGS: emu/cm³, numerically ≈ Gauss).
  • Ms: saturation magnetization (same units as M).
  • K: uniaxial anisotropy energy density (CGS: erg/cm³).
  • θ: angle between applied field axis and magnetization direction (radians).
  • Ba: applied magnetic field along the easy axis (CGS often reported in Oe/G; here used in the Zeeman term).
  • UK = K sin²θ: anisotropy energy density (minimum at θ=0,π for easy-axis uniaxial case).
  • UM = −BaM cosθ: interaction (Zeeman) energy density favoring alignment with the field.
  • ωw: domain wall energy per unit area (CGS: erg/cm²).
  • d: particle diameter; V: particle volume.

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Anisotropy acts like an internal “spring” that tries to keep magnetization on the easy axis.
  • Zeeman energy is the field’s “tilt” of the energy landscape: it prefers magnetization aligned with the applied field.
  • Coercive field (here for ideal single-domain switching) is the reverse field at which the metastable orientation loses stability.
  • Magnetostatic (demagnetizing) energy penalizes a uniformly magnetized body because it creates stray fields; domains reduce this.
  • Domain wall energy penalizes splitting into domains; walls cost exchange + anisotropy energy across a transition region.

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

Macrospin (single-domain) assumption: magnetization is spatially uniform in the particle, i.e., M(r) ≈ constant direction throughout the volume. This is valid when the particle is small enough that forming nonuniform patterns (domains) is energetically unfavorable.
  • Energy minimization: Equilibria satisfy dU/dθ = 0; stability requires d²U/dθ² > 0.
  • Small-angle expansion: For θ near a candidate equilibrium θ0, expand U(θ) in a Taylor series; the quadratic term controls stability.
  • Magnetostatics (no currents): ∇×H = 0, ∇·B = 0 with B = H + 4πM (CGS). For a uniformly magnetized ellipsoid, Hd = −N M (demag factor).

Common models/approximations used here

  • Uniaxial anisotropy: UK=K sin²θ (lowest-order symmetry-allowed term for a uniaxial crystal).
  • Field applied along the easy axis: reduces the problem to one angular coordinate θ.
  • Single planar domain wall estimate: wall area ≈ cross-section of the sphere by a plane: A ≈ π(d/2)².
  • Order-of-magnitude magnetostatic energy: Emag ~ (energy density)×V with energy density ~ Ms² (up to geometric factors).

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • If Ba=0, anisotropy alone gives two equally stable orientations (θ=0 and θ=π).
  • A small reverse field tilts the energy landscape: θ=0 becomes deeper, θ=π becomes a shallower metastable well.
  • When the reverse field is strong enough, the metastable well disappears—magnetization must reverse.
What to watch for (pitfalls)
  • Angle definition: θ is between the field axis and magnetization; reversing the field changes the energy tilt.
  • Stability vs. stationarity: dU/dθ=0 gives an extremum, but you must check d²U/dθ² to know if it is a minimum.
  • Energy density vs total energy: K, U are often per unit volume; multiply by V when comparing with wall energies (per area).
  • Unit systems: CGS factors (4π) appear in magnetostatics; keep consistent when estimating sizes.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this shows up (3+ applications)

  • Magnetic recording & MRAM: switching fields, stability margins, and thermal reliability of nanoscale bits.
  • Permanent-magnet powders & sintered magnets: coercivity vs. particle size distribution; demagnetization resistance.
  • Biomedical magnetic nanoparticles: stable single-domain moments for targeting, imaging (MPI), and hyperthermia design.
  • Magnetic sensors/actuators: hysteresis control and repeatability in micro-magnetic components.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: coercive field sets switching thresholds and noise immunity.
  • Reliability: stable single-domain states reduce unpredictable domain-wall motion (a major source of hysteresis dispersion).
  • Cost & manufacturability: controlling particle size (milling, precipitation, sputtering conditions) tunes coercivity without changing chemistry.
  • Energy efficiency: lower switching fields reduce write energy (memory) but must not sacrifice thermal stability.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Hysteresis loops (VSM, SQUID): extract coercivity, remanence, saturation.
  • Ferromagnetic resonance (FMR): infer anisotropy constants and damping; validates macrospin assumptions for small particles.
  • Microscopy (MFM, Lorentz TEM): visualize domain structures; confirm single-domain vs multidomain regimes.

Design trade-offs & constraints (real materials)

  • Non-ideal shapes change demagnetizing factors → changes Emag and critical size.
  • Surface oxidation, defects, stress modify effective anisotropy K and wall energy ωw.
  • Temperature reduces Ms and can activate thermal switching (superparamagnetism) even if single-domain.
From lab to product: The idealized macrospin model becomes a distribution-aware model in manufacturing: engineers track coercivity vs. size distribution, texture (anisotropy axis alignment), and shape factors. Safety margins are set by worst-case tails (e.g., slightly larger grains that become multidomain, or smaller grains that become thermally unstable).

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Problem rewritten (in plain words)

We have a small spherical single-domain particle of a uniaxial ferromagnet. (a) Using the anisotropy energy UK=K sin²θ and the field interaction UM=−BaM cosθ, show that the reverse field along the easy axis required to reverse the magnetization is Ba=2K/M (CGS magnitude). The hint suggests expanding about θ=π and finding the field where the local minimum disappears. (b) Estimate when a sphere stays single-domain by comparing magnetostatic energy of a saturated sphere (∝Ms²d³) with the domain-wall cost for introducing one planar wall (≈ ωwπd²/4). Use an iron-like ωw to estimate cobalt’s critical radius.

Given

  • Uniaxial anisotropy energy density: UK=K sin²θ
  • Zeeman interaction density: UM=−BaM cosθ
  • θ is angle between Ba and M
  • Sphere of diameter d; saturation magnetization Ms
  • Wall energy per area ωw

Unknowns / required results

  • Show coercive reverse field: Bc = 2K/M
  • Show Emag ~ Ms² d³ (and give a geometric-factor argument)
  • Show Ew ≈ ωwπd²/4
  • Estimate critical radius rc for cobalt below which single-domain is stable

Relevant principles (why they apply)

  • Energy landscape and stability: single variable θ → treat U(θ) as a 1D potential; metastability disappears when curvature at the extremum vanishes.
  • Magnetostatic self-energy: uniformly magnetized sphere has a known demagnetizing factor; this gives a clean scaling in d³.
  • Domain formation criterion: compare reduction in stray-field energy by forming two domains to the cost of a domain wall.
Assumptions (explicit)
  • Single-domain (macrospin): magnetization direction is uniform inside the particle.
  • Uniaxial anisotropy dominates other anisotropies; lowest-order term K sin²θ is sufficient.
  • Applied field is along the easy axis (axial Stoner–Wohlfarth case).
  • For the domain estimate: wall treated as planar with area ≈ π(d/2)²; ignore wall thickness and curvature corrections.
  • Ignore thermal activation (i.e., coercive field is purely energetic, not thermally assisted).

Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

  • (A) Taylor expansion near θ=π (hinted): simplest, directly tests stability via the quadratic coefficient.
  • (B) Stationary point + inflection condition: solve dU/dθ=0 and simultaneously d²U/dθ²=0; more general but slightly longer.
  • (C) Full Stoner–Wohlfarth astroid method: powerful for arbitrary field angles, but overkill here since field is axial.

Chosen: (A) for part (a) (matches hint, minimal algebra), plus a standard demagnetizing-factor argument for part (b).

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Write the total energy density U(θ)=UK+UM. (Tool: given model)
  2. Identify the metastable orientation under a reverse field: magnetization opposite the field corresponds to θ≈π. (Physical reasoning)
  3. Expand U(θ) about θ=π using θ=π+φ with |φ|≪1. (Tool: Taylor series)
  4. Read off the quadratic coefficient in φ²; stability requires it be positive. (Tool: second-derivative test)
  5. Set the quadratic coefficient to zero to find the critical reverse field Bc. (Meaning: loss of local minimum)
  6. For magnetostatic energy: use Hd=−N M (uniformly magnetized sphere) and E=(1/2)∫(−Hd·M)dV. (Tool: magnetostatics + symmetry)
  7. Express Emag in terms of d to show Emag ∝ Ms² d³. (Dimensional scaling)
  8. Estimate wall energy Ew≈ωw×A with A≈π(d/2)². (Geometry)
  9. Balance energies Emag≈Ew to obtain dc (and rc=dc/2). (Design threshold)
  10. Plug representative cobalt values (Ms, ωw order) for a numeric radius estimate. (Applied estimate)
Common mistakes
  • Expanding about the wrong angle (θ=0 instead of θ=π for the metastable reversed state).
  • Forgetting that stability is about the second derivative (curvature), not only dU/dθ=0.
  • Mixing “energy density” with “total energy” when comparing with ωw (per area).
  • Dropping CGS geometric factors incorrectly; keep them for a clean sphere estimate, then simplify to scaling.

PART 3 — Full Solution (DETAILED + TEACHING)

Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

With no field, θ=0 and θ=π are equivalent easy-axis minima. A reverse field (pointing opposite the initial magnetization) makes θ=π metastable: it remains a local minimum for small fields, but the well gets shallower. At a critical field, the well disappears (curvature goes to zero), and magnetization must switch.

(a) Coercive reverse field along the easy axis

Mathematical model (1D variational problem). Let θ∈[0,π] be the angle between applied field (easy axis direction) and magnetization. The total energy density is

U(θ) = U_K + U_M = K sin^2(θ) - B_a M cos(θ).

Here K is the uniaxial anisotropy energy density and the Zeeman term is written in the form provided. The equilibrium angles solve dU/dθ=0; stability requires d²U/dθ²>0.

Step 1 — Expand near the metastable configuration θ = π

Under a reverse field, the magnetization can sit (metastably) near θ=π (opposite the field direction). Define a small deviation φ by θ = π + φ, with |φ| ≪ 1. We now expand sinθ and cosθ for small φ: 

sin(π+φ) = -sinφ ≈ -φ  ⇒  sin^2(π+φ) ≈ φ^2
cos(π+φ) = -cosφ ≈ -(1 - φ^2/2) = -1 + φ^2/2

Substitute into U(θ)=K sin²θ − B_a M cosθ: 

U(π+φ) ≈ K(φ^2) - B_a M(-1 + φ^2/2)
       = B_a M + (K - (B_a M)/2) φ^2.

The constant term B_a M just shifts the reference energy and does not affect stability. The key is the quadratic coefficient multiplying φ².

Step 2 — Apply the stability criterion

Near φ=0, the local behavior is U ≈ const + A φ² with A = K − (B_a M)/2. A local minimum at φ=0 requires A > 0. The metastable minimum disappears when A = 0. 

Stability near θ=π requires:  K - (B_a M)/2 > 0
Loss of metastability at:      K - (B_a M)/2 = 0  ⇒  B_c = 2K/M.
Result (coercive reverse field along easy axis): B_c = 2K/M (CGS magnitude, for ideal coherent rotation along the easy axis).

Sanity checks

  • Units: K has units of energy/volume; dividing by M (magnetization) gives field units (consistent with Zeeman density ~ −BM).
  • Limits: larger anisotropy K → harder to switch → larger B_c. Larger magnetization M → field couples more strongly → smaller B_c.
  • Sign logic: we used the magnitude of the reverse field; physically, reversal happens when the field opposes the original magnetization.
Optional deeper note: same criterion via derivatives

You can compute derivatives directly: U(θ)=K sin²θ − B_a M cosθ. Then dU/dθ = 2K sinθ cosθ + B_a M sinθ = sinθ(2K cosθ + B_a M). At θ=π, sinθ=0 so it is stationary for any field. Stability uses d²U/dθ² = 2K(cos²θ − sin²θ) + B_a M cosθ. At θ=π: cosθ=−1, sinθ=0 ⇒ d²U/dθ² = 2K − B_a M. Stability requires 2K − B_a M > 0, and the threshold is B_a = 2K/M.

(b) Magnetostatic energy vs. domain-wall energy → critical single-domain size

Step 1 — Magnetostatic energy of a uniformly magnetized sphere

In magnetostatics (no free currents), ∇×H=0 and ∇·B=0 with B=H+4πM (CGS). For a uniformly magnetized ellipsoid (sphere is a special case), symmetry implies a uniform demagnetizing field H_d = −N M, where N is the demagnetizing factor. For a sphere, N = 4π/3 (CGS), corresponding to N=1/3 in SI conventions.

The magnetostatic self-energy (total) can be written as 

E_mag = (1/2) ∫_V ( - H_d · M ) dV
     = (1/2) ∫_V ( N M^2 ) dV
     = (1/2) N M_s^2 V    (for saturation |M|=M_s).

For a sphere: N=4π/3 and V = (π/6) d³. Therefore 

E_mag = (1/2)(4π/3) M_s^2 (π d^3 / 6)
     = (2π/3) M_s^2 (π d^3 / 6)
     = (π^2 / 9) M_s^2 d^3  ≈ 1.10 * M_s^2 d^3.
So the requested scaling is immediate: E_mag ∝ M_s² d³, i.e. “energy density ~ M_s²” times “volume ~ d³”.

Step 2 — Domain wall energy for one planar wall

Introduce a single planar domain wall that splits the sphere into two domains (a crude but useful estimate). The wall area is approximately the cross-section of the sphere by a plane through its center: 

A_wall ≈ π (d/2)^2 = π d^2 / 4
E_wall ≈ ω_w A_wall = ω_w π d^2 / 4.

This matches the problem statement: the domain wall energy will be π ω_w d²/4.

Step 3 — Balance energies to estimate the critical size

A particle will tend to remain single-domain if creating a wall is not worth the magnetostatic energy reduction. A simple threshold estimate sets the single-domain (uniform) magnetostatic energy scale equal to the cost of one wall: E_mag ≈ E_wall.

Using the sphere prefactor we derived:

(π^2/9) M_s^2 d^3  ≈  (π/4) ω_w d^2
⇒ d_c ≈ [(π/4) ω_w] / [(π^2/9) M_s^2]
⇒ d_c ≈ (9 ω_w) / (4 π M_s^2)
⇒ r_c = d_c/2 ≈ (9 ω_w) / (8 π M_s^2).
Critical-size estimate (sphere, CGS): r_c ≈ (9 ω_w) / (8π M_s²). Scaling-wise: r_c ~ ω_w/M_s².

Numerical estimate for cobalt (using iron-like wall energy scale)

The problem asks: “Estimate for cobalt the critical radius below which particles are stable as single domains, taking the value ω_w ~ J s²/a as for iron.” In practice, this indicates using an iron-order wall energy magnitude. A representative engineering-scale value for wall energy in strong ferromagnets is on the order of ω_w ~ 10 erg/cm² (order-of-magnitude; “iron-like”). Cobalt has saturation magnetization roughly M_s ~ 1400 emu/cm³ (order-of-magnitude).

We keep the main result symbolic; the numeric below is an applied estimate only. 

Example values (order estimate):
ω_w ≈ 10 erg/cm^2
M_s ≈ 1.4×10^3 emu/cm^3  ⇒  M_s^2 ≈ 1.96×10^6 (emu/cm^3)^2

r_c ≈ (9 ω_w)/(8π M_s^2)
    ≈ (9×10)/(8π×1.96×10^6) cm
    ≈ 1.8×10^-6 cm  ≈ 18 nm.
Interpretation: This estimate suggests cobalt spheres below roughly tens of nanometers in radius are energetically favored to be single-domain (within this simplified one-wall vs uniform-energy balance). If ω_w is closer to a few erg/cm², the estimate drops toward ~5–10 nm; if ω_w is higher (tens of erg/cm²), it rises toward ~30–60 nm.

Final Answer (copy-friendly)

Coercive reverse field (easy axis, single-domain coherent rotation):
B_c = 2K / M   (CGS magnitude).

Magnetostatic energy of saturated sphere (diameter d):
E_mag = (1/2) N M_s^2 V with N = 4π/3 (sphere, CGS) and V = π d^3/6
⇒ E_mag = (π^2/9) M_s^2 d^3  ~ M_s^2 d^3.

Single planar domain wall energy across the sphere:
E_wall ≈ ω_w (π d^2/4).

Single-domain critical size estimate (sphere):
Set E_mag ≈ E_wall ⇒ d_c ≈ (9 ω_w)/(4π M_s^2),
so r_c = d_c/2 ≈ (9 ω_w)/(8π M_s^2).

Example cobalt estimate using iron-like ω_w ~ 10 erg/cm^2 and M_s ~ 1.4×10^3 emu/cm^3:
r_c ~ 2×10^-6 cm ~ 20 nm (order of tens of nm).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (THEORY AROUND THE RESULT)

Re-interpreting the coercive field B_c=2K/M

  • K sets the height/curvature of the anisotropy barrier: stronger anisotropy makes reversal harder.
  • M sets how strongly the field couples: larger magnetization means the field “tilt” is more effective.
  • The factor 2 emerges because near θ=π the Zeeman term contributes a stabilizing/destabilizing curvature of (−B_a M/2) in the small-angle expansion.

Parameter dependence of the single-domain size

  • Increase ω_w (more expensive walls) ⇒ larger r_c: particles stay single-domain up to larger sizes.
  • Increase M_s ⇒ smaller r_c: magnetostatic energy grows as M_s², pushing earlier domain formation.
  • Shape effects: spheres have a clean demag factor; elongated particles can have smaller demag energy in certain directions, shifting the threshold.
Alternative derivation idea (meaningful but brief)

Instead of Taylor expanding, you can impose the saddle-node condition for the metastable minimum: solve simultaneously dU/dθ=0 and d²U/dθ²=0. This is the general method used to derive the full Stoner–Wohlfarth astroid for arbitrary field angle. In 1D, it is exactly the condition that an extremum becomes an inflection point.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why is θ=π stationary for any field in this model?
    A: Because dU/dθ has a factor sinθ; sinπ=0 makes dU/dθ=0 regardless of field.
  • Q: What mathematically signals switching at B_c?
    A: The curvature at the metastable point goes to zero: d²U/dθ²|θ=π=0.
  • Q: Why does E_mag scale as d³ but E_w as d²?
    A: Magnetostatic energy is volumetric (∝V∝d³), wall energy is areal (∝A∝d²).
  • Q: If M_s doubles, what happens to r_c?
    A: r_c scales as 1/M_s², so it drops by a factor 4.
