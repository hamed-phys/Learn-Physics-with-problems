Saturation Magnetization Near Tc (Mean-Field, Spin-1/2)

Quick Summary

What we prove, the key equations, and what the result means physically.
  • Goal: Show the spontaneous (zero-field) magnetization just below the Curie point obeys M(T) ∝ (Tc − T)1/2 in mean-field theory.
  • Key physics idea: Near Tc, magnetization is small, so we can expand the Brillouin function (for spin-1/2 it is tanh) and keep the leading nonlinear term.
  • Governing self-consistency: For spin-1/2 in Weiss mean field (zero external field), m = tanh[(Tc/T) m], where m = M/Ms is reduced magnetization.
  • Mathematical move: Use tanh x = x − x^3/3 + O(x^5) and solve the resulting cubic for small m.
  • Main result (symbolic): m(T) ≈ √[3( Tc − T )/Tc] as T → Tc− (dominant dependence).
  • Physical meaning: Magnetization rises continuously from zero at Tc, but with a square-root onset (mean-field critical exponent β = 1/2).
  • Industrial relevance: This scaling informs temperature drift of magnetic sensors/actuators, magnetocaloric performance near transition points, and process windows for annealing and heat management.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Definitions, governing principles, mean-field model, and what to watch for.

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • M: magnetization (A/m). For N spins in volume V, often M = μ_tot/V.
  • Ms: saturation magnetization (A/m), the maximum magnetization when all spins align.
  • m: reduced magnetization, m = M/Ms (dimensionless).
  • H: magnetic field strength (A/m). (In SI, B = μ0(H + M).)
  • T: temperature (K), Tc: Curie temperature (K).
  • kB: Boltzmann constant (J/K).
  • μ: magnetic moment per spin (A·m2 = J/T). For electron spin, μ ≈ g μB S.
Critical exponent (mean field)
β = 1/2
Spin-1/2 Brillouin
B1/2(x) = tanh(x)

Physical meaning & intuition

A ferromagnet below Tc can magnetize spontaneously even without an applied field: microscopic exchange interactions favor alignment, producing a macroscopic order parameter M. Thermal agitation opposes alignment; at T = Tc the ordered phase disappears.

Near Tc from below, the magnetization is small, so you can treat the ordering as a weak symmetry-breaking effect and expand the response nonlinearly. That’s the origin of the square-root onset.

Validity conditions (mean-field)

Mean-field assumes each spin feels an average internal field proportional to the macroscopic magnetization. It becomes exact in high dimensions / long-range interactions, but it misses critical fluctuations in real 3D magnets close enough to Tc (hence experimental β ≈ 0.33 rather than 0.5).

Key laws & the mean-field model (Weiss)

In Weiss mean field, the local microscopic exchange is represented as an effective internal field: Heff = H + λ M, where λ is a material-dependent coupling (units: (A/m)−1). The magnetization then follows the equilibrium single-spin response in this effective field.

Key Equation — Weiss effective field 
H_eff = H + λ M

For spin S, the equilibrium magnetization is given by a Brillouin function. For S = 1/2, it simplifies to a hyperbolic tangent.

Common models/approximations & pitfalls

  • Near-critical expansion: Expand tanh for small argument; mathematically this is a Taylor series around 0.
  • Zero-field spontaneous magnetization: Set external field H = 0 and solve the self-consistent equation for nonzero M (only exists for T < Tc).
  • Order parameter symmetry: At H=0, solutions come in ±M due to spin-flip symmetry; we usually report |M|.
  • Pitfall: Confusing “saturation” in the strict sense (very large applied field aligning all spins) with the “spontaneous/saturation magnetization” meaning M(T) at zero field below Tc. In many texts, “saturation magnetization” refers to the spontaneous magnitude below Tc.
  • Pitfall: Forgetting that the cubic term in the expansion is essential; without it you cannot get a stable finite solution.
Mini intuition examples

(1) If T is high, thermal disorder dominates ⇒ only M=0 is stable.

(2) If T is low, alignment wins ⇒ a large |M| is stable.

(3) Right below Tc, alignment barely wins ⇒ |M| is small and grows like a square root.

Math Insight (core equation as a mathematical object)

The mean-field condition is a nonlinear fixed-point equation for the order parameter: m = F(m;T). The Curie point is a bifurcation: at T=Tc, the slope F'(0;T) crosses 1, turning the trivial fixed point (m=0) from stable to unstable and creating two new stable fixed points ±m. The square-root scaling is the normal form of a supercritical pitchfork bifurcation.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Why engineers care, how it’s measured, and how the ideal model is adapted.

Where this appears in industry (examples)

  • Electric machines & transformers: temperature dependence of magnetization influences core losses, torque density, and demagnetization margins.
  • Magnetic sensing & metrology: Hall/AMR/GMR/TMR sensor calibration depends on magnetic properties drifting with temperature.
  • Magnetic recording & spintronics: thermal stability and switching thresholds depend on the order parameter near transitions.
  • Magnetocaloric refrigeration: performance peaks near phase transitions; understanding scaling helps tune materials and operating temperature.
  • Materials processing: annealing/heat treatments can traverse Tc, changing microstructure and magnetic performance.

Why engineers care (performance, reliability, cost)

  • Thermal derating: components may lose magnetic strength as temperature rises; near Tc the loss is rapid (square-root close to Tc in mean-field).
  • Safety & margins: avoiding operation too close to Tc can prevent sudden performance collapse in harsh environments.
  • Control & compensation: knowing M(T) enables temperature compensation algorithms and robust control in actuators.
  • Cost & material selection: raising Tc or choosing alloys with stable M(T) can reduce cooling requirements.

How it’s measured/validated in practice

  • VSM (Vibrating Sample Magnetometry): M(H,T) curves; can extract spontaneous magnetization via extrapolation.
  • SQUID magnetometry: high sensitivity near Tc where signals are small.
  • Curie-temperature identification: from susceptibility peaks, Arrott plots, or M(T) extrapolation.
  • Process monitoring: in-line magnetic property checks for manufacturing consistency (e.g., steel grades).

Design trade-offs & “lab to product” reality

  • Non-idealities: domains, anisotropy, defects, stresses, and finite-size effects modify M(T) near Tc.
  • Critical fluctuations: real 3D materials often show β ≈ 0.33 (Ising-like) rather than 0.5; mean-field is a good baseline but not the last word.
  • Constraints: manufacturing tolerances and composition gradients shift Tc, broadening transitions and smoothing sharp scalings.
  • Adaptation: engineers fit empirical M(T) curves over operating ranges, using mean-field/critical forms as physics-informed templates and adding correction terms.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Restate the task, list knowns/unknowns, assumptions, and choose a method.

Problem restatement (in plain words)

Using the Weiss mean-field approximation for a ferromagnet with spin S = 1/2, show that the spontaneous magnetization (the magnitude of magnetization at H=0) just below the Curie temperature behaves as M(T) ∝ (Tc − T)1/2.

Given quantities

  • Mean-field (Weiss) model applies: Heff = H + λM.
  • Spin is S = 1/2 ⇒ Brillouin function reduces to tanh.
  • We focus on just below Tc ⇒ small order parameter (M ≪ Ms).

Unknowns / what to prove

  • Find the leading dependence of M(T) as T → Tc−.
  • Show the dominant power law is (Tc − T)1/2.
  • Optionally express the prefactor in terms of mean-field parameters (or in reduced form).
Relevant principles & why they apply

The problem is fundamentally equilibrium statistical mechanics: the magnetization is the thermal average of spin moments in an effective field. Mean-field reduces the interacting many-body problem to a single-spin problem in Heff, plus a self-consistency condition. Close to Tc, we use analytic expansion because the order parameter is small.

Assumptions (explicit)
  • Uniform magnetization: ignore spatial variations/domains (no ∇ terms), i.e., a “zero-mode” model.
  • Thermal equilibrium: no time dependence (no d/dt); we solve an algebraic fixed-point problem.
  • Mean-field coupling: interactions enter only through λM.
  • Near-critical: keep lowest nonlinearity (cubic) in tanh expansion; neglect O(m5).

Possible approaches (compare)

  • (A) Self-consistent tanh + Taylor expansion (best here): Direct and uses the given S=1/2 simplification. Shows the exponent cleanly.
  • (B) Landau free energy expansion: Write F(M)=a(T−Tc)M2+bM4 and minimize. Very elegant; exponent also 1/2, but you must justify coefficients from mean-field.
  • (C) Susceptibility bifurcation argument: Use slope condition at m=0 and then include leading nonlinearity. This is essentially (A) in dynamical-systems language.

Choice: We use (A) because the problem explicitly mentions mean-field and spin-1/2; tanh makes the math transparent and fully controlled near Tc.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

A minimal plan with meaning, plus a common-mistakes box.

Plan (7 steps)

  1. Define the order parameter: set m = M/Ms (dimensionless). (Goal: simplify algebra.)
  2. Write Weiss effective field: Heff = H + λM, then set H=0 for spontaneous magnetization. (Goal: self-consistent equation.)
  3. Use spin-1/2 thermal response: magnetization follows m = tanh(μHeff/kBT). (Goal: explicit nonlinearity.)
  4. Identify Tc: from the linearized equation near m=0, determine the combination of parameters that equals Tc. (Goal: rewrite everything in terms of T/Tc.)
  5. Expand tanh: for small argument, tanh x ≈ x − x^3/3. (Goal: keep leading stabilizing nonlinearity.)
  6. Solve for m: cancel the trivial m=0 solution and solve the remaining cubic to obtain m(T). (Goal: extract scaling.)
  7. Asymptotics near Tc: set T = Tc − ΔT with ΔT ≪ Tc, and keep leading order. (Goal: show exponent 1/2.)
Common mistakes (quick tips)
  • Dropping the cubic term in tanh expansion: then you cannot get a finite magnetization below Tc.
  • Forgetting that near Tc, m is small but Tc/T ≈ 1; expand consistently in ΔT/Tc.
  • Mixing SI fields (H) with B without μ0. Here we only need ratios that become dimensionless in μH/kBT.
  • Calling the result “exact”: it is mean-field asymptotic, valid near Tc within that approximation.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Step-by-step derivation with explicit approximations, checks, and boxed final result.

Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

At T = Tc, spontaneous magnetization should vanish continuously (second-order transition). Just below Tc, order is weak, so M should be small; because the system is symmetric under M → −M at H=0, the leading stable free-energy-like balance is between a term ∝ M2 that changes sign at Tc and a stabilizing term ∝ M4. That competition generically gives M ∝ √(Tc−T). We now show it directly from the mean-field tanh equation.

Mathematical model (explicit)
  • Unknown: uniform magnetization M (scalar magnitude along some chosen axis) at equilibrium.
  • Domain/geometry: spatially uniform “single-mode” model (no coordinate dependence), so no ∇ operators appear.
  • External field: we set H = 0 to obtain spontaneous magnetization.
  • Constitutive mean field: Heff = λM.
  • Spin-1/2 equilibrium relation: M = Ms tanh( μ Heff / (kBT) ).
  • Boundary/initial conditions: not applicable (equilibrium algebraic problem). Symmetry yields solutions ±M.

Step 1 — Write the spin-1/2 mean-field self-consistency

For a spin-1/2 paramagnet in a field, the thermal average moment aligns according to a tanh. In mean field, the field acting on a spin is the effective field Heff. Let Ms be the saturation magnetization (all spins aligned). Define the reduced magnetization:

Definition — Reduced magnetization 
m = M / M_s   (dimensionless, with 0 ≤ |m| ≤ 1)

For spin-1/2, the Brillouin function is B1/2(x) = tanh(x). Thus,

Mean-field self-consistency (spin-1/2) 
m = tanh( (μ H_eff) / (k_B T) )

In Weiss mean field, Heff = H + λM. For spontaneous magnetization we take H = 0, so:

Zero-field effective field 
H = 0  ⇒  H_eff = λ M = λ M_s m

Substitute into the tanh equation:

Self-consistent equation in m 
m = tanh( (μ λ M_s / (k_B T)) m )
Dimensional analysis check

The tanh argument must be dimensionless. Indeed, μ has units J/T, H is A/m, and in SI μH is an energy scale (up to μ0 conventions depending on definition of μ and H). The ratio μH / (kBT) is dimensionless, as required. (For scaling near Tc, the exact SI prefactors are not needed; the key is a dimensionless coupling.)

Step 2 — Identify the Curie temperature via linearization

Near the transition, the spontaneous magnetization is small, |m| ≪ 1. Then the argument of tanh is also small, and we can linearize: tanh(x) ≈ x for small x.

Apply this to the self-consistency equation:

Linearized equation near m = 0 
m ≈ (μ λ M_s / (k_B T)) m

A nontrivial solution m ≠ 0 requires the prefactor to be 1. This defines the Curie temperature:

Definition of T_c (mean-field, spin-1/2 form) 
T_c = (μ λ M_s) / k_B

Using this, the self-consistent equation becomes a clean dimensionless relation:

Dimensionless mean-field equation 
m = tanh( (T_c/T) m )

This step is valid because linearization uses the differentiability of tanh at 0 and the assumption that near the critical point the relevant solutions have small m.

Step 3 — Expand tanh to cubic order (the key approximation)

To obtain the leading nonzero magnetization below Tc, we must keep the first nonlinear term:

Series expansion (small argument) 
tanh(x) = x − x^3/3 + O(x^5)

Here the argument is x = (Tc/T)m. Close to Tc from below, m is small, so x is small as well (since Tc/T ≈ 1). Substitute the expansion into the self-consistency equation:

Cubic approximation of the self-consistency equation 
m = (T_c/T)m − (1/3)(T_c/T)^3 m^3 + O(m^5)

Bring terms to one side and factor out m:

Factorized form 
0 = m[(T_c/T) − 1] − (1/3)(T_c/T)^3 m^3 + O(m^5)

The m = 0 solution always exists. For the nonzero branch, divide by m (allowed for m ≠ 0):

Nontrivial branch equation 
(T_c/T) − 1 = (1/3)(T_c/T)^3 m^2 + O(m^4)

Now solve for m2 at leading order:

Leading-order solution for m^2 
m^2 ≈ 3 * [ (T_c/T) − 1 ] / (T_c/T)^3

Step 4 — Asymptotics just below Tc: extract the (Tc − T)1/2 law

Let T = Tc − ΔT with 0 < ΔT ≪ Tc. Then:

Small-ΔT expansions 
T_c/T = T_c/(T_c − ΔT) = 1/(1 − ΔT/T_c)
≈ 1 + (ΔT/T_c) + O((ΔT/T_c)^2)

Therefore,

Leading behavior of (T_c/T) − 1 
(T_c/T) − 1 ≈ ΔT/T_c = (T_c − T)/T_c

Also, (Tc/T)3 ≈ 1 + O(ΔT/Tc), so to leading order we can replace it by 1 in the denominator (this is a controlled approximation because we only keep the dominant term in ΔT/Tc):

Dominant approximation near T_c 
m^2 ≈ 3 * (T_c − T)/T_c

Taking the positive root for the magnitude (the negative root corresponds to the opposite symmetry-broken direction):

Final scaling (reduced magnetization) 
m(T) = |M|/M_s  ≈  √[ 3 (T_c − T) / T_c ]    as  T → T_c−
FINAL ANSWER — Magnetization near T_c (mean field, S = 1/2) 
|M(T)| ≈ M_s √[ 3 (T_c − T) / T_c ]   ∝ (T_c − T)^(1/2)   (dominant dependence)
What we proved (in words)

The spontaneous magnetization turns on with a square-root as temperature drops below the Curie point. In mean-field language, the critical exponent is β = 1/2.

Sanity check: limiting cases
  • As T → Tc−, (Tc−T) → 0 ⇒ M → 0, as required for a continuous transition.
  • For modest separation below Tc, higher-order terms O(m5) become important; the square-root is the dominant near-critical behavior.
Sanity check: units
  • The factor √[3(Tc−T)/Tc] is dimensionless.
  • Thus |M| has the same units as Ms (A/m), consistent.
Optional deeper note: how this matches Landau theory

Landau theory posits a symmetry-allowed expansion (for H=0):

Landau free energy (schematic) 
F(M) = F0 + a(T − T_c) M^2 + b M^4 + ...   (b > 0)

Minimizing, dF/dM = 0 gives 2a(T − T_c)M + 4bM^3 = 0, hence M^2 = [a(T_c − T)]/(2b) for T<Tc. Again M ∝ (Tc−T)1/2. The mean-field tanh derivation effectively computes the same structure by expanding the microscopic equation of state.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Interpretation, parameter dependence, alternative derivations, and concept checks.

Interpreting the final formula

|M(T)| ≈ Ms √[3(Tc−T)/Tc] says:

  • Ms sets the overall scale (how large the fully aligned magnetization is).
  • (Tc−T)/Tc is the dimensionless distance below the critical point.
  • The square root reflects a balance between the first destabilizing linear term and the first stabilizing nonlinear term in the equation of state.

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Lowering T below Tc increases |M| like √(Tc−T) (near Tc).
  • Increasing Tc (stronger exchange coupling) shifts the onset to higher temperatures—important for high-temp applications.
  • Mean-field prefactors depend on the microscopic coupling packaged into Tc and on Ms.
Optional: connect T_c back to mean-field coupling

In the simplified spin-1/2 mean-field form used here, T_c = (μ λ M_s)/k_B. More general mean-field treatments express Tc using S(S+1) factors and microscopic exchange constants. The scaling exponent near Tc remains 1/2 in mean field.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of expanding tanh, you can derive an effective Landau functional by integrating the equation of state and identifying coefficients of M2, M4, ... near Tc. Minimizing that functional yields the same square-root onset. Mathematically, both routes are controlled expansions around a bifurcation point.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why must we include the cubic term in the tanh expansion?
    A: Because it provides the leading nonlinearity that stabilizes a finite m. Without it, the only solution near Tc is m=0 or an inconsistent divergence.
  • Q: What symmetry produces ±M solutions at H=0?
    A: Spin-flip (time-reversal) symmetry of the Hamiltonian at zero field: if M is a solution, so is −M.
  • Q: What does “dominant dependence” mean here?
    A: As T→Tc−, the leading term in |M(T)| is proportional to (Tc−T)1/2; higher-order corrections vanish faster (e.g., proportional to (Tc−T)3/2, etc.).
  • Q: Why do real ferromagnets often show β≈0.33 instead of 0.5?
    A: Critical fluctuations neglected by mean-field theory become important near Tc in 3D; universality classes (e.g., 3D Ising/Heisenberg) yield different β.
