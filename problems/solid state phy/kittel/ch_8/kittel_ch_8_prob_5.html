Magnetoresistance with Two Carrier Types (Strong-Field Limit)

Two-carrier Drude transport in crossed electric and magnetic fields: how electrons and holes together produce a nontrivial Hall field and an effective longitudinal conductivity that can saturate at large magnetic field if the carrier densities are unequal.

Quick Summary

  • What this is about: magnetotransport in a conductor containing electrons (density n) and holes (density p) using the Drude (relaxation-time) model.
  • Key physics idea: each carrier’s Lorentz force bends its drift; with two carrier types, their Hall responses partly cancel, producing a Hall field and a longitudinal response that can saturate at large B.
  • Governing equation (steady Drude-Lorentz): 0 = q(E + v×B/c) − m v/τ (solved for v(E)).
  • Mathematical object: a 2×2 conductivity tensor σ mapping E ↦ J; Hall effect comes from antisymmetric off-diagonal terms.
  • Strong-field parameter: Q ≡ ωcτ (dimensionless); “very strong” means Q ≫ 1 for each carrier.
  • Result type: fully symbolic formulas for σyx, Hall field Ey, and effective σ along x under Jy=0.
  • Main results (B→∞): σyx → (n−p) e c/B; Ey expressed by densities and Q’s; σeff has a term that becomes B-independent if n≠p (saturation).
  • Physical meaning: if n=p, compensation removes the dominant Hall term and σ scales like B−2; if n≠p, σ approaches a constant at large B.
Strong-field Hall conductivity
σyx ≈ (n − p) e c / B
Hall field (open y-circuit)
Ey = − (n − p) ( n/Qe + p/Qh )−1 Ex
Effective longitudinal conductivity
σeff = (e c/B) [ A + (n − p)2/A ]
A = n/Qe + p/Qh

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols & SI-style meaning)

  • n: electron concentration (m−3), p: hole concentration (m−3)
  • me, mh: effective masses (kg)
  • τe, τh: relaxation times (s)
  • qe=−e, qh=+e (C)
  • B: magnetic flux density (T in SI; here formulas use a c factor consistent with Gaussian/cgs Lorentz force q(v×B)/c)
  • ωc,e=eB/(mec), ωc,h=eB/(mhc) (s−1)
  • Qe≡ωc,eτe, Qh≡ωc,hτh (dimensionless)
  • J: current density (A/m2), E: electric field (V/m)
  • σ: conductivity tensor (S/m), J = σ·E

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Relaxation time τ sets how quickly drift velocity relaxes back to zero due to scattering.
  • Cyclotron frequency ωc sets how fast B bends trajectories.
  • Q = ωcτ compares bending to scattering: Q ≪ 1 means weak Hall deflection; Q ≫ 1 means strong deflection.
  • Two carriers (electrons + holes) can produce partial cancellation in Hall response, modifying magnetoresistance qualitatively.
Key law/model (Drude-Lorentz): In the relaxation-time approximation for a carrier with charge q and mass m, the mean drift velocity v obeys a linear ODE: 
m dv/dt = q ( E + (v × B)/c ) - (m/τ) v
In steady state (dc transport), set dv/dt = 0 and solve a linear algebra problem for v(E).

Validity conditions & approximations

Assumptions used throughout:
  • Homogeneous, isotropic conductor; uniform n, p, τ, m (no spatial dependence ⇒ no PDE needed).
  • Steady-state dc transport: ∂/∂t = 0.
  • Linear response in E: drift velocities are small enough that transport coefficients are constant.
  • Single relaxation time per carrier type (effective momentum relaxation).
  • Magnetic field along z: B = B ẑ; electric field in x–y plane.
  • Strong-field limit: Qe ≫ 1 and Qh ≫ 1.

Mini intuition examples (no heavy algebra)

  • One carrier (electrons only): as B increases, current deflects sideways, producing a Hall field; the Hall conductivity scales like ~ 1/B.
  • Perfect compensation (n = p): electron and hole Hall currents oppose each other; the net Hall term can vanish, revealing a weaker B dependence in the longitudinal channel (often ~B−2 in strong field).
  • Imperfect compensation (n ≠ p): a residual Hall response survives; enforcing Jy=0 requires a Hall field that can feed back into Jx, producing saturation.

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Sign conventions: σxy = −σyx and electron charge is negative; many “sign mistakes” come from mixing q with e.
  • Which quantity is held at zero: Hall measurements typically impose Jy=0 (open circuit), not Ey=0.
  • Strong-field expansion: keep the leading terms in 1/Q carefully; σxx is smaller than σxy by ~1/Q.
  • Units system: the appearance of c indicates a Gaussian/cgs-style Lorentz force q(v×B)/c; keep c consistently.
Math Insight: The steady-state Drude equation is a linear operator equation: 
( (m/τ) I - (q/c) [B×] ) v = q E
where [B×] is the matrix representation of the cross-product operator v ↦ v×B. Solving transport is literally “invert a 2×2 matrix” (in the x–y plane) and then sum currents from each carrier type.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (examples)

  • Semiconductor characterization: Hall measurements in Si, GaAs, InP, organic semiconductors to extract n, p, and mobilities (μ ∝ τ/m).
  • Magnetic field sensors: Hall plates and magnetoresistive devices; two-carrier effects matter in narrow-gap materials and compensated semimetals.
  • Power electronics & reliability: carrier compensation and mobility changes with temperature/processing affect conduction losses and sensing accuracy.
  • Metrology for thin films: extracting carrier type mixture in polycrystalline films (ITO, ZnO, graphene stacks, semimetals).

Why engineers care

  • Performance: saturation vs B2 growth of resistivity changes device transfer functions.
  • Cost & yield: dopant control (n−p) impacts whether magnetotransport behaves “compensated” or not.
  • Reliability: τ depends on impurities, grain boundaries, and temperature; two-carrier interplay can mask degradation unless modeled.
  • Design margins: in high-B environments (MRI, accelerators), transport can enter Q ≫ 1 regime, changing calibration.
How it’s measured/validated in practice (brief): Apply a known current I along x, measure transverse voltage VH (Hall) and longitudinal voltage Vxx versus B. Fit to a two-carrier model to extract n, p, μe, μh (with μ = eτ/m in SI; here the same scaling holds with the cgs factor handled in ωc).
Design trade-offs & non-idealities:
  • Real materials often have multiple bands, anisotropic masses, and field-dependent τ (not captured by constant-τ Drude).
  • Contact misalignment mixes Hall and longitudinal voltages; symmetry procedures (B reversal) are used to separate them.
  • In manufacturing, thickness variation and inhomogeneous doping create spatially varying n, p; effective-medium corrections may be needed.
  • At high fields/low temperatures, quantum oscillations and Landau quantization can invalidate purely classical Drude transport.
From lab to product: how the ideal model is adapted

Product-level models typically incorporate (i) temperature-dependent τ(T), (ii) geometry factors and contact resistance, and (iii) sometimes a third “parallel conduction” channel (e.g., substrate leakage). The two-carrier Drude tensor is still the backbone, but it becomes part of a larger parameter-estimation workflow.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement (in plain words)

A conductor contains electrons (density n, mass me, relaxation time τe) and holes (density p, mass mh, relaxation time τh). In the limit of very strong magnetic field (ωcτ ≫ 1 for each carrier), prove:
(a) σyx = (n−p) e c / B, (b) the Hall field satisfies Ey = −(n−p)( n/Qe + p/Qh )−1 Ex, (c) the effective conductivity along x under open-circuit y condition is σeff = (e c/B)[ (n/Qe + p/Qh) + (n−p)2(n/Qe + p/Qh)−1 ].

Given quantities

  • Carrier parameters: (n, me, τe) for electrons; (p, mh, τh) for holes
  • Fields: B (assume B along z), electric field components Ex, Ey
  • Strong field regime: Qe=ωc,eτe ≫ 1 and Qh=ωc,hτh ≫ 1

Unknowns / what must be shown

  • Conductivity tensor element σyx in the strong-field limit
  • Hall field Ey when Jy=0 (open transverse circuit)
  • Effective longitudinal conductivity σeff relating Jx=σeffEx under Jy=0

Relevant principles (why they apply)

  • Drude-Lorentz dynamics applies because the problem explicitly references drift velocity approximation and relaxation times.
  • Linearity: the steady-state equation is linear in v and E, so the solution is a linear map (tensor) σ.
  • Symmetry: with B along z and isotropic scattering, σ in x–y has the form σ = [[σxx, σxy], [σyx, σyy]] with σxx=σyy and σxy=−σyx.
Geometry & boundary condition used implicitly: Hall measurements correspond to open-circuit in y: Jy=0. This is the “boundary condition” that determines Ey.

Possible approaches (compare)

Approach A: Solve for each carrier’s conductivity tensor then add
  • Pros: systematic; easiest to take strong-field limits; cleanly separates electron vs hole signs.
  • Cons: requires careful algebra with signs and tensor components.
Approach B: Solve directly for ve, vh from vector equations
  • Pros: very physical; you see Lorentz deflection immediately.
  • Cons: still becomes a 2×2 linear system; equivalent to Approach A but less “packaged”.
Approach C: Use resistivity tensor and add conductivities via matrix inversion tricks
  • Pros: can highlight magnetoresistance structure.
  • Cons: more abstract; adding resistivities is not correct (conductivities add for parallel channels).

Chosen approach: Approach A (conductivity-tensor per carrier, then sum), because the problem asks for explicit σ components and strong-field asymptotics in Q.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Set coordinates: choose B = B ẑ; write the steady-state Drude equation in components (x,y). Tool: vector cross product.
  2. Write the linear system: express vx, vy in terms of Ex, Ey by solving a 2×2 matrix equation. Tool: matrix inversion.
  3. Compute current: J = Σ ni qi vi and identify σxx, σyx for each carrier; then add electrons + holes. Tool: linearity.
  4. Take strong-field limit: expand in 1/Q (Q ≫ 1) to obtain leading terms for σxx and σyx. Tool: asymptotics.
  5. Impose Hall condition: set Jy=0, solve for Ey/Ex. Tool: boundary condition.
  6. Compute σeff: substitute Ey into Jx=σxxEx+σxyEy to get Jx=σeffEx. Tool: elimination.
  7. Sanity checks: verify units; check n=p and n≠p scaling with B; confirm antisymmetry σxy=−σyx. Tool: dimensional analysis.
Common mistakes (quick fixes)
  • Mixing σxy and σyx: remember σxy=−σyx.
  • Forgetting the electron’s negative charge: do not set q=+e for electrons.
  • Imposing Ey=0 instead of Jy=0 for Hall geometry.
  • Dropping the subleading σxx~1/Q term too early: you need it to compute Ey.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation before calculating

In strong magnetic field, each carrier’s motion is dominated by the Lorentz force, so its current tends to flow almost perpendicular to E. That produces a large Hall response. However, electrons and holes bend in opposite directions (because their charges differ), so their Hall currents partially cancel. If n=p, that cancellation can be nearly perfect, leaving a much smaller longitudinal conduction. If n≠p, a residual Hall imbalance remains and can feed a B-independent contribution to the longitudinal current under Jy=0, producing saturation.

Mathematical model (explicit):
  • Domain: homogeneous bulk conductor (no spatial variation), so ∇·J=0 is automatically satisfied for uniform J.
  • Coordinates: (x,y,z) with B = B ẑ; fields E=(Ex,Ey,0); drift velocities v=(vx,vy,0).
  • Steady state ODE → algebra: set dv/dt=0 in the Drude-Lorentz ODE, giving a linear algebra system in (vx, vy).
  • Boundary condition for Hall setup: Jy=0 (open circuit transverse to the imposed current direction).

Step 1: Write the steady-state Drude-Lorentz equation in components

Start from (for one carrier species with charge q, mass m, relaxation time τ): 

0 = q ( E + (v × B)/c ) - (m/τ) v

With B = (0,0,B), the cross product is v×B = (vyB, −vxB, 0). Therefore, the x and y components are: 

(m/τ) v_x - (qB/c) v_y = q E_x
(m/τ) v_y + (qB/c) v_x = q E_y

This is a linear 2×2 system. Linearity is guaranteed because we kept only (i) the Lorentz force (linear in v) and (ii) linear drag −(m/τ)v.

Step 2: Solve the linear system (matrix inversion)

Write the system as A v = q E in the x–y plane: 

A = [[m/τ, -(qB/c)],
     [qB/c,  m/τ ]],
v = [v_x, v_y]^T,   E = [E_x, E_y]^T

The determinant is det(A) = (m/τ)2 + (qB/c)2. Since det(A)>0 for B≠0, A is invertible, so the solution exists and is unique (a key mathematical justification step).

The inverse is (standard 2×2 inversion): 

A^{-1} = 1/det(A) * [[m/τ,  (qB/c)],
                    [-(qB/c), m/τ]]

Thus v = A−1(qE), giving: 

v_x = q/det(A) * [ (m/τ) E_x + (qB/c) E_y ]
v_y = q/det(A) * [ -(qB/c) E_x + (m/τ) E_y ]

Step 3: Convert drift velocity to current and read off the conductivity tensor

For a species with density ns, current density contribution is Js = ns q v. Substitute v from above: 

J_x = (n_s q^2/det(A)) * [ (m/τ) E_x + (qB/c) E_y ]
J_y = (n_s q^2/det(A)) * [ -(qB/c) E_x + (m/τ) E_y ]

Identify J = σ·E, so for this species: 

σ_xx = σ_yy = (n_s q^2/det(A)) * (m/τ)
σ_xy = (n_s q^2/det(A)) * (qB/c)
σ_yx = -(n_s q^2/det(A)) * (qB/c) = -σ_xy
Optional: rewrite in terms of cyclotron frequency ωc and Q=ωcτ

Define ωc = |q|B/(mc) (always positive) and Q = ωcτ. Also note (qB/c) = (q/|q|) m ωc. Then det(A) = (m/τ)2 + (qB/c)2 = (m/τ)2(1+Q2). This yields the familiar Drude tensor scalings: σxx ~ 1/(1+Q2) and σxy ~ Q/(1+Q2) with a sign from q.

Step 4: Apply to electrons and holes, then take the strong-field limit

We have two species:

  • Electrons: density n, charge qe=−e, mass me, τe, Qe=ωc,eτe, ωc,e=eB/(mec).
  • Holes: density p, charge qh=+e, mass mh, τh, Qh=ωc,hτh, ωc,h=eB/(mhc).

In the strong-field limit Q ≫ 1, use the asymptotic expansions 1/(1+Q2) ≈ 1/Q2 and Q/(1+Q2) ≈ 1/Q. Doing this at the tensor level gives the leading behaviors: 

For each species s ∈ {e,h} (with Q_s ≫ 1):
σ_xx^(s) = σ_yy^(s) ≈ (n_s e c/B) * (1/Q_s)
σ_yx^(s) ≈ (n_s q_s c/B)   (note: q_s = ±e)
σ_xy^(s) = -σ_yx^(s)

Why these forms are valid: the exact expressions are rational functions of Q with denominators (1+Q2). For Q≫1, keeping the leading term in 1/Q (for σxy) and in 1/Q2 (for σxx) is a controlled asymptotic expansion.

(a) Compute σyx in the strong-field limit

Add electron and hole contributions (conductivities add because currents add in parallel channels): σ = σ(e) + σ(h). Using σyx(s) ≈ (ns qs c/B): 

σ_yx ≈ (n q_e c/B) + (p q_h c/B)
     = (n (-e) c/B) + (p (+e) c/B)
     = (p - n) e c/B
So, equivalently: σ_yx = (n - p) e c/B  (depending on index/sign convention)
Result (a) — as stated in the problem: 
σ_yx = (n - p) e c / B    (strong-field limit)
Note: Many texts define σxy instead; since σxy=−σyx, a sign flip is purely a convention.

(b) Hall field Ey from the condition Jy=0

The total current in y is Jy = σyxEx + σyyEy. In our isotropic setting, σyy=σxx. The Hall boundary condition is Jy=0, hence: 

0 = σ_yx E_x + σ_xx E_y
⇒ E_y = - (σ_yx/σ_xx) E_x

Now compute σxx in strong field by adding the two species: 

σ_xx ≈ (e c/B) ( n/Q_e + p/Q_h )

Substitute σyx from part (a) and σxx above: 

E_y = - (n - p) ( n/Q_e + p/Q_h )^{-1} E_x

If n=p (perfect compensation), the numerator vanishes and the Hall field is zero: Ey=0. This matches the problem statement.

(c) Effective conductivity along x under Jy=0

The x-current is Jx = σxxEx + σxyEy. Using σxy=−σyx: 

J_x = σ_xx E_x - σ_yx E_y

Substitute Ey = −(σyx/σxx)Ex: 

J_x = σ_xx E_x - σ_yx [ -(σ_yx/σ_xx) E_x ]
    = ( σ_xx + σ_yx^2/σ_xx ) E_x

Define σ_eff by J_x = σ_eff E_x:
σ_eff = σ_xx + σ_yx^2/σ_xx

Insert the strong-field expressions: σxx = (ec/B)A with A ≡ n/Qe + p/Qh, and σyx = (ec/B)(n−p). 

Let A = n/Q_e + p/Q_h.

σ_eff = (e c/B) A + [ (e c/B)^2 (n - p)^2 ] / [ (e c/B) A ]
      = (e c/B) [ A + (n - p)^2 / A ]

So:
σ_eff = (e c/B) [ (n/Q_e + p/Q_h) + (n - p)^2 (n/Q_e + p/Q_h)^{-1} ]

Final Answer (All Parts)

(Strong-field limit: Q_e = ω_{c,e} τ_e ≫ 1, Q_h = ω_{c,h} τ_h ≫ 1; B along z)

(a)  σ_yx = (n - p) e c / B

(b)  E_y = - (n - p) ( n/Q_e + p/Q_h )^{-1} E_x
     (⇒ E_y = 0 if n = p)

(c)  σ_eff = (e c/B) [ (n/Q_e + p/Q_h) + (n - p)^2 (n/Q_e + p/Q_h)^{-1} ]

where ω_{c,e} = eB/(m_e c),  ω_{c,h} = eB/(m_h c).

Sanity checks

Units / dimensions
  • Q is dimensionless by construction (ω has s−1, τ has s).
  • σyx ~ (density)·(charge)·(speed)/(field): consistent with the problem’s unit convention (c present).
  • σeff has the same units as σ because it is formed from σxx plus σyx2/σxx.
Limiting cases (B → ∞)
  • Since Q ∝ B, A = n/Qe + p/Qh ∝ 1/B.
  • First term: (ec/B)A ∝ 1/B2.
  • Second term: (ec/B)(n−p)2/A ∝ (ec/B)·(n−p)2·B = constant (if n≠p).
Physical interpretation in one sentence: In strong B, the Hall deflection is dominant; enforcing Jy=0 generates a Hall field that can convert a residual Hall imbalance (n−p) into a B-independent contribution to the longitudinal current (saturation).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpreting the final formula

Write σeff = (ec/B)[A + (n−p)2/A], with A = n/Qe + p/Qh.

  • A term: A measures how much “dissipative” longitudinal response remains for each species in strong field (since σxx is suppressed by 1/Q).
  • (n−p)2/A term: comes from eliminating Ey under Jy=0; it is a feedback term generated by the Hall field.
  • Compensation: if n=p, the feedback term vanishes identically; the remaining σ scales like 1/B2.
  • Imbalance: if n≠p, the feedback term dominates as B→∞, yielding a B-independent σeff (saturation).
Parameter dependence (qualitative)
  • Increasing τ (less scattering) increases Q, which decreases A (since A ∝ 1/Q) and thus reduces the 1/B2 term.
  • Increasing |n−p| increases the saturation term strongly (quadratically).
  • Larger effective mass m reduces ωc and Q, increasing A and making saturation weaker (since A sits in the denominator of the saturation term).

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of solving for v and then J, you can start from the known single-carrier Drude conductivity tensor in B, sum the tensors for electrons and holes, then impose Jy=0 and eliminate Ey. Mathematically this is the same because all steps are linear: σ totals by superposition, and boundary conditions are applied by solving a 2×2 linear system.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why does σxx scale like 1/Q in strong field, while σyx approaches ~1/B?
    A: σxx is the dissipative component suppressed by rapid cyclotron motion relative to scattering, giving an extra 1/Q factor; σyx is set by transverse drift and becomes geometry/Lorentz-force dominated, leaving the 1/B scaling.
  • Q: Why do conductivities add for electrons and holes?
    A: Currents add: J = Je + Jh = σeE + σhE = (σe+σh)E.
  • Q: What is the physical meaning of imposing Jy=0?
    A: It represents an open circuit in the transverse direction: charge accumulates until Ey builds up to stop net sideways current.
  • Q: If n=p, why is there no Hall field?
    A: The net Hall conductivity σyx ∝ (n−p) vanishes, so there is no need to develop Ey to enforce Jy=0.
