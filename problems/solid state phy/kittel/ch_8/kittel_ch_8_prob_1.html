Quick Summary

  • What this problem is about: hydrogen-like donor electrons in a semiconductor, where Coulomb attraction is screened by the dielectric constant and electron inertia is altered by the effective mass.
  • Key physics idea: replace the vacuum hydrogen atom by an effective hydrogen atom: \( m_0 \to m^* \), \( e^2/(4\pi\epsilon_0 r)\to e^2/(4\pi\epsilon_0\epsilon_r r)\).
  • Governing equation (eigenvalue problem): stationary Schrödinger equation with Coulomb potential: \(\left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m^*}\nabla^2 - \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\epsilon_r r}\right]\psi = E\psi\).
  • Main results: donor ionization energy \(E_D\) (effective Rydberg), orbit radius \(a^*\) (effective Bohr radius), and a concentration scale where neighboring orbits overlap.
  • Numerical outputs for InSb: \(E_D \approx 6.3\times 10^{-4}\,\text{eV} = 0.63\,\text{meV}\), \(a^*\approx 6.35\times 10^{-8}\,\text{m}=63.5\,\text{nm}\).
  • Overlap onset (order-of-magnitude): when mean spacing \(\sim 2a^*\): \(N_{D,\min}\sim 4.9\times 10^{14}\,\text{cm}^{-3}\) (and the more refined Mott criterion gives \(\sim 6\times 10^{13}\,\text{cm}^{-3}\)).
  • Physical meaning: very weak binding + very large donor orbit in narrow-gap InSb → donors ionize easily and overlap at comparatively low doping, enabling impurity-band conduction.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • εr (dimensionless): relative dielectric constant, so \(\epsilon=\epsilon_0\epsilon_r\).
  • m* (kg): conduction-band effective mass of the electron.
  • a0 (m): Bohr radius in vacuum, \(a_0=\frac{4\pi\epsilon_0\hbar^2}{m_0 e^2}\approx 5.29\times 10^{-11}\,\text{m}\).
  • Ry (eV): Rydberg energy in vacuum, \(Ry=\frac{m_0 e^4}{2(4\pi\epsilon_0)^2\hbar^2}\approx 13.6\,\text{eV}\).
  • ED (eV): donor ionization energy (binding of the 1s donor state relative to the conduction band edge).
  • a* (m): effective Bohr radius (size scale of the donor ground-state wavefunction).
  • ND (m−3 or cm−3): donor concentration; mean spacing \(\ell \sim N_D^{-1/3}\).

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Screening: a large εr weakens Coulomb attraction → smaller binding energy, larger orbit.
  • Effective mass: smaller m* increases kinetic “quantum pressure” length scale → larger orbit and smaller binding.
  • Overlap: when donor orbits extend far enough that neighboring wavefunctions are non-negligible at each other’s sites, discrete donor levels broaden into an impurity band.

Rule-of-thumb

Larger εr and smaller m* both make donors very shallow (easily ionized) and very large (overlap at relatively low doping).

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

  • Effective-mass approximation: near the band edge, electrons behave like free particles with mass m*, so the kinetic operator is \(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m^*}\nabla^2\). Valid for wavefunctions varying slowly on the lattice scale (i.e., donor orbit radius \(\gg\) lattice constant).
  • Dielectric screening: Coulomb potential is reduced by εr: \(V(r)= -\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\epsilon_r r}\). Valid when screening can be treated as static and homogeneous (low-frequency, long-wavelength).
  • Single-impurity limit: use isolated donor model for parts (a)–(b), and then compare with inter-donor spacing for (c).

Common models/approximations (and why)

Hydrogenic donor model

Treat the donor as a +e Coulomb center binding an electron in a dielectric medium with effective mass. Mathematically, it is the hydrogen atom eigenvalue problem with parameters rescaled.
  • Neglect central-cell corrections: near the impurity core the potential deviates from pure Coulomb; ignored because the orbit is large in InSb (a* tens of nm), so short-range details have small relative impact.
  • Neglect band nonparabolicity: In very narrow-gap materials (like InSb) this can matter; we still use m* given, as the problem instructs.

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • If εr doubles, Coulomb attraction halves → the binding energy drops by about a factor 4 (because \(E_D\propto 1/\epsilon_r^2\)).
  • If m* is 10× smaller, binding energy drops by 10× and orbit size grows by 10× (because \(E_D\propto m^*\), \(a^*\propto 1/m^*\)).

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Mixing SI and eV units without checking dimensions.
  • For overlap concentration: forgetting that spacing scales as \(N^{-1/3}\), not \(N^{-1}\).
  • Using ε instead of εr in the hydrogenic scaling formulas (the scaling uses the relative dielectric constant).
  • Over-interpreting part (c): “appreciable overlap” is an order-of-magnitude criterion; state the criterion you adopt.

Math Insight

The equation \(\left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m^*}\nabla^2 + V(r)\right]\psi = E\psi\) is a Sturm–Liouville / self-adjoint eigenvalue problem for the Hamiltonian operator on \(L^2(\mathbb{R}^3)\). For a central potential \(V(r)\), rotational symmetry lets you separate variables in spherical coordinates; the ground state is the spherically symmetric \(1s\) eigenfunction.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (examples)

  • Infrared detectors & imaging (InSb): doping controls carrier density, dark current, and resistivity in IR focal plane arrays.
  • High-mobility electronics / Hall sensors: shallow donors provide carriers while preserving mobility; overlap/impurity band affects low-temperature transport.
  • Quantum devices & cryogenic electronics: at low T, whether donors remain bound vs ionized impacts charge noise, freeze-out, and device stability.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: carrier concentration sets conductivity \(\sigma\), contact resistance, and detector responsivity.
  • Reliability: freeze-out at low temperature can cause gain drift or increased resistance; impurity band conduction can mitigate freeze-out but can add noise.
  • Cost & manufacturability: target doping windows must be achievable with diffusion/implantation/MBE, while maintaining uniformity across wafers.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Temperature-dependent resistivity: activation energy extraction from \(\rho(T)\) in the freeze-out regime (Arrhenius-type behavior).
  • Hall measurements: carrier density and mobility vs temperature and doping.
  • Optical spectroscopy (IR/THz): bound-to-continuum or impurity-band absorption features.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Higher doping: increases conductivity but can introduce impurity scattering → mobility loss; can create impurity bands → altered noise/absorption.
  • Non-idealities: compensation (acceptors), clustering, and inhomogeneous dopant profiles shift the real overlap threshold.
  • Model adaptation: for narrow-gap materials, nonparabolicity and many-body screening may require corrected m*(E) and ε(ω,T).
From lab to product: how the idealized model is used
Engineers often start with the hydrogenic estimate to set a first-pass doping target (binding energy and orbit size). Then, they refine using measured Hall/transport data, incorporate compensation, and use process simulations for dopant activation and diffusion. The “overlap” estimate becomes a guide for when impurity-band conduction might appear and alter low-temperature behavior.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Problem restatement (in my own words)

For indium antimonide (InSb) with band gap \(E_g = 0.23\ \text{eV}\), dielectric constant \(\epsilon_r = 18\), and electron effective mass \(m^* = 0.015 m_0\), treat a donor as a hydrogenic impurity. Find: (a) the donor ionization (binding) energy \(E_D\), (b) the radius \(a^*\) of the ground-state donor orbit, and (c) the minimum donor concentration \(N_{D,\min}\) such that neighboring donor orbits significantly overlap.

Given

  • \(E_g = 0.23\ \text{eV}\) (context: narrow gap)
  • \(\epsilon_r = 18\)
  • \(m^* = 0.015\,m_0\)
  • Constants: \(a_0 = 5.29\times 10^{-11}\ \text{m}\), \(Ry = 13.6\ \text{eV}\)

Unknowns

  • (a) \(E_D\) in eV (and meV)
  • (b) \(a^*\) in meters (and nm)
  • (c) \(N_{D,\min}\) in cm−3 (order of magnitude)

Relevant principles (why they apply)

  • Effective-mass Schrödinger equation: applies because donor electron is near the conduction band edge and can be approximated as a quasi-free particle with mass m*.
  • Electrostatics in a dielectric: Coulomb potential is reduced by εr.
  • Symmetry/separability: Coulomb potential is central (\(V=V(r)\)), enabling separation in spherical coordinates and direct mapping to hydrogen spectrum.

Note: \(E_g\) is not directly used in the hydrogenic formulas, but it signals narrow-gap physics where the given m* is very small—leading to extremely shallow donors.

Assumptions

  • Single donor (isolated impurity) for (a)–(b).
  • Static, isotropic dielectric screening characterized by εr.
  • Parabolic conduction band with constant m*.
  • Neglect central-cell corrections, many-body effects, and compensation.
  • For (c), define a clear “overlap” criterion based on mean spacing vs orbit size.

Possible approaches (compare and choose)

Approach A: Hydrogenic scaling (fast)

  • Use known hydrogen results and rescale by m* and εr.
  • Pros: minimal algebra; standard for shallow donors.
  • Cons: ignores central-cell/nonparabolic corrections.

Approach B: Full separation of variables (derivation)

  • Write Schrödinger equation in spherical coordinates, solve radial ODE.
  • Pros: shows mathematical structure explicitly.
  • Cons: longer; result identical after rescaling.

Approach C: Variational estimate (useful check)

  • Use trial wavefunction \(\psi\propto e^{-r/a}\), minimize \(\langle H\rangle\) to get \(a^*\) and \(E_D\).
  • Pros: physically transparent; generalizable.
  • Cons: approximate (but exact for Coulomb with correct form).

Choice: We use Approach A for clean results and then briefly connect to the eigenvalue structure (Approach B) so the math is fully justified. For overlap concentration, we use the mean-spacing argument and also mention the Mott criterion as a refined engineering estimate.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Set the model: Write the effective-mass Hamiltonian \(H = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m^*}\nabla^2 - \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\epsilon_r r}\) on \(\mathbb{R}^3\).
  2. Use symmetry: Central potential ⇒ separate in spherical coordinates; ground state is s-wave.
  3. Map to hydrogen: Identify how \(m^*\) and \(\epsilon_r\) rescale the Bohr radius and Rydberg energy.
  4. Compute \(E_D\): Use effective Rydberg: \(E_D = \left(\frac{m^*}{m_0}\right)\frac{1}{\epsilon_r^2}\,13.6\,\text{eV}\).
  5. Compute \(a^*\): Use effective Bohr radius: \(a^* = \epsilon_r\left(\frac{m_0}{m^*}\right)a_0\).
  6. Define overlap criterion: mean spacing \(\ell\sim N_D^{-1/3}\). Choose “appreciable overlap” when \(\ell \approx 2a^*\) (wavefunctions significantly nonzero at neighbors).
  7. Convert units: return \(N_{D,\min}\) in cm−3.
  8. Sanity checks: verify dimensions; verify expected scaling with εr and m*.

Common mistakes

  • Using \(\ell \sim 1/N_D\) instead of \(\ell\sim N_D^{-1/3}\).
  • Forgetting the square on εr in the energy scaling.
  • Mixing nm and cm when cubing lengths for concentration.
  • Not stating the chosen overlap criterion (there is no single “exact” threshold).

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

InSb has a very small effective mass (\(m^* = 0.015m_0\)) and a large dielectric constant (\(\epsilon_r=18\)). Both effects strongly reduce donor binding and greatly enlarge the donor orbit. So we expect a very small ionization energy (sub-meV) and a very large radius (tens of nm), implying overlap at relatively low donor concentrations.

Mathematical model (variables, domain, operators, boundary conditions)

Model statement

  • Unknown: donor electron wavefunction \(\psi(\mathbf{r})\), \(\mathbf{r}\in\mathbb{R}^3\).
  • Coordinate system: spherical coordinates \((r,\theta,\phi)\) due to central symmetry.
  • PDE (eigenvalue problem): \[ \left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m^*}\nabla^2 - \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\epsilon_r r}\right]\psi(\mathbf{r}) = E\,\psi(\mathbf{r}). \]
  • Boundary conditions: \(\psi\) finite at \(r=0\) and \(\psi\to 0\) as \(r\to\infty\); normalization \(\int_{\mathbb{R}^3}|\psi|^2\,d^3r=1\).
  • Operator note: \(\nabla^2=\nabla\cdot\nabla\) is the Laplacian; the Hamiltonian is linear and self-adjoint on appropriate domain → real eigenvalues, orthogonal eigenstates.

Why the hydrogenic rescaling is valid (no “magic jumps”)

The vacuum hydrogen Hamiltonian is \[ H_H = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_0}\nabla^2 - \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r}. \] The semiconductor donor Hamiltonian differs only by the replacements: \[ m_0 \to m^*, \qquad \epsilon_0 \to \epsilon_0\epsilon_r. \] Because the Schrödinger equation is linear and the Coulomb potential retains the same 1/r form, the entire eigen-spectrum keeps the hydrogen functional form but with new characteristic length and energy scales.

Nondimensionalization (scaling argument)
Choose a length scale \(a\) and define \(\mathbf{r}=a\,\mathbf{x}\). Then \(\nabla_r^2 = a^{-2}\nabla_x^2\). The Hamiltonian becomes \[ H = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m^* a^2}\nabla_x^2 - \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\epsilon_r}\frac{1}{a|\mathbf{x}|}. \] Factor out \(\frac{\hbar^2}{2m^* a^2}\): \[ H = \frac{\hbar^2}{2m^* a^2}\left[-\nabla_x^2 - \frac{2m^* a}{\hbar^2}\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\epsilon_r}\frac{1}{|\mathbf{x}|}\right]. \] Choose \(a\) so the Coulomb coefficient becomes 2 (matching hydrogen conventions): \[ \frac{2m^* a}{\hbar^2}\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\epsilon_r}=2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad a=\frac{4\pi\epsilon_0\epsilon_r\hbar^2}{m^* e^2}\equiv a^*. \] This identifies the effective Bohr radius. The corresponding energy scale is then \[ E^*=\frac{\hbar^2}{2m^*(a^*)^2}=\frac{m^* e^4}{2(4\pi\epsilon_0\epsilon_r)^2\hbar^2}, \] i.e., the effective Rydberg (binding energy scale).

(a) Donor ionization energy \(E_D\) (effective Rydberg)

Hydrogen ground-state binding energy magnitude is \(Ry = 13.6\,\text{eV}\). With the rescaling \(m_0\to m^*\) and \(\epsilon_0\to \epsilon_0\epsilon_r\), the energy scale becomes: 

E_D = (m*/m0) * (1/εr^2) * 13.6 eV

Now plug in the given numbers: 

m*/m0 = 0.015
εr^2 = 18^2 = 324
(m*/m0)/εr^2 = 0.015 / 324 = 4.6296×10^-5

E_D = 13.6 eV × 4.6296×10^-5
    = 6.296×10^-4 eV
    = 0.6296 meV

Interpretation

\(E_D \approx 0.63\) meV is extremely small. At modest temperatures, donors in InSb are easily ionized, giving free carriers.

(b) Radius of the ground-state donor orbit \(a^*\) (effective Bohr radius)

Hydrogen’s Bohr radius is \(a_0=\frac{4\pi\epsilon_0\hbar^2}{m_0 e^2}\). With the same replacements, the effective Bohr radius is: 

a* = εr * (m0/m*) * a0

Insert values: 

εr*(m0/m*) = 18 / 0.015 = 1200
a0 = 5.29×10^-11 m

a* = 1200 × 5.29×10^-11 m
   = 6.348×10^-8 m
   = 63.48 nm

Dimensional check

εr and \(m_0/m^*\) are dimensionless, so \(a^*\) has units of length (same as \(a_0\)). ✅

(c) Minimum donor concentration for appreciable overlap

Let \(N_D\) be the donor concentration. A standard spacing estimate is: 

mean spacing:  ℓ ≈ N_D^(-1/3)

“Appreciable overlap” is not a single universal number; we must choose a criterion. A simple, transparent choice is:

Overlap criterion used here

Take overlap to become appreciable when neighboring donors are separated by about two effective Bohr radii: \(\ell \approx 2a^*\). This is an order-of-magnitude condition meaning the 1s wavefunction tails are significant at the neighbor site.

Then \[ N_{D,\min} \approx \frac{1}{(2a^*)^3}. \] Convert \(a^*\) to cm to get \(N\) in cm−3: \(a^* = 63.48\,\text{nm} = 6.348\times 10^{-6}\,\text{cm}\). 

a* = 63.48 nm = 6.348×10^-6 cm
2a* = 1.2696×10^-5 cm

(2a*)^3 = (1.2696×10^-5)^3 cm^3
        ≈ 2.05×10^-15 cm^3

N_D,min ≈ 1 / (2.05×10^-15) cm^-3
       ≈ 4.9×10^14 cm^-3
Refined engineering estimate: Mott criterion (optional)
A widely used empirical criterion for the onset of impurity-band metallicity is: \[ N_c^{1/3} a^* \approx 0.25. \] Solving gives: \[ N_c \approx \left(\frac{0.25}{a^*}\right)^3. \] With \(a^* = 6.348\times 10^{-6}\,\text{cm}\), \(\frac{0.25}{a^*}\approx 3.94\times 10^4\ \text{cm}^{-1}\), so \(N_c \approx 6\times 10^{13}\ \text{cm}^{-3}\). This is lower than the \(\ell\approx 2a^*\) estimate because the numerical overlap threshold is model-dependent.

Sanity checks

  • Scaling: \(E_D \propto m^*/\epsilon_r^2\) → small m* and large εr give tiny \(E_D\). Matches expectation.
  • Magnitude: \(a^* \sim 63\) nm \(\gg\) typical lattice constants (~0.6 nm scale), supporting the effective-mass model.
  • Overlap: with \(a^*\) tens of nm, overlap can occur at \(10^{14}\)–\(10^{15}\,\text{cm}^{-3}\), which is plausibly “low” compared to many semiconductor doping levels.

Final Answer (boxed)

Given: εr = 18, m* = 0.015 m0 (InSb) (a) Donor ionization (binding) energy: E_D = (m*/m0)(1/εr^2)·13.6 eV = (0.015/18^2)·13.6 eV = 6.30×10^-4 eV ≈ 0.63 meV (b) Ground-state orbit radius (effective Bohr radius): a* = εr (m0/m*) a0 = 18/0.015 · 5.29×10^-11 m = 6.35×10^-8 m ≈ 63.5 nm (c) Minimum donor concentration for appreciable overlap (order-of-magnitude): Use mean spacing ℓ ≈ N_D^(-1/3) and overlap criterion ℓ ≈ 2a*. Then N_D,min ≈ 1/(2a*)^3 ≈ 4.9×10^14 cm^-3 (Refined optional: Mott criterion N_c^{1/3} a* ≈ 0.25 ⇒ N_c ≈ 6×10^13 cm^-3)

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpreting the formulas: what controls what?

Energy control

E_D ∝ m* / εr^2
  • Increase εr ⇒ stronger screening ⇒ much smaller binding (quadratic effect).
  • Decrease m* ⇒ electron is “lighter” ⇒ larger delocalization ⇒ smaller binding (linear effect).

Size control

a* ∝ εr / m*
  • Increase εr ⇒ larger orbit (linear).
  • Decrease m* ⇒ much larger orbit (inverse).

Parameter dependence (qualitative + quantitative)

  • If εr increases by 10%, then \(E_D\) decreases by about 19% (since \(E_D\propto 1/\epsilon_r^2\)), while \(a^*\) increases by 10%.
  • If m* increases by 20%, then \(E_D\) increases by 20% and \(a^*\) decreases by about 17% (since \(a^*\propto 1/m^*\)).
  • Overlap threshold from \(\ell\sim 2a^*\): \(N_{D,\min}\propto 1/(a^*)^3 \propto (m^*)^3/\epsilon_r^3\). So small changes in \(a^*\) produce large changes in concentration.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful): variational principle

Choose a trial ground-state wavefunction \(\psi_a(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi a^3}}e^{-r/a}\) and compute \(\langle H\rangle(a)=\langle T\rangle(a)+\langle V\rangle(a)\). Minimizing \(d\langle H\rangle/da=0\) yields the optimal \(a=a^*\) and the minimum energy \(E=-E_D\). This method is powerful when the potential is not exactly Coulombic, or when screening is more complex.

Math Insight (core equation as a mathematical object)

The donor problem is a bound-state spectral problem for a self-adjoint Hamiltonian: the Coulomb potential is long-range and attractive, producing a discrete set of negative eigenvalues (bound states) accumulating at 0 from below. Overlap between donors is mathematically like forming linear combinations of localized eigenfunctions, leading to band formation (tight-binding picture).

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: If εr doubles, what happens to \(E_D\)? A: It decreases by a factor of 4.
  • Q: If m* is halved, what happens to \(a^*\)? A: It doubles.
  • Q: Why does overlap depend on \(N_D^{-1/3}\) rather than \(N_D^{-1}\)? A: In 3D, number density scales as inverse volume; spacing is cube-root of volume per donor.
  • Q: What physical effect can overlap produce? A: An impurity band enabling hopping/impurity-band conduction.
  • Q: Why is effective-mass approximation reasonable here? A: The orbit is large (\(\sim 63\) nm), so the wavefunction varies slowly on the lattice scale.
