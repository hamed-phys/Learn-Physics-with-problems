Quick Summary

  • What this is about: Geometry of hexagonal close-packed crystals built from identical touching spheres.
  • Key physics idea: In close packing, atom centers form regular tetrahedra between stacked triangular layers.
  • Core relationship: Basal-plane lattice parameter a = 2r (nearest-neighbor distance); interlayer spacing comes from tetrahedron height.
  • Governing equation(s): Height of a regular tetrahedron of edge a is h = √(2/3)·a.
  • Result type: Symbolic (pure geometry) → c/a = √(8/3) ≈ 1.633.
  • Physical meaning: This ratio is the tightest stacking compatible with close-packed layers; larger c/a means layers are farther apart (weaker interlayer contact).
  • Engineering lens: The c/a ratio controls anisotropy in stiffness, slip, diffusion paths, and manufacturability (forming/extrusion of Mg/Ti alloys).

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions
  • hcp lattice parameters:
    • a (meters): in-plane hexagonal lattice constant (distance between equivalent sites in the basal plane).
    • c (meters): out-of-plane lattice constant (repeat distance along the hexagonal axis).
  • Atomic radius: r (meters), in the hard-sphere model.
  • Close-packed planes: dense triangular layers, often labeled A, B, C by lateral shift.
  • hcp stacking sequence: ABAB… (two-layer repeat).
Physical meaning (intuition)
  • a is essentially the nearest-neighbor spacing within a close-packed layer.
  • c measures how far you must go along the stacking direction until the pattern repeats (A returns after B).
  • The ideal c/a is set by the requirement that atoms in adjacent layers also touch (maximum packing density).

Think: stack oranges in a grocery display—within one layer they form a hexagonal pattern (sets a); the vertical rise to place the next orange into the “hollow” sets the interlayer spacing (sets c).

Key principles & validity conditions

  • Hard-sphere (contact) model: atoms are identical spheres that “touch” at nearest neighbors. Valid when you want a geometric ideal reference.
  • Close packing assumption: adjacent layers are positioned so a sphere in layer B sits in a triangular hollow of layer A and touches the three spheres below.
  • Ideal hcp: no distortion; all nearest-neighbor distances are equal.

Common models/approximations used here (and why)

  • Equal-sphere approximation: makes the lattice purely geometric → yields a universal ratio c/a independent of material chemistry.
  • Ignore bonding directionality: real crystals deviate because bonding, electron density, and temperature change equilibrium spacing.

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • 2D close packing: circles in a plane pack in a triangular lattice; nearest-neighbor center spacing equals 2r.
  • 3D stacking: the next layer sits in the “valleys” of the first; the vertical rise is less than 2r because the new sphere nestles between three below.
  • Repeat distance: ABAB means after two layer steps you are directly above an A-site again, giving c = 2 × (layer spacing).
What to watch for (pitfalls)
  • Confusing a with the hexagon “diameter.” In ideal hcp, a equals the nearest-neighbor distance in the basal plane.
  • Using the wrong repeat length: in hcp, the stacking repeat is AB, so c spans two interlayer gaps.
  • Mixing up “layer spacing” with “atomic diameter”: adjacent layers are closer than 2r because of geometric nesting.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry
  • Lightweight structural metals: Mg, Ti, Zn and their alloys have hcp or near-hcp phases; c/a influences formability.
  • Additive manufacturing & heat treatment: phase selection (hcp ↔ bcc/fcc transformations in Ti alloys) depends on lattice energetics tied to geometry.
  • Thin films & coatings: hcp textured films (e.g., Ti, Co) show anisotropic properties; knowing ideal geometry helps interpret strain and texture.
  • Battery & diffusion materials: diffusion pathways and anisotropic expansion can correlate with hexagonal-axis spacing.
Why engineers care
  • Performance: elastic modulus, yield strength, and creep can be direction-dependent in hcp crystals; c/a affects anisotropy.
  • Manufacturability: slip systems in hcp are fewer than in fcc; changing geometry (including c/a) shifts which slip modes activate during rolling/extrusion.
  • Reliability: thermal expansion anisotropy and fatigue crack growth can be texture-dependent—critical in aerospace Ti and Mg components.
  • Cost & quality: controlling texture/strain requires understanding ideal vs distorted lattice parameters.

How it’s measured/validated in practice

  • X-ray diffraction (XRD): lattice spacings → extract a and c from peak positions (Bragg’s law).
  • Electron diffraction (TEM/SAED): local lattice parameters and strain in thin foils or films.
  • Neutron diffraction: useful for bulk and light elements; good for residual strain and texture.

Design trade-offs & non-idealities

  • Temperature: thermal expansion can change c/a (often anisotropically).
  • Alloying: solutes distort the lattice; “ideal” becomes a reference baseline.
  • Stress/strain: processing induces elastic strain and defects; measured c/a can differ from equilibrium.
  • Bonding directionality: real interatomic potentials do not enforce perfect sphere contact—so many materials have c/a ≠ √(8/3).
From lab to product: how the ideal model is adapted

Engineers treat c/a = √(8/3) as a geometric benchmark for “fully close-packed” stacking. Real products use measured a and c (from diffraction), then connect deviations to composition, temperature, stress state, and texture. In simulations (e.g., crystal plasticity), the lattice geometry informs slip-plane spacings and anisotropic stiffness inputs.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Restate the problem (in plain words)

For an ideal hcp crystal modeled as identical touching spheres, show that the lattice parameter ratio c/a equals √(8/3) (about 1.633). Then explain why if c/a is much larger, the structure can be viewed as close-packed planes that are more loosely stacked.

Given
  • hcp stacking: ABAB…
  • Atoms are identical spheres in close packing (hard-sphere geometry).
  • Basal planes are close-packed triangular lattices.
Unknown / must prove
  • Derive c/a = √(8/3).
  • Interpret the statement about c/a being significantly larger.

Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Close-packing geometry: A sphere in layer B sits in a triangular hollow of layer A and touches the three spheres below.
  • Regular tetrahedron: The four sphere centers (three in A + one in B) form a tetrahedron with equal edges (all nearest neighbors touch).
  • hcp repeat distance: Since stacking repeats after AB, c spans two interlayer gaps.
Assumptions (explicit)
  • Ideal hard spheres of radius r; nearest neighbors are in contact.
  • No lattice distortion (no thermal strain, no alloying, no bonding anisotropy).
  • Perfect AB stacking with B atoms in the “hollows” of A (max density).

Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

  • Approach A (tetrahedron height): Use the fact that the centers form a regular tetrahedron of edge a. Fast, clean, minimal algebra.
  • Approach B (3D coordinates): Place three atoms in the plane at known coordinates and solve for the fourth center height by distance constraints. More general, but longer.
  • Approach C (packing fraction/volume): Use known packing density and unit cell contents to infer geometry. Indirect and easiest to mess up.

Choice: We’ll use Approach A because it directly targets c/a with the least bookkeeping and strongest geometric intuition.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

Plan (7 steps)
  1. Identify the basal-plane nearest-neighbor distance in ideal close packing. Tool: hard-sphere contact → distance = 2r.
  2. Relate that nearest-neighbor distance to the hcp lattice parameter a. Meaning: sets in-plane geometry.
  3. Form the geometric cluster: three atoms in layer A + one atom in layer B above a triangular hollow. Meaning: local stacking geometry.
  4. Recognize the four centers make a regular tetrahedron with edge a. Reason: all contacts are equal.
  5. Compute the tetrahedron height h (A→B layer spacing). Tool: tetrahedron height formula.
  6. Use ABAB periodicity to relate c to h. Meaning: unit-cell repeat along hex axis.
  7. Simplify to obtain c/a and interpret deviations. Meaning: loose vs tight stacking.
Common mistakes
  • Using c = h instead of c = 2h for hcp.
  • Forgetting that tetrahedron edge length equals a, not c.
  • Mixing the hexagon’s “width” with the lattice constant a.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

In perfect close packing, layers are as close as possible while keeping all spheres touching. So c should be fixed by geometry, and c/a should come out as a universal number (no material parameters). Since c spans two layer spacings, it should be somewhat larger than a, but not by a huge factor.

Step 1: Relate a to sphere radius r

In a close-packed (triangular) basal plane, nearest neighbors touch. If r is the sphere radius, then the center-to-center distance between touching spheres is:

a = 2r

Here we also used the fact that in ideal hcp, the basal-plane lattice parameter a equals the nearest-neighbor spacing in that plane.

Step 2: Build the A–B stacking tetrahedron

Take three spheres in layer A whose centers form an equilateral triangle of side length a. A sphere in layer B sits in the triangular hollow and touches all three below.

Therefore, the distance from the B-sphere center to each of the three A-sphere centers is also a. The four centers (3 in A + 1 in B) form a regular tetrahedron with edge length a.

Step 3: Compute the tetrahedron height h (layer spacing)

Let h be the vertical distance between the A-plane of centers and the B-sphere center above the hollow. In a regular tetrahedron of edge length a, the height is:

h = √(2/3) · a
Derivation of the tetrahedron height (no skipped steps)

The base is an equilateral triangle of side a. The projection of the top vertex onto the base lies at the triangle’s centroid. The distance from the centroid to any vertex is:

R = a/√3

(This is the circumradius of an equilateral triangle.) The edge from the top vertex to a base vertex has length a, forming a right triangle with legs h and R:

a² = h² + R² = h² + (a/√3)² = h² + a²/3

So:

h² = a² − a²/3 = (2/3)a² ⟹ h = √(2/3) · a

Physical meaning: h is the “step up” to place the next layer’s atoms into the hollows while keeping contact.

Step 4: Relate c to h using ABAB periodicity

In hcp, the stacking repeats after two layers: A → B → A. The lattice parameter c is the repeat distance along the hexagonal axis, so it spans two interlayer spacings:

c = 2h

Substitute h = √(2/3)·a:

c = 2(√(2/3)·a) = √(8/3) · a
Final Result
c/a = √(8/3) ≈ 1.633

Interpretation: This is the ideal close-packed stacking geometry. It is the ratio you get when atoms in adjacent planes also touch, maximizing packing efficiency.

Sanity checks

Dimensions

c/a is a ratio of lengths, so it must be dimensionless. √(8/3) is dimensionless → consistent.

Reasonableness

√(8/3) ≈ 1.633: c is larger than a (as expected because it spans two layer steps), but not excessively large—consistent with dense stacking.

Why “larger c/a” implies loosely stacked planes

The derivation assumes the B-layer atoms touch the three A-layer atoms below. That fixes the interlayer spacing to h = √(2/3)a. If a real crystal has c/a significantly larger than √(8/3), then the interlayer spacing is larger than the close-packed contact value. Geometrically, the basal planes can still be close-packed within each plane, but the planes are separated more than required for touching, meaning the stacking is less dense (more “open” along the hexagonal axis).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the final formula

The result c = √(8/3) · a is purely geometric. It says: once you fix the in-plane nearest-neighbor spacing (a), the ideal close-packed stacking uniquely fixes the out-of-plane repeat (c).

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • If you scale the atomic size (increase r), then a = 2r scales linearly, and c scales linearly too. The ratio stays constant.
  • If bonding/temperature causes the interlayer spacing to expand more than the in-plane spacing, then c/a increases → planes become relatively more separated.
  • If the structure compresses along the hex axis (e.g., under uniaxial stress), c/a decreases → closer stacking along c.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

A coordinate method: place three A-layer centers at (0,0,0), (a,0,0), (a/2, (√3/2)a, 0). The B-layer center lies above the centroid at (a/2, (√3/6)a, h). Enforce equal distance a from the B center to each A center, solve for h, then set c = 2h. This reproduces c/a = √(8/3).

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why is the tetrahedron “regular”? A: Because the B-sphere touches all three A-spheres, so all center-to-center distances among nearest neighbors equal a.
  • Q: Why is c = 2h in hcp? A: The stacking repeats after two layers (ABAB…), so the unit cell must include A→B→A, i.e., two interlayer gaps.
  • Q: If c/a is larger than √(8/3), are the planes still close-packed? A: They can be close-packed within each plane, but the planes are farther apart than the close-packed contact distance.
  • Q: Does √(8/3) depend on the element? A: No—only on the ideal hard-sphere geometry. Real elements can deviate due to bonding and temperature.
Extra note: why real materials deviate from the ideal value

The hard-sphere model enforces “touching” constraints. Real atoms interact via interatomic potentials and electron density distributions, so the equilibrium spacing can differ in-plane vs out-of-plane. That’s why measured c/a values for hcp metals are often near, but not exactly equal to √(8/3). Engineers use the ideal value as a reference for interpreting anisotropic bonding, alloying effects, and thermal strains.

Summary: In ideal hcp close packing, the A–B layer geometry forms a regular tetrahedron of edge a. The interlayer spacing is the tetrahedron height h = √(2/3)·a, and the hcp repeat distance is c = 2h, giving c/a = √(8/3) ≈ 1.633. If c/a is larger, planes remain close-packed in-plane but are separated more along the hex axis—i.e., loosely stacked.
