Quick Summary

  • Goal: Find the linear electron density (electrons per unit length) at which the transverse subband (2,2) in a square GaAs quantum wire is first populated at T = 0.
  • Key physics: Quantum confinement in the cross-section quantizes transverse motion into subbands; along the wire electrons behave as a 1D free Fermi gas in each subband.
  • Governing eigenproblem: 2D infinite square well (hard-wall) for the transverse wavefunction: -(ħ²/2m*)∇⊥² ψ = E⊥ ψ with ψ=0 on the boundary.
  • Subband energies: E(nₓ,nᵧ)= (ħ²π²/2m*W²)(nₓ²+nᵧ²) for width W in both x and y.
  • Filling rule at T=0: In each occupied subband i, states fill up to k_F,i where E_F = E_i + ħ²k_F,i²/(2m*).
  • Threshold condition: The (2,2) subband begins to populate when E_F = E(2,2).
  • Main result (closed form): n₁D,th = (2/W)(√6 + 2√3) (includes spin degeneracy, GaAs single valley).
  • Numeric for W=20 nm: n₁D,th ≈ 5.91×10⁸ m⁻¹ (≈ 5.91×10⁶ cm⁻¹, ≈ 591 μm⁻¹).
  • Interesting feature: The threshold density is independent of effective mass m* here because we set E_F equal to another hard-wall subband edge, causing m* to cancel analytically.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • W — wire width in x and y (m). Here W = 20 nm = 20×10−9 m.
  • m* — electron effective mass in GaAs (kg). (Used in eigenenergies; cancels in the final threshold density.)
  • ħ — reduced Planck constant (J·s).
  • ψ(x,y) — transverse wavefunction (m−1 for 2D normalization).
  • E⊥ — transverse (subband-edge) energy (J or eV).
  • k — longitudinal wavevector along wire axis z (m−1).
  • E_F — Fermi energy at T=0 (J or eV).
  • n₁D — linear electron density (m−1): electrons per unit length.

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Confinement in the cross-section forces standing waves → discrete transverse energies subbands.
  • Along the wire, electrons move freely → each subband behaves like a 1D free-electron band starting at its edge energy.
  • At T=0, electrons fill all available states up to E_F; higher subbands remain empty until E_F reaches their edges.

Key laws/principles + validity conditions

  • Effective-mass Schrödinger model: valid when the electron’s de Broglie wavelength is large compared with the lattice spacing and band nonparabolicity is negligible.
  • Hard-wall (infinite) confinement: approximates very large band offsets and abrupt interfaces; good for a clean conceptual threshold estimate.
  • Non-interacting Fermi gas at T=0: assumes weak interactions and negligible disorder broadening (or that we seek the ideal threshold).

Common models/approximations used and why

  • Infinite square well in x and y: yields analytic eigenenergies and clear subband ordering.
  • Parabolic dispersion along z: E = E⊥ + ħ²k²/(2m*).
  • Spin degeneracy gs=2, and for GaAs conduction band single valley (gv=1) in the simplest model.

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • If you halve W, confinement energies scale like 1/W², so subbands spread apart dramatically.
  • For a fixed E_F, tighter confinement means fewer subbands are accessible → more strongly 1D transport.
  • At T=0, each occupied subband contributes a 1D density proportional to its available k-range: larger (E_F - E_i) → larger k_F → larger density.
What to watch for (pitfalls):
  • Confusing 2D sheet density with 1D linear density.
  • Forgetting that degenerate subbands like (1,2) and (2,1) count as two separate modes.
  • Missing the factor of 2 from spin degeneracy in 1D density-of-states counting.
  • Mixing boundary conditions: hard-wall means ψ=0 at boundaries.
Math Insight

The transverse confinement is a Sturm–Liouville eigenvalue problem for the Laplacian: the operator -∇⊥² on a square domain with Dirichlet boundary conditions has discrete eigenpairs (ψ_n, λ_n). The subband edges are proportional to the eigenvalues: E⊥ = (ħ²/2m*) λ, while longitudinal motion simply adds a continuous k² term.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (examples)

  • Nanoscale FET channels (III–V or hybrid): subband occupancy affects mobility, threshold voltage, and scattering.
  • Quantum point contacts / nanowires in sensors: conductance steps depend on the number of occupied 1D modes.
  • High-frequency electronics (mmWave/THz): subband structure impacts carrier transport and dynamic response.
  • Spintronics / mesoscopic devices: mode count influences spin injection efficiency and magnetoresistance signatures.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: more occupied subbands → more conduction channels (higher conductance), but also more inter-subband scattering.
  • Reliability: confinement sensitivity to width variation means process tolerances can shift subband thresholds.
  • Cost & manufacturability: tight lithography control (few nm) becomes critical when W ~ 20 nm.
  • Efficiency: controlling mode occupancy can reduce parasitic capacitances and unwanted leakage paths.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Low-temperature transport: observe conductance quantization (steps of ~2e²/h per mode) as gate voltage sweeps occupancy.
  • Magnetotransport (Shubnikov–de Haas / subband spectroscopy): infer subband spacings from oscillations and depopulation fields.
  • Capacitance/charge sensing: extract density vs gate bias and identify kinks at subband thresholds.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Non-ideal confinement: finite barriers, interface roughness, and strain modify the simple infinite-well energies.
  • Effective mass variation: in heterostructures, m* can be position-dependent; strict cancellation shown here may not hold.
  • Disorder & temperature: subband edges broaden; “first populated” becomes a crossover rather than a sharp point.
From lab to product: how the ideal model is adapted

In real GaAs/AlGaAs or InGaAs systems, confinement is usually finite, often approximately triangular under gate fields. Engineers typically replace the hard-wall square well with a self-consistent Schrödinger–Poisson calculation: [-(ħ²/2)∇·(m*⁻¹∇) + V_conf(x,y) + eφ(x,y)]ψ = Eψ, coupled to Poisson’s equation for φ. The present hard-wall calculation is still valuable as a quick, explainable order-of-magnitude and scaling estimate.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement (in my own words)

We have a square GaAs quantum wire whose cross-section is a square of side W = 20 nm. The electron is confined by an infinite potential barrier at the wire boundary (hard wall). At T = 0, electrons fill available quantum states up to the Fermi energy. Find the linear electron density n₁D at which the transverse subband labeled by (nₓ,nᵧ)=(2,2) is just starting to be occupied.

Given
  • Square wire width: W = 20 nm
  • Material: GaAs (use effective-mass model)
  • Boundary: infinite confinement (Dirichlet ψ=0)
  • Equilibrium, T=0
Unknown / required
  • Threshold linear density n₁D,th where (2,2) is first populated
  • Give in closed form and numeric value

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Quantum confinement eigenmodes: cross-section is a square → separable Schrödinger equation with standing-wave solutions.
  • Fermi filling at T=0: occupation is 1 below E_F and 0 above; threshold means E_F equals the (2,2) subband edge.
  • 1D density counting: for each occupied subband, the number of states per length is controlled by the k-space interval [-k_F, +k_F].
Assumptions (explicit)
  • Non-interacting electrons; no exchange-correlation corrections to E_F.
  • Single conduction valley (GaAs) and spin degeneracy g_s=2.
  • Perfect square cross-section, width exactly W.
  • Infinite confinement: potential is 0 inside, ∞ outside.
  • Long wire: treat z-motion as free and continuous.

Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

  • Approach A (best): Solve the 2D infinite-well eigenenergies, then use 1D Fermi counting per subband and apply the threshold condition E_F=E(2,2).
    Pros: direct, analytic, minimal ambiguity. Cons: assumes ideal hard walls.
  • Approach B: Use 1D density of states formula and integrate up to E_F for each subband.
    Pros: generalizes to finite temperature. Cons: more steps; same physics.
  • Approach C: Start from phase-space counting (k-space volume per length) and sum modes.
    Pros: very physical. Cons: easy to miss degeneracy factors.

We choose Approach A because the problem explicitly specifies an infinite confinement potential, making the eigenenergies exactly solvable and the threshold condition clean.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

Plan (5–12 steps)

  1. Model the wire: define coordinates with z along the wire and (x,y) across the square cross-section.
  2. Write the transverse Schrödinger eigenproblem: use ∇⊥² with Dirichlet boundary conditions at x=0,W and y=0,W.
  3. Solve by separability: set ψ(x,y)=X(x)Y(y) and obtain quantized energies indexed by nₓ,nᵧ.
  4. Write total dispersion: E = E(nₓ,nᵧ) + ħ²k²/(2m*).
  5. Impose threshold condition: “(2,2) first populated” means E_F = E(2,2).
  6. List occupied subbands just below threshold: all (nₓ,nᵧ) with E(nₓ,nᵧ) < E(2,2).
  7. Compute each subband’s k-range: k_F,i = sqrt(2m*(E_F - E_i))/ħ.
  8. Convert k-range to 1D density: with spin degeneracy, n_i = (2/π)k_F,i.
  9. Sum densities: n₁D = Σ_i n_i, carefully counting degeneracies.
  10. Plug in W=20 nm and compute the numeric threshold density; then check units and limiting behavior.
Common mistakes (quick fixes)
  • Forgetting degeneracy: (1,2) and (2,1) are distinct subbands; include both.
  • Dropping spin factor: for electrons, gs=2 unless explicitly lifted.
  • Wrong density formula: in 1D at T=0, per subband n = (2/π)k_F (not proportional to k2).
  • Unit mismatch: W must be in meters for n in m−1.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition before calculating

As we increase the linear electron density, the Fermi energy E_F rises. Initially only the lowest transverse mode (1,1) is occupied. When E_F reaches the next subband edge, new 1D conduction channels open. The (2,2) mode is higher in energy than (1,2)/(2,1), so by the time (2,2) begins to fill, three lower subbands should already contribute to the density: (1,1), (1,2), (2,1).

Step 1: Mathematical model (variables, domain, PDE, boundary conditions)

Let z be the wire axis (free motion), and (x,y) span the square cross-section: 0 < x < W, 0 < y < W.

Inside the wire, the confining potential is V(x,y)=0, and outside it is infinite. Thus the transverse wavefunction must satisfy Dirichlet boundary conditions: ψ(0,y)=ψ(W,y)=ψ(x,0)=ψ(x,W)=0. 

Transverse eigenproblem (effective-mass Schrödinger equation):
-(ħ²/2m*) ∇⊥² ψ(x,y) = E⊥ ψ(x,y),    on 0

This is valid because the Hamiltonian is separable: the operator ∇⊥² is linear, the domain is a rectangle, and the boundary conditions are homogeneous and separable. Therefore, a product ansatz is justified.

Step 2: Solve by separability (showing the algebra)

Assume a separable solution ψ(x,y)=X(x)Y(y). Substitute into the PDE: 

-(ħ²/2m*) (X''(x)Y(y) + X(x)Y''(y)) = E⊥ X(x)Y(y)

Divide by X(x)Y(y) (allowed where ψ≠0; eigenfunctions are nontrivial):
-(ħ²/2m*) (X''/X + Y''/Y) = E⊥

Rearranging gives X''/X + Y''/Y = -(2m*E⊥/ħ²). The left side is a sum of a function of x only and a function of y only, so each must equal a constant (separation of variables): 

Let X''/X = -kₓ² and Y''/Y = -kᵧ², with kₓ² + kᵧ² = 2m*E⊥/ħ².

Then:
X'' + kₓ² X = 0,   with X(0)=X(W)=0
Y'' + kᵧ² Y = 0,   with Y(0)=Y(W)=0

These are standard 1D infinite-well problems. Nontrivial solutions exist only for kₓ = nₓπ/W, kᵧ = nᵧπ/W with integers nₓ,nᵧ = 1,2,3,....

Step 3: Transverse energies (subband edges)

Using kₓ²+kᵧ² = 2m*E⊥/ħ²: 

E(nₓ,nᵧ) = E⊥ = (ħ²/2m*) (kₓ² + kᵧ²)
        = (ħ²/2m*) [ (nₓπ/W)² + (nᵧπ/W)² ]
        = (ħ²π² / (2m*W²)) (nₓ² + nᵧ²).

Define a convenient energy scale: E0 = ħ²π²/(2m*W²) so E(nₓ,nᵧ)=E0(nₓ²+nᵧ²).

Step 4: Add longitudinal motion (dispersion in each subband)

Along z the electron is free, so the total energy is 

E(nₓ,nᵧ; k) = E(nₓ,nᵧ) + ħ²k²/(2m*),
where k is the longitudinal wavevector (units m⁻¹).

Step 5: Threshold condition for first population of (2,2)

“First populated” at T=0 means the Fermi energy is exactly at the subband edge: 

Threshold condition:
E_F = E(2,2) = E0(2² + 2²) = 8E0.

Step 6: Identify which subbands are occupied just below the threshold

Any subband with E(nₓ,nᵧ) < E_F = 8E0 is occupied. List by nₓ²+nᵧ²:

Subband ordering (square well)
  • (1,1): 1²+1²=2 → energy 2E0
  • (1,2) and (2,1): 1²+2²=5 → energy 5E0 (two distinct subbands)
  • (2,2): 2²+2²=8 → energy 8E0 (threshold)
  • (1,3)/(3,1): sum=10 > 8 → not yet occupied at threshold

Therefore, at the instant (2,2) begins to fill, the occupied subbands are: (1,1), (1,2), (2,1).

Step 7: 1D Fermi wavevectors in each occupied subband at threshold

For a given occupied subband i with edge energy E_i, the highest occupied longitudinal k satisfies: E_F = E_i + ħ²k_F,i²/(2m*). So, 

k_F,i = sqrt( 2m*(E_F - E_i) ) / ħ  ,   valid only if E_F ≥ E_i.

Now compute energy differences using E_F = 8E0: 

E_F - E(1,1) = 8E0 - 2E0 = 6E0
E_F - E(1,2) = 8E0 - 5E0 = 3E0
E_F - E(2,1) = 8E0 - 5E0 = 3E0

Substitute E0 = ħ²π²/(2m*W²) into k_F. Watch the cancellation carefully: 

For (1,1):
k_F,11 = sqrt( 2m*(6E0) ) / ħ
       = sqrt( 2m*6 * [ħ²π²/(2m*W²)] ) / ħ
       = sqrt( 6 * ħ²π²/W² ) / ħ
       = (π/W) √6.

For (1,2) and (2,1):
k_F,12 = k_F,21 = sqrt( 2m*(3E0) ) / ħ
                 = (π/W) √3.
Key observation: Because E0 ∝ 1/m*, the factor m* cancels inside the square root when evaluating k_F at a fixed multiple of E0. That is why the final threshold density depends only on W here.

Step 8: Convert k-space filling to linear density per subband

In 1D with a long wire (periodic boundary conditions over length L), allowed k values are spaced by 2π/L. Counting states from -k_F to +k_F gives, per subband: 

Number of k-states (per spin) in [-k_F, +k_F]:
N = (L/(2π)) · (2k_F) = L k_F/π

Including spin degeneracy g_s=2:
N_total = 2 L k_F/π

So the linear density in that subband is:
n_i = N_total/L = (2/π) k_F,i.

Step 9: Sum over occupied subbands at threshold (include degeneracy)

At threshold, occupied subbands are (1,1), (1,2), (2,1). Thus: 

n₁D,th = (2/π)(k_F,11 + k_F,12 + k_F,21)
       = (2/π)[ (π/W)√6 + (π/W)√3 + (π/W)√3 ]
       = (2/W)( √6 + 2√3 ).

Final numeric evaluation for W = 20 nm

Convert W to meters: W = 20 nm = 20×10⁻⁹ m. Compute the bracket: √6 ≈ 2.44949, √3 ≈ 1.73205, so √6 + 2√3 ≈ 2.44949 + 3.46410 = 5.91359. 

FINAL ANSWER (T=0, hard-wall square W×W, spin-degenerate, single valley):

n₁D,th = (2/W)( √6 + 2√3 )

For W = 20 nm:
n₁D,th ≈ (2 / (20×10⁻⁹ m)) · 5.91359
       ≈ 5.91×10⁸ m⁻¹
       ≈ 5.91×10⁶ cm⁻¹
       ≈ 591 μm⁻¹.

Sanity checks

Units / dimensions
  • n₁D,th = (2/W)(dimensionless) → units are 1/m. ✅
  • k_F came out as (π/W)×(dimensionless) → units 1/m. ✅
Limiting behavior
  • If W increases, confinement weakens, so subband edges get closer and the threshold density should drop: n₁D,th ∝ 1/W. ✅
  • Tighter wire (smaller W) needs larger density to raise E_F up to E(2,2). ✅
Physical interpretation (plain language): At about 5.9×10⁸ electrons per meter of wire length, the Fermi level has risen enough that the (2,2) transverse standing-wave pattern becomes energetically accessible, opening an additional 1D conduction channel.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpreting the final formula

The threshold density n₁D,th = (2/W)(√6 + 2√3) can be read as:

  • (2/W) sets the overall scale: 1D density is essentially “how many half-wavelengths fit across the width.”
  • √6 comes from how far below the threshold the (1,1) edge lies: E_F − E(1,1) = 6E0.
  • 2√3 accounts for the two degenerate subbands (1,2) and (2,1), each with E_F − E = 3E0.

Parameter dependence (qualitative + useful quantitative)

  • Width W: n₁D,th ∝ 1/W. Doubling W halves the threshold linear density.
  • Effective mass m*: cancels in this specific hard-wall threshold-to-threshold comparison. In more realistic confinement (finite barriers, electrostatic potentials), m* generally affects the result.
  • Degeneracy factors: If valley degeneracy existed (not typical for GaAs conduction band), multiply by gv. Spin splitting (strong B field) would reduce the factor 2.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of counting k-states directly, you can use the 1D density of states per subband: D_i(E) = (g_s/π) sqrt(m*/(2ħ²)) · (E − E_i)^(-1/2) for E>E_i. Then integrate n_i = ∫_{E_i}^{E_F} D_i(E) dE, which yields n_i = (2/π)k_F,i again. Same physics, different mathematical route.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why do (1,2) and (2,1) both contribute at threshold?
    A: They are distinct eigenstates with the same energy in a square well, so both sets of 1D k-states fill.
  • Q: Why is the 1D density proportional to kF rather than kF2?
    A: Because in 1D the number of states grows linearly with the length of the k-interval.
  • Q: What changes if temperature is nonzero?
    A: Occupation becomes Fermi–Dirac smeared; “first populated” becomes gradual and depends on kBT relative to subband spacing.
  • Q: What is the mathematical role of the boundary condition ψ=0?
    A: It makes the Laplacian eigenproblem have discrete sine eigenfunctions and quantized k-values nπ/W.
