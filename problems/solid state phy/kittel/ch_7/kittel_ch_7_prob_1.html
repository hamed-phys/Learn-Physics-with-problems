Free-Electron Energies in Square & Simple-Cubic Lattices: Zone Corners vs Face Midpoints

We use the dispersion relation of a free electron to compare kinetic energies at special points of the first Brillouin zone and interpret what this implies for the conductivity of divalent metals in a lattice context.

Quick Summary

  • What this problem is about: Compare free-electron kinetic energies at special points of the first Brillouin zone for square (2D) and simple-cubic (3D) lattices.
  • Key physics idea: For a free electron, energy depends only on the magnitude of the wavevector: E ∝ |k|². So the ratio of energies is the ratio of |k|².
  • Governing equation: E(k) = ħ²|k|² / (2m), with Brillouin-zone boundaries at k_i = ±π/a.
  • 2D result: At a zone corner (π/a, π/a) vs side midpoint (π/a, 0), the kinetic energy ratio is 2.
  • 3D result: At a zone corner (π/a, π/a, π/a) vs face center (π/a, 0, 0), the kinetic energy ratio is 3.
  • Physical meaning: Corners are “farther” in k-space, so free-electron energies are higher there.
  • Conductivity bearing (qualitative): Divalent metals often place the Fermi level near Brillouin-zone boundaries, where Bragg scattering opens gaps and increases effective mass / reduces velocity, tending to lower conductivity and increase anisotropy—especially if a band becomes nearly full.
  • Mathematical theme: This is a dispersion relation problem: geometry in reciprocal space controls energy through a quadratic form in k.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • a — lattice constant (m)
  • k — crystal wavevector (m−1)
  • ħ — reduced Planck constant (J·s)
  • m — electron mass (kg)
  • E(k) — electron energy (J or eV)
  • G — reciprocal lattice vector (m−1)
  • 1st Brillouin zone — Wigner–Seitz cell in reciprocal space (k-space)

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • k encodes the electron’s de Broglie wavelength and momentum: p = ħk.
  • For a free electron, energy is purely kinetic: quadratic in momentum → quadratic in |k|.
  • The Brillouin-zone boundary marks where Bragg reflection by the lattice becomes important in a periodic potential (nearly-free electron picture).
Key law / model: The free-electron Hamiltonian is 
H = - (ħ²/2m) ∇²
ψ_k(r) = V^(-1/2) e^{i k·r}
E(k) = ħ² |k|² / (2m)
Validity: electrons treated as free (periodic potential neglected) or as the starting point for the nearly-free-electron approximation.

What a Brillouin zone is (and why ±π/a appears)

For a simple square (2D) or simple-cubic (3D) lattice with real-space spacing a, the reciprocal lattice has primitive vectors of magnitude 2π/a. The first Brillouin zone is the set of k points closer to k=0 than to any other reciprocal-lattice point. For these lattices, it becomes a square (2D) or cube (3D) with boundary planes at: 

k_x ∈ [-π/a, +π/a]
k_y ∈ [-π/a, +π/a]
(3D also: k_z ∈ [-π/a, +π/a])

Common approximations and why used

  • Free-electron model: set periodic potential V(r)=0 → simplest dispersion E ∝ k².
  • Nearly-free-electron model: treat V(r) as a small perturbation; gaps open at zone boundaries due to coupling of k and k±G.
  • Effective mass approximation: near an extremum, E(k) becomes approximately quadratic with an effective mass tensor.
Watch for: When comparing energies, the prefactor ħ²/2m cancels; only |k|² matters.
Mini intuition examples (quick)

(1) If you double |k|, energy quadruples: E ∝ |k|².
(2) Points on a zone face (e.g., (π/a,0,0)) are closer to the origin than zone corners (e.g., (π/a,π/a,π/a)), so they have lower free-electron energy.
(3) In nearly-free electrons, the zone boundary is where a small periodic potential can strongly modify motion (Bragg reflection), which matters for transport.

What to watch for (pitfalls):
  • Confusing the real-space unit cell with the reciprocal-space Brillouin zone.
  • Using 2π/a instead of π/a for the zone boundary (the boundary is halfway to the nearest reciprocal-lattice point).
  • Comparing |k| rather than |k|² (energy scales with the square).
Math Insight (core equation as a mathematical object): The dispersion E(k)=ħ²|k|²/(2m) is a quadratic form in k (a paraboloid in 2D, a paraboloid hypersurface in 3D). Comparing energies at symmetry points reduces to comparing Euclidean norms in k-space: a geometry problem embedded in an eigenvalue problem of the Laplacian.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this shows up in industry (examples)

  • Metals & alloys design: conductivity, resistivity, and their temperature dependence track band structure near the Fermi level.
  • Semiconductor process engineering: understanding zone boundaries and gaps is foundational for doping, mobility, and device performance.
  • Thin films & interconnects (microelectronics): electron mean free path, effective mass, and anisotropy affect line resistance and reliability (electromigration).
  • Materials selection in power systems: choosing conductors with stable transport under thermal cycling relies on electronic structure.

Why industrial engineers care

  • Performance: higher conductivity lowers I²R losses and heat generation.
  • Reliability: electronic transport influences Joule heating, thermal budgets, and failure rates.
  • Cost/efficiency: alloying decisions balance conductivity, strength, corrosion resistance, and manufacturability.
  • Scalability: in nanostructures, boundary scattering and band effects can dominate bulk intuition.

How it is measured/validated (brief)

  • Electrical resistivity via four-point probe; temperature-dependent curves reveal scattering regimes.
  • Hall effect to estimate carrier density and sign; mobility connects to effective mass and scattering.
  • Photoemission (ARPES) in research to map E(k) and Fermi surfaces directly.
  • Quantum oscillations (de Haas–van Alphen / Shubnikov–de Haas) to infer Fermi-surface geometry in clean samples.

Design trade-offs & non-idealities

  • Real crystals: defects, impurities, grain boundaries add scattering beyond “free electron” motion.
  • Band gaps at zone boundaries: even small periodic potentials modify transport near the Fermi level.
  • Texture & anisotropy: processing can orient grains, changing effective conductivity.
  • Thermal expansion: changes a, shifting zone boundaries and subtly affecting electronic structure.
From lab to product: adapting the ideal model

Engineers rarely use a pure free-electron model alone. Instead, it becomes a baseline: (i) incorporate periodic potential → nearly-free electrons and gaps, (ii) include scattering (phonons, impurities), (iii) fold in microstructure (grain size, film thickness) for effective transport, and (iv) validate with resistivity/Hall data. The “zone geometry” part of this problem is still valuable because it predicts where band effects become important: at the boundaries.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Restate the problem (in plain words)

For electrons treated as free (so E ∝ |k|²), compare the kinetic energy at: (a) a corner of the first Brillouin zone vs the midpoint of a zone side for a 2D square lattice, (b) the analogous comparison for a 3D simple-cubic lattice, and (c) explain what the 3D result suggests about the conductivity of divalent metals.

Given quantities

  • Lattice type: square (2D) and simple cubic (3D)
  • Lattice constant: a (symbolic)
  • Free-electron dispersion: E(k)=ħ²|k|²/(2m)
  • First BZ boundaries: k_i = ±π/a

Unknowns / what must be found

  • (a) Energy ratio E_corner / E_side-midpoint in 2D
  • (b) Energy ratio E_corner / E_face-center in 3D
  • (c) Qualitative implication for divalent-metal conductivity (band filling & zone-boundary effects)
Relevant principles (and why they apply):
  • Eigenvalue problem of the Laplacian: free-particle Schrödinger equation in k-space yields E ∝ k².
  • Reciprocal-space symmetry: zone corner/face midpoint are symmetry points in the first BZ; compare their |k|.
  • Nearly-free electron insight (for part c): band gaps open at zone boundaries; transport is sensitive to whether the Fermi level lies near such boundaries.
Other laws (e.g., Maxwell’s equations) are not central because the question is purely about single-electron band geometry and kinetic energy.
Explicit assumptions:
  • Free-electron (or baseline of nearly-free electron): periodic potential neglected for parts (a) and (b).
  • First Brillouin zone for square/cubic has boundaries at ±π/a.
  • Spin degeneracy does not affect energy ratios (it affects filling and conductivity discussion in part (c)).

Approach options (2–3) and comparison

  • Geometry in k-space (best): compute |k| at the specified points; ratio of energies is ratio of |k|². Fast and transparent.
  • Reciprocal lattice / Wigner–Seitz construction: derive the BZ boundaries and points first, then proceed. More foundational, slightly longer.
  • Nearly-free electron perturbation viewpoint: discuss coupling at boundaries and consequences for conductivity. Necessary mainly for (c), not for ratios (a,b).

Chosen approach

Use k-space geometry plus the free-electron dispersion to get (a) and (b) cleanly. Then use the nearly-free-electron interpretation (gaps near zone boundaries, band filling for divalent metals) for (c).

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Qualitative expectation (before calculating): Zone corners are farther from k=0 than face midpoints, so |k| is larger at corners. Since E ∝ |k|², the corner energy should be larger by a factor equal to the number of nonzero components (2 in 2D, 3 in 3D).

Mathematical model (variables, domain, coordinates, conditions)

We treat the electron as free inside a crystalline volume V (conceptually with periodic boundary conditions). The quantum model is the time-independent Schrödinger eigenvalue problem: 

H ψ = E ψ
H = - (ħ²/2m) ∇²
r ∈ ℝ^d,  d = 2 or 3
ψ_k(r) = V^(-1/2) e^{i k·r}

Applying the Laplacian to a plane wave gives ∇² e^{i k·r} = -|k|² e^{i k·r}. By linearity of ∇² and the eigenfunction property, this yields the dispersion relation: 

E(k) = (ħ² / 2m) |k|²
|k|² = k_x² + k_y²        (2D)
|k|² = k_x² + k_y² + k_z² (3D)

Boundary/initial conditions: For bulk crystals, we use periodic boundary conditions (Born–von Kármán) to discretize k, but the ratios here depend only on geometry and remain the same in the continuum limit.

Dimensional analysis check:
[ħ²|k|²/(2m)] = (J²·s²)(m⁻²)/kg = (kg²·m⁴/s²)(m⁻²)/kg = kg·m²/s² = J, correct for energy.

(a) 2D square lattice: corner vs midpoint of a side

For a square lattice (real-space spacing a), the first Brillouin zone is the square: k_x, k_y ∈ [-π/a, +π/a].

Identify the k-points

  • Corner of 1st BZ: choose k_c = (π/a, π/a) (any sign combination is equivalent by symmetry).
  • Midpoint of a side (zone face in 2D): choose k_m = (π/a, 0) (or (0, π/a)).

Compute |k|² at each point

|k_c|² = (π/a)² + (π/a)² = 2(π/a)²
|k_m|² = (π/a)² + 0²      =  (π/a)²

Since E(k) = (ħ²/2m)|k|², the prefactor cancels in the ratio: 

(a)  E_corner / E_mid
   = [ (ħ²/2m) |k_c|² ] / [ (ħ²/2m) |k_m|² ]
   = |k_c|² / |k_m|²
   = 2(π/a)² / (π/a)²
   = 2

(b) 3D simple-cubic lattice: corner vs midpoint of a face

For a simple-cubic lattice, the first Brillouin zone is a cube: k_x, k_y, k_z ∈ [-π/a, +π/a].

Identify the k-points

  • Corner of 1st BZ: choose k_c = (π/a, π/a, π/a).
  • Midpoint of a face (face center): choose k_f = (π/a, 0, 0) (or permutations).

Compute |k|² at each point

|k_c|² = (π/a)² + (π/a)² + (π/a)² = 3(π/a)²
|k_f|² = (π/a)² + 0² + 0²          =  (π/a)²

Again, the prefactor cancels: 

(b)  E_corner / E_face-center
   = |k_c|² / |k_f|²
   = 3(π/a)² / (π/a)²
   = 3

(c) Bearing on conductivity of divalent metals (qualitative but physics-grounded)

Here we step slightly beyond the “free” model: conductivity depends strongly on states near the Fermi energy, and in a crystal those states are shaped by zone-boundary physics (Bragg scattering and band gaps).

Key transport link (applied-physics view): In semiclassical transport, electron group velocity is v(k) = (1/ħ) ∇_k E(k). For a free electron, E=(ħ²/2m)k² so v = ħk/m. Near zone boundaries in a nearly-free electron model, band curvature can flatten (larger effective mass), reducing velocities and mobility.
Brief formal structure (nearly-free electron, why zone boundaries matter)

In a periodic potential V(r), Bloch states couple k and k±G. At Brillouin-zone boundaries, the free-electron energies of such coupled states become degenerate, and even a small V_G opens a gap. Mathematically this is a small eigenvalue-splitting problem in a 2×2 subspace (degenerate perturbation theory).

Now interpret the geometric result from (b): the free-electron energy at corners is 3× that at face centers. That means, as energy increases from the zone center outward, electrons reach the faces of the BZ before they reach the corners. For a given electron density, the Fermi surface will therefore tend to encounter face boundary planes earlier than it reaches corners.

Conductivity bearing for divalent metals: Divalent metals contribute roughly 2 conduction electrons per atom. In many lattice/band pictures, that places the Fermi level relatively high in energy, often near zone boundaries, where band gaps and strong Bragg scattering can occur. The practical implications are:
  • More band-structure influence on transport: states near the Fermi level can be strongly modified at zone faces (flattening bands → larger effective mass).
  • Potential reduction in conductivity (relative to an ideal free-electron metal): reduced group velocity and enhanced scattering near boundaries can lower mobility.
  • Anisotropy and “necking” of the Fermi surface: the Fermi surface may touch faces and form features that make conductivity direction-dependent.
  • Extreme idealized case (simple cubic, one-atom basis): a completely filled first band would produce an insulating tendency (no available states for acceleration), illustrating why band filling matters as much as carrier count.

Important nuance: real divalent metals (e.g., Mg, Ca) are not simple-cubic at ambient conditions and have complex band structures; the question asks for the qualitative “bearing” suggested by the zone-geometry result, not a universal quantitative rule.

Final Answers (boxed) 
(a) Square lattice (2D):   E_corner / E_side-midpoint = 2
(b) Simple cubic (3D):     E_corner / E_face-center   = 3
(c) Bearing (qualitative): Divalent metals often place EF nearer Brillouin-zone
    boundaries; Bragg scattering/gaps and altered curvature (larger effective mass)
    can reduce conductivity and increase anisotropy (especially as a band nears full).
Sanity checks
  • Units: Ratios are dimensionless, as expected.
  • Limiting-case logic: In d dimensions, corner vs face-center ratio becomes d (2D→2, 3D→3), matching the idea “number of nonzero components.”
  • Symmetry: Signs of components do not matter because energy depends on k².

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the final formulas

The result is fundamentally geometric: corners have multiple components at the boundary value π/a. Because |k|² sums the squares of components, each additional nonzero component adds another (π/a)². 

If k_corner = (π/a, …, π/a)  (d components)
and  k_face   = (π/a, 0, …, 0),
then |k_corner|² = d(π/a)² and |k_face|² = (π/a)²
⇒ E_corner / E_face = d.

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Lattice constant a: sets the k-scale (π/a). Larger a shrinks the BZ, lowering boundary energies as 1/a².
  • Mass m: energy scale is ∝ 1/m; heavier effective mass lowers energies for a given k.
  • Dimension d: corner/face ratio equals d in this symmetric case, reflecting how many components are “maxed out.”

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of “plugging points,” you can view the free-electron energy as proportional to the squared distance from the origin in k-space. The midpoint of a face is distance π/a; the corner is distance √d · π/a. Squaring the distance immediately yields the ratio d.

Concept check (self-test with answers)
  • Q: Why doesn’t the factor ħ²/2m matter for parts (a) and (b)?
    A: It cancels in the ratio; only |k|² matters.
  • Q: In 2D, what is the energy ratio of a corner to the zone center?
    A: Zone center has |k|=0 so energy is 0; ratio is not meaningful (division by zero). Energy difference is meaningful: it equals the corner energy.
  • Q: Why are zone boundaries special in real crystals?
    A: They are where Bragg reflection makes k and k−G states degenerate, so a periodic potential opens a band gap.
  • Q: Does “higher electron count” always mean “higher conductivity”?
    A: Not necessarily; if the Fermi level lies near a gap or band curvature flattens (large effective mass), mobility can drop and conductivity can decrease.
