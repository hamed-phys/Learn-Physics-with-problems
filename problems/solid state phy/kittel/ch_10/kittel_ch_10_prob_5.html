London Penetration Depth: From the London Equation to the Supercurrent “Acceleration” Law

We derive (a) the London time-evolution relation between current density and electric field and (b) the standard formula for the London penetration depth λL in terms of carrier density and effective mass—using mathematically explicit steps and the Maxwell–London framework.

Quick Summary

  • What this is about: Connecting the London equations to how supercurrents respond to fields and how magnetic fields penetrate superconductors.
  • Key physics idea: In a superconductor, the supercurrent responds to electromagnetic fields in a way that implies nondissipative acceleration and magnetic field expulsion (Meissner effect).
  • Governing relations: London (curl) equation and Faraday’s law: ∇×j = −(c/4πλL2) B and ∇×E = −(1/c) ∂B/∂t.
  • Part (a) result: London acceleration equation (Gaussian-cgs): ∂j/∂t = (c2/4πλL2) E (under standard superconducting assumptions).
  • Part (b) result: Using carrier dynamics m dv/dt = qE and j = nqv, obtain: λL2 = m c2 / (4π n q2).
  • Interpretation: Larger carrier density n or charge magnitude |q| → smaller λL (better screening / shallower field penetration).
  • Result type: Purely symbolic; no numbers needed.

Math Insight (core equation as a mathematical object)

The London curl equation plus Maxwell’s equations form a linear PDE system. In static situations it reduces to a Helmholtz-type equation for magnetic field inside a superconductor: ∇2B = B/λL2, whose solutions decay exponentially over the length scale λL.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (THEORY BEFORE SOLVING)

Core definitions (symbols + units)

  • j = current density (Gaussian-cgs: statA/cm2; SI: A/m2).
  • E = electric field (Gaussian-cgs: statV/cm; SI: V/m).
  • B = magnetic flux density / magnetic induction (Gaussian-cgs: gauss; SI: tesla).
  • λL = London penetration depth (length; SI: m).
  • c = speed of light (cm/s in cgs; m/s in SI).
  • n = number density of superconducting carriers (cm−3 or m−3).
  • q = carrier charge (e.g., −e for electrons, −2e for Cooper pairs).
  • m = (effective) carrier mass (or pair mass).
  • v = drift/superfluid velocity of carriers.

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • λL sets how deeply static magnetic fields can penetrate before being exponentially suppressed.
  • London theory treats the superconducting component as a rigid, nondissipative fluid.
  • The “acceleration” relation ∂j/∂t ∝ E says an electric field causes the supercurrent to build up without resistive damping (contrast Ohm’s law j=σE).

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

London electrodynamics (phenomenological model)

The London equations are linear constitutive relations relating electromagnetic fields to supercurrent. A common form (Gaussian-cgs) is the curl London equation: 

∇×j = -(c / (4π λ_L^2)) B

Validity: low-frequency/long-wavelength electrodynamics of a superconductor; linear response; uniform λL; neglects normal-fluid dissipation and nonlocal effects (Pippard) and strong spatial inhomogeneity.

Common models/approximations (and why)

  • Single-fluid London model: only superconducting carriers contribute; simplest to capture Meissner screening.
  • Local electrodynamics: j(r) depends on fields at the same r (no nonlocal kernel); good when mean free path ≪ coherence length scale for local response assumptions.
  • Charge neutrality in bulk: suppresses large internal electrostatic fields; helps eliminate gradient “gauge” terms when integrating curl relations.

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • Apply E briefly: In a perfect superconductor, a pulse of E increases j; after E is removed, j persists (no resistance).
  • Apply B statically: B decays from the surface into the bulk roughly as exp(−x/λL).

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Units: the given target formulas use Gaussian-cgs conventions (4π and c appear). In SI, 4π is replaced by μ0.
  • “Take time derivative” is not enough alone—you must use Faraday’s law to swap ∂B/∂t for ∇×E.
  • Curl equality implies vector equality only up to a gradient field; you need a physical/mathematical condition to set that gradient to zero.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (examples)

  • MRI/NMR magnets: field stability and screening currents depend on superconducting electrodynamics.
  • Superconducting RF cavities & accelerators: surface currents and penetration depth influence RF losses and quench margins.
  • Quantum tech (qubits, resonators): λL impacts kinetic inductance, impedance, and device frequency.
  • Power devices (fault current limiters, cables): transient fields induce supercurrents; penetration depth affects AC performance.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: smaller λL usually means better field exclusion and lower kinetic inductance.
  • Reliability/safety: current crowding near surfaces can trigger quench if local heating exceeds cooling.
  • Cost/manufacturability: material purity, thickness, and multilayer stacks change effective penetration and losses.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Mutual inductance / resonator shifts: infer λL from changes in inductance/frequency.
  • Microwave surface impedance: λL relates to reactive (inductive) response and penetration of RF fields.
  • Magnetometry (SQUID/VSM): measure screening (Meissner) response vs geometry and temperature.

Design trade-offs and constraints (real materials)

  • Non-idealities: impurities, grain boundaries, thin-film roughness → effective λ increases and losses rise.
  • Two-fluid behavior: at finite temperature, a normal component adds dissipation; London is the low-loss ideal baseline.
  • Geometry & thickness: when film thickness ≲ λL, currents spread across thickness, changing inductance and AC response.
From lab to product: how the ideal London model is adapted

In products, λL is often treated as an effective parameter that absorbs microstructure, temperature dependence, and multilayer geometry. Engineers pair London-type formulas with empirical calibration (e.g., fitting resonator data) and safety margins for quench, vibration, and thermal gradients.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Problem restated (in plain words)

You are given a London equation (labeled “(10)” in your text). By differentiating it in time and using Maxwell’s equations, show that the supercurrent density responds to the electric field via ∂j/∂t = (c2/4πλL2) E. Then, by modeling superconducting carriers as free charges with equation of motion m dv/dt = qE, deduce the standard expression for the London penetration depth: λL2 = m c2 / (4π n q2).

Given

  • London equation (10) (used in curl form): ∇×j = −(c/4πλL2) B.
  • Maxwell–Faraday law: ∇×E = −(1/c) ∂B/∂t.
  • Carrier dynamics for free carriers: m dv/dt = qE.
  • Current relation: j = n q v (superfluid drift).

Unknowns / to prove

  • (a) Prove: ∂j/∂t = (c2/4πλL2) E.
  • (b) Prove: λL2 = m c2 / (4π n q2).

Mathematical model (variables, domain, operators, conditions)

Model statement

  • Fields: j(r,t), E(r,t), B(r,t) defined in a superconducting region Ω ⊂ ℝ3.
  • Operators: ∇× (curl), ∇· (divergence), and time derivative ∂/∂t.
  • Constitutive law: local London relation (linear, homogeneous) connecting ∇×j to B.
  • Maxwell law used: Faraday’s law (curl E linked to ∂B/∂t).
  • Boundary/initial conditions (implicit/typical): fields finite in the bulk; no singular sources inside Ω; in steady bulk, no macroscopic charge accumulation (supports setting gradient terms to zero, explained later).

Relevant principles (why they apply)

  • Linearity: London equation is linear; time derivative commutes with ∇× for smooth fields.
  • Faraday’s law: provides the required link between ∂B/∂t and ∇×E.
  • Microscopic carrier dynamics: m dv/dt = qE gives a direct expression for ∂j/∂t via j = nqv.

Assumptions (explicit)

Assumptions used to get the exact vector equality in (a)

  • λL is spatially uniform (constant) over the region considered.
  • The superconducting region is simply connected and the physically relevant solution has no arbitrary “gradient” additions that would represent electrostatic potential-driven ambiguities.
  • Bulk charge neutrality/no sustained charge buildup: eliminates extra ∇φ terms when integrating curl relations.

Possible approaches (compare)

Approach A: Curl + Faraday (most direct)

Differentiate ∇×j relation, substitute ∂B/∂t from ∇×E, then infer ∂j/∂t ∝ E.

Pros: exactly matches problem statement. Cons: must justify dropping gradient term.

Approach B: Microscopic equation first

Use m dv/dt = qE and j = nqv to get ∂j/∂t = (nq2/m)E, then connect to λL.

Pros: intuitive. Cons: needs link back to London parameterization.

Chosen approach: Use Approach A for (a) (as requested), then Approach B for (b) by comparing coefficients.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Write the London curl equation and identify constants; goal: relate j to B.
  2. Differentiate both sides in time; tool: linearity and commutation of ∂/∂t with ∇×.
  3. Use Faraday’s law to replace ∂B/∂t with −c(∇×E); goal: introduce E.
  4. Simplify to an equality of curls: ∇×(∂j/∂t) = (c2/4πλL2) ∇×E.
  5. Infer vector relation up to a gradient: ∂j/∂t − (c2/4πλL2)E = ∇φ; then justify φ = const (so ∇φ=0) under superconducting bulk assumptions.
  6. For part (b): start from m dv/dt = qE and j = nqv.
  7. Differentiate j to get ∂j/∂t = (nq2/m)E.
  8. Compare coefficients with (a) to solve for λL2.
  9. Sanity checks: units/dimensions; parameter dependence.

Common mistakes

  • Forgetting Faraday’s law and trying to “differentiate B into E” without Maxwell.
  • Dropping the curl operator incorrectly (curl equality implies vector equality only up to ∇φ).
  • Mixing SI and cgs constants: in SI, the coefficient involves μ0 instead of 4π/c2.

PART 3 — Full Solution (DETAILED + TEACHING)

Physical intuition (before calculating)

In a superconductor, the charge carriers can move without resistive dissipation. So an applied electric field does not produce a steady Ohmic current j=σE; instead, it accelerates the superfluid: current builds up over time while the field acts. That intuition suggests a relation of the form ∂j/∂t ∝ E. The London equations quantify this and tie it to magnetic screening through λL.

(a) Differentiate the London equation to obtain ∂j/∂t = (c²/4πλL²)E

Starting point: London curl equation (Gaussian-cgs)

We assume the London equation (10) is in the standard curl form:

∇×j(r,t) = -(c / (4π λ_L^2)) B(r,t)

Here λL is treated as constant (homogeneous superconductor), so it can be pulled through derivatives.

Step 1: Take the time derivative of both sides

Apply ∂/∂t to the entire equation. Because the curl operator acts only on spatial coordinates, for sufficiently smooth fields we use the commutation relation: ∂/∂t (∇×j) = ∇×(∂j/∂t). This is a standard property of linear differential operators. 

∂/∂t [∇×j] = ∂/∂t [-(c / (4π λ_L^2)) B]
⇒ ∇×(∂j/∂t) = -(c / (4π λ_L^2)) ∂B/∂t

Step 2: Use Faraday’s law to replace ∂B/∂t

Maxwell–Faraday law in Gaussian-cgs is: 

∇×E = -(1/c) ∂B/∂t
⇒ ∂B/∂t = -c (∇×E)

Substitute this into the previous result: 

∇×(∂j/∂t) = -(c / (4π λ_L^2)) [ -c (∇×E) ]
= (c^2 / (4π λ_L^2)) (∇×E)

Step 3: Convert “equality of curls” into “equality of vectors” (with justification)

From ∇×(∂j/∂t) = (c2/4πλL2) ∇×E, subtract the right-hand side to obtain: 

∇×[ ∂j/∂t - (c^2 / (4π λ_L^2)) E ] = 0

A vector field with zero curl is (locally) the gradient of a scalar potential: if ∇×F = 0 in a simply connected region, then F = ∇φ. Therefore, 

∂j/∂t - (c^2 / (4π λ_L^2)) E = ∇φ

Why can we set ∇φ = 0 in the standard London result?

The London “acceleration equation” is usually stated for the superconducting response where no additional conservative (pure-gradient) term is present. Physically, in a bulk superconductor, sustained charge separation is strongly suppressed (quasi-neutrality), so macroscopic electrostatic potential gradients inside the bulk are not maintained in the ideal model. Mathematically, one fixes this freedom by choosing boundary/initial conditions consistent with no internal charge buildup, leading to φ = const and hence ∇φ = 0.

With that standard London choice (or in scenarios where the relevant E is the nonconservative, inductive field), we obtain the desired relation:

Result (a): London acceleration equation (Gaussian-cgs)

∂j/∂t = (c^2 / (4π λ_L^2)) E

Sanity check for (a): dimensions (quick)

In Gaussian-cgs, the combination c2/λL2 has units of 1/time2 times length2/length2, and with the electromagnetic unit conventions the prefactor matches the required “acceleration-like” mapping from E to ∂j/∂t. The presence of 4π signals cgs conventions. (In SI the analogous coefficient is 1/(μ0 λL2).)

(b) Use m dv/dt = qE to show λL² = mc²/(4πnq²)

Step 1: Relate current density to carrier velocity

For a carrier number density n and carrier charge q, the current density is (superfluid drift model): 

j = n q v

Step 2: Differentiate in time

Assuming n is constant in time (no macroscopic creation/annihilation of supercarriers during the process), we differentiate: 

∂j/∂t = n q ∂v/∂t

Step 3: Use the carrier equation of motion

The problem states the (free-carrier) dynamical law: 

m dv/dt = q E
⇒ dv/dt = (q/m) E

Substitute into ∂j/∂t: 

∂j/∂t = n q (q/m) E = (n q^2 / m) E

Step 4: Compare with result (a) to solve for λL

From part (a), we also have: ∂j/∂t = (c2/4πλL2) E. Since both expressions hold for the same E, equate coefficients: 

(n q^2 / m) = (c^2 / (4π λ_L^2))

Solve for λL2: 

λ_L^2 = (m c^2) / (4π n q^2)

Final result (b): London penetration depth (Gaussian-cgs)

λ_L^2 = (m c^2) / (4π n q^2)

(SI note) The corresponding SI expression is: λ_L^2 = m / (μ_0 n q^2).

Final Answer (copy-ready)

(a) From ∇×j = -(c/(4πλ_L^2))B and ∇×E = -(1/c)∂B/∂t:
    ∇×(∂j/∂t) = (c^2/(4πλ_L^2))∇×E
    ⇒ ∂j/∂t = (c^2/(4πλ_L^2))E   (with the standard London choice eliminating ∇φ)

(b) With j = nqv and m dv/dt = qE:
    ∂j/∂t = nq dv/dt = (nq^2/m)E
    Compare with (a) ⇒ λ_L^2 = (m c^2)/(4π n q^2).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (THEORY AROUND THE RESULT)

Re-interpreting the final formula

The expression λL2 = (m c2)/(4π n q2) shows λL is a collective electromagnetic length scale: it is smaller when the superfluid is “stiffer” electromagnetically (large n, large |q|) and larger when carriers are heavier (large m).

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • n ↑ ⇒ λL ↓ (stronger screening; fields die out faster).
  • |q| ↑ ⇒ λL ↓ (charge couples more strongly to E/B).
  • m ↑ ⇒ λL ↑ (heavier carriers accelerate less; weaker current response).
  • c appears due to electromagnetic unit conventions in cgs (and relativistic structure of Maxwell equations).

Why the math structure matters

The curl-based London equation is designed to enforce magnetic screening. Combined with Maxwell’s equations, it produces a linear, homogeneous operator equation that yields exponential decay solutions (a hallmark of Helmholtz operators).

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Starting from the (often-quoted) “first London equation” directly, ∂j/∂t = (nq2/m)E, and combining with Ampère’s law (in magnetostatics, ∇×B = (4π/c)j), you can derive a Helmholtz equation for B in the static limit: ∇×(∇×B) = (4π/c)∇×j = -(1/λL2)B which (with ∇·B=0) reduces to ∇2B = B/λL2. This exposes λL as the decay length for B.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why doesn’t a superconductor obey Ohm’s law for the supercurrent?
    A: Because the superfluid component is nondissipative; E causes acceleration (∂j/∂t ∝ E), not a steady resistive proportionality (j ∝ E).
  • Q: Where does Faraday’s law enter in part (a)?
    A: It converts ∂B/∂t into −c(∇×E), allowing E to appear after differentiating the London equation.
  • Q: Why is there a potential ambiguity (∇φ) after equating curls?
    A: Because curl(F)=0 implies F is a gradient; fixing φ requires physical boundary/neutrality conditions.
  • Q: If n increases by 4× (with m and q fixed), what happens to λL?
    A: Since λL ∝ 1/√n, it decreases by a factor of 2.
