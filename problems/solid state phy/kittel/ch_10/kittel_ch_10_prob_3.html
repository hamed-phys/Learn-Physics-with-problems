Two-Fluid Model of a Superconductor: Dispersion of Electromagnetic Waves

We derive the wave dispersion relation inside a superconductor using Maxwell’s equations plus the two-fluid current (normal Ohmic + superconducting London response), and we explain why, at low frequencies, the superfluid “short-circuits” the normal electrons.

Quick Summary

  • What this is about: Electromagnetic wave propagation in a superconductor using the two-fluid model (normal + superfluid currents).
  • Key physics idea: The superfluid obeys the London equation, producing the Meissner effect (field expulsion) and an effective inductive response; the normal fluid produces Ohmic loss.
  • Governing laws: Maxwell’s equations + constitutive current model j = j_n + j_s with j_n = σ0 E and London relation for j_s.
  • Mathematical object: A linear PDE eigenmode problem; plane-wave ansatz turns it into a dispersion relation for k(ω).
  • Main result (CGS): k^2 c^2 = 4π σ0 i ω − c^2/λ_L^2 + ω^2.
  • Main result (SI): k^2 c^2 = (σ0/ε0) i ω − c^2/λ_L^2 + ω^2.
  • Physical meaning: The term −c^2/λ_L^2 makes k largely imaginary at low ω → fields decay over the London penetration depth; the i ω term gives small attenuation from normal electrons.
  • Low-frequency insight: For ω ≪ 1/τ, the superfluid behaves like a huge imaginary conductivity σ_s(ω) ~ i/(μ0 ω λ_L^2) and short-circuits the normal current.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • E electric field (V/m), B magnetic flux density (T), H magnetic field (A/m).
  • j current density (A/m²) with two-fluid split: j = j_n + j_s.
  • σ0 (real) normal conductivity (S/m), reduced at T by reduced normal carrier density n_n(T).
  • λL London penetration depth (m): sets exponential decay length of static B inside a superconductor.
  • n_s, n_n superfluid and normal-fluid electron concentrations (m⁻³), with n_s + n_n ≈ n.
  • τ normal-electron relaxation time (s) (Drude scattering time).
  • ω angular frequency (rad/s), k wavevector (1/m), c speed of light (m/s).

0.2 Physical meaning of key quantities

  • σ0: dissipative channel; converts EM energy to heat (Joule loss).
  • λL: reactive (inductive) channel; encodes how the superfluid screens magnetic fields (Meissner effect).
  • Two-fluid picture: at 0 < T < T_c, part of electrons behave as a coherent condensate (no DC resistance), the remainder behave as a normal metal.

0.3 Key laws / principles and validity conditions

Validity Linear response + local electrodynamics
  • We assume linear constitutive relations: j_n = σ0 E and London equation for j_s.
  • We neglect thermal/quantum nonlocality (no Pippard kernel), i.e. local relation between fields and currents.
  • We neglect dielectric polarization beyond vacuum constants (as in the problem statement).
  • We work with harmonic fields ~ exp(i k·r − i ω t); boundary conditions are not needed for the bulk dispersion.

0.4 Common models/approximations in this topic

  • Ohm’s law (normal fluid): j_n = σ(ω) E, with Drude σ(ω)=σ0/(1−iωτ). In part (a) the problem uses the low-ω Ohmic form σ(ω)≈σ0.
  • London model (superfluid): either ∂j_s/∂t = (n_s e^2/m) E (first London equation), or equivalently ∇×j_s = − (n_s e^2/m) B (second London equation, SI).
  • Plane-wave eigenmodes: In homogeneous media, linear PDEs allow Fourier modes; each mode gives an algebraic dispersion relation.

0.5 Mini intuition examples (quick)

  • Static magnetic field: In a superconductor, B(x) ~ e^{−x/λ_L}. That scale will reappear inside the wave dispersion as c^2/λ_L^2.
  • Very low-frequency AC: The superfluid current is approximately 90° out of phase with E (inductive), so it carries current with minimal dissipation.
  • Normal metal limit: If n_s → 0, then λ_L → ∞ and you recover the usual conducting-medium dispersion with an i ω σ term.

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Sign conventions: Using e^{i(k·r − ωt)} implies ∂/∂t → −iω and ∇ → i k.
  • Vector identity: ∇×(∇×B) = ∇(∇·B) − ∇²B = −∇²B because ∇·B=0.
  • Which London equation to use: For dispersion, the curl form is especially convenient because it directly relates ∇×j_s to B.
  • Units: The CGS vs SI prefactors differ; keep μ0, ε0, 4π, c consistent.
Math Insight Dispersion relation as an eigenvalue condition

Maxwell + constitutive laws form a linear operator equation on fields (a coupled PDE system). In a uniform medium, Fourier modes diagonalize the differential operators: ∇ → i k. The dispersion relation is the condition for a nontrivial solution (an “eigenvalue equation” for k(ω)).

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Why engineers care

  • RF/microwave superconducting circuits: resonators and filters (e.g., low-loss front ends, quantum circuits) depend on penetration depth and residual loss.
  • Superconducting magnets: transient fields during ramping induce AC losses; understanding field diffusion/penetration helps manage cryogenic load.
  • Power devices & fault current limiters: time-varying currents/fields couple into superconductors; dispersion/attenuation informs stability and protection design.

Measurement & validation (brief)

  • Surface impedance measurements (microwave cavity perturbation, resonator Q) infer complex conductivity and λL.
  • Mutual inductance / AC susceptibility probes penetration depth and superfluid density vs temperature.
  • Time-domain transient tests (pulse fields) assess dynamic screening and loss (important for magnets and rotating machinery).

Design trade-offs & constraints

  • Loss vs temperature margin: lower T increases ns (smaller λL) and reduces nn, but increases cryocooler cost/complexity.
  • Geometry & thickness: films thinner than λL behave differently (kinetic inductance dominates), affecting device impedance and speed.
  • Material non-idealities: impurities, granularity, and vortex motion add extra dissipation beyond the ideal two-fluid picture.

From lab to product

Real designs often replace the simple σ0 + London model by a complex conductivity σ(ω,T) = σ1(ω,T) − i σ2(ω,T) (from Mattis–Bardeen or measured data), then compute propagation/impedance with EM solvers. The derivation here is the “clean-room” baseline that explains why an i/ω inductive response and λL set the fundamental scales.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

1.1 Restate the problem (in plain words)

In the two-fluid model for a superconductor (for 0 < T < T_c), the current density is the sum of a normal Ohmic current j_n = σ0 E and a superconducting current j_s governed by the London equation. (a) Starting from Maxwell’s equations, derive the dispersion relation between wavevector k and frequency ω for electromagnetic waves inside the superconductor (CGS and SI forms). (b) Using σ0 = n_n e^2 τ/m and assuming ω ≪ 1/τ, argue that the dispersion does not depend importantly on the normal electrons: the supercurrent effectively short-circuits them, and electron motion is described mainly by the London equation. Also note the frequency regime ω ≪ ω_p.

1.2 Given quantities

  • Two-fluid current split: j = j_n + j_s.
  • Normal current: j_n = σ0 E.
  • London equation for j_s (implies London penetration depth λL depends on ns).
  • Vector identity reminder: ∇×(∇×B) = −∇²B (since ∇·B=0).
  • For part (b): σ0 = n_n e^2 τ/m, and frequencies satisfy ω ≪ 1/τ and ω ≪ ω_p.

1.3 Unknowns / what must be found

  • Dispersion relation k(ω) in CGS and SI.
  • Physical/mathematical explanation for why the normal-fluid contribution becomes negligible at low ω (short-circuiting by superfluid).

1.4 Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Maxwell’s equations: govern E and B for any EM wave.
  • Constitutive current model: closes the system by linking current to fields (Ohm + London).
  • Linearity: allows Fourier mode decomposition; each plane wave solves a linear algebraic system.

1.5 Assumptions (explicit)

Assumptions What we neglect and why
  • Homogeneous, isotropic superconductor (no spatial variation of material parameters).
  • No free charge accumulation in the bulk for the wave mode considered (transverse EM wave).
  • Local London electrodynamics; neglect nonlocality and vortex physics.
  • Normal current uses constant σ0 for part (a) (valid when ωτ ≪ 1).

1.6 Candidate approaches (compare 2–3)

  • Approach A (curl-curl on B): Take curl of Ampère’s law, use ∇×(∇×B) identity, express ∇×j via Ohm + London. Pros: direct, matches problem hint. Cons: must track SI/CGS factors carefully.
  • Approach B (wave equation for E): Eliminate B using Faraday, build wave equation in E with constitutive current. Pros: also standard; Cons: London curl form couples more naturally to B.
  • Approach C (complex conductivity): Combine normal + superfluid into an effective complex σ(ω), then use known conducting-medium dispersion. Pros: compact; Cons: hides the London physics unless derived.

Chosen approach: Approach A, because the problem explicitly reminds the curl-curl identity and London naturally supplies ∇×j_s in terms of B.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

Plan (8 steps)

  1. Set the model: Write j = σ0 E + j_s and choose the London relation needed (∇×j_s vs B).
  2. Start from Ampère–Maxwell: Use the correct form (CGS or SI).
  3. Take curl of Ampère: Convert to ∇×(∇×B) and apply the identity using ∇·B=0.
  4. Replace curl E: Use Faraday’s law to express ∇×E in terms of ∂B/∂t.
  5. Compute curl j: Split into σ0 ∇×E plus ∇×j_s, then use London for the latter.
  6. Assume plane waves: Insert B ~ e^{i(k·r − ωt)} so ∇² → −k² and ∂/∂t → −iω.
  7. Read off dispersion: Collect terms to obtain k² as a function of ω and parameters.
  8. Low-ω argument: Express σ0 via Drude (n_n, τ) and compare magnitude of the normal term to the London (superfluid) term.
Common mistakes Quick tips
  • Dropping ∇·B=0 too late/early: you need it to replace curl-curl by −∇².
  • Mixing CGS/SI factors: in CGS Faraday has 1/c; in SI it does not.
  • Forgetting that σ_s(ω) is imaginary and scales like 1/ω, which is the key to “short-circuiting”.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

In a superconductor, magnetic fields are expelled from the bulk (Meissner effect), so we expect EM waves at low frequency to be strongly attenuated (evanescent) with a characteristic decay length near λL. Normal electrons add a small dissipative correction (an imaginary term in the dispersion) that produces additional attenuation/phase shift.

3.2 Mathematical model (explicit)

Model Variables, domain, operators
  • Fields: E(r,t), B(r,t) in an infinite homogeneous medium (bulk superconductor).
  • Current: j(r,t) = j_n + j_s with j_n = σ0 E.
  • London (curl form):
    • CGS: ∇×j_s = −(c/4πλ_L^2) B
    • SI: ∇×j_s = −(1/μ0λ_L^2) B
  • Harmonic plane-wave ansatz (eigenmodes): E,B ∝ exp(i k·r − i ω t).
  • Operator replacements for the ansatz: ∇ → i k, ∇² → −k², ∂/∂t → −iω.

3.3 Derivation in CGS (step-by-step)

Step 1 — Start with Maxwell (CGS):

∇×B = (4π/c) j + (1/c) ∂E/∂t ∇×E = −(1/c) ∂B/∂t ∇·B = 0

Step 2 — Take curl of Ampère–Maxwell:

∇×(∇×B) = (4π/c) ∇×j + (1/c) ∂/∂t (∇×E)

Step 3 — Use vector identity and ∇·B = 0:

∇×(∇×B) = ∇(∇·B) − ∇²B = −∇²B (since ∇·B = 0)

Step 4 — Substitute Faraday for ∇×E:

∇×E = −(1/c) ∂B/∂t ⇒ (1/c) ∂/∂t (∇×E) = −(1/c^2) ∂^2 B/∂t^2

Step 5 — Compute ∇×j using the two-fluid model:

j = σ0 E + j_s ⇒ ∇×j = σ0 (∇×E) + ∇×j_s

Step 6 — Use London (curl form) for the supercurrent:

∇×j_s = −(c/4πλ_L^2) B

Step 7 — Assemble the wave equation for B (time-domain):

−∇²B = (4π/c)[ σ0 ( −(1/c) ∂B/∂t ) − (c/4πλ_L^2) B ] − (1/c^2) ∂^2 B/∂t^2

Step 8 — Plane-wave substitution and algebra:

With B ∝ e^{i k·r − i ω t}: −∇²B = k²B, ∂B/∂t = −iωB, ∂²B/∂t² = −ω²B ⇒ k²B = (4πσ0 iω / c²) B − (1/λ_L²) B + (ω²/c²) B ⇒ k² c² = 4π σ0 i ω − c²/λ_L² + ω²
Final (CGS) Dispersion relation
k² c² = 4π σ0 i ω − c²/λ_L² + ω²

3.4 Derivation in SI (compact but explicit)

∇×B = μ0 j + μ0ε0 ∂E/∂t ∇×E = − ∂B/∂t ∇·B = 0
Take curl of Ampère: −∇²B = μ0 ∇×j + μ0ε0 ∂/∂t(∇×E) Use j = σ0 E + j_s: ∇×j = σ0 ∇×E + ∇×j_s Use Faraday: ∇×E = −∂B/∂t Use London (SI): ∇×j_s = −(1/μ0λ_L²) B ⇒ −∇²B = μ0[ σ0(−∂B/∂t) − (1/μ0λ_L²)B ] + μ0ε0 ∂/∂t(−∂B/∂t) ⇒ −∇²B = −μ0σ0 ∂B/∂t − (1/λ_L²)B − μ0ε0 ∂²B/∂t² Plane wave B ∝ e^{i k·r − i ω t}: −∇²B = k²B, ∂B/∂t = −iωB, ∂²B/∂t² = −ω²B ⇒ k² = iμ0σ0 ω − 1/λ_L² + μ0ε0 ω² Using c² = 1/(μ0ε0): ⇒ k² c² = (σ0/ε0) i ω − c²/λ_L² + ω²
Final (SI) Dispersion relation
k² c² = (σ0/ε0) i ω − c²/λ_L² + ω²

3.5 London depth in terms of ns (as requested)

(SI) λ_L² = m/(μ0 n_s e²) ⇒ c²/λ_L² = (1/ε0)(n_s e²/m)
(CGS) λ_L² = m c²/(4π n_s e²) ⇒ c²/λ_L² = 4π(n_s e²/m)

3.6 Part (b): show normal electrons become unimportant for ω ≪ 1/τ

Use SI for clarity (CGS follows identically in dimensionless ratios).

Dispersion: k²c² = ω² − c²/λ_L² + iω(σ0/ε0) With σ0 = n_n e² τ/m and c²/λ_L² = (1/ε0)(n_s e²/m): Ratio of normal term to London term: |iω(σ0/ε0)| / (c²/λ_L²) = [ω (σ0/ε0)] / [(1/ε0)(n_s e²/m)] = ω (n_n e² τ/m) / (n_s e²/m) = (n_n/n_s)(ωτ) ≪ 1 when ωτ ≪ 1
Equivalent “short-circuit” view via effective complex conductivity
London (time domain): ∂j_s/∂t = (n_s e²/m) E Harmonic fields (∝ e^{-iωt}): (−iω) j_s = (n_s e²/m) E ⇒ j_s = i(n_s e²/mω) E So σ_s(ω) = i(n_s e²/mω) = i/(μ0 ω λ_L²) Compare to σ0 = n_n e² τ/m: |σ_s|/σ0 = (n_s/n_n)(1/ωτ) ≫ 1 when ωτ ≪ 1 ⇒ supercurrent dominates and effectively short-circuits normal current.

The condition ω ≪ ωp (plasma frequency) ensures we are far below the regime where collective charge oscillations dominate and where additional dispersion physics (plasmons, nonlocality) must be included.

Final answer (plain text) Copy-ready
(a) Dispersion in a two-fluid superconductor (j = σ0 E + j_s, London for j_s): CGS: k^2 c^2 = 4π σ0 i ω − c^2/λ_L^2 + ω^2 SI: k^2 c^2 = (σ0/ε0) i ω − c^2/λ_L^2 + ω^2 with London depth using superfluid density n_s: SI: λ_L^2 = m/(μ0 n_s e^2) (CGS: λ_L^2 = m c^2/(4π n_s e^2)). (b) With σ0 = n_n e^2 τ/m and ωτ ≪ 1: | iω(σ0/ε0) | / (c^2/λ_L^2) = (n_n/n_s)(ωτ) ≪ 1, so the normal-fluid term is a small correction; equivalently |σ_s(ω)|/σ0 = (n_s/n_n)(1/ωτ) ≫ 1, meaning the supercurrent short-circuits the normal current.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpret the dispersion term-by-term

  • ω² term: ordinary wave propagation in vacuum-like electrodynamics.
  • −c²/λL² term: superconducting screening; acts like a “mass term” for the EM field, causing evanescence when ω is small.
  • + i ω (σ0/ε0) (SI) or + 4πσ0 i ω (CGS): dissipative correction from normal electrons; introduces attenuation/complex k.
Math Insight Helmholtz-type operator with complex coefficient

In frequency domain, the field satisfies a Helmholtz-like equation: ∇²B + k² B = 0, where k² is complex. The real part controls oscillation; the imaginary part controls exponential decay. The London term effectively shifts k² negative at low ω, forcing evanescent solutions.

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Increase ns: λL decreases → stronger screening.
  • Increase σ0: more dissipation (larger imaginary contribution).
  • Increase ω: ω² competes with c²/λ_L²; above ω ≳ c/λ_L propagation becomes less evanescent.

4.3 Alternative derivation idea (brief)

Derive σ_s(ω)= i/(μ0 ω λ_L²), form σ_eff = σ0 + σ_s, then use the standard conductor dispersion k² = ω²/c² + i μ0 ω σ_eff (SI) to reproduce the same result.

4.4 Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: What sets the decay length for low-ω fields? A: λL, because k ≈ i/λ_L when ω is small.
  • Q: Why is the superfluid response “inductive”? A: j_s ∝ iE/ω is 90° out of phase with E.
  • Q: What is the small dimensionless parameter suppressing normal effects? A: (n_n/n_s)(ωτ).
