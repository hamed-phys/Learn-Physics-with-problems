Quick Summary

  • We analyze a superconducting film of thickness δ in an applied magnetic field Ba at T=0.
  • Regime: thin film δ ≪ λ, where λ is the London penetration depth.
  • Key physics idea: the field varies across the thickness according to the London equation, so the magnetic energy stored inside the film is reduced when the film is thin.
  • Governing PDE (static, London): ∇²B = B/λ² (inside the superconductor, in absence of vortices).
  • Magnetic energy density (CGS): um=B²/8π. In SI: um=B²/2μ₀.
  • (a) Using the prior result, the free energy density in CGS can be written (given):
    Fs(x,Ba) = Us(0) + (δ²−4x²)Ba²/(64πλ²) for δ≪λ.
  • (b) Averaging over thickness yields a magnetic contribution ΔF̄m= Ba² δ² /(96πλ²) (CGS).
  • (c) Comparing with bulk condensation energy gives the thin-film critical field scaling:
    Hc(film) ≈ (λ/δ) Hc (up to order-1 geometric conventions).

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Geometry and key symbols

  • Film thickness: δ (m). We take the film occupying x ∈ [−δ/2, +δ/2].
  • Applied field: Ba (Tesla in SI; Gauss in CGS). We assume it is uniform outside the film and parallel to the film plane, so variation is mainly across x.
  • London penetration depth: λ (m), the length scale over which magnetic fields decay in a bulk superconductor.
  • Free energy density: F (energy per volume). At T=0, bulk condensation energy is Hc²/(8π) in CGS (or Hc²/(2μ₀) in SI, with appropriate conventions).

0.2 Mathematical model (field equation + energy functional)

In London theory (no vortices, static), the supercurrent is proportional to the vector potential A: J = −(c/4πλ²) A (CGS), and Maxwell gives ∇×B = (4π/c)J. Combining (and using B=∇×A, Coulomb-like gauge) leads to the London PDE:

Key equation (London PDE)
Inside the superconductor (static, no vortices):
∇² B = B / λ²

For a film infinite in y,z and varying only across x, this reduces to the 1D ODE: d²B/dx² = B/λ².

The magnetic energy density (field energy) is: um = B²/(8π) (CGS), or um = B²/(2μ₀) (SI). The film free energy density is the sum of condensation part and magnetic part (here we neglect kinetic-energy contribution per the problem statement).

0.3 Common approximations and why they’re used

Assumption set (validity)
  • Thin-film limit: δ/λ ≪ 1. Mathematically: expand solutions in powers of (δ/λ).
  • Linear London response: no vortices, no strong pair-breaking; B small enough that λ is approximately constant.
  • 1D reduction: translational invariance in the film plane → B = B(x).
  • Neglect kinetic term: energy contribution from superflow (beyond magnetic field energy) ignored as instructed.

0.4 Mini intuition examples (no heavy algebra)

  • Bulk slab (thick): B decays over λ, storing substantial field energy near the surface.
  • Very thin film: there is not enough thickness for exponential decay; the field stays nearly uniform → the incremental energy penalty can scale like (δ/λ)² compared to bulk.
  • Engineering intuition: less stored magnetic energy means the superconducting state can survive to higher external fields before condensation energy is overcome.

0.5 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Mixing CGS vs SI factors: 8π vs 2μ₀.
  • Forgetting the average over thickness when asked for a thickness-averaged contribution.
  • Dropping terms incorrectly in the δ≪λ expansion (keep the leading δ²/λ² correction).
  • Confusing B and H: in these thermodynamic energy balances, be consistent with the convention used (the problem states the needed replacements).
Math Insight

The London equation ∇²B = B/λ² is a screened Laplace (Helmholtz) equation. Mathematically, it says the Laplacian is proportional to the field itself, so solutions are combinations of hyperbolic functions (in 1D), reflecting exponential screening. It’s the magnetostatics analogue of a massive field: the operator (∇² − 1/λ²) acting on B vanishes.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this shows up in real systems (3+ areas)

  • Superconducting electronics: thin-film Nb/Al devices, Josephson junction circuits, qubits (field tolerance affects yield and stability).
  • Magnetic sensing: SQUIDs and kinetic inductance detectors (thin-film geometry controls screening and noise).
  • Superconducting interconnects and power micro-devices: thin films on substrates for low-loss routing in cryogenic systems.
  • Thin-film RF/microwave components: resonators and filters, where field penetration impacts Q-factor and power handling.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: larger effective critical fields allow operation in higher ambient magnetic environments.
  • Reliability: fewer field-induced transitions → fewer failures and reduced thermal cycling stress.
  • Manufacturability & cost: film thickness is a controllable process parameter; knowing scaling guides spec tolerances.

How it’s measured/validated in practice

  • Magnetization vs field (SQUID/VSM) to infer critical fields.
  • Transport measurements: sudden rise in resistance vs B at fixed T.
  • Microwave response: Q-factor degradation with B in resonators.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Thinner films can raise Hc(film) but may worsen granularity, increase disorder, and change λ.
  • Substrate strain and roughness can create weak spots and vortex nucleation sites.
  • Edge effects (finite width) can dominate in real layouts; fields concentrate at corners.
From lab to product

The ideal model treats a uniform film with a single λ and perfect boundaries. In products, engineers use an effective λ (including disorder), include demagnetization and edge fields, and validate via process control (thickness metrology, critical current mapping). The key takeaway—thin geometry enhances magnetic tolerance—still guides design.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

1.1 Restate the problem

A superconducting film of thickness δ is placed in an external magnetic field Ba. In the thin-film limit δ ≪ λ and at T=0, you are given (from Problem 1b) an expression for the free energy density in CGS:

Given result (CGS, δ ≪ λ)
F_s(x, B_a) = U_s(0) + [(δ^2 - 4 x^2) B_a^2] / (64 π λ^2)

In SI, “the factor π is replaced by (1/4)μ₀” (as stated). We also neglect kinetic energy contribution.

1.2 Given quantities

  • δ: film thickness
  • λ: penetration depth
  • Ba: applied field (assumed uniform outside)
  • Us(0): zero-field superconducting free energy density baseline

1.3 Unknowns / tasks

  • (b) Show thickness-averaged magnetic contribution is Ba² δ²/(96πλ²) (CGS).
  • (c) Show the thin-film critical field scales as Hc(film) ∝ (λ/δ) Hc when only magnetic contribution is considered.

1.4 Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Energy balance at the thermodynamic critical field: superconducting condensation energy vs magnetic energy cost.
  • Symmetry and averaging: film is symmetric about x=0, so averages simplify.
  • Thin-film expansion: keep leading correction in (δ/λ)².

1.5 Explicit assumptions

Assumptions used
  • δ ≪ λ, so we keep only leading-order terms in δ²/λ².
  • Field varies only along x: B = B(x); no vortices.
  • Use provided expression for Fs(x,Ba) from Problem 1b.
  • Neglect kinetic-energy contribution (problem instruction).

1.6 Possible approaches (compare)

  • Approach A (use given F directly): integrate/average over x to get magnetic term; then energy balance. Fast and exactly what (b) asks.
  • Approach B (re-derive B(x) from London ODE): solve d²B/dx²=B/λ² and compute energy. More physics insight, but longer.
  • Approach C (variational free energy): minimize London free-energy functional. Elegant, but beyond scope.

Chosen approach: A, with brief notes from B for intuition/validation.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Identify the magnetic term in Fs(x,Ba) (it’s proportional to Ba²).
  2. Average over thickness: compute F̄ = (1/δ)∫_{−δ/2}^{δ/2} F_s(x,B_a) dx.
  3. Extract the averaged magnetic contribution ΔF̄m (the part that depends on Ba).
  4. Relate to condensation energy: at T=0, bulk energy difference is H_c^2/(8π) (CGS).
  5. Set energy balance: magnetic cost equals available condensation energy → solve for Ba giving Hc(film).
  6. Show scaling: isolate (λ/δ) dependence.
  7. Sanity checks: units, δ→0 trend, δ→∞ trend (outside validity), sign.
Common mistakes (quick fixes)
  • Forgetting the average: problem (b) explicitly says “averaged over the thickness”.
  • Wrong limits: use x ∈ [−δ/2, +δ/2].
  • Mixing B and H conventions: keep the same convention used by the condensation energy formula in your unit system.
  • Dropping δ² term: the leading magnetic term scales as δ²/λ² in this thin-film expansion.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.0 Qualitative expectation

In a bulk superconductor, a magnetic field penetrates only to depth λ, storing magnetic energy near the surface. In a thin film with δ ≪ λ, the field cannot “fully screen” inside the film; the deviation from uniformity is small. Therefore, the incremental magnetic energy cost inside the film scales like (δ/λ)², which is small. As a result, it takes a larger applied field to overcome the condensation energy: Hc(film) increases roughly like λ/δ.

3.1 Mathematical model (variables, domain, boundary conditions)

Coordinate: x is normal to the film plane. Domain: x ∈ [−δ/2, +δ/2]. The film is infinite in y,z. Field inside is quasi-1D: B = B(x), with symmetry B(x)=B(−x). The given free-energy density already encodes the thin-film solution of the London equation to leading order in δ/λ.

Optional validation: why the London equation gives hyperbolic functions

If you solve d²B/dx² = B/λ², you get B(x)=C cosh(x/λ)+D sinh(x/λ). Symmetry about x=0 sets D=0. Boundary matching to B(±δ/2)=Ba fixes C. Expanding cosh for δ≪λ yields a polynomial in x with leading correction ∝ (x²/λ²), consistent with the quadratic form in the given Fs(x,Ba).

3.2 Part (a): Use the result of Problem 1b (given)

We accept the provided thin-film free energy density (CGS):

Part (a) (CGS)
F_s(x, B_a) = U_s(0) + [(δ^2 - 4 x^2) B_a^2] / (64 π λ^2),   for δ ≪ λ

Interpretation: Us(0) is the zero-field superconducting free-energy density; the second term is the leading magnetic contribution in δ/λ.

3.3 Part (b): Thickness-averaged magnetic contribution

The thickness-averaged free energy density is

Thickness average definition
F̄_s(B_a) = (1/δ) ∫_{-δ/2}^{+δ/2} F_s(x, B_a) dx

Substitute Fs and separate the constant term:

F̄s(Ba) = Us(0) + (Ba² / (64πλ²)) · (1/δ) ∫−δ/2δ/2 (δ² − 4x²) dx.

Compute the integral step-by-step:

  • ∫ (δ²) dx from −δ/2 to δ/2:
    δ² · [x]−δ/2δ/2 = δ² · (δ/2 − (−δ/2)) = δ² · δ = δ³.
  • ∫ (4x²) dx from −δ/2 to δ/2:
    4 · [x³/3]−δ/2δ/2 = 4 · ( ( (δ/2)³/3 ) − ( (−δ/2)³/3 ) )
    Since x³ is odd, the difference doubles the positive value:
    = 4 · ( 2 · (δ/2)³ / 3 ) = 8 · (δ³ / 8) / 3 = δ³ / 3.
  • Therefore ∫ (δ² − 4x²) dx = δ³ − δ³/3 = (2/3) δ³.

Plug back into the average:

(1/δ) ∫ (δ² − 4x²) dx = (1/δ) · (2/3) δ³ = (2/3) δ².

Hence the thickness-averaged free energy is

Part (b) result (CGS)
F̄_s(B_a) = U_s(0) + (B_a^2 / (64 π λ^2)) * (2/3) δ^2
          = U_s(0) + (B_a^2 δ^2) / (96 π λ^2)

So the magnetic contribution to the thickness-averaged superconducting free energy density is: ΔF̄m = Ba² δ² /(96πλ²) (CGS), as required.

Dimensional check

In CGS, B² has dimensions of energy density (via B²/8π). The factor δ²/λ² is dimensionless. Therefore ΔF̄m has units of energy/volume.

3.4 Part (c): Thin-film critical field scaling

The thermodynamic critical field Hc for a bulk superconductor at T=0 is defined by equating the condensation energy density to the magnetic energy density at the transition. In CGS, the bulk condensation energy per unit volume is:

Bulk condensation energy (CGS convention)
U_cond = H_c^2 / (8 π)

(This is the standard thermodynamic relation for type-I superconductors; the problem asks only for proportionality, so order-1 convention differences between B and H won’t change the scaling.)

For the thin film, we are instructed to consider only the magnetic contribution to Us. The superconducting state remains favorable until the magnetic penalty matches the available condensation energy:

ΔF̄m(Ba=Hc(film)) ≈ Hc²/(8π).

Using ΔF̄m from part (b):

(Hc(film))² δ² /(96πλ²) = Hc² /(8π).

Multiply both sides by 96πλ²/δ²:

(Hc(film))² = Hc² · (96πλ²/δ²) · (1/(8π)) = Hc² · (12) · (λ²/δ²).

So:

Thin-film critical field (from this magnetic-only model)
H_c(film) = √12 * (λ/δ) * H_c

The problem statement asks to show proportionality:

FINAL SCALING (what the problem asks for)

Hc(film) ∝ (λ/δ) Hc

Copy-friendly final answer
Thin-film critical field scaling (δ ≪ λ):
H_c(film) ∝ (λ/δ) H_c
More specifically from the given magnetic term:
H_c(film) = √12 (λ/δ) H_c  (CGS convention used here)

3.5 Sanity checks

  • Units: λ/δ is dimensionless → H has correct units.
  • Thin limit δ→0: Hc(film) grows → the film becomes harder to quench by magnetic energy (within model validity).
  • Outside regime δ ~ λ or larger: the δ≪λ expansion fails; need the full cosh profile and additional terms.
  • Sign/positivity: ΔF̄m is positive (magnetic cost) and increases with Ba², as required for stability reasoning.
CGS → SI note (as the problem states)

In SI, magnetic energy density is u = B²/(2μ₀) rather than B²/8π, and the problem states that “the factor π is replaced by (1/4)μ₀”. That replacement preserves the same scaling with δ and λ; only prefactors differ.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 What controls the result?

  • δ controls how much room the field has to vary: thinner → smaller magnetic energy penalty.
  • λ controls screening length: larger λ means weaker screening in bulk, and thus stronger relative enhancement when δ is fixed.
  • Quadratic dependence in B: the energy penalty is ∝ B², so critical field comes from a square-root balance.

4.2 Parameter dependence

From the magnetic-only model: Hc(film) ~ (λ/δ) Hc.

  • Halving δ roughly doubles Hc(film) (linear in 1/δ).
  • Increasing λ (e.g., via disorder increasing penetration depth) increases Hc(film) in this simplified picture.

4.3 Alternative derivation idea (meaningful but brief)

One can start from the London free-energy functional: F = U_s(0) + ∫ (B²/8π + (λ²/8π)(∇×B)² ) dV (schematic, CGS), and minimize with respect to B subject to boundary conditions. The Euler–Lagrange equation yields the London PDE. Expanding the minimized functional in δ/λ produces the same δ²/λ² scaling.

4.4 Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why does the averaged magnetic contribution scale like δ²/λ²?
    A: In the thin-film limit the field profile deviates from uniformity only weakly; the leading correction from the screened-Laplace operator appears at second order in thickness, giving δ²/λ².
  • Q: Why does Hc(film) scale like λ/δ (not δ/λ)?
    A: Energy balance uses B² times (δ²/λ²). Solving for B gives B ∝ (λ/δ).
  • Q: What breaks if δ is not ≪ λ?
    A: The Taylor expansion fails; higher-order terms and the full hyperbolic solution must be used, and edge/demagnetization may matter.
  • Q: If we included kinetic-energy contributions, would scaling necessarily remain identical?
    A: The scaling often remains similar in leading form but prefactors and even effective dependence can change depending on geometry and current distribution; the problem explicitly tells us to neglect it.

PART 5 — Optional Plot Guide

No plot is necessary here: the problem is purely symbolic and the main learning outcome is the scaling law Hc(film) ∝ (λ/δ)Hc, which is already transparent from the algebraic energy balance.
