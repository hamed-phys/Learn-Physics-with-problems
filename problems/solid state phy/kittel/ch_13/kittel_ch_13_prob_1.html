Equivalent Electrical Circuit: Inductor Filled with a Spin System (Complex Susceptibility)

We derive how a magnetic (spin) medium with complex rf susceptibility modifies an inductor’s impedance—turning part of the inductive reactance into an effective resistance (loss) and shifting the inductance. The derivation is done in CGS form where χ = χ′ + iχ″ and B = H + 4πM.

Quick Summary

  • What this is about: A coil (inductor) is filled with a spin system whose rf response is characterized by a complex susceptibility χ(ω)=χ′(ω)+iχ″(ω).
  • Key physics idea: The medium changes the magnetic flux linkage. The in-phase response χ′ shifts the inductance, while the out-of-phase response χ″ produces dissipation that appears as an effective resistance.
  • Governing relations (CGS): M = χ H, B = H + 4πM = (1+4πχ)H, and circuit impedance Z = R + iωL.
  • Result type: Symbolic expressions for the effective inductance and resistance vs frequency.
  • Main result: L(ω) = [1 + 4πχ′(ω)] L₀ and R(ω) = R₀ + 4π ω χ″(ω) L₀.
  • Physical meaning: The spin system acts like a frequency-dependent “complex permeability” that makes the inductor behave like an inductor plus a loss resistor (magnetic loss → heat).
  • Industrial relevance: This is exactly how magnetic cores and resonant spin/ferromagnetic systems are modeled in rf coils, NMR/EPR probes, sensors, and impedance-matching networks.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (with units)

Key quantities symbols

  • Inductance: L (henry in SI; in CGS, inductive reactance still enters as iωL in impedance form)
  • Resistance: R (ohm)
  • Angular frequency: ω (rad/s)
  • Magnetization: M (emu/cm³ in CGS)
  • Magnetic field: H (oersted in CGS)
  • Magnetic induction: B (gauss in CGS)
  • Susceptibility: χ(ω) = χ′(ω) + iχ″(ω) (dimensionless)

CGS constitutive relation magnetostatics / linear response

In CGS (Gaussian) form, the medium’s response is written as: 

M(ω) = χ(ω) H(ω)
B(ω) = H(ω) + 4π M(ω) = [1 + 4πχ(ω)] H(ω)

Thus the effective (complex) permeability factor is μ(ω) = 1 + 4πχ(ω).

0.2 Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Inductance is “flux per current”: in a coil, the current creates a magnetic field; the field creates magnetic flux; the flux links turns and produces an induced voltage opposing changes in current.
  • In-phase vs out-of-phase response: If the magnetization oscillates in phase with H, it stores energy (reactive). If it lags by 90° (out of phase), it dissipates energy (loss).
  • Complex susceptibility encodes both: χ′ modifies stored magnetic energy → inductance shift; χ″ produces power absorption → effective resistance.

0.3 Key laws / principles and validity conditions

Validity assumptions what must hold

  • Linear response: magnetization is proportional to field at the driving frequency: M = χH (small rf excitation).
  • Harmonic steady state: time dependence e^{-iωt}; transients ignored.
  • Quasi-static coil fields: coil dimensions ≪ wavelength, so we use lumped-element impedance Z(ω).
  • Uniform filling / coupling: the sample fills the coil so that the same field region that sets L₀ is modified by μ(ω).
  • CGS constitutive form: B = H + 4πM. (In SI, the numerical factors differ.)

0.4 Common models / approximations used here (and why)

  • Lumped circuit model: represent the coil by a series R₀ and L₀. This is appropriate when distributed effects (transmission line behavior) are negligible.
  • Complex inductance idea: treat the magnetic medium as making the inductance complex: L̃(ω) = L₀ μ(ω). The imaginary part is then reinterpreted as an effective resistance because iωL̃ produces a real part in impedance.
  • Neglect radiation: energy loss is dominated by ohmic and magnetic absorption, not antenna radiation.

0.5 Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • Perfectly lossless magnetic medium: χ″=0 ⇒ no added resistance, only inductance shift.
  • Strong absorption near resonance: χ″(ω) peaks ⇒ coil appears “more resistive” at that ω, lowering Q.
  • Weak coupling / partial fill: the same formulas hold with a geometric “filling factor” multiplying 4πχ (not required here because the problem says the coil is completely filled).

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Sign convention: whether you use e^{+iωt} or e^{-iωt} changes where the minus sign appears in the imaginary part. The final physical statements (added loss ∝ χ″) remain the same.
  • CGS vs SI factors: the 4π is a CGS feature. Don’t “import” SI formulas without conversion.
  • Geometry dependence hidden in L₀: L₀ already accounts for coil shape and dimensions; the medium scales it via μ only under uniform filling/coupling.

Math Insight complex analysis / linear systems

The whole problem is an instance of a linear time-invariant (LTI) system in the frequency domain: the constitutive law M(ω)=χ(ω)H(ω) makes the coil’s impedance a complex-valued transfer function Z(ω). Splitting Z = Re(Z) + i Im(Z) is exactly the mathematical separation into dissipative and reactive parts of a passive response.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Why this matters in real systems

  • RF coils with magnetic cores: transformers, inductors, EMI filters—core losses map into an effective series resistance that changes efficiency and temperature rise.
  • NMR / EPR probes and resonators: the sample’s spin susceptibility changes the coil’s tuning and Q, affecting signal-to-noise ratio and matching.
  • Magnetic sensors & metrology: susceptibility spectroscopy uses impedance shifts to characterize materials (quality control, composition, phase transitions).

Why engineers care (industrial engineering lens)

  • Performance: added R(ω) lowers Q and narrows usable bandwidth; inductance shift detunes resonant tanks.
  • Reliability & safety: magnetic loss → heat; thermal management and derating require accurate loss modeling.
  • Cost & manufacturability: choosing core/material and geometry is a trade-off among size, efficiency, tolerances, and supply constraints.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Impedance analyzer / VNA: measure Z(ω), fit to a series (or parallel) R–L model to extract effective L(ω) and R(ω).
  • Q-factor and resonance tracking: observe resonance frequency shifts (→ χ′) and linewidth/bandwidth changes (→ χ″).
  • Calorimetry / temperature rise: validate magnetic loss by direct heating measurements in power applications.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Non-idealities: finite conductivity (eddy currents), hysteresis, partial filling factors, fringing fields, and saturation can break simple linear scaling.
  • Frequency dependence: χ′(ω), χ″(ω) can vary strongly (especially near resonances), so single-number “inductance” is not enough in wideband designs.
  • Thermal coupling: as temperature changes, susceptibility and resistance both change—feedback that impacts process stability and reliability.
From lab to product: how idealized models are adapted

In production, engineers often introduce a filling factor η (0–1) and sometimes a demagnetizing correction to account for geometry, partial filling, and field nonuniformity: μ_eff(ω) ≈ 1 + 4π η χ(ω). They also choose a series or parallel equivalent circuit depending on whether the device is used near resonance, and they validate the model by fitting measured Z(ω) over the operating band.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

1.1 Restate the problem (in plain words)

We have an empty coil with inductance L₀ and a series resistance R₀. When the coil is completely filled with a spin system whose rf response is described by a complex susceptibility χ(ω)=χ′(ω)+iχ″(ω), show that: 

L(ω) = [1 + 4πχ′(ω)] L₀
R(ω) = R₀ + 4π ω χ″(ω) L₀

The hint says: “Consider the impedance of the circuit (CGS units).”

1.2 Given, unknowns, and targets

Given inputs

  • Empty-coil inductance L₀
  • Series coil resistance R₀
  • Spin-system susceptibility components χ′(ω), χ″(ω)
  • Frequency (angular) ω
  • CGS relation B = H + 4πM

Unknowns outputs

  • Effective inductance L(ω)
  • Effective resistance R(ω)
  • Equivalently, the full impedance Z(ω)

1.3 Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Faraday’s law (field → induced emf): in quasi-static conditions, the induced voltage is tied to time-varying magnetic flux.
  • Linear material response in frequency domain: M(ω)=χ(ω)H(ω) is the defining model for a spin system under small rf drive.
  • Lumped-element impedance: the coil behaves like Z = R + iωL. This is the natural setting for interpreting how χ modifies the coil response.

We do not need full wave propagation (no Helmholtz PDE) because the problem is explicitly about an equivalent circuit and gives a lumped inductor.

Assumptions used explicit

  • Small-signal rf field so χ is independent of field amplitude (linearity).
  • Harmonic time dependence at a single frequency ω (steady state).
  • Coil is completely filled and uniformly coupled to the medium.
  • Loss mechanisms are captured by χ″ (magnetic absorption); coil copper loss stays as R₀.

1.4 Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

  • Approach A — Impedance with complex permeability (best): Treat the medium as giving a complex factor μ(ω)=1+4πχ(ω) multiplying the inductance. Then rewrite Z(ω) and identify effective R and L by real/imag parts.
  • Approach B — Energy + power loss: Use stored magnetic energy to get L and use absorbed power (∝ χ″) to infer an equivalent series resistance. More physically rich, but longer.
  • Approach C — Field-circuit coupling from Maxwell equations: Start from ∇×E = -(1/c) ∂B/∂t (CGS) and compute emf from flux; then express B in terms of H and χ. Correct but more formal than necessary here.

Choice: Approach A is the most direct and matches the hint: “consider the impedance.”

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Write the empty-coil impedance using the series R–L model: Z₀(ω)=R₀+iωL₀.
  2. Model the filled coil by replacing the magnetic response factor in flux: in CGS, B=(1+4πχ)H.
  3. Translate “more B for the same H” into “more flux for the same current,” hence inductance scales by μ(ω)=1+4πχ(ω).
  4. Introduce a complex inductance L̃(ω)=L₀[1+4πχ(ω)].
  5. Compute the new impedance Z(ω)=R₀+iωL̃(ω).
  6. Separate real and imaginary parts of Z(ω) to identify effective series R(ω) and L(ω).
  7. Sanity-check units (ωL has resistance units), limiting cases (χ″→0 gives no added loss), and signs (loss must increase resistance).

Common mistakes quick fixes

  • Forgetting the 4π: It’s a CGS factor from B = H + 4πM.
  • Mixing SI/CGS: In SI you’d write B = μ₀(H+M), so the numerical prefactors change.
  • Misidentifying χ″: χ″ contributes to Re(Z) because i × i = -1 when you expand iωL₀( i4πχ″ ).

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Qualitative expectation (before calculation)

Filling a coil with a magnetically responsive medium increases magnetic induction B for the same driving field H. Since flux linkage grows, the inductance should increase in proportion to the in-phase response (χ′). If the magnetization lags the field (encoded by χ″), the medium absorbs energy each cycle, so the coil should appear to have an additional series resistance.

3.2 Mathematical model (variables, domain, operators, conditions)

Model setup mathematical physics

  • Time dependence: harmonic steady state with phasors: any field F(t)=Re{F(ω)e^{-iωt}}.
  • Constitutive law (linear response): M(ω) = χ(ω)H(ω), with χ = χ′ + iχ″.
  • Maxwell–Faraday (CGS operator form): ∇×E = -(1/c) ∂B/∂t. In phasor form: ∇×E(ω) = (iω/c) B(ω) for the e^{-iωt} convention.
  • Flux linkage definition (lumped element): coil emf relates to flux: V(ω) = iω Φ_link(ω). (This follows from integrating Faraday’s law over the coil loop; it is valid under quasi-static conditions.)
  • Geometry/domain: a coil volume fully filled by a uniform medium; boundary effects are absorbed into the known empty-coil inductance L₀.

3.3 Start from the circuit impedance (empty coil)

The empty coil is modeled as an inductor L₀ in series with a resistance R₀. In sinusoidal steady state, the impedance is: 

Z₀(ω) = R₀ + iωL₀

3.4 How the spin system modifies the inductive part

In a coil, inductance is proportional to flux linkage per current: L ∝ Φ/I. For a given current I, the coil produces a magnetic field intensity H (set by geometry). If the coil is filled with a medium, the induction becomes (CGS): 

B(ω) = H(ω) + 4πM(ω) = [1 + 4πχ(ω)] H(ω)

Because magnetic flux through the coil is an area integral of B (schematically Φ = ∬ B·dA), and because H for a given current is fixed by the coil geometry in this lumped model, the flux scales by the same multiplicative factor μ(ω)=1+4πχ(ω) when the coil is completely filled and uniformly coupled.

Key step (complex inductance) linearity

Since the system is linear, scaling B by μ(ω) scales the flux linkage, hence scales the inductance by the same factor: 

L̃(ω) = L₀ [1 + 4πχ(ω)]  = L₀ [1 + 4π(χ′(ω) + iχ″(ω))]

3.5 Compute the filled-coil impedance and separate real/imag parts

Replace L₀ by the complex inductance L̃(ω) in the impedance formula: 

Z(ω) = R₀ + iω L̃(ω)
     = R₀ + iω L₀ [1 + 4π(χ′ + iχ″)]

Now expand step-by-step, carefully using i·i = -1: 

Z(ω) = R₀ + iωL₀[1 + 4πχ′ + i4πχ″]
     = R₀ + iωL₀(1 + 4πχ′) + iωL₀(i4πχ″)
     = R₀ + iωL₀(1 + 4πχ′) - ωL₀(4πχ″)

Group the real and imaginary parts: 

Re[Z(ω)] = R₀ + 4π ω L₀ χ″(ω)
Im[Z(ω)] = ω L₀ [1 + 4π χ′(ω)]

Since a series R–L element has impedance Z = R + iωL, we identify:

Final result (equivalent series circuit) boxed

L(ω) = [1 + 4πχ′(ω)] L₀
R(ω) = R₀ + 4π ω χ″(ω) L₀

3.6 Sanity checks

Dimensional analysis units check

  • χ′, χ″ are dimensionless (susceptibility).
  • ωL₀ has units of resistance (ohm) in the impedance iωL.
  • Therefore 4π ω χ″ L₀ has units of resistance and can legitimately add to R₀.

Limiting cases asymptotics

  • No spins / vacuum: χ′=χ″=0 ⇒ L=L₀, R=R₀.
  • Lossless medium: χ″→0 ⇒ no added resistance; only L shifts by 4πχ′.
  • Strong absorption band: if χ″(ω) is large near a resonance, then R(ω) increases strongly, lowering Q.

Physical interpretation plain language

The medium behaves like a frequency-dependent complex permeability. The in-phase part increases/decreases the coil’s energy storage (inductance), while the out-of-phase part converts some of the “inductive” voltage into a real voltage drop, i.e., an effective resistance representing dissipated power in the spin system.

Optional deeper note: why the “complex inductance” trick is mathematically legitimate

In steady-state sinusoidal analysis, any linear constitutive law can be represented by a complex coefficient in the frequency domain. Here, B(ω) = μ(ω)H(ω) with complex μ implies the flux linkage Φ(ω) is proportional to current with a complex proportionality constant. Writing Φ(ω)=L̃(ω) I(ω) defines L̃(ω). Then V(ω)=iωΦ(ω)=iωL̃(ω)I(ω), so the impedance is Z=iωL̃. The real part of Z is dissipative; the imaginary part is reactive—exactly the standard passivity decomposition.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpret the final formula term-by-term

  • Baseline: R₀ and L₀ capture copper loss and vacuum/air inductance.
  • Reactive shift: 4πχ′(ω)L₀ is the inductance change from in-phase magnetization (energy storage).
  • Loss term: 4π ω χ″(ω)L₀ is the added resistance from magnetic absorption (energy dissipation per cycle).

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Increasing χ′ increases L linearly (in this model).
  • Increasing χ″ increases R linearly and also increases with frequency via ω.
  • Larger coils (larger L₀) amplify both effects proportionally—important for scaling in product design.

4.3 Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

You can derive the same result by matching power: in a series circuit, the average dissipated power is P = (1/2)|I|² R. In magnetic absorption theory, the absorbed power density in a linear medium is proportional to ω χ″ |H|². Integrating over the coil volume and relating H to I gives an effective resistance proportional to ωχ″, consistent with R = R₀ + 4π ω χ″ L₀.

Math Insight positive real functions

For a passive linear medium, the impedance Z(ω) must be a positive-real function (in appropriate analytic continuation), implying Re[Z(ω)] ≥ 0. Here that corresponds to χ″(ω) ≥ 0 for absorption—exactly the physical requirement that the medium cannot generate net energy in linear response.

4.4 Concept checks (self-test with answers)

  • Q: If χ″(ω) doubles at a fixed ω, what happens to the added loss? A: The added resistance term 4π ω χ″ L₀ doubles.
  • Q: Why does χ″ affect resistance rather than inductance? A: Because it appears multiplied by i inside iωL̃, producing a real contribution via i·i=-1.
  • Q: What changes if the coil is only partially filled? A: A geometry/filling factor multiplies the 4πχ contribution (the scaling is reduced).
  • Q: What does a peak in χ″(ω) do to a resonant circuit’s Q? A: It increases R near that ω, reducing Q and broadening/damping resonance.
