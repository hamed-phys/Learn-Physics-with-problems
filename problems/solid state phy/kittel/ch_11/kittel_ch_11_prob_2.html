Hund’s Rules Ground States for 4f Ions: Eu2+, Yb3+, Tb3+

We determine the LS-coupled ground-state term symbols of rare-earth ions by applying Hund’s rules to the open 4f subshell: maximize total spin S, then total orbital angular momentum L, then pick J based on whether the subshell is less than or more than half-filled.

Quick Summary

  • What: Find the ground-state (lowest-energy) term symbols for Eu2+, Yb3+, Tb3+ using Hund’s rules.
  • Key physics idea: For an open subshell, Coulomb exchange favors maximum spin S, then maximum orbital L; spin–orbit coupling then selects J.
  • Governing operators: Total angular momenta in LS coupling: L̂ = Σ l̂_i, Ŝ = Σ ŝ_i, Ĵ = L̂ + Ŝ.
  • Energy ordering tool: Spin–orbit perturbation Ĥ_SO ≈ ζ (L̂·Ŝ) gives level energies proportional to [J(J+1) − L(L+1) − S(S+1)].
  • Subshell specifics: 4f has l = 3 and capacity 2(2l+1) = 14 electrons.
  • Results (term symbols):
    • Eu2+ : 4f7 → ^8S7/2 (half-filled, L = 0).
    • Yb3+ : 4f13 → ^2F7/2 (one hole in f).
    • Tb3+ : 4f8 → ^7F6 (equivalent to 4f6 holes, then J = L+S).
  • Meaning: These (L,S,J) determine degeneracies, magnetic moments, and spectroscopic/selection rules that matter in materials and devices.

Final Answers (copy-friendly)

Eu2+ (4f^7): ^8S_{7/2} Yb3+ (4f^13): ^2F_{7/2} Tb3+ (4f^8): ^7F_{6}

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + units)

  • Single-electron orbital angular momentum: l (dimensionless quantum number), operator l̂ (units of ħ).
  • Single-electron spin: s = 1/2, operator ŝ (units of ħ).
  • Total orbital: L̂ = Σ_i l̂_i, with eigenvalues L(L+1)ħ2.
  • Total spin: Ŝ = Σ_i ŝ_i, with eigenvalues S(S+1)ħ2.
  • Total angular momentum: Ĵ = L̂ + Ŝ, with eigenvalues J(J+1)ħ2.
  • Term symbol: 2S+1LJ (L = 0,1,2,3,4,5,6 ↔ S,P,D,F,G,H,I).
  • Spin–orbit constant: ζ (energy scale; SI: J, often tabulated in eV or cm−1).

Physical meaning

  • S maximization: Exchange interaction lowers energy when more spins are parallel (Pauli + Coulomb).
  • L maximization (given S): Electron–electron repulsion favors larger orbital alignment consistent with that spin configuration.
  • J selection: Spin–orbit coupling splits the LS term into J-levels; which J is lowest depends on shell filling.
  • Why 4f is special: 4f electrons are shielded (inside 5s/5p), giving sharp atomic-like lines and robust moments in solids.

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

  • LS coupling (Russell–Saunders): Good when electrostatic (Coulomb) interactions dominate spin–orbit splitting (often valid for light-to-mid Z; still widely used for rare-earth 4f term identification).
  • Hund’s rules: Empirical/derived ordering rules for lowest term in a subshell configuration.
  • Spin–orbit as a perturbation: Use Ĥ_SO ≈ ζ L̂·Ŝ within a fixed (L,S) manifold to order J.

Mathematical model (many-electron atom, LS coupling)

We model the open-shell electrons with the nonrelativistic many-electron Hamiltonian plus a spin–orbit perturbation:

Key model Ĥ = Σ_i [ p̂_i^2/(2m) + V(r_i) ] + Σ_{i

Domain: electronic coordinates {r_i} in 3D space; boundary condition: normalizable wavefunctions (bound states). We solve an eigenvalue problem for Ĥ; in LS coupling we diagonalize L̂², Ŝ², Ĵ² simultaneously within a configuration subspace.

Why each step is valid (math justification)

  • Angular momentum addition: Because the Hamiltonian (without Ĥ_SO) is rotationally invariant, L̂² and Ŝ² commute with Ĥ (good quantum numbers).
  • Linearity: Eigenstates form linear subspaces labeled by (L,S); within that subspace, Ĥ_SO is a Hermitian operator, so it can be diagonalized.
  • Operator identity: 2 L̂·Ŝ = Ĵ² − L̂² − Ŝ², letting us express spin–orbit energies in terms of quantum numbers.

Math Insight (core equation as a mathematical object)

The spin–orbit term is an operator on a finite-dimensional angular-momentum representation space. Within fixed (L,S), finding the lowest J is essentially an eigenvalue ordering problem for Ĵ², because Ĥ_SO ∝ Ĵ² up to constants:

Operator identity E_SO(J) ∝ ⟨L̂·Ŝ⟩ = [J(J+1) − L(L+1) − S(S+1)]/2

Common models/approximations (and why)

  • Equivalent electrons in one subshell: For 4fn, we focus on the open subshell and treat closed shells as inert (they contribute L = S = 0).
  • Hole formalism: For more-than-half-filled subshells, treat n_h = (4l+2) − n holes; (L,S) match the holes’ configuration, then Hund’s 3rd rule flips J selection.
  • Neglect configuration interaction: Good for identifying ground terms; fine structure and mixing can refine energies but usually not the ground term label.

Mini intuition examples (quick)

  • Half-filled subshell: When a subshell is exactly half-filled (e.g., f7), symmetry forces L = 0 (no net orbital alignment) while S is maximal.
  • One electron vs one hole: f1 and f13 have the same (L,S) but differ in whether the shell is less or more than half-filled, which changes the lowest J.

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Don’t let closed shells (like [Xe]) contribute: they are L = S = 0.
  • For n > half-filled, Hund’s 3rd rule picks J = L + S, not |L−S|.
  • Half-filled is a special case: L = 0 for f7.
  • Be consistent: term symbols depend on LS coupling; in very strong spin–orbit (jj coupling) the reasoning differs.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this matters (3+ application areas)

  • Permanent magnets & motors: Rare-earth magnetic moments (from L,S,J) underpin NdFeB/SmCo magnet performance and temperature stability.
  • Laser and phosphor materials: 4f–4f transitions in lanthanides enable sharp emission lines (LED phosphors, solid-state lasers, fiber amplifiers like Er3+).
  • MRI contrast & cryogenic refrigeration: Large magnetic entropy changes relate to J-level structure (magnetocaloric effects, paramagnetic salts).
  • Quantum devices & sensing: Rare-earth ions in crystals provide long coherence optical/spin states; term structure sets selection rules and Zeeman splitting.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: Magnetic moment and anisotropy trace back to L and J.
  • Reliability: Temperature dependence of magnetic/optical properties depends on level splittings.
  • Cost & materials selection: Choosing ions/hosts with desirable term structures reduces redesign cycles.
  • Manufacturability: Host lattice strain and defects can split levels (crystal-field effects) affecting yield.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Optical spectroscopy: Absorption/emission lines identify term multiplets and crystal-field splitting.
  • Magnetometry (VSM/SQUID): Measures effective magnetic moments and Curie behavior linked to (L,S,J).
  • EPR/ESR: Resolves g-factors and fine structure; sensitive to ground-state J and splitting.

Design trade-offs & real constraints

  • Crystal field vs free-ion terms: In solids, the host breaks rotational symmetry; LSJ remains a powerful starting label, then crystal field splits degeneracies.
  • Non-idealities: Mixed valence, strain, and defects can change effective configuration and broaden lines.
  • Thermal budgets: Level separations and phonon coupling control temperature quenching and magnet temperature coefficients.
From lab to product: how idealized Hund’s-rule labels are used in practice

Industrial workflows often start with the free-ion ground term (from Hund’s rules) to estimate magnetic moments and selection rules. Then the model is adapted by adding crystal-field Hamiltonians (Stevens operators) and fitting parameters to spectroscopy/magnetometry. This hybrid approach keeps design iterations fast while remaining physically anchored.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

Restate the problem

Apply Hund’s rules to find the ground-state term symbols for: (a) Eu2+ with configuration 4f7 (core [Xe] is closed), (b) Yb3+ (open 4f subshell), (c) Tb3+ (open 4f subshell). For (b) and (c), show the separate steps of applying Hund’s rules.

Given

  • Ions: Eu2+, Yb3+, Tb3+
  • Relevant open-shell configurations:
    • Eu2+: [Xe]4f7
    • Yb3+: [Xe]4f13
    • Tb3+: [Xe]4f8
  • Subshell: 4f → l = 3, capacity 14 electrons

Unknowns (to find)

  • Total spin quantum number S
  • Total orbital quantum number L
  • Total angular quantum number J (ground J-level)
  • Final term symbols 2S+1LJ

Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Hund’s 1st rule: maximize S → lowest energy due to exchange (applies to electrons in same subshell).
  • Hund’s 2nd rule: for that S, maximize L → lowest electrostatic energy.
  • Hund’s 3rd rule: pick J:
    • If subshell is less than half-filled: smallest J (J = |L − S|) is lowest.
    • If subshell is more than half-filled: largest J (J = L + S) is lowest.
    • If half-filled: typically L = 0 so J = S.

Assumptions (explicit)

  • Closed shells inert: [Xe] contributes L = S = 0.
  • LS coupling framework: good for identifying free-ion ground term labels of 4f configurations.
  • Neglect crystal-field effects: we are finding the free-ion ground state, not solid-state splittings.

Possible approaches (compare 2–3)

Approach A: Direct Hund’s-rule counting

Assign electrons to ml values to maximize parallel spins (S), then maximize L by choosing largest |ml|.

  • Pros: constructive, transparent.
  • Cons: can be tedious for large n.

Approach B: Hole formalism

For n > 7 in f shell, use holes nh = 14 − n. Find (L,S) for holes (like electrons with nh), then use Hund’s 3rd rule for “more than half-filled.”

  • Pros: fast for f13, f8, etc.
  • Cons: must remember which J rule applies after mapping.

Chosen approach: Use direct counting for the special half-filled Eu2+ case, and hole formalism for Yb3+ (f13) and Tb3+ (f8) to keep steps clean and rigorous.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

Roadmap (5–12 steps)

  1. Identify the open subshell (ignore closed shells) → isolates the angular momentum problem.
  2. Note l and capacity for that subshell (4f: l = 3, capacity 14) → needed for “half-filled?” and hole count.
  3. Apply Hund’s 1st rule: maximize S → set the multiplicity (2S+1).
  4. Apply Hund’s 2nd rule: for that S, maximize L → pick the letter (S,P,D,F,G,H,I...).
  5. Apply Hund’s 3rd rule: choose J using filling (n < 7, n > 7, or n = 7) → gives subscript J.
  6. Write the term symbol 2S+1LJ.
  7. Sanity check: half-filled → L = 0; one electron/hole → doublet; J bounds |L−S| ≤ J ≤ L+S.

Common mistakes (quick tips)

  • Using J = |L−S| for a more-than-half-filled subshell (should be J = L+S).
  • Letting core electrons contribute to L or S (closed shells contribute zero).
  • Forgetting that f has l = 3 and capacity 14.
  • Mislabeling L letters: L=0 S, 1 P, 2 D, 3 F, 4 G, 5 H, 6 I.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition (before calculating)

In 4f shells, electrons are strongly correlated within the subshell. Exchange favors many parallel spins (large S). When the subshell is half-filled, symmetry cancels orbital angular momentum (L ≈ 0), leaving a large spin-only moment. Near a filled shell (like f13), the ion behaves like “one hole,” giving a simple doublet term with F character (L = 3).

Hund’s rules (stated cleanly)

  1. Maximize S (highest multiplicity 2S+1).
  2. Maximize L for that S.
  3. Choose J:
    • if less than half-filled → lowest J = |L − S|
    • if more than half-filled → highest J = L + S
    • if half-filled → L = 0 so J = S

(a) Eu2+ : [Xe]4f7

Step 1: Identify open shell. [Xe] is closed → contributes L = S = 0. Open shell is 4f7.

Step 2: Hund’s 1st rule (maximize S).

  • For an f subshell: ml ∈ {−3,−2,−1,0,1,2,3} (7 orbitals). Each can host one ↑ electron before pairing.
  • With 7 electrons, we can place one electron in each ml with parallel spins.
  • Thus all 7 spins are aligned: S = 7 × (1/2) = 7/2, multiplicity 2S+1 = 8.

Step 3: Hund’s 2nd rule (maximize L).

  • In the maximally spin-aligned arrangement for half-filling, the occupied ml values are symmetric: −3 to +3.
  • Total ML = Σ ml = (−3−2−1+0+1+2+3) = 0.
  • The half-filled shell has maximal symmetry → the resulting total L is 0 (an S term).

Step 4: Hund’s 3rd rule (choose J).

  • Half-filled (n = 7) and L = 0 → only possible J is J = S = 7/2.
Result (Eu2+):
Eu2+ (4f^7) → ^8S_{7/2}

(b) Yb3+ : [Xe]4f13 (show steps)

Step 1: Open shell and hole count. 4f capacity is 14. For 4f13, the number of holes is:

Hole count n_h = 14 − n = 14 − 13 = 1 hole

Step 2: Determine (L,S) for one hole.

  • A single hole in an f subshell behaves (for L and S values) like a single f electron: l = 3, s = 1/2.
  • Thus L = 3 (letter F) and S = 1/2 (doublet, multiplicity 2).

Step 3: Apply Hund’s 3rd rule (more-than-half-filled).

  • Because n = 13 > 7, the subshell is more than half-filled.
  • Hund’s 3rd rule then selects the largest J:
J selection J = L + S = 3 + 1/2 = 7/2
Result (Yb3+):
Yb3+ (4f^13) → ^2F_{7/2}

(c) Tb3+ : [Xe]4f8 (show steps)

Step 1: Hole count. For 4f8:

Hole count n_h = 14 − n = 14 − 8 = 6 holes

Step 2: Find (L,S) for 6 holes (i.e., f6).

  • nh = 6 is less than half-filled (6 < 7), so apply Hund’s 1st & 2nd rules to holes as if electrons in f6.
  • For f6, the ground term has:
LS for f^6 S = 3 (multiplicity 2S+1 = 7) L = 3 (F term)

Step 3: Apply Hund’s 3rd rule for the original f8 (more-than-half-filled).

  • Electron count n = 8 > 7 → more than half-filled → pick largest J.
J selection J = L + S = 3 + 3 = 6
Result (Tb3+):
Tb3+ (4f^8) → ^7F_{6}

Sanity checks

Dimensional check

  • L, S, J are dimensionless; operators carry ħ.
  • Term symbols label eigenstates; energies need ζ and interactions.

Limiting-case intuition

  • Half-filled f^7 → L = 0, J = S (Eu2+).
  • f^13 → one hole → doublet F, J = 7/2 (Yb3+).
  • f^8 more than half-filled → large J (Tb3+ gives J = 6).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpretation

  • 2S+1 tells unpaired-spin count (exchange favored).
  • L encodes orbital alignment and impacts anisotropy in solids.
  • J sets multiplet degeneracy (2J+1) and Zeeman behavior.

Parameter dependence

  • Higher ζ → larger fine-structure splitting among J values within the same term.
  • Different valence (changing n) can flip whether J is minimized or maximized.
  • Crystal fields split J multiplets further in real materials.

Alternative derivation idea

Construct microstates (ml, ms) consistent with Pauli, find maximum MS to fix S, then maximum ML consistent with that S to fix L, and finally apply Hund’s 3rd rule for J.

Concept check (with answers)

  • Q: Why is Eu2+ “S” term? A: Half-filled shell has symmetric ml occupation → L=0.
  • Q: Why Yb3+ is a doublet? A: One hole → S=1/2 → multiplicity 2.
  • Q: Why Tb3+ uses J=L+S? A: f^8 is more than half-filled.
