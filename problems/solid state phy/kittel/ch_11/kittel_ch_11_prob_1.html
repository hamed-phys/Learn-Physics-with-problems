Diamagnetic Susceptibility of Atomic Hydrogen (1s)

We compute the ground-state mean-square radius ⟨r²⟩ for the hydrogen 1s orbital and use quantum diamagnetism (vector potential & second-order-in-B energy shift) to obtain the molar diamagnetic susceptibility of atomic hydrogen.

Topic: Quantum magnetism (diamagnetism) Key output: χmol ≈ −2.36×10−6 cm³/mol State: Hydrogen 1s (isotropic)
Quick Summary
  • Problem: For hydrogen 1s, show ⟨r²⟩ = 3a0² and compute molar diamagnetic susceptibility.
  • Physics idea: Diamagnetism comes from orbital response to B via the A² term in the Hamiltonian.
  • Wavefunction: ψ1s(r) = (1/√(πa0³)) e−r/a0.
  • Governing operator: H = (1/2m)(p + (e/c)A)² + V(r) (Gaussian-cgs form used for χ in cm³/mol).
  • Key expectation: For isotropic 1s, ⟨r² − z²⟩ = (2/3)⟨r²⟩.
  • Energy shift: ΔE = (e²B²/(12mc²))⟨r²⟩ (diamagnetic, quadratic in B).
  • Atomic susceptibility: χatom = − e²⟨r²⟩/(6mc²).

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + units)

Magnetic response
  • M: magnetization (magnetic dipole moment per volume). SI: A/m. Gaussian-cgs: emu/cm³.
  • χ: magnetic susceptibility. In SI, dimensionless volume susceptibility: M = χH. In Gaussian-cgs, also dimensionless, but molar susceptibility is often quoted in cm³/mol.
  • χmol: molar susceptibility (Gaussian-cgs convention): χmol = NAχatom, reported in cm³/mol.
  • B, H: magnetic induction and magnetic field. In weakly magnetic materials, B≈H in Gaussian-cgs (for our purpose, linear response).
Atomic/quantum quantities
  • ψ(r): electron wavefunction (normalized). Probability density: |ψ|².
  • ρ(r): charge density (electron): ρ = −e|ψ|².
  • a0: Bohr radius ≈ 0.529×10−8 cm (given).
  • ⟨r²⟩: expectation value of r²: ⟨r²⟩ = ∫ r² |ψ|² d³r.
  • m: electron mass; e: elementary charge magnitude; c: speed of light (Gaussian-cgs usage below).

0.2 Physical meaning: what diamagnetism “is”

Diamagnetism is an induced magnetic moment that opposes the applied magnetic field. In atoms, it arises because the electron’s orbital motion adjusts in a magnetic field, producing a current loop whose moment is opposite to the applied B (Lenz’s-law flavor). For closed-shell / spherically symmetric states (like hydrogen 1s), this response is purely diamagnetic.

0.3 Governing law: minimal coupling Hamiltonian

In quantum mechanics, a magnetic field enters through the vector potential A with B = ∇×A. The canonical momentum operator is p = −iħ∇. The magnetic interaction is introduced by minimal coupling: 

Hamiltonian (Gaussian-cgs form):
H = (1/(2m)) [ p + (e/c) A(r) ]^2 + V(r)

with  p = -i ħ ∇,   B = ∇×A.
Copied ✓

0.4 Validity conditions and approximations (what regime we’re in)

Assumptions used
  • Weak-field (linear response): keep terms up to O(B²) in energy, which gives M ∝ B and χ constant.
  • Nonrelativistic electron: Schrödinger framework; spin paramagnetism ignored because the asked quantity is the orbital diamagnetic susceptibility of 1s.
  • Isotropic 1s state: spherical symmetry simplifies angular averages.
Limitations
  • Strong B fields can mix levels (beyond simple perturbation) and alter χ.
  • In real gases/solids, collisions, bonding, and many-electron effects shift χ.
  • Unit conventions differ (SI vs Gaussian-cgs). Here we target the stated answer in cm³/mol (cgs).

0.5 Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • Larger electron cloud ⇒ stronger diamagnetism: χ scales with ⟨r²⟩, so more spatially extended orbitals are more diamagnetic.
  • Closed-shell atoms are usually diamagnetic: no unpaired spins to give strong paramagnetism; orbital response dominates and is negative.
  • Quadratic energy shift: induced currents cost energy ~ B², so ΔE ∝ +B² while induced moment opposes B.

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

Pitfalls & sign conventions
  • Sign of electron charge: using e as magnitude; the electron charge is −e, but the diamagnetic contribution ends up negative susceptibility.
  • Gauge choice: A is not unique; choose a convenient gauge (symmetric gauge) but compute gauge-invariant observables (ΔE, χ).
  • Unit systems: the factor of c (and μ0 in SI) changes. The provided numerical answer matches Gaussian-cgs molar susceptibility.
  • Angular averages: for isotropic states, ⟨z²⟩ = ⟨x²⟩ = ⟨y²⟩ = (1/3)⟨r²⟩.
Math Insight: the core equation as a mathematical object Click to expand

The Hamiltonian with minimal coupling, H(A) = (1/2m)(p + (e/c)A)² + V, is a self-adjoint operator on an appropriate domain in L²(R³) (square-integrable wavefunctions), typically a Sobolev space ensuring pψ and Aψ are meaningful. Expanding (p + αA)² produces: 

(p + αA)^2 = p^2 + α(A·p + p·A) + α^2 A^2
with α = e/c.
Copied ✓

This is a controlled perturbation expansion in the field amplitude: the linear term (∝B) relates to paramagnetic response, while the quadratic term A² (∝B²) yields the diamagnetic energy shift. For a non-degenerate isotropic ground state, the leading orbital susceptibility can be extracted cleanly from the A² expectation value.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Even though hydrogen is the simplest atom, the same diamagnetic mechanism underlies how real materials respond to magnetic fields. Engineers care because magnetic response affects sensing, actuation, stability, and even manufacturability in magnetically sensitive systems.

Where this appears in industry (concrete areas)

Applications
  • Magnetic levitation & stabilization: diamagnetism contributes to forces in strong-field environments (e.g., diamagnetic materials in high-B magnets).
  • Precision instrumentation: susceptibility sets background signals in NMR/MRI, SQUID magnetometry, atomic clocks, and vacuum metrology.
  • Semiconductor & thin-film process control: orbital magnetism influences magneto-transport and calibration of magnetic-field tools used in fabrication.
  • Hydrogen technologies: in cryogenics and hydrogen storage, knowing magnetic properties helps with sensor design and diagnostics.
Why engineers care
  • Performance: magnetic susceptibility shifts resonance frequencies, affects field homogeneity, and alters sensor baselines.
  • Reliability: unwanted magnetic forces/torques can cause drift or vibration in precision assemblies.
  • Cost & manufacturability: material selection often balances magnetic “quietness” with mechanical and thermal constraints.

Measurement/validation methods in practice

  • Gouy balance / Evans balance: classical methods for susceptibility (especially fluids/gases).
  • SQUID magnetometry: ultra-sensitive χ measurements for solids, thin films, and small samples.
  • NMR/MRI susceptibility mapping: infer χ from field distortions and frequency shifts.

Design trade-offs & constraints

Non-idealities
  • Temperature dependence (paramagnetic impurities, Curie tails).
  • Microstructure and defects can add magnetic contributions.
  • In solids, bonding changes ⟨r²⟩-like measures (electron cloud redistribution).
From lab to product
  • Idealized single-atom χ becomes a material χ after accounting for density, bonding, mixtures, and processing history.
  • Engineers often build an uncertainty budget (tolerances, temperature drift, calibration offsets) because χ enters precision error models.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

1.1 Restate the problem

The hydrogen atom in its ground state has wavefunction ψ(r) = (1/√(πa03)) e−r/a0. Using the statistical interpretation (probability density |ψ|²), show that ⟨r²⟩ = 3a0². Then compute the molar diamagnetic susceptibility of atomic hydrogen and verify the quoted value (approximately −2.36×10−6 cm³/mol).

1.2 Given quantities

  • ψ(r) = (1/√(πa03)) e−r/a0 (hydrogen 1s).
  • a0 = 0.529×10−8 cm (given).
  • Charge density: ρ(x,y,z) = −e |ψ|² (electron charge −e).
  • Requested numerical check: χmol ≈ −2.36×10−6 cm³/mol.

1.3 Unknowns / targets

  • Compute and show: ⟨r²⟩ = 3a0².
  • Derive molar diamagnetic susceptibility: χmol (Gaussian-cgs, in cm³/mol).

1.4 Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Normalization & expectation values: ⟨f(r)⟩ = ∫ f(r)|ψ|² d³r, valid since ψ is a normalized bound-state wavefunction in L²(R³).
  • Minimal coupling to B-field: H = (1/2m)(p + (e/c)A)² + V, standard nonrelativistic magnetic interaction.
  • Symmetry: 1s state is isotropic ⇒ angular averages like ⟨z²⟩ = (1/3)⟨r²⟩.
  • Perturbation theory: in weak B, energy shift ΔE can be expanded to O(B²); diamagnetism comes from A² term directly.

1.5 Explicit assumptions

Assumptions (stated clearly)
  • Weak, uniform magnetic field B (spatially constant over atomic length scale a0).
  • Nonrelativistic electron; ignore fine structure and spin contributions (orbital diamagnetism focus).
  • Ground state is non-degenerate and spherically symmetric, enabling isotropic averages.
  • Use Gaussian-cgs susceptibility conventions to match units cm³/mol in the stated answer.

1.6 Possible approaches (compare)

Approach A: A² energy shift (best here)
  • Expand Hamiltonian with symmetric gauge A = (1/2)B×r.
  • For 1s, linear (paramagnetic) term vanishes; use ⟨A²⟩.
  • Pros: short, symmetry-based, directly yields χ in terms of ⟨r²⟩.
  • Cons: must track unit conventions carefully.
Approach B: Larmor precession / classical Langevin
  • Use Larmor diamagnetism result for bound electrons and relate to quantum ⟨r²⟩.
  • Pros: strong intuition.
  • Cons: less rigor unless derived from quantum Hamiltonian; still needs ⟨r²⟩.

1.7 Chosen approach

We choose Approach A because it is the most direct, mathematically controlled (operator expansion), and uses the 1s symmetry to simplify the angular part exactly.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

Minimal plan (5–12 steps)

  1. Normalize & set coordinates: Use spherical coordinates for ψ(r). (Tool: d³r = r² sinθ dr dθ dφ.)
  2. Compute ⟨r²⟩: Evaluate ⟨r²⟩ = ∫ r²|ψ|² d³r by reducing to a 1D radial integral. (Tool: Gamma-function integral.)
  3. Introduce B via vector potential: Choose symmetric gauge A = (1/2)B×r for uniform B. (Tool: ∇×A = B.)
  4. Expand Hamiltonian: H = H₀ + H₁ + H₂ with H₂ ∝ A². (Tool: operator algebra.)
  5. Use isotropy of 1s: Replace ⟨r² − z²⟩ with (2/3)⟨r²⟩. (Tool: symmetry / rotational invariance.)
  6. Get ΔE(B): Compute diamagnetic energy shift ΔE = ⟨H₂⟩ ∝ B².
  7. Extract induced moment: μ = −∂ΔE/∂B (for field along z). (Tool: thermodynamic definition of moment from energy.)
  8. Define χ: μ = χatom B ⇒ χatom is the proportionality constant.
  9. Convert to molar: χmol = NA χatom and plug ⟨r²⟩ = 3a₀².
  10. Numerical check: Insert constants in Gaussian-cgs to verify −2.36×10⁻⁶ cm³/mol.
Common mistakes (quick tips)
  • Forgetting the Jacobian: d³r = r² sinθ dr dθ dφ.
  • Mixing unit systems: SI adds μ₀; cgs has c in minimal coupling; the target number is in cgs molar units.
  • Wrong angular average: for isotropic states, ⟨z²⟩ = (1/3)⟨r²⟩, not (1/2).
  • Sign confusion: χ must be negative for diamagnetism.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Qualitative expectation (before calculating)

Hydrogen 1s is spherically symmetric with no permanent orbital magnetic moment. In a weak applied field, the induced orbital current should oppose the applied B (Lenz’s law), so we expect χ < 0. Also, a larger electron cloud means larger induced current loop area, so we expect |χ| to scale with ⟨r²⟩.

3.2 Mathematical model (variables, domain, operators, conditions)

Model statement
  • Domain: electron position r ∈ R³; use spherical coordinates (r,θ,φ), with r ∈ [0,∞), θ ∈ [0,π], φ ∈ [0,2π).
  • Wavefunction: ψ(r) = (1/√(πa₀³)) e^(−r/a₀), normalized: ∫ |ψ|² d³r = 1.
  • Operators: p = −iħ∇, B = ∇×A, Laplacian ∇² appears in H₀ via p² = −ħ²∇².
  • Boundary/regularity: ψ finite at r=0 and decays as r→∞ to be square-integrable.
  • Field: uniform static magnetic field B = B ẑ (constant in space and time).

3.3 Step 1 — Compute ⟨r²⟩ for the 1s state

The probability density is |ψ|². For ψ(r) = (1/√(πa₀³)) e^(−r/a₀), 

|ψ(r)|^2 = (1/(π a0^3)) e^(-2r/a0).
Copied ✓

The expectation value ⟨r²⟩ is: 

⟨r^2⟩ = ∫_{R^3} r^2 |ψ(r)|^2 d^3r
     = ∫_0^∞ ∫_0^π ∫_0^{2π} r^2 |ψ(r)|^2 (r^2 sinθ) dφ dθ dr.
Copied ✓

Perform the angular integrals first: ∫₀^{2π} dφ = 2π and ∫₀^{π} sinθ dθ = 2, so the angular factor is 4π. Then: 

⟨r^2⟩ = 4π ∫_0^∞ r^4 (1/(π a0^3)) e^(-2r/a0) dr
     = (4/a0^3) ∫_0^∞ r^4 e^(-2r/a0) dr.
Copied ✓

Evaluate the radial integral using the standard Gamma-function identity: for α>0 and integer n≥0, ∫₀^∞ r^n e^(−αr) dr = n! / α^(n+1). Here n=4 and α = 2/a₀: 

∫_0^∞ r^4 e^(-α r) dr = 4! / α^5.

With α = 2/a0:
∫_0^∞ r^4 e^(-(2/a0) r) dr
= 24 / (2/a0)^5
= 24 / (32/a0^5)
= (24 a0^5)/32
= (3/4) a0^5.
Copied ✓

Substitute back: 

⟨r^2⟩ = (4/a0^3) * (3/4) a0^5
     = 3 a0^2.
Copied ✓

3.4 Step 2 — Diamagnetic energy shift from minimal coupling

Choose a uniform magnetic field along z: B = B ẑ. A convenient gauge for uniform B is the symmetric gauge: 

A(r) = (1/2) (B × r).
For B = B z-hat:  A = (B/2)(-y, x, 0).
Then ∇×A = B (uniform), as required.
Copied ✓

Expand the Hamiltonian: H = (1/2m)[p + (e/c)A]² + V = H₀ + H₁ + H₂, where: 

H0 = p^2/(2m) + V(r)
H1 = (e/(2mc)) (A·p + p·A)      [linear in B]
H2 = (e^2/(2mc^2)) A^2          [quadratic in B]
Copied ✓

For the hydrogen 1s ground state (real, isotropic, non-degenerate), the leading orbital susceptibility is captured cleanly by the diamagnetic term ⟨H₂⟩, because the paramagnetic contribution is absent or cancels at this level for an s state in this context. We compute ⟨A²⟩: 

A^2 = (1/4) |B × r|^2
    = (1/4) (B^2 r^2 - (B·r)^2).

With B = B z-hat:  (B·r) = B z
⇒ A^2 = (B^2/4) (r^2 - z^2).
Copied ✓

Therefore, 

ΔE_dia(B) = ⟨H2⟩ = (e^2/(2mc^2)) ⟨A^2⟩
         = (e^2/(2mc^2)) * (B^2/4) ⟨r^2 - z^2⟩
         = (e^2 B^2 /(8 m c^2)) ⟨r^2 - z^2⟩.
Copied ✓

3.5 Step 3 — Use spherical symmetry to simplify ⟨r² − z²⟩

In an isotropic (spherically symmetric) state, the second moments are equal: ⟨x²⟩ = ⟨y²⟩ = ⟨z²⟩. Also, r² = x² + y² + z², so ⟨r²⟩ = ⟨x²⟩ + ⟨y²⟩ + ⟨z²⟩ = 3⟨z²⟩. Hence: 

⟨z^2⟩ = (1/3) ⟨r^2⟩
⇒ ⟨r^2 - z^2⟩ = ⟨r^2⟩ - ⟨z^2⟩ = (2/3) ⟨r^2⟩.
Copied ✓

Substitute into ΔE: 

ΔE_dia(B) = (e^2 B^2 /(8 m c^2)) * (2/3) ⟨r^2⟩
         = (e^2 B^2 /(12 m c^2)) ⟨r^2⟩.
Copied ✓

3.6 Step 4 — From energy shift to susceptibility

For a magnetic dipole moment μ aligned with B, the energy is E = −μB. For an induced moment in weak field, μ is proportional to B: μ = χatom B (Gaussian-cgs linear response for a single atom). Because ΔE is quadratic, we extract μ by differentiating: 

μ_ind(B) = - d(ΔE_dia)/dB
         = - d/dB [ (e^2 /(12 m c^2)) ⟨r^2⟩ B^2 ]
         = - (e^2 /(6 m c^2)) ⟨r^2⟩ B.
Copied ✓

Comparing μind(B) = χatom B gives the atomic susceptibility: 

χ_atom = - (e^2 /(6 m c^2)) ⟨r^2⟩.
Copied ✓

The molar susceptibility is χmol = NA χatom. Using ⟨r²⟩ = 3a₀²: 

⟨r^2⟩_(1s) = 3 a0^2

χ_atom = - (e^2 /(6 m c^2)) ⟨r^2⟩ = - (e^2 /(6 m c^2)) (3 a0^2)
      = - (e^2 a0^2)/(2 m c^2)

χ_mol = N_A χ_atom
      = - N_A (e^2 a0^2)/(2 m c^2)
      ≈ -2.36 × 10^(-6)  cm^3/mol   (Gaussian-cgs molar susceptibility).
Copied ✓

3.7 Numerical verification (to match the stated value)

Using Gaussian-cgs constants (representative values): NA = 6.022×10²³ mol⁻¹, e = 4.803×10⁻¹⁰ statC, m = 9.109×10⁻²⁸ g, c = 2.998×10¹⁰ cm/s, a₀ = 0.529×10⁻⁸ cm, we obtain χmol ≈ −2.37×10⁻⁶ cm³/mol, consistent with the requested −2.36×10⁻⁶ cm³/mol (rounding).

3.8 Sanity checks

Units / dimensions

In Gaussian-cgs, e² has dimensions of energy·length, and dividing by (m c²) gives length. Multiplying by a₀² gives length³, matching molar susceptibility units cm³/mol after multiplying by NA.

Signs & limits
  • Sign: χatom ∝ −⟨r²⟩ is negative ⇒ diamagnetic (opposes B).
  • Scaling: If the orbital size grows (a₀ larger), |χ| grows like a₀².
  • Weak field: ΔE ∝ B² ensures linear μ ∝ B for small B.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpret the final formula

The key result χatom = −(e²/(6mc²))⟨r²⟩ says: diamagnetism is geometric at leading order—set by the mean-square radius of the electron cloud. The factor e²/(mc²) reflects how strongly charge couples to magnetic vector potential in nonrelativistic dynamics.

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • ⟨r²⟩ ↑ ⇒ χ becomes more negative (stronger diamagnetism).
  • Mass m ↑ ⇒ weaker response (heavier charged particle resists orbital adjustment).
  • Charge magnitude e ↑ ⇒ stronger induced currents ⇒ stronger diamagnetism.
  • Relativistic corrections: would modify coefficients when velocities are high or in very strong B.

4.3 Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Alternative viewpoint: Larmor diamagnetism Click to expand

A complementary route uses Larmor precession: in a weak magnetic field, an electron orbit effectively acquires an angular frequency ωL proportional to B. This induces a circulating current and thus a magnetic moment opposing B. Quantum mechanically, averaging over orbital area yields a susceptibility proportional to the mean-square radius, reproducing the same scaling χ ∝ −⟨r²⟩. The A²-perturbation method used in the solution is essentially the operator-level derivation of this classical picture.

4.4 Concept check (self-test Q&A)

  • Q: Why does spherical symmetry imply ⟨z²⟩ = (1/3)⟨r²⟩? A: Rotational invariance makes x, y, z statistically equivalent, and r² = x² + y² + z².
  • Q: Why is ΔE ∝ B² for diamagnetism? A: The diamagnetic term comes from A², and for uniform B, A ∝ B, so A² ∝ B².
  • Q: Why is χ negative? A: The induced moment is μ = −dΔE/dB, and ΔE increases with B², so μ is opposite to B.
  • Q: If an orbital is more spread out, what happens to χ? A: ⟨r²⟩ increases, making χ more negative (stronger diamagnetism).
