Quick Summary

  • What this is: Find the longitudinal acoustic wave speed in a cubic crystal propagating along the [111] direction (body diagonal).
  • Key physics idea: In anisotropic solids, wave speed depends on direction because stress–strain coupling depends on crystal symmetry.
  • Governing model: Linear elastodynamics: ρ ∂²u/∂t² = ∇·σ with σ = C:ε and ε = (∇u + (∇u)ᵀ)/2.
  • Math structure: Plane-wave substitution turns the PDE into an eigenvalue problem (Christoffel equation) for polarization and v = ω/k.
  • Result type: Symbolic closed form in terms of elastic constants C11, C12, C44 and density ρ.
  • Final result: v_L([111]) = √((C11 + 2C12 + 4C44)/(3ρ)).
  • Physical meaning: The longitudinal mode samples a weighted combination of stiffnesses; C44 enters strongly along [111] because shear components are inevitably involved by symmetry.
  • Engineering use: Predict acoustic time-of-flight, resonance frequencies, and elastic property extraction (ultrasound, NDE, MEMS, crystal wafer metrology).

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • u(x,t): displacement vector field (m).
  • ρ: mass density (kg·m−3).
  • ε: small-strain tensor (dimensionless), ε = (∇u + (∇u)ᵀ)/2.
  • σ: Cauchy stress tensor (Pa).
  • C: fourth-order stiffness tensor (Pa). For cubic crystals: three independent constants C11, C12, C44 (Pa).
  • k: wave number magnitude (m−1), ω: angular frequency (s−1), v = ω/k (m·s−1).
  • n: unit propagation direction vector (dimensionless). [111] means n = (1,1,1)/√3 in cube-edge coordinates.

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Elastic waves are restoring-force waves: stiffness provides the restoring stress, inertia (density) provides the lag.
  • In crystals, stiffness depends on direction: pushing along different lattice directions “feels” different combinations of bond/plane resistances.
  • “Longitudinal” means particle motion (polarization) parallel to propagation: u ∥ n.
  • In anisotropic media, longitudinal and transverse are best understood as eigenmodes of the Christoffel matrix; “mostly longitudinal” becomes “exactly longitudinal” only in symmetry directions (like [111] for cubic).

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

  • Linear momentum balance (no body forces): ρ ∂²u/∂t² = ∇·σ.
  • Linear (Hooke) constitutive law: σ = C:ε.
  • Small-strain kinematics: valid when displacement gradients are small: |∂u_i/∂x_j| ≪ 1.
  • Homogeneous, infinite (or locally uniform) medium: stiffnesses and density constant in space; boundaries far away so plane waves are meaningful.
Validity regime: This derivation assumes linear elasticity, no damping, and wavelengths large enough that continuum elasticity applies (typically much larger than the lattice spacing).

Common models/approximations (and why)

  • Plane-wave ansatz: assumes spatial sinusoid and single frequency; justified by linearity and translational invariance: Fourier modes decouple.
  • Christoffel formulation: turns PDEs into a finite-dimensional eigenproblem; efficient for directional wave speeds.
  • Ignoring dispersion: in classical elasticity of an ideal continuum, v is independent of k. (Real crystals can show dispersion at very high frequencies; not here.)

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • Isotropic limit: if the crystal behaved isotropically with Lamé constants λ, μ, then v_L = √((λ+2μ)/ρ). Cubic crystals mimic this only if C44 = (C11 − C12)/2.
  • Stiffer → faster: increasing any stiffness constant that contributes to the mode increases v; increasing density decreases v.
  • Direction matters: along [100] the longitudinal speed is √(C11/ρ), but along [111] shear stiffness C44 contributes strongly.

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Confusing Miller indices [hkl] with a unit vector: you must normalize.
  • Using isotropic formulas in anisotropic crystals without checking symmetry.
  • Dropping off-diagonal Christoffel terms; they control mode coupling.
  • Mixing Voigt notation with tensor notation without consistent factors of 2 (especially for shear strains). In the Christoffel form shown below, the standard cubic formulas already account for these conventions.
Math Insight: The wave-speed problem is an eigenvalue problem for a symmetric positive-definite matrix (the Christoffel matrix). Its eigenvalues are “directional stiffnesses” and the square-roots give phase velocities.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (concrete areas)

  • Ultrasonic nondestructive evaluation (NDE): Time-of-flight depends on directional wave speed; single-crystal turbine blades and semiconductor boules require anisotropic corrections.
  • Semiconductor wafer metrology: Elastic constants and crystal orientation influence acoustic resonances; used in process control and defect detection.
  • MEMS/SAW devices: Surface and bulk acoustic wave devices rely on precise acoustic velocities (frequency set by geometry and v).
  • Reliability & packaging: Thermal-stress waves, cracking, and delamination risk can involve anisotropic elastic wave propagation and reflections.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: resonance frequency f scales like v/L; wrong v → wrong device frequency.
  • Safety & reliability: accurate wave speeds improve damage localization in NDE and structural health monitoring.
  • Cost & yield: in wafer fabs, faster and more accurate acoustic-based inspection improves throughput and yield.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Pulse-echo ultrasonics: measure travel time through known thickness; invert for v.
  • Brillouin light scattering: optical measurement of phonon velocities (directional) in crystals.
  • Resonant ultrasound spectroscopy (RUS): fit resonance spectrum to obtain elastic constants.

Design trade-offs and real-world constraints

  • Orientation tolerances: small miscuts from ideal [111] change the effective stiffness combination and thus v.
  • Temperature dependence: Cij(T) and ρ(T) vary with temperature; product specs often require temperature compensation.
  • Damping and defects: real materials have attenuation and scattering; the idealized model predicts phase speed, not loss.
From lab to product: how the ideal model is adapted tap to expand
In products, engineers typically: (i) use measured Cij(T) tables rather than constants, (ii) include damping via complex stiffness or viscoelastic models, (iii) incorporate finite geometry/boundary conditions using FEM, and (iv) treat orientation uncertainty statistically for robust design.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement

Show that the longitudinal wave velocity for a plane elastic wave propagating in the [111] direction of a cubic crystal is v_L = √((C11 + 2C12 + 4C44)/(3ρ)). You are hinted to use a plane-wave form and a component elastodynamic equation (similar to Eq. (57a)).

Given

  • Cubic elastic constants: C11, C12, C44 (Pa).
  • Density: ρ (kg·m−3).
  • Propagation direction: [111] (body diagonal) in cube-edge coordinates.
  • Linear elastodynamic PDEs (component forms like Eq. (57a)).

Unknowns

  • Longitudinal phase velocity v_L along [111].

What must be proved

  • Derive v_L([111]) = √((C11 + 2C12 + 4C44)/(3ρ)).

Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Linearity: allows superposition and Fourier/plane-wave decomposition.
  • Homogeneity: constant ρ and Cij makes plane waves exact solutions (no mode conversion in the bulk).
  • Cubic symmetry: reduces stiffness to three constants and guarantees special symmetry directions (including [111]) where eigenvectors are simple.
Assumptions (explicit):
  • Small-strain linear elasticity; no damping; no body forces.
  • Infinite (or effectively unbounded) medium so boundary conditions are not required for the bulk wave speed.
  • Plane-wave solutions with constant amplitude (or slowly varying compared to wavelength).

Possible approaches (compare)

Approach A: Component PDE substitution (Eq. 57a style)

  • Pros: Directly uses the given hint; shows how derivatives reduce to algebra with plane waves.
  • Cons: Can be algebra-heavy unless organized into matrix form.

Approach B: Christoffel matrix (standard anisotropic acoustics)

  • Pros: Clean, systematic; immediately an eigenvalue problem; naturally handles anisotropy.
  • Cons: Requires knowing/deriving the Christoffel form (but we will derive it from the PDE, so it stays self-contained).

Choice: We will combine both: start from the elastodynamic PDEs (as in Eq. 57a) and organize them into the Christoffel eigenvalue problem. This gives maximum mathematical clarity with minimal “magic jumps.”

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Set the mathematical model: define domain (infinite solid), coordinates along cube edges, unknown field u(x,t), and governing PDEs ρ ü = ∇·(C:ε).
  2. Insert plane-wave ansatz: u(x,t) = U exp(i(k·x − ωt)) and compute how ∂/∂x_j becomes i k_j, and ∂²/∂t² becomes −ω².
  3. Reduce PDE → algebra: obtain a linear system Γ(n) U = ρ v² U, where n = k/|k| and v = ω/|k|.
  4. Write Γ for cubic symmetry: express the Christoffel matrix entries in terms of C11, C12, C44 and direction cosines n1,n2,n3.
  5. Specialize to [111]: set n1=n2=n3=1/√3 and simplify the matrix.
  6. Identify longitudinal eigenvector: show that U ∥ n (i.e., U ∝ (1,1,1)) is an eigenvector by symmetry (all components equal).
  7. Compute eigenvalue: for a “diagonal=a, off-diagonal=b” matrix, eigenvalue for (1,1,1) is a+2b.
  8. Convert eigenvalue → speed: use ρ v_L² = λ_L to get the final expression.
  9. Sanity checks: dimensions, isotropic limit, and qualitative parameter dependence.
Common mistakes
  • Using [111] as (1,1,1) without normalization; wave speeds depend on the unit vector n.
  • Dropping the (C12 + C44) coupling terms in the off-diagonal Christoffel entries.
  • Confusing phase velocity v=ω/k with group velocity (not needed here).
  • Sign slips: ∂²/∂t² on exp(i(k·x − ωt)) gives −ω².

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition (before calculation)

A longitudinal wave speed scales like √(effective stiffness / density). Along [111], the deformation is not purely “axial” in the cube-edge basis; symmetry forces a mixture of normal and shear strain components. Therefore, the effective stiffness must combine C11, C12, and C44. We expect a weighted average, and the final expression indeed has a 4C44 contribution.

Step 1 — Mathematical model (variables, domain, operators)

Let the displacement field be u(x,t) = (u1,u2,u3) in a Cartesian coordinate system aligned with cube edges (x1,x2,x3) = (x,y,z). We consider a homogeneous cubic crystal filling all space: x ∈ ℝ³, t ≥ 0.

Boundary/initial conditions (modeling choice): For a bulk plane-wave speed, we take an effectively infinite domain, so boundary conditions are not needed. A typical initial condition would be u(x,0) and u̇(x,0) that are sinusoidal or localized; by linearity, Fourier modes evolve independently.

Governing equations (no body force f=0):

Key equations: linear elastodynamics (operator form)
ρ ∂²u/∂t² = ∇·σ
σ = C:ε
ε = (∇u + (∇u)ᵀ)/2

Here ∇u is the displacement gradient with components (∂u_i/∂x_j), and ∇·σ is the divergence of the stress tensor: (∇·σ)_i = ∂σ_ij/∂x_j (Einstein summation).

Step 2 — Cubic constitutive structure (what “cubic” means mathematically)

A cubic crystal has three independent stiffness constants in Voigt notation: C11, C12, C44. This symmetry implies that, in cube-edge coordinates, the Christoffel matrix (defined below) has a specific form in terms of the direction cosines n1,n2,n3.

Optional note: Why symmetry reduces parameters tap to expand
The stiffness tensor C_ijkl has up to 21 independent components in the most general linear elastic solid. Cubic symmetry (invariance under 90° rotations about cube axes and permutations) forces many components to be equal or zero, leaving only three independent constants. This is a group-theoretic constraint: C must be invariant under the cubic point group.

Step 3 — Plane-wave ansatz and derivative rules (no “magic jumps”)

Assume a harmonic plane wave:

Plane-wave form (hint used)
u(x,t) = U · exp(i(k·x − ω t))
k = wavevector, ω = angular frequency, U = constant polarization vector

For this ansatz, partial derivatives act as multiplication:

  • ∂/∂x_j gives i k_j because ∂/∂x_j (k·x) = k_j.
  • ∂²/∂t² gives −ω² because ∂/∂t (−ω t) = −ω and squaring yields (−iω)² = −ω².
Derivative substitutions
∂u/∂x_j = i k_j u
∂²u/∂x_j∂x_l = − k_j k_l u
∂²u/∂t² = − ω² u

These steps are valid because the PDE is linear with constant coefficients, so the exponential is an eigenfunction of derivative operators.

Step 4 — PDE → algebraic eigenvalue problem (Christoffel form)

Start from ρ ü = ∇·σ. Using σ = C:ε and ε = sym(∇u), one can show (standard in anisotropic acoustics) that plane waves satisfy:

Christoffel equation (eigenproblem)
Γ(n) U = ρ v² U
v = ω/|k|,  n = k/|k|
Γ_ij(n) = C_ijkl n_k n_l

Why this step is valid: after substituting the plane wave, every spatial derivative contributes a factor of i k. Two spatial derivatives give k_k k_l. Factoring out |k|² leaves only the direction n = k/|k|. The remaining relationship is linear in U, hence an eigenvalue problem.

Link to Eq. (57a) style (component PDE) — what’s happening under the hood tap to expand
Equations like Eq. (57a) are second-order linear PDEs in u,v,w with mixed derivatives. When you insert exp(i(k·x−ωt)), each mixed derivative ∂²/∂x∂y becomes −k_x k_y, etc. Collecting coefficients of U=(U1,U2,U3) yields a 3×3 matrix multiplying U, i.e. the Christoffel matrix. This repackages the three coupled PDEs into one compact eigenproblem.

Step 5 — Christoffel matrix for cubic symmetry

For a cubic crystal in cube-edge coordinates, the Christoffel matrix entries in terms of direction cosines n = (n1,n2,n3) are:

Γ(n) for a cubic crystal
Γ11 = C11 n1^2 + C44 (n2^2 + n3^2)
Γ22 = C11 n2^2 + C44 (n1^2 + n3^2)
Γ33 = C11 n3^2 + C44 (n1^2 + n2^2)

Γ12 = (C12 + C44) n1 n2
Γ13 = (C12 + C44) n1 n3
Γ23 = (C12 + C44) n2 n3

Γ is symmetric: Γij = Γji

Symmetry (Γ=Γᵀ) ensures real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors (mathematically: a symmetric matrix defines a self-adjoint operator on ℝ³).

Step 6 — Specialize to the [111] direction

The direction [111] corresponds to the unit vector n = (1,1,1)/√3, so:

  • n1 = n2 = n3 = 1/√3
  • n1² = n2² = n3² = 1/3
  • n1 n2 = n1 n3 = n2 n3 = 1/3

Plug these into the cubic formulas:

Diagonal terms

Γ11 = C11(1/3) + C44( (1/3)+(1/3) ) = (C11 + 2C44)/3
Γ22 = (C11 + 2C44)/3
Γ33 = (C11 + 2C44)/3

Off-diagonal terms

Γ12 = (C12 + C44)(1/3)
Γ13 = (C12 + C44)(1/3)
Γ23 = (C12 + C44)(1/3)

Therefore, the Christoffel matrix has the special “all-diagonal-equal, all-offdiagonal-equal” form: 

Let a = (C11 + 2C44)/3
Let b = (C12 + C44)/3

Γ = [ a  b  b
      b  a  b
      b  b  a ]

Step 7 — Identify the longitudinal eigenmode and compute its eigenvalue

A longitudinal mode has polarization parallel to propagation: U ∥ n. For [111], that means U proportional to (1,1,1).

Verify this is an eigenvector by direct multiplication. Let e = (1,1,1). 

Γ e = [ a  b  b ] [1]   [ a + b + b ]
      [ b  a  b ] [1] = [ b + a + b ] = (a + 2b) [1,1,1]
      [ b  b  a ] [1]   [ b + b + a ]

So e is indeed an eigenvector with eigenvalue λ_L = a + 2b.

Substitute the definitions of a and b: 

λ_L = a + 2b
    = (C11 + 2C44)/3 + 2 (C12 + C44)/3
    = (C11 + 2C44 + 2C12 + 2C44)/3
    = (C11 + 2C12 + 4C44)/3

Step 8 — Convert eigenvalue to wave speed

The Christoffel equation is Γ U = ρ v² U. For an eigenmode, Γ U = λ U, so λ = ρ v². Hence the longitudinal speed is:

Final result (boxed)
v_L([111]) = sqrt( (C11 + 2 C12 + 4 C44) / (3 ρ) )

Sanity checks

Dimensional analysis:
  • Cij has units Pa = kg·m−1·s−2.
  • Cij/ρ has units (kg·m−1·s−2)/(kg·m−3) = m2·s−2.
  • Square root gives m·s−1, correct for a speed.
Limiting-case / isotropic check: In an isotropic solid, cubic constants satisfy C44 = (C11 − C12)/2. Substitute into the numerator: 
C11 + 2C12 + 4C44
= C11 + 2C12 + 4*(C11 − C12)/2
= C11 + 2C12 + 2(C11 − C12)
= 3C11
Then v_L([111]) = √(3C11/(3ρ)) = √(C11/ρ), which matches isotropy (direction independence).
Physical interpretation: The factor 4C44 indicates that along [111], the longitudinal wave “feels” significant shear stiffness. This is a geometric/symmetry consequence: the strain induced by motion along the body diagonal is not purely normal in the cube-edge basis.
Nondimensionalization / scaling (optional): why only ratios matter tap to expand
Introduce a characteristic stiffness C0 and define dimensionless constants ĉ11=C11/C0, ĉ12=C12/C0, ĉ44=C44/C0. Then v_L = √(C0/ρ) · √((ĉ11 + 2ĉ12 + 4ĉ44)/3). This shows the speed scale is √(stiffness/density), times a pure number determined by anisotropy and direction.
Copy-friendly final answer (plain text)
Longitudinal phase velocity along [111] in a cubic crystal:
v_L = sqrt((C11 + 2 C12 + 4 C44)/(3 ρ)).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the formula: what each term controls

  • C11: resistance to normal strain along cube axes; contributes to all directions.
  • C12: coupling between normal stresses and orthogonal normal strains; enters because deformation along [111] correlates axes.
  • C44: shear stiffness; enters with a large weight (4) because the mode’s strain state includes shear components when expressed in the cube-edge basis.
  • ρ: inertia; larger density slows waves as v ∝ 1/√ρ.

Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Increasing C11, C12, or C44 increases v_L (stiffer solid → faster wave).
  • Increasing ρ decreases v_L (heavier solid → slower wave).
  • Because the numerator is linear in the stiffnesses, small fractional changes propagate as: δv/v ≈ (1/2) δN/N where N = C11 + 2C12 + 4C44.

Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of the Christoffel matrix, one can derive the same result using an energy/variational viewpoint: for harmonic motion, the phase velocity relates to the ratio of elastic energy density to kinetic energy density for a mode. Using symmetry for [111] and a trial displacement u ∥ (1,1,1), compute strain components, then compute W = (1/2) ε:C:ε and T = (1/2) ρ |u̇|². Minimizing W/T over admissible polarizations reproduces the eigenvalue λ_L.

Math Insight (revisited): The operator governing waves is a symmetric, positive-definite “stiffness operator” acting on polarization vectors. For each direction n, the Christoffel matrix Γ(n) is the finite-dimensional representation of that operator. The problem is essentially a spectral decomposition (eigenvalues/eigenvectors), just like normal modes of coupled oscillators.

Concept checks (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why does v not depend on k here? A: The PDE has constant coefficients and no internal length scale; after substituting plane waves, k factors out, leaving only direction n.
  • Q: Why is the eigenproblem guaranteed to have real eigenvalues? A: Γ is symmetric (from elastic reciprocity and symmetry of C), hence self-adjoint → real spectrum.
  • Q: What does “longitudinal” mean in the eigenproblem language? A: The eigenvector (polarization) is parallel to n; i.e., U = α n.
  • Q: If ρ doubles (stiffness fixed), what happens to v_L? A: It decreases by 1/√2.
