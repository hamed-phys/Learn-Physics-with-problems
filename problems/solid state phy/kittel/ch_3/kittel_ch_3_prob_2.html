Quick Summary

  • What this is about: comparing how strongly neon atoms bind (cohesive energy) in two crystal packings: bcc and fcc.
  • Key physics idea: in rare-gas solids, bonding is dominated by van der Waals attraction + Pauli repulsion, well-modeled by the Lennard–Jones (12–6) pair potential.
  • Governing equation: per-atom lattice energy is a pair-sum over neighbors: U(r)=½Σj&phi(rj) with &phi(r)=4ε[(σ/r)12-( σ/r)6].
  • Mathematical core: reduce to a one-variable minimization problem using nondimensionalization and the lattice sums An=Σ'j pj-n.
  • Equilibrium energy: after minimization, the minimum per-atom energy is Umin=- (ε A62)/(2A12).
  • Result type: numeric ratio (dimensionless) because ε cancels: Ecoh(bcc)/Ecoh(fcc) ≈ 0.958.
  • Physical meaning: fcc binds slightly more strongly than bcc for a Lennard–Jones solid (bcc has ~4% lower cohesive energy).
Key equation to copy
U(r) = 2 ε [ (σ/r)^12 A12  −  (σ/r)^6 A6 ]

Here A6 = Σ′ p_j^−6, A12 = Σ′ p_j^−12, and p_j = r_j/r.

Final answer to copy
E_coh(bcc)/E_coh(fcc)
= (A6_bcc^2/A12_bcc) / (A6_fcc^2/A12_fcc)
≈ 0.958

Ratio < 1 ⇒ fcc is energetically favored for LJ neon.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • Pair potential (Lennard–Jones 12–6): φ(r) = 4ε[(σ/r)12 − (σ/r)6]
    • r (m): separation between two atoms.
    • ε (J): depth of the potential well (energy scale).
    • σ (m): length scale where φ crosses zero (often called the “collision diameter”).
  • Lattice energy per atom: U = (1/2)Σj φ(rj) (J/atom) where the sum runs over all other atoms j; the factor 1/2 avoids double counting pairs.
  • Cohesive energy (engineering convention): Ecoh = −Umin (positive number, J/atom), i.e., energy required to separate atoms to infinity at 0 K (in this simplified model).
  • Geometry scaling: pj = rj/r where r is the nearest-neighbor distance (m) and rj are all neighbor-shell distances.

0.2 Physical meaning (intuition)

  • The r−12 term models steep repulsion (Pauli exclusion due to overlapping electron clouds).
  • The r−6 term models dispersion attraction (induced-dipole/van der Waals interactions).
  • A crystal “chooses” a nearest-neighbor distance r that minimizes total energy: attraction dominates at larger r, repulsion dominates at smaller r.

0.3 Validity conditions (assumptions + limitations)

Model assumptions
  • Pairwise additivity: total energy is a sum of pair potentials (neglects 3-body terms like Axilrod–Teller).
  • Central forces only: φ depends only on r, so the lattice sum depends only on distances (not angles).
  • 0 K static lattice: no thermal vibrations, no zero-point motion corrections (important for light rare gases).
  • Infinite perfect crystal: no surfaces, defects, or finite-size effects.

0.4 Common models/approximations used here

  • Dimensionless lattice sums compress the entire geometry into constants: An = Σ′ pj−n. Once A6 and A12 are known for a structure, energy minimization becomes algebraic.
  • Nondimensionalization using x = (σ/r)6 reduces the problem to a quadratic polynomial in x.

0.5 Mini intuition examples (quick)

  • If the lattice were “more crowded” (larger A12 relative to A6), repulsion rises faster and equilibrium shifts to larger r.
  • If attraction dominates (large A6), the minimum energy becomes more negative (stronger binding).

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Double counting: forgetting the 1/2 factor in U = (1/2)Σφ.
  • Sign convention: cohesive energy is positive; lattice energy at equilibrium is negative.
  • Which distance defines pj: here pj is normalized by nearest-neighbor distance r (not lattice parameter a).
  • Equilibrium vs fixed r: cohesive energy must use the minimized energy (different structures generally have different optimal r).
Optional deeper note: why no ∇ operators appear here

This is a discrete many-body energy minimization in a perfect periodic lattice. There is no field variable u(x) governed by a PDE (so operators like ∇· or ∇² are not central). If we were doing elasticity (continuum limit), then a displacement field u(x) would enter and equilibrium would involve ∇·σ = 0. Here we stay at the atomistic level with algebraic minimization.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (concrete areas)

  • Cryogenics & refrigeration: neon is used in low-temperature systems; understanding cohesive energy supports predictions of solidification behavior and thermal properties.
  • Vacuum & surface engineering: rare-gas adsorption and condensation (e.g., neon films) depend on dispersion forces; LJ-like models are baseline tools for process design.
  • Semiconductor & optics manufacturing: inert gas environments and cryopumping rely on how rare gases bind/condense on cold surfaces; cohesion impacts sticking probabilities and frost formation.
  • Molecular simulation in materials design: LJ potentials are the “Hello World” for MD/MC; lattice sums validate codes and parameter choices against known crystalline benchmarks.

Why engineers care

  • Reliability: condensation/solidification can clog lines, alter heat transfer, or contaminate surfaces.
  • Efficiency: phase behavior affects cryogenic cycle efficiency and cold-trap performance.
  • Cost and manufacturability: using simplified models (like LJ + lattice sums) gives fast estimates before expensive experiments or high-fidelity simulations.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • X-ray or neutron diffraction to identify structure (fcc vs bcc) and lattice spacing.
  • Calorimetry for latent heats and cohesive-energy-related thermodynamic quantities.
  • Elastic constants / sound speed measurements (indirectly constrain interatomic potential curvature near equilibrium).

Design trade-offs & non-idealities

  • Many-body forces: real rare-gas solids include 3-body dispersion corrections; LJ is a first approximation.
  • Quantum effects: zero-point motion can be non-negligible (especially for He/Ne), shifting equilibrium properties.
  • Defects and finite size: in thin films or nanoparticles, surfaces dominate; bulk lattice sums become insufficient.
From lab to product

Engineers often start with a simple pair potential (LJ) for rapid screening and then “upgrade”: fit a more accurate potential (e.g., including 3-body terms), validate with diffraction/thermodynamics, and finally embed it in system-level models (heat transfer, flow, contamination risk).

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

1.1 Restate the problem

Using the Lennard–Jones interatomic potential, compute the ratio of cohesive energies for neon in a bcc lattice versus an fcc lattice. The problem provides lattice sums for the structures (dimensionless distance sums over all neighbors): Σ′ pj−12 and Σ′ pj−6.

Given
  • LJ potential: φ(r)=4ε[(σ/r)12 − (σ/r)6].
  • For bcc: A12bcc=Σ′pj−12=9.11418, A6bcc=Σ′pj−6=12.2533.
  • For fcc: the standard LJ lattice sums are A12fcc≈12.1319 and A6fcc≈14.4539 (commonly tabulated alongside bcc).
Unknowns / target
  • Equilibrium lattice energy per atom Umin for each structure.
  • Cohesive energy Ecoh=−Umin for each structure.
  • Ratio: Ecoh(bcc)/Ecoh(fcc).

1.2 Mathematical physics model (explicit)

Mathematical model statement
  • State variable: nearest-neighbor distance r ∈ (0,∞).
  • Domain: an infinite periodic lattice (bcc or fcc). Neighbor positions are discrete points Rj.
  • Coordinate system: Euclidean 3D; but due to spherical symmetry of φ(r), only distances rj=|Rj| matter.
  • Energy functional (discrete sum): U(r)=½Σj≠0&phi(rj) with rj=pj r.
  • Boundary condition: implicitly rj→∞ terms vanish; the lattice sums converge for n=6,12.
  • Equilibrium condition: minimize U(r) over r: dU/dr=0 and d²U/dr²>0 at the minimum.

1.3 Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Pairwise additivity: allows total energy as a sum of pair potentials (the central modeling assumption).
  • Symmetry / periodicity: distances occur in shells; their contributions are captured by lattice sums A6, A12.
  • Calculus of minima (1D): since U depends only on a single scale r, equilibrium is found by dU/dr=0.

1.4 Assumptions (explicit)

Assumptions used in the solution
  • Perfect infinite bcc/fcc lattices; one-atom basis; no defects.
  • Atoms interact via LJ 12–6 only; no many-body corrections.
  • We compute cohesive energy at equilibrium (minimized over r) for each lattice.

1.5 Possible approaches (compare)

  • Approach A: Direct r-minimization — write U(r) in terms of r, differentiate, solve. Pros: transparent physics. Cons: algebra can look messy if not nondimensionalized.
  • Approach B: Nondimensionalization with x=(σ/r)6 — reduces U to a quadratic in x and minimization becomes one line. Pros: clean math, easy ratio. Cons: requires careful substitution interpretation.
  • Approach C: Use known LJ equilibrium energy formula per lattice — if memorized/tabulated. Pros: fastest. Cons: least educational; easy to misuse conventions.

We choose Approach B because it is mathematically rigorous, shows why the ratio is independent of (ε,σ), and avoids “magic jumps.”

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Write the per-atom energy U(r) = (1/2)Σφ(pjr). (Goal: convert geometry into sums.)
  2. Insert LJ potential and factor out common powers of r. (Tool: algebra; meaning: separate repulsion/attraction contributions.)
  3. Define lattice sums A12=Σ′pj−12, A6=Σ′pj−6. (Meaning: structure fingerprints.)
  4. Nondimensionalize with x=(σ/r)6 to rewrite U as a quadratic in x. (Tool: scaling; meaning: reduce to a single parameter.)
  5. Minimize U(x) by dU/dx=0 (or complete the square). (Tool: calculus; meaning: equilibrium spacing.)
  6. Extract Umin and define Ecoh=−Umin. (Meaning: binding strength.)
  7. Form the ratio Ecoh(bcc)/Ecoh(fcc); cancel ε and confirm it’s dimensionless. (Meaning: relative stability.)
  8. Plug lattice sums and compute the final number (~0.958). (Meaning: fcc slightly favored.)
Common mistakes
  • Using U(r) at an arbitrary r instead of the minimized Umin.
  • Forgetting the factor 1/2 in the pair sum.
  • Mixing definitions of pj (nearest-neighbor distance vs lattice constant).
  • Reporting Umin (negative) as “cohesive energy” without flipping the sign.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Physical intuition first (qualitative expectation)

For a Lennard–Jones solid, close packing (fcc/hcp) usually yields a slightly more negative minimum energy than bcc, because the balance of attraction (r−6) and repulsion (r−12) over many neighbors tends to favor the denser, more efficiently packed arrangement. So we expect: Ecoh(bcc) < Ecoh(fcc) ⇒ ratio < 1.

3.2 Step 1: Write the discrete lattice energy per atom

Consider one atom at the origin interacting with all other atoms at positions Rj. The total potential energy of the crystal is the sum over all pairs. Per atom we write 

U(r) = (1/2) Σ_{j≠0} φ(r_j),   with   r_j = p_j r

Why this step is valid: the factor 1/2 enforces correct pair counting (each pair i–j would otherwise appear twice). The geometry enters only through the set of dimensionless ratios pj.

3.3 Step 2: Insert Lennard–Jones and factor out the r dependence

Insert φ(r)=4ε[(σ/r)12−(σ/r)6] with r→rj=pjr:

U(r) = (1/2) Σ_{j≠0} 4 ε [ (σ/(p_j r))^12  −  (σ/(p_j r))^6 ]

Now pull out the common factors (linearity of summation): 

U(r) = 2 ε [ (σ/r)^12 Σ' p_j^(-12)  −  (σ/r)^6 Σ' p_j^(-6) ]

Why this step is valid: since (σ/(pjr))n = (σ/r)n pj−n, and Σ is linear, we can separate the r-dependent scaling from the geometric sums.

3.4 Step 3: Define lattice sums (structure constants)

Define the dimensionless lattice sums (the prime means j≠0, i.e., excluding the origin): 

A12 = Σ' p_j^(-12),      A6 = Σ' p_j^(-6)

Then the energy becomes

U(r) = 2 ε [ (σ/r)^12 A12  −  (σ/r)^6 A6 ]

3.5 Step 4: Nondimensionalization (scaling argument)

Notice two powers: (σ/r)12 and (σ/r)6. A standard scaling trick is to define a single dimensionless variable 

x = (σ/r)^6   (dimensionless)

Then (σ/r)12 = x2. Substitute: 

U(x) = 2 ε [ A12 x^2  −  A6 x ]

Dimensional analysis check: x is dimensionless; A6, A12 are dimensionless sums; therefore U has units of ε (J), as required.

3.6 Step 5: Minimize the energy (calculus, no “magic jumps”)

Since x=(σ/r)6 maps r∈(0,∞) monotonically to x∈(0,∞), minimizing U(r) is equivalent to minimizing U(x). Differentiate and set to zero: 

dU/dx = 2 ε ( 2 A12 x  −  A6 ) = 0

Therefore

x* = A6 / (2 A12)

Why the minimum is guaranteed: U(x)=2ε(A12x2−A6x) is a convex quadratic in x because A12>0, so d²U/dx² = 4εA12 > 0.

Optional: complete-the-square version (same result) 
U(x) = 2 ε A12 [ (x − A6/(2A12))^2  −  (A6/(2A12))^2 ]

The squared term is ≥0, so the minimum occurs at x=A6/(2A12) and equals the negative constant term.

3.7 Step 6: Evaluate the minimum energy and cohesive energy

Substitute x* into U(x): 

U_min = 2 ε [ A12 (A6^2/(4 A12^2))  −  A6 (A6/(2 A12)) ]

Now simplify carefully (showing each algebra step):

U_min = 2 ε [ A6^2/(4 A12)  −  A6^2/(2 A12) ]
      = 2 ε [ − A6^2/(4 A12) ]
      = − ε A6^2/(2 A12)

Define cohesive energy (positive): 

E_coh = −U_min = ε A6^2 / (2 A12)

3.8 Step 7: Form the ratio bcc/fcc (ε cancels)

For structure S ∈ {bcc, fcc}: EcohS=ε (A6S)2/(2A12S). Therefore 

E_coh(bcc)/E_coh(fcc)
= [ (A6_bcc^2)/(A12_bcc) ] / [ (A6_fcc^2)/(A12_fcc) ]

Why ε cancels: it is a global energy scale in the LJ potential; when comparing two lattices of the same material, only the geometric sums differ.

3.9 Step 8: Insert numbers and compute

Use the lattice sums:

  • bcc: A12bcc=9.11418, A6bcc=12.2533.
  • fcc: A12fcc≈12.1319, A6fcc≈14.4539 (standard tabulated values).
Ratio ≈ (12.2533^2 / 9.11418) / (14.4539^2 / 12.1319)
      ≈ 0.958  (≈0.957 with rounded sums)
Final result (boxed)
E_coh(bcc)/E_coh(fcc) ≈ 0.958
(fcc binds slightly more strongly than bcc for an LJ neon crystal)

3.10 Sanity checks

  • Units: ratio is dimensionless; Ecoh has units of ε (J), consistent.
  • Sign: Umin is negative; Ecoh is positive by definition.
  • Limiting behavior: if A6 increases with fixed A12, Ecoh increases ∝ A62; if A12 increases with fixed A6, Ecoh decreases ∝ 1/A12 (stronger repulsive crowding reduces binding).
  • Physical interpretation: ratio < 1 matches intuition that close-packed fcc is energetically preferred for LJ solids.
Math Insight

After nondimensionalization, the energy becomes a convex quadratic polynomial in x=(σ/r)^6. The equilibrium is the unique minimizer of this quadratic. Mathematically, the “physics of balance” (repulsion vs attraction) is encoded as the competition between the x² and x terms.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpret the final formula

The minimized cohesive energy per atom for an LJ lattice is 

E_coh = ε A6^2 / (2 A12)
  • A6 measures the “total attraction strength” from all neighbors (weighted by r−6).
  • A12 measures the “total repulsive crowding” (weighted by r−12).
  • The square on A6 shows that stronger attraction both deepens the well and shifts equilibrium, compounding the effect.

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Material dependence: Ecoh scales linearly with ε (deeper LJ well → stronger binding).
  • Structure dependence: determined entirely by (A6, A12).
  • Equilibrium spacing: from x* = A6/(2A12), r* = σ (2A12/A6)1/6. Thus larger A12/A6 pushes r* larger (repulsion forces expansion).

4.3 Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of differentiating, you can complete the square in U(x)=2ε(A12x²−A6x), which instantly yields both the minimizer x* and the minimum value. This is a classic technique when the energy reduces to a quadratic form—an algebraic analogue of minimizing a convex functional.

4.4 Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why does ε cancel in the ratio? A: Both cohesive energies are proportional to ε; it is a common multiplicative scale.
  • Q: What does ratio < 1 mean physically? A: bcc is less strongly bound (smaller cohesive energy) than fcc.
  • Q: Why can we minimize in x instead of r? A: x=(σ/r)6 is monotonic on r∈(0,∞), so argmin is preserved under the reparameterization.
  • Q: What mathematical property guarantees a unique minimum? A: U(x) is a convex quadratic since A12>0 ⇒ d²U/dx²>0.
