Goal: Express E[100] and ν[100] using C11, C12, (C44 not needed for this direction).

Quick Summary

  • What: A cubic crystal is pulled in the [100] direction; find Young’s modulus and Poisson’s ratio.
  • Key physics idea: In small-strain linear elasticity, stress and strain are linearly related by a stiffness (or compliance) tensor; cubic symmetry reduces the number of independent constants.
  • Governing equations: Static equilibrium ∇·σ = 0, strain–displacement ε = sym(∇u), and Hooke’s law σ = C:ε (or ε = S:σ).
  • Model type: Symbolic closed-form expressions for E[100] and ν[100].
  • Main result: E[100] = (C11 − C12)(C11 + 2C12)/(C11 + C12), ν[100] = C12/(C11 + C12).
  • Physical meaning: Along a cube axis, lateral contraction is governed by the coupling stiffness C12, while axial stiffness mixes C11 and C12.
  • Sanity checks: Units: C’s are Pa → E is Pa; ν is dimensionless. Limits: if C12→0 then ν→0 and E→C11.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

Stress tensor σ (Pa): components σ_ij are force per area acting on planes.

Small strain tensor ε (dimensionless): components ε_ij measure relative deformation.

Stiffness tensor C (Pa): 4th-order tensor in Hooke’s law σ = C:ε.

Compliance tensor S = C^{-1} (Pa−1): ε = S:σ.

Young’s modulus E (Pa): for uniaxial loading, E = σ_axial / ε_axial.

Poisson’s ratio ν (dimensionless): under uniaxial stress, ν = - ε_transverse / ε_axial (sign convention: transverse strain is negative in tension).

Crystal directions: [100] means along the x-axis of the cubic crystal axes.

0.2 Mathematical physics foundations (field model)

In continuum mechanics, deformation is described by a displacement field u(x) (vector), defined over a domain Ω ⊂ ℝ³. For small deformations:

Copied 
Kinematics (small strain):
ε = (1/2)(∇u + (∇u)^T)
i.e., ε_ij = (1/2)(∂u_i/∂x_j + ∂u_j/∂x_i)

Static equilibrium (no inertia) plus possible body force density b(x) (N/m³) gives the PDE:

Copied 
Balance of linear momentum (static):
∇·σ + b = 0
i.e., ∂σ_ij/∂x_j + b_i = 0

A linear elastic constitutive law closes the system:

Copied 
Hooke's law (anisotropic linear elasticity):
σ = C:ε     (stiffness form)
ε = S:σ     (compliance form, S = C^{-1})

Why these steps are valid: (i) small strain linearization uses a first-order Taylor expansion in displacement gradients; (ii) equilibrium is a conservation law (force balance); (iii) Hooke’s law assumes linear response about a stress-free reference state.

0.3 Cubic symmetry and the reduced elastic constants

A general anisotropic solid has 21 independent elastic constants, but cubic symmetry reduces this to three: C11, C12, C44 (Voigt notation). In the cubic crystal axes (x∥[100], y∥[010], z∥[001]) the stiffness matrix is:

Copied 
Voigt form for cubic crystals (σ = C ε):

[σ1]   [C11 C12 C12  0   0   0 ] [ε1]
[σ2]   [C12 C11 C12  0   0   0 ] [ε2]
[σ3] = [C12 C12 C11  0   0   0 ] [ε3]
[σ4]   [ 0   0   0  C44  0   0 ] [ε4]
[σ5]   [ 0   0   0   0  C44  0 ] [ε5]
[σ6]   [ 0   0   0   0   0  C44] [ε6]

(ε4,ε5,ε6 are engineering shear strains.)
What to watch for (pitfalls):
  • Direction matters: For cubic crystals, E and ν depend on loading direction. Here it is specifically [100].
  • Voigt shear convention: Engineering shear strains differ by a factor 2 from tensor shear components.
  • Free lateral surfaces: “Sides left free” means transverse tractions vanish → transverse stresses are zero (not transverse strains).

0.4 Mini intuition examples

  • If C12 = 0, stretching in x does not “pull” on y or z → no lateral contraction: ν = 0.
  • If C12 is significant, axial stress causes negative transverse strains through elastic coupling → larger ν.
  • C44 controls shear response; for pure [100] uniaxial tension with no shear stress/strain, it won’t enter E and ν.
Math Insight (core equation as a mathematical object):

The linear elastic problem is a linear elliptic PDE system for u: combining σ = C:ε and ε = sym(∇u) into ∇·(C:sym(∇u)) + b = 0. In our special case (uniform uniaxial loading of a homogeneous crystal), symmetry and boundary conditions reduce the PDE to a finite-dimensional linear algebra problem (inverting part of C).

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Direction-dependent elastic properties are not “academic details” for crystals—industry uses them constantly when designing, processing, and validating materials.

Where this appears in industry (concrete examples)

  • Semiconductor wafers (Si, GaAs): Mechanical stresses during thermal cycling and packaging depend on crystalline orientation; fracture risk and wafer bow relate to E and ν along specific directions.
  • Single-crystal turbine blades (Ni-based superalloys): Orientation control improves creep and fatigue performance; directional stiffness affects vibration and stress concentrations.
  • MEMS/NEMS devices: Resonant frequencies and stiffness of beams/plates are orientation-dependent; designers choose wafer cuts to tune E and ν for sensitivity and robustness.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: Stiffness affects deflection, resonance, and precision positioning.
  • Safety & reliability: ν influences lateral strains → affects delamination, cracking, and interfacial stress in multilayers.
  • Manufacturability & cost: Orientation impacts machining/grinding behavior, yield, and allowable process windows.

Measurement/validation in practice

  • Ultrasonic methods: Measure acoustic velocities to extract Cij.
  • Nanoindentation / micro-bending: Effective modulus depends on orientation and geometry; used for thin films and small devices.
  • X-ray diffraction stress analysis: Links lattice strain to stress; requires correct anisotropic elastic constants.

Design trade-offs & non-idealities

  • Real materials have defects, texture, grain boundaries → effective properties may be averaged (polycrystal) rather than single-crystal.
  • Temperature dependence of Cij alters stiffness; high-temp design must use temperature-dependent data.
  • Finite geometry and constraints introduce multiaxial stress states; “uniaxial” is an idealization.
From lab to product:

Engineers often start with single-crystal constants C11, C12, C44 (measured ultrasonically), then compute direction-dependent moduli for the specific wafer or component orientation. For polycrystals, they apply homogenization (Voigt/Reuss/Hill averages) to get isotropic effective E and ν.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

1.1 Restate the problem

A cubic crystal is subjected to uniaxial tension along the [100] direction. The lateral surfaces are free (no traction). Find expressions for Young’s modulus E and Poisson’s ratio ν in terms of the elastic stiffnesses (cubic constants) defined in the stiffness matrix.

1.2 Given, unknowns, required

  • Given: Cubic stiffnesses C11, C12, C44; loading direction [100]; “sides left free”.
  • Unknowns: E_[100], ν_[100].
  • Find: Closed-form symbolic formulas in terms of C11, C12, C44 (if it appears).

1.3 Relevant principles and why they apply

  • Linear elasticity: small strains → linear constitutive relation σ = C:ε.
  • Symmetry: cubic symmetry reduces constants to C11, C12, C44.
  • Boundary conditions: “sides free” means transverse tractions vanish → transverse stresses are zero.

What we do not need: dynamics (no time dependence), plasticity (assumed elastic), and shear constants for this specific uniaxial direction (unless shear stress appears, which it doesn’t).

Assumptions (explicit):
  • Homogeneous single crystal, cubic symmetry axes aligned with coordinate axes.
  • Small strain (|∇u| ≪ 1), linear material response.
  • Uniform uniaxial stress state in the gauge region (Saint-Venant idealization).
  • Traction-free lateral faces: σ·n = 0 on side surfaces ⇒ σ_yy = σ_zz = 0 (and shear components zero).

1.4 Approaches (compare 2–3)

  1. Compliance method (best here): Invert stiffness relations to get ε = Sσ, then apply uniaxial stress. Pros: direct for E and ν; minimal algebra once S is known.
  2. Direct stiffness + solve for strains: Use σ = Cε, set σ2=σ3=0, solve linear system for ε1, ε2, ε3. Pros: avoids full matrix inversion; still simple for cubic.
  3. Energy/variational approach: Use strain energy density U = (1/2) ε:C:ε and minimize with constraints. Pros: conceptually deep; Cons: more setup than necessary.

Chosen approach: Use the stiffness-form linear system (Approach 2) plus a compact inversion of the normal-stress block. It is the clearest and shows every step without “magic”.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Set coordinates: x∥[100], y∥[010], z∥[001]. This aligns the cubic stiffness matrix with the axes.
  2. State the boundary conditions: uniaxial traction gives σ_xx = σ; free sides give σ_yy = σ_zz = 0; no shear → σ_xy=σ_xz=σ_yz=0.
  3. Write Hooke’s law in Voigt form for the normal components (3×3 block involving C11 and C12).
  4. Solve the linear system for ε_xx and ε_yy (= ε_zz) in terms of σ.
  5. Compute E: E = σ / ε_xx.
  6. Compute ν: ν = - ε_yy / ε_xx.
  7. Sanity checks: units, limiting cases, signs.
Common mistakes
  • Setting transverse strains to zero instead of transverse stresses for free sides.
  • Mixing tensor shear strain with engineering shear strain in Voigt notation.
  • Forgetting that ν uses a minus sign: ν = -ε_trans/ε_axial.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Qualitative expectation (before algebra)

Under tensile stress along x, the material extends in x (ε_xx > 0) and contracts laterally (ε_yy, ε_zz < 0) if the coupling constant C12 is positive. Because the load is aligned with a cubic axis, the response should be symmetric in y and z, so we expect ε_yy = ε_zz.

3.2 Mathematical model (variables, domain, BCs)

  • Domain: Specimen region Ω ⊂ ℝ³ (a prismatic bar). We focus on the uniform “gauge” region away from grips (Saint-Venant).
  • Unknown field: displacement u(x).
  • Kinematics: ε = (1/2)(∇u + (∇u)^T).
  • Statics: ∇·σ = 0 (take b = 0 in the gauge region).
  • Constitutive law: σ = C:ε with cubic C in crystal axes.
  • Boundary conditions:
    • On end faces: traction t = σ·n = ±σ e_x (uniaxial tension in x).
    • On lateral faces: t = σ·n = 0 (traction-free).

Reduction to uniform stress: with homogeneous material and far from boundaries, equilibrium + symmetry supports a constant stress state consistent with the applied tractions. Then we can treat stress/strain as constants and solve algebraically.

3.3 Apply boundary conditions → stress state

Choose axes aligned with the cubic crystal: x ≡ [100], y ≡ [010], z ≡ [001]. Uniaxial tension along x with free lateral surfaces implies:

Copied 
Stress state (uniaxial in x, free sides):
σ_xx = σ
σ_yy = 0
σ_zz = 0
σ_xy = σ_xz = σ_yz = 0

Why this is correct: traction-free side faces mean the stress vector t = σ·n vanishes for normals n = e_y and n = e_z, giving σ_yy=σ_zz=σ_yz=0 (and similarly for shear on those faces).

3.4 Hooke’s law for cubic crystals: normal components

Since there is no shear stress, we only need the 3×3 normal block of σ = Cε. In Voigt notation (1→xx, 2→yy, 3→zz):

Copied 
Normal-stress block for cubic symmetry:
σ1 = C11 ε1 + C12 ε2 + C12 ε3
σ2 = C12 ε1 + C11 ε2 + C12 ε3
σ3 = C12 ε1 + C12 ε2 + C11 ε3

3.5 Use symmetry to reduce unknowns

Because the loading is along x and the crystal is cubic (y and z equivalent under rotation about x), the transverse strains must be equal: ε2 = ε3 (i.e., ε_yy = ε_zz). Let:

Copied 
Define:
ε1 = ε_xx
εt = ε2 = ε3 = ε_yy = ε_zz

Why symmetry applies: the constitutive law is invariant under permutations of y and z for cubic materials, and the boundary conditions are also symmetric in y and z. Therefore the solution must satisfy ε_yy = ε_zz (uniqueness of linear elastic solutions supports this).

3.6 Solve the linear system (show every step)

Insert the stress state σ1=σ, σ2=0, σ3=0 and ε2=ε3=εt into the equations:

Copied 
From σ1 equation:
σ = C11 ε1 + C12 εt + C12 εt = C11 ε1 + 2 C12 εt      (1)

From σ2 equation (and same as σ3):
0 = C12 ε1 + C11 εt + C12 εt = C12 ε1 + (C11 + C12) εt  (2)

Now solve (2) for εt in terms of ε1:

Copied 
Equation (2):
C12 ε1 + (C11 + C12) εt = 0

⇒ (C11 + C12) εt = - C12 ε1
⇒ εt = - (C12/(C11 + C12)) ε1

Substitute this εt into (1):

Copied 
Equation (1):
σ = C11 ε1 + 2 C12 εt
  = C11 ε1 + 2 C12 [ - (C12/(C11 + C12)) ε1 ]
  = ε1 [ C11 - (2 C12^2)/(C11 + C12) ]

Put over a common denominator to simplify:

Copied 
C11 - (2 C12^2)/(C11 + C12)
= [ C11(C11 + C12) - 2 C12^2 ]/(C11 + C12)
= [ C11^2 + C11 C12 - 2 C12^2 ]/(C11 + C12)

Factor numerator:
C11^2 + C11 C12 - 2 C12^2
= (C11 - C12)(C11 + 2 C12)

Therefore:

Copied 
σ = ε1 * ( (C11 - C12)(C11 + 2 C12) / (C11 + C12) )

⇒ ε1 = σ * ( (C11 + C12) / ((C11 - C12)(C11 + 2 C12)) )

3.7 Compute Young’s modulus E and Poisson’s ratio ν

By definition for uniaxial loading: E = σ / ε1, and ν = - εt / ε1. Use the expressions derived above.

Copied 
Young's modulus along [100]:
E_[100] = σ/ε1
        = ((C11 - C12)(C11 + 2 C12)) / (C11 + C12)

Poisson's ratio for transverse y (or z) strain:
ν_[100] = - εt/ε1
        = - [ - (C12/(C11 + C12)) ε1 ] / ε1
        = C12/(C11 + C12)
Final Answer (boxed)
Copied 
For a cubic crystal loaded in uniaxial tension along [100] (x-axis), with free lateral surfaces:

E_[100] = ((C11 − C12)(C11 + 2 C12)) / (C11 + C12)

ν_[100] = C12 / (C11 + C12)

(C44 does not enter for this pure [100] uniaxial case.)

3.8 Sanity checks (dimensions, limits, signs)

Dimensional analysis
  • C11, C12 have units Pa.
  • E formula is a product/ratio of Pa → Pa. ✅
  • ν is a ratio of Pa → dimensionless. ✅
Limiting cases
  • If C12 → 0: ν → 0, E → C11. ✅
  • Stability requires C11 − C12 > 0 and C11 + 2C12 > 0 → ensures E > 0. ✅
Optional deeper note: compliance form and explicit S11, S12
Copied 
Cubic compliances:
S11 = (C11 + C12)/((C11 − C12)(C11 + 2 C12))
S12 = − C12/((C11 − C12)(C11 + 2 C12))
S44 = 1/C44

For uniaxial σ1=σ, σ2=σ3=0:
ε1 = S11 σ,  ε2=ε3 = S12 σ
⇒ E = 1/S11,  ν = −S12/S11

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpret the final formulas

  • ν[100] = C12/(C11 + C12): controlled by normal coupling C12.
  • E[100]: combines stability factor (C11 − C12) with bulk-like factor (C11 + 2C12).

4.2 Parameter dependence

  • Increase C11 → generally increases E and decreases ν.
  • Increase C12 → increases ν; E changes depending on ratios, within stability constraints.
  • C44 matters for off-axis directions, not for pure [100] uniaxial tension.
Alternative derivation idea

Use elastic energy U = (1/2) ε:C:ε and apply natural boundary conditions (traction-free sides). Minimizing under the loading constraints yields the same ε–σ relations, hence the same E and ν.

4.3 Concept check (with answers)

  • Q: Why set σ_yy = σ_zz = 0 (not strains)? A: Free sides impose traction (stress) boundary conditions.
  • Q: Why is ε_yy = ε_zz? A: Cubic symmetry + symmetric BCs imply equal transverse response.
  • Q: Does C44 ever matter for E? A: Yes for off-axis loading (e.g., [110], [111]) where shear couples in.
  • Q: What ensures E > 0? A: Stability: C11 − C12 > 0, C11 + 2C12 > 0 (and C44 > 0).
