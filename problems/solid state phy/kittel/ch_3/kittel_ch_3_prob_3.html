Solid Molecular Hydrogen (H₂): Cohesive Energy from Lennard–Jones Parameters (fcc)

We model H₂ molecules as spherical particles interacting via the Lennard–Jones pair potential, place them on an fcc lattice, compute the lattice-sum energy per molecule, then minimize with respect to the lattice spacing to predict the cohesive energy. This classic calculation intentionally overestimates cohesion for quantum solids like H₂—highlighting the role of zero-point motion.

Quick Summary

  • What this is: Predict the cohesive energy of solid molecular hydrogen by modeling H₂–H₂ interactions with a Lennard–Jones (LJ) pair potential and placing molecules on an fcc lattice.
  • Key physics idea: In a crystal, the potential energy per molecule is a lattice sum over pair interactions; equilibrium spacing comes from energy minimization (force balance).
  • Governing model: Pair potential φ(r)=4ε[(σ/r)^12 − (σ/r)^6], total energy per particle u = (1/2)∑_{j≠i} φ(r_ij).

Big takeaway: For classical pair potentials, crystal cohesion reduces to “compute lattice sums + minimize with respect to spacing”. For light quantum solids (H₂, He), the classical minimum is significantly weakened by zero-point motion.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • Pair potential φ(r) [J]: interaction energy between two molecules separated by distance r [m].
  • Lennard–Jones parameters: ε [J] sets energy scale; σ [m] sets length scale.
  • Cohesive energy E_coh [J per particle, or kJ/mol]: energy required to separate the crystal into isolated particles (magnitude of binding).
  • fcc lattice: each particle has a periodic arrangement; nearest-neighbor distance r1 [m].
  • Lattice sum: ∑_{j≠i} f(r_ij) over all neighbors j.

Physical meaning (intuition)

  • (σ/r)^12 term: steep repulsion (Pauli exclusion / overlap), dominates at small r.
  • (σ/r)^6 term: dispersion attraction (van der Waals), dominates at moderate r.
  • Crystal equilibrium spacing occurs where net force sums to zero, equivalently where u(r1) is minimized.
  • Cohesive energy is essentially the depth of the “many-body” potential well at equilibrium.

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

  • Pairwise additivity: total potential energy U = (1/2)∑_{i≠j} φ(r_ij). Valid when many-body forces are small.
  • Periodic symmetry: in a perfect crystal, energy per particle is same for every site (translational invariance).
  • Zero-temperature cohesive energy: here we compute the static lattice (classical) minimum. Quantum solids require adding zero-point energy of lattice vibrations.
  • fcc assumption: the prompt asks to “do the calculation for an fcc structure”. (Real H₂ phases can be more complex.)

Common models/approximations (and why)

  • LJ potential: a simple, analytically tractable model capturing repulsion + dispersion attraction.
  • Spherical molecules: H₂ treated as isotropic spheres; neglects orientation and quadrupole interactions.
  • Static lattice: compute U from fixed positions; neglect thermal motion and quantum delocalization.
  • Infinite crystal: lattice sums are taken over all neighbors; convergence is good for r^{-6} and faster for r^{-12}.

Mini intuition examples (quick)

  • Single pair: LJ minimum at r = 2^{1/6} σ with depth φ_min = −ε.
  • Crystal vs pair: in a crystal, each molecule interacts with many neighbors, so the energy well is deeper than a single pair—scaled by lattice sums.
  • Why H₂ is special: very light mass ⇒ large zero-point vibration amplitude ⇒ effective “smearing” of r upward ⇒ weaker binding than the static minimum.
What to watch for (pitfalls)
  • Forgetting the 1/2 factor in pair-energy sums (double counting pairs).
  • Mixing units (erg vs J, Å vs m). Do explicit conversions.
  • Confusing σ (LJ length) with equilibrium spacing r1 (crystal geometry variable).
  • Using nearest-neighbor approximation only (bad for LJ; you need lattice sums or at least many shells).
Math Insight: The crystal cohesion problem becomes a one-parameter optimization: define a dimensionless spacing x = r1/σ, write u(x) = 2ε(A12 x^{-12} − A6 x^{-6}), then solve du/dx = 0. This is a clean example of reducing an infinite-dimensional sum to a finite-dimensional minimization using symmetry + nondimensionalization.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this model appears (real applications)

  • Cryogenic engineering: modeling cohesion, phase stability, and sublimation energetics for cryo-fuels and cryo-storage (H₂, N₂, O₂).
  • Materials & surfaces: van der Waals adhesion, physisorption on catalysts/porous media (e.g., hydrogen storage materials).
  • Molecular simulation (industry R&D): LJ potentials underpin many force fields used in MD to predict density, elastic constants, and thermal expansion.

Why engineers care

  • Performance: cohesive energy relates to vapor pressure and sublimation enthalpy—key for boil-off rates in cryo tanks.
  • Reliability: weakly bound solids can sinter, crack, or densify under thermal cycling.
  • Cost/efficiency: accurate cohesion reduces overdesign in insulation and refrigeration loads.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Calorimetry (latent heats, heat capacities) and vapor pressure measurements (via Clausius–Clapeyron) to infer binding energetics.
  • X-ray / neutron diffraction to determine lattice structure and spacing (especially neutron scattering for hydrogenous materials).
  • Inelastic neutron scattering / Raman to measure phonons; from them infer zero-point and anharmonic effects.

Design trade-offs & constraints

  • Non-ideality: real H₂ has quantum rotations, ortho/para mixtures, and anisotropic interactions not captured by a spherical LJ model.
  • Tolerances: small errors in σ or ε can shift predicted cohesion significantly because of steep repulsion (r^{-12}).
  • Model fidelity vs speed: LJ is fast and interpretable; more accurate potentials (e.g., ab initio, many-body) are costlier but necessary for quantum solids.
From lab to product: Engineers often start with simple pair potentials for quick estimates, then calibrate to measured phase data and incorporate corrections (quantum zero-point, many-body dispersion, anisotropy) when the operating regime (cryogenic, light molecules) demands it.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

Problem restatement (clear wording)

You are given Lennard–Jones parameters for H₂ from gas-phase measurements: ε = 50 × 10−16 erg and σ = 2.96 Å. Model solid H₂ as an fcc crystal of spherical molecules interacting via the LJ pair potential. Compute the cohesive energy (binding energy) in kJ per mole of H₂.

Given quantities

  • ε = 50 × 10^−16 erg
  • σ = 2.96 Å
  • Structure: fcc
  • Molecules: spherical (isotropic pair potential)

Unknowns / targets

  • Cohesive energy per molecule u_min [J]
  • Cohesive energy per mole E_coh [kJ/mol]
  • (Intermediate) equilibrium nearest-neighbor spacing r1 [m]

Relevant principles (and why)

  • Zero-T lattice cohesion: cohesive energy is the minimum of the lattice potential energy per particle.
  • Pairwise additivity: LJ provides φ(r); crystal energy is sum over neighbors.
  • Symmetry: fcc periodicity allows expressing the sum using dimensionless neighbor distances.
Explicit mathematical model
  • Domain: infinite discrete lattice sites R_i of an fcc Bravais lattice in ℝ^3.
  • Coordinates: Cartesian; distances r_ij = |R_i − R_j|.
  • Parameter: nearest-neighbor distance r1 sets all r_ij by scaling.
  • Energy functional: U({R_i}) = (1/2)∑_{i≠j} φ(|R_i−R_j|).
  • Boundary condition: infinite-lattice / periodic limit.
  • Equilibrium condition: du/dr1 = 0 and stability via d²u/dr1² > 0.

Assumptions

  • Isotropic spherical H₂; pairwise LJ interactions.
  • Static classical lattice; ignore phonons and quantum delocalization.
  • Perfect fcc crystal.

Approaches

Approach A: Use known fcc lattice sums A6, A12 (fast, accurate within model).
Approach B: Numerically sum neighbor shells (needs convergence control).

We choose Approach A for a clean analytic derivation and robust result.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Write φ(r) and u=(1/2)∑φ.
  2. Scale geometry with r_ij=r1 ρ_j.
  3. Nondimensionalize to pull out (σ/r1)^6, (σ/r1)^12.
  4. Define fcc constants A6=∑ρ^−6, A12=∑ρ^−12.
  5. Minimize: solve du/dr1=0 (or equivalent in x=(σ/r1)^6).
  6. Compute u_min, then convert to kJ/mol.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting the 1/2 factor (double counting).
  • Unit conversion errors (1 erg = 10^−7 J, 1 Å = 10^−10 m).
  • Confusing σ with equilibrium r1.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Step 1 — Lennard–Jones potential and crystal energy per molecule

φ(r) = 4 ε [ (σ/r)^12 − (σ/r)^6 ]
u = U/N = (1/2) ∑_{j≠i} φ(r_ij)

Step 2 — Scale distances and nondimensionalize (why it works)

In an fcc lattice, all neighbor distances scale with the nearest-neighbor distance r1. Write r_ij = r1 ρ_j with dimensionless ρ_j determined by lattice geometry. 

u(r1) = (1/2) ∑ 4ε [ (σ/r_ij)^12 − (σ/r_ij)^6 ]
      = 2ε ∑ [ (σ/r_ij)^12 − (σ/r_ij)^6 ]
r_ij = r1 ρ_j  ⇒  (σ/r_ij)^n = (σ/r1)^n ρ_j^−n

u(r1) = 2ε [ (σ/r1)^12 ∑ ρ_j^−12 − (σ/r1)^6 ∑ ρ_j^−6 ]

Step 3 — fcc lattice sums

A6  ≡ ∑_{j≠i} ρ_j^−6
A12 ≡ ∑_{j≠i} ρ_j^−12

For fcc: A6 ≈ 14.45392, A12 ≈ 12.13188. Then 

u(r1) = 2ε [ A12 (σ/r1)^12 − A6 (σ/r1)^6 ]
Dimensional check: (σ/r1) and A6,A12 are dimensionless ⇒ u has units of ε (energy). ✅

Step 4 — Minimization (rigorous math steps)

Define the dimensionless variable x = (σ/r1)^6 so (σ/r1)^12 = x^2. This converts the minimization over r1>0 to minimization over x>0. 

x ≡ (σ/r1)^6  > 0
u(x) = 2ε [ A12 x^2 − A6 x ]
du/dx = 2ε [ 2A12 x − A6 ]
Set du/dx = 0 ⇒ 2A12 x − A6 = 0 ⇒ x* = A6/(2A12)

d²u/dx² = 4εA12 > 0 ⇒ x* is a (unique) minimum

Step 5 — Minimum energy (no skipped algebra)

u_min = 2ε [ A12 (x*)^2 − A6 x* ]
     = 2ε [ A12 (A6^2/(4A12^2)) − A6 (A6/(2A12)) ]
     = 2ε [ (A6^2/(4A12)) − (A6^2/(2A12)) ]
     = 2ε [ −A6^2/(4A12) ]
     = − ε (A6^2)/(2A12)
Equilibrium nearest-neighbor spacing: from x* = (σ/r1)^6, 
r1 = σ (2A12/A6)^(1/6)

Step 6 — Insert numbers + unit conversion (carefully)

1 erg = 10^−7 J
ε = 50×10^−16 erg = 50×10^−16×10^−7 J = 50×10^−23 J = 5.0×10^−22 J
(A6^2)/(2A12) ≈ (14.45392^2)/(2×12.13188) ≈ 8.6102

u_min ≈ − (5.0×10^−22 J) × 8.6102 ≈ −4.31×10^−21 J per molecule
N_A = 6.02214076×10^23 mol^−1

U_min (per mol) = u_min N_A
≈ (−4.31×10^−21 J)(6.022×10^23 mol^−1)
≈ −2.59×10^3 J/mol
≈ −2.59 kJ/mol

Final Answer (copy-ready)

fcc Lennard–Jones cohesive energy for H₂:

u_min = − ε (A6^2)/(2A12), with fcc A6 ≈ 14.45392, A12 ≈ 12.13188
ε = 50×10^−16 erg = 5.0×10^−22 J

⇒ u_min ≈ −4.31×10^−21 J per molecule
⇒ |E_coh| = |u_min| N_A ≈ 2.59 kJ/mol (predicted by the classical LJ+fcc model)
Compare with experiment: observed cohesion is 0.751 kJ/mol (given). The model overbinds because H₂ is a quantum solid; zero-point motion reduces binding substantially.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpretation

  • ε sets the energy scale: |u_min| ∝ ε.
  • fcc geometry enters only through (A6^2)/(2A12).
  • σ sets length scale: r1 ∝ σ.
Math Insight: After substituting x=(σ/r1)^6, the energy becomes a convex quadratic: u(x)=2ε(A12 x^2 − A6 x). Convexity (d²u/dx²>0) guarantees a unique global minimum.

Alternative derivation idea

Use force balance: F_i = −∇_{R_i}U. Under uniform scaling, this reduces to du/dr1=0.

Concept check (Q&A)

  • Q: Why the factor 1/2? A: To avoid double counting pairs.
  • Q: Why do lattice sums converge? A: Terms decay as ρ^−6 and ρ^−12.
  • Q: Why does the classical model overestimate H₂ binding? A: Quantum zero-point motion increases effective separations and adds positive energy.

Extra Notes (optional deeper dives)

Where do A6 and A12 come from?

They are infinite sums over fcc neighbor shells: A_n=∑ρ^−n. Computed numerically to convergence and tabulated.

Quantum correction idea

Zero-point phonon energy E0=(1/2)∑_k ħω_k adds positive energy and shifts equilibrium to larger separations, reducing net cohesion.
