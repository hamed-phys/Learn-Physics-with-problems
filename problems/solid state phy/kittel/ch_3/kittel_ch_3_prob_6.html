Quick Summary

  • Goal: Compute the cohesive (lattice) energy of KCl if it had the cubic ZnS (zincblende) structure, and compare to KCl in the NaCl structure.
  • Key physics idea: Lattice energy is the sum of a long-range Coulomb (Madelung) attraction and a short-range Born–Mayer exponential repulsion.
  • Governing model (per ion pair): U(R) = −α (e²/4πϵ₀)/R + λ exp(−R/ρ)
  • Structure dependence: Only the Madelung constant α changes between NaCl and zincblende (and we keep the same λ, ρ from the table, as instructed).
  • Numerical result type: Numeric (kcal/mol and eV per ion pair).
  • Result (ZnS structure): |U| ≈ 153.9 kcal/mol (≈ 6.67 eV per ion pair).
  • Comparison (NaCl structure): |U| ≈ 165.5 kcal/mol (≈ 7.17 eV per ion pair).
  • Physical meaning: ZnS has smaller α than NaCl, so weaker electrostatic binding → smaller cohesive energy magnitude by ≈ 11.6 kcal/mol (~7%).
Math Insight

The energy model is a scalar potential U(R) depending on a single geometric degree of freedom (nearest-neighbor separation R). Mathematically, it’s a one-dimensional variational minimization problem (find equilibrium by minimizing U), with a long-range term behaving like −1/R and a short-range term behaving like exp(−R/ρ).

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

Core Definitions (symbols & SI units)
  • R (m): nearest-neighbor separation between unlike ions (K+ and Cl−).
  • α (dimensionless): Madelung constant, geometry-dependent lattice sum for Coulomb interactions.
  • e (C): elementary charge.
  • ϵ0 (F/m): vacuum permittivity.
  • λ (J): repulsive-energy prefactor (table gives in erg, where 1 erg = 10−7 J).
  • ρ (m): repulsive range parameter (table gives in Å, where 1 Å = 10−10 m).
  • U(R) (J per ion pair): potential energy relative to free ions at infinity (negative means bound).
Physical meaning (intuition)
  • Coulomb term: ions attract/repel via electrostatics; in a crystal, the net result is −α e²/(4πϵ₀ R).
  • Repulsion term: at short distances, electron clouds overlap; Pauli exclusion produces steep exponential repulsion.
  • Why α matters: α encodes how effectively the lattice geometry “packs” opposite charges around each ion.
  • Why λ, ρ matter: they encode the chemistry (electron density overlap scale) for the specific ion pair.

Key laws/principles and validity conditions

  • Electrostatics in crystals: Coulomb energy is computed as a conditionally convergent lattice sum; the result is summarized by α.
  • Born–Mayer repulsion: empirical but physically motivated by overlap of exponentially decaying wavefunctions.
  • Regime: ionic solids where charge transfer is close to ±e and the structure is well-described by point charges plus short-range repulsion.
  • Limitations: ignores polarization, covalency, many-body repulsion details, and zero-point/thermal corrections.

Common models/approximations used here

Assumptions used by the Born–Mayer + Madelung model
  • Point-ion Coulomb interactions: charges located on lattice sites, interacting via 1/r potential.
  • Short-range repulsion captured by one exponential: λ exp(−R/ρ).
  • Structure dependence enters mainly via α: for this textbook-style estimate, λ and ρ are taken as ion-pair parameters.
  • Nearest-neighbor distance R is taken as the tabulated R0: (as in many Kittel-style exercises) unless re-minimization is explicitly requested.

Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • If α increases (denser opposite-charge environment), the Coulomb attraction strengthens, so the lattice energy becomes more negative.
  • If R increases while keeping everything else fixed, Coulomb attraction weakens like 1/R, while repulsion falls exponentially—so U(R) becomes less negative (weaker binding).
  • If ρ decreases, the repulsive wall becomes “harder” (steeper), typically increasing equilibrium spacing in a full minimization.

What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Units: table uses Å and erg; Coulomb constant is often in eV·Å. Convert carefully.
  • Sign conventions: cohesive energy is typically reported as a positive magnitude |U| even though U is negative.
  • Structure change: the biggest controlled change is α; do not “invent” new λ, ρ unless instructed.
  • Per-ion vs per-mole: be clear whether energy is per ion pair or per mole of ion pairs.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Where this appears in industry (examples)
  • Crystal growth & materials selection: cohesion affects melting point, defect formation, and mechanical stability (e.g., halide crystals for optics).
  • Reliability of ionic devices: solid electrolytes, ion conductors, and halide perovskites—cohesive energies influence ion migration and degradation pathways.
  • Powder processing & sintering: lattice energy relates to surface energies and thus impacts densification and grain growth in ceramic processing.
Why engineers care
  • Performance: stronger cohesion often correlates with higher stiffness and thermal stability.
  • Safety & durability: cohesion affects fracture toughness trends and thermal shock resistance.
  • Cost & manufacturability: cohesive energy impacts processing temperature windows and defect sensitivity.

Typical measurement/validation methods

  • Born–Haber cycle (thermochemistry): combine ionization energies, electron affinities, enthalpies.
  • Elastic constants / bulk modulus (ultrasonics, Brillouin scattering): indirectly constrain interionic potential parameters.
  • X-ray/neutron diffraction: provides lattice constants → nearest-neighbor distance R.

Design trade-offs and non-idealities

  • Polarization & covalency: real ions polarize; effective charges deviate from ±e, changing the Coulomb term.
  • Defects and dopants: vacancies/interstitials alter local fields, affecting cohesive energy locally and diffusion barriers.
  • Temperature & pressure: thermal expansion changes R; high pressure modifies both attraction and repulsion regimes.
From lab to product: how the ideal model gets adapted

In engineering-grade simulations, the simple U(R) is replaced by fitted interatomic potentials (e.g., Buckingham, shell model, or DFT-derived force fields). The Madelung term is still central (often handled via Ewald summation), but repulsion and polarization are treated more explicitly to match elastic constants, phonons, and defect energetics.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

Physical intuition before calculating

The ZnS (zincblende) structure has a smaller Madelung constant than NaCl. Since the dominant binding is electrostatic, a smaller α means the Coulomb attraction is weaker at the same nearest-neighbor distance, so the cohesive energy magnitude should be smaller in ZnS than in NaCl. The repulsive term is identical (same ions, same R), so the change is mainly the Coulomb term.

Step 1 — State the mathematical energy model

For an ionic crystal, model the potential energy per ion pair (one K+ and one Cl−) as a sum of (i) a Coulomb lattice energy and (ii) a short-range exponential repulsion: 

U(R) = U_C(R) + U_rep(R)
U_C(R)   = − α * (e^2 / (4πϵ0)) * (1/R)
U_rep(R) =   λ * exp(−R/ρ)
Scalar potential U(R) on domain R>0

Why this form is valid (mathematical physics justification):

  • Linearity of electrostatics: Coulomb energy is a quadratic functional of charge density; in a periodic crystal, the lattice sum is linear in 1/R with a geometry factor α.
  • Symmetry reduction: For a fixed lattice type, uniform scaling of all distances by R makes Coulomb energy scale as 1/R (homogeneity of degree −1).
  • Short-range approximation: Overlap of ionic electron densities decays roughly exponentially; keeping the leading exponential term gives λ e−R/ρ.
Optional deeper note: where α comes from (Ewald/Green’s function idea)

In a periodic, neutral crystal, the electrostatic potential φ solves Poisson’s equation with periodic boundary conditions. The energy can be expressed using a Green’s function G of the Laplacian on a torus (periodic cell), and Ewald summation is a practical method to evaluate the conditionally convergent lattice sums. The final Coulomb energy per ion pair reduces to −α(e²/4πϵ₀)/R, where α is a dimensionless lattice sum depending only on geometry.

Step 3 — Compute the repulsive contribution at R = R0

First compute the exponential factor: 

R0/ρ = 3.147 / 0.326 ≈ 9.654
exp(−R0/ρ) ≈ exp(−9.654) ≈ 6.42×10^−5
Dimensionless exponent check

Then: 

U_rep(R0) = λ exp(−R0/ρ)
          ≈ (2.05×10^−8 erg) * (6.42×10^−5)
          ≈ 1.32×10^−12 erg  (per ion pair)
Repulsion is small but crucial for equilibrium

Step 4 — Compute the Coulomb contribution (structure-dependent)

Use the convenient constant: 

e^2/(4πϵ0) = 14.3996 eV·Å
1 eV = 1.60218×10^−12 erg
⇒ e^2/(4πϵ0) ≈ 14.3996*(1.60218×10^−12) erg·Å
            ≈ 2.307×10^−11 erg·Å
Dimensional analysis: (energy·length)

Therefore, at R = R0: 

U_C(R0) = − α * (2.307×10^−11 erg·Å) / (3.147 Å)
        = − α * (7.33×10^−12 erg)
Coulomb scales with α

Sanity checks (units, limits, interpretation)

Units/dimensions
  • Coulomb term: (energy·length)/length → energy ✔
  • Exponent: R/ρ is dimensionless ✔
  • λ has energy units ✔
  • Conversion: erg → J → kcal/mol uses Avogadro’s number ✔
Limiting cases
  • If α → 0, Coulomb binding disappears and U → small positive repulsion at fixed R (no cohesion) ✔
  • If R → ∞, both terms → 0 (free ions) ✔
  • If α increases, U becomes more negative (stronger binding) ✔
Why the trend is guaranteed (mathematically)

At fixed R, λ, ρ, the energy depends on α only through a linear term: U(R) = −α C/R + λ e−R/ρ, with C = e²/(4πϵ₀) > 0. Therefore ∂U/∂α = −C/R < 0, so decreasing α must increase U (make it less negative), i.e., reduce the cohesive energy magnitude. No “hand-waving” needed—this is a strict monotonicity result.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

Re-interpret the final formula

The model U(R)=−α(e²/4πϵ₀)/R + λ e−R/ρ has a clean “division of labor”:

  • α term: purely geometric/electrostatic; controls how much long-range attraction the lattice provides.
  • λ, ρ term: short-range quantum-mechanical repulsion; controls the “hardness” of the ionic cores.
  • R: scale parameter set by lattice spacing/ionic sizes; strongly affects Coulomb (1/R) and repulsion (exp).

Parameter dependence (qualitative + quantitative)

  • α: U changes linearly with α at fixed R. Here Δα = 1.74756 − 1.63806 ≈ 0.1095 produces ΔU ≈ +11.6 kcal/mol (less negative).
  • R: if R increases slightly, Coulomb weakens slowly (~1/R), but repulsion weakens rapidly (~exp). In a full minimization, equilibrium is where these slopes balance.
  • ρ: smaller ρ makes the repulsion steeper; tends to push equilibrium R larger in self-consistent models.
Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful): energy minimization & Lambert W

If you were asked to find the equilibrium spacing for each structure, you would minimize U(R): 

dU/dR = 0
⇒ d/dR[ −α C (1/R) + λ e^(−R/ρ) ] = 0
⇒ α C / R^2  = (λ/ρ) e^(−R/ρ)   where C = e^2/(4πϵ0)
Stationary condition (force balance)

This transcendental equation can be rearranged into a Lambert-W form (because R appears both in an exponential and algebraic terms). That is a classic mathematical physics signature: competition of power-law and exponential behaviors. In this problem, however, the exercise typically fixes R = R0 and compares structures via α.

Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why does changing structure change only α in this simplified calculation? A: α is the geometry-dependent result of the Coulomb lattice sum; λ and ρ are treated as ion-pair repulsion parameters provided by the table.
  • Q: If α were identical for two structures, would their cohesive energies be identical here? A: Yes, at fixed R, λ, ρ, because U depends on structure only through α.
  • Q: Why is U negative? A: The attractive Coulomb term dominates at the equilibrium spacing; the negative sign indicates a bound state relative to free ions.
  • Q: What mathematical property ensures “smaller α → smaller |U|”? A: Monotonicity: ∂U/∂α = −C/R < 0.
