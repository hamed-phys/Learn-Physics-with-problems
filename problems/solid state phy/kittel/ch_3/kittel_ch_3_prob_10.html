Wave Speeds in a Cubic Crystal Along [111]: Longitudinal & Transverse Modes

We derive the phase velocities of elastic plane waves propagating in the [111] direction of a cubic crystal, starting from the full anisotropic elastodynamic PDE and reducing it to an eigenvalue problem (the Christoffel equation). The result is purely symbolic and expressed in terms of C11, C12, C44 and density ρ.

Quick Summary

  • What: Prove the longitudinal and transverse elastic-wave velocities for propagation along the cubic-crystal [111] direction.
  • Key physics idea: In an anisotropic solid, wave speeds come from an eigenvalue problem (Christoffel equation), not a single scalar modulus.
  • Governing PDE: Linear elastodynamics: ρ ∂²u/∂t² = ∇·σ, with σ = C : ε and ε = (∇u + (∇u)ᵀ)/2.
  • Plane-wave reduction: Use u(x,t) = U exp[i(k·x − ωt)] ⇒ (Γ(n) − ρv² I)U = 0, where v = ω/|k|.
  • Direction: n = [111]/√3, so nx = ny = nz = 1/√3.
  • Result type: Symbolic speeds in terms of C11, C12, C44, ρ (SI units: Pa, kg·m⁻³).
  • Final longitudinal speed: vL = √[(C11 + 2C12 + 4C44)/(3ρ)].
  • Final transverse speed: vT = √[(C11 − C12 + C44)/(3ρ)] (two degenerate shear polarizations for [111]).

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • Displacement field u(x,t) [m], where x ∈ ℝ³, t ∈ ℝ.
  • Strain tensor ε = (1/2)(∇u + (∇u)ᵀ) [dimensionless].
  • Stress tensor σ [Pa = N·m⁻²].
  • Stiffness tensor C with components Cijkl [Pa]. For a cubic crystal: independent constants C11, C12, C44.
  • Mass density ρ [kg·m⁻³].
  • Wave vector k [m⁻¹], angular frequency ω [s⁻¹], phase speed v = ω/|k| [m·s⁻¹].
  • Propagation direction n = k/|k| (unit vector).

0.2 Physical meaning (intuition)

  • Elastic waves are small disturbances in displacement that propagate because stress acts as a restoring force and inertia resists acceleration.
  • In anisotropic crystals, the restoring force depends on direction, so wave speeds depend on n and polarization.
  • The “longitudinal” mode has U ∥ n (mostly compression), and “transverse” modes have U ⟂ n (mostly shear), but in anisotropy the distinction is “quasi-” unless symmetry makes it exact.

0.3 Key laws / principles and validity conditions

Assumptions (linear elasticity wave regime)
  • Small strain: |∇u| ≪ 1 so ε is linear in u.
  • Homogeneous medium: C and ρ are constant in space.
  • No body forces: f = 0 (free wave propagation).
  • Lossless: no viscosity/damping terms (purely elastic).
  • Infinite or periodic domain: allows plane waves (or wavelengths ≪ sample dimensions).

0.4 Common models / approximations (why used)

  • Plane-wave ansatz: u = U e^{i(k·x − ωt)} converts the PDE into algebraic eigenvalue equations; valid for uniform media.
  • Voigt notation: compresses tensor relations; for cubic crystals it shows only three independent stiffnesses.
  • High-symmetry directions ([100], [110], [111]): crystal symmetry can make longitudinal and transverse modes “pure” and simplify eigenvectors.

0.5 Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • Isotropic limit: If the crystal behaves isotropically with Lamé parameters (λ, μ), then vL = √[(λ+2μ)/ρ], vT = √[μ/ρ]. The anisotropic result should reduce to this when cubic constants satisfy isotropy relations.
  • Why eigenvalues? For a fixed direction n, different polarizations “feel” different effective stiffness. The speeds are the square roots of those effective stiffnesses divided by ρ.

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Confusing direction indices [111] with a unit vector: you must normalize to n = (1,1,1)/√3.
  • Dropped factors of 2 in strain: ε = (∂u/∂x + (∂u/∂x)ᵀ)/2.
  • Mixing tensor and Voigt forms incorrectly: C44 corresponds to shear components, not a “44-direction”.
  • For transverse modes: there are two polarizations; along [111] they are degenerate (same speed), but not in general directions.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Direction-dependent acoustic velocities in crystals are not just textbook details—they are measurable, design-relevant properties. Engineers use them to infer stiffness, detect defects, and predict vibration and wave-guiding behavior.

Where it appears (applications)
  • Ultrasonic NDE (non-destructive evaluation): wave speeds reveal elastic constants, texture, and microcracks.
  • SAW/BAW devices (RF filters, sensors): crystal cut and propagation direction set resonance frequencies and coupling.
  • Semiconductor manufacturing (Si wafers): anisotropic elasticity affects wafer bow, stress, and acoustic metrology.
  • Structural health monitoring in anisotropic composites & single-crystal turbine blades.
Why engineers care
  • Performance: device frequency ∝ v/λ, so direction-dependent v shifts specs.
  • Reliability: stress waves concentrate differently by direction; impacts, fatigue, and delamination can be direction-sensitive.
  • Cost & manufacturability: choosing a crystal orientation can simplify packaging constraints or reduce undesired modes.

Typical measurement / validation methods

  • Pulse-echo ultrasonics: measure time-of-flight along known paths → v.
  • Brillouin light scattering: optical probe of acoustic phonons → direction-resolved velocities.
  • Resonant ultrasound spectroscopy: invert resonant frequencies to obtain elastic constants.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Real materials have temperature dependence, residual stress, and defects; Cij(T, stress) may vary.
  • Finite geometry introduces boundary conditions (traction-free surfaces) and mode conversion; “bulk” wave speeds are the starting point.
  • Manufacturing tolerances: a small miscut from [111] can split shear-mode degeneracy and change device behavior.
From lab to product: how the ideal model is adapted

The plane-wave, infinite-medium model gives intrinsic bulk speeds. In products, engineers embed these speeds into finite-element models (FEM) with anisotropic constitutive laws, incorporate damping (viscoelasticity), and impose real boundary conditions (clamping, layered stacks, electrodes). Calibration uses measured time-of-flight/resonances to update effective Cij and account for processing-induced anisotropy or texture.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

1.1 Restate the problem (in plain words)

For a cubic crystal with elastic constants C11, C12, C44 and density ρ, consider plane elastic waves propagating in the crystallographic [111] direction. Show that:

  • Longitudinal (compressional) wave speed is vL = √[(C11 + 2C12 + 4C44)/(3ρ)].
  • Transverse (shear) wave speed is vT = √[(C11 − C12 + C44)/(3ρ)].

1.2 Given quantities

  • Elastic constants: C11, C12, C44 [Pa]
  • Density: ρ [kg·m⁻³]
  • Propagation direction: [111] → n = (1,1,1)/√3

1.3 Unknowns

  • Wave speeds vL, vT [m·s⁻¹]
  • Implicitly: eigenvectors (polarizations) U

1.4 Relevant principles (and why they apply)

  • Linear momentum conservation (Newton’s 2nd law in continuum form): gives ρ ü = ∇·σ.
  • Linear constitutive law for a cubic crystal: σ = C : ε.
  • Plane-wave method: for homogeneous coefficients, Fourier modes decouple, reducing PDE to algebraic eigenproblem.
Explicit mathematical model
  • Domain: x ∈ ℝ³ (or periodic box), t ∈ ℝ.
  • Unknown field: u(x,t) ∈ ℝ³.
  • PDE: ρ ∂²u/∂t² = ∇·(C : ε), with ε = (∇u + (∇u)ᵀ)/2.
  • Boundary/initial conditions: for plane waves, effectively take infinite/periodic domain; initial data represented by Fourier superposition. No external traction/boundary needed for the bulk-wave dispersion relation.

1.5 Candidate approaches (compare)

  • (A) Christoffel matrix method (best): Derive Γik(n) = Cijkl nj nl, solve eigenvalues → v². Pros: systematic, clean, reveals degeneracies. Cons: requires tensor bookkeeping.
  • (B) Component PDE substitution: Write u = u e^{i(k·x−ωt)}, plug into full PDE in components and simplify using cubic symmetry. Pros: direct. Cons: long algebra without the Γ shortcut.
  • (C) Energy/variational approach: Use Rayleigh quotient for v² as ratio of elastic energy density to kinetic energy density for a trial polarization. Pros: conceptual, connects to eigenvalue bounds. Cons: still needs Γ to evaluate exactly.

Chosen approach: (A) Christoffel method, because it is the standard mathematically rigorous reduction of the elastodynamic PDE to a symmetric eigenvalue problem.

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Write the PDE model (ρ ü = ∇·σ) with σ = C:ε and ε = sym(∇u). Goal: set the mathematical starting point.
  2. Insert plane-wave ansatz u = U e^{i(k·x−ωt)}. Tool: ∇ → i k, ∂/∂t → −iω.
  3. Reduce to an eigenvalue problem (Γ(n) − ρv² I)U = 0, where v = ω/|k| and n = k/|k|. Meaning: nontrivial U requires det = 0.
  4. Construct Γ(n) for a cubic crystal using C11, C12, C44. Key step: Γ is symmetric.
  5. Specialize to [111]: n = (1,1,1)/√3, compute Γ explicitly.
  6. Find eigenvalues of Γ: one longitudinal, two transverse (degenerate). Use symmetry: Γ has equal diagonals and equal off-diagonals.
  7. Convert eigenvalues to speeds: v² = λ/ρ.
  8. Sanity checks: dimensions, isotropic limit, degeneracy explanation.
Common mistakes (quick tips)
  • Normalization: [111] means n = (1,1,1)/√3, not (1,1,1).
  • Operator mapping: for plane waves, ∂j → i kj, not i nj (n is unitless).
  • Forgetting symmetry: Γ is symmetric → real eigenvalues → real wave speeds in stable materials.
  • Units check: C/ρ has units (m²/s²). If not, something is off.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Qualitative expectation (before math)

Along a high-symmetry direction like [111] in a cubic crystal, symmetry suggests: (i) one mode polarized along n (pure longitudinal) and (ii) two equivalent shear polarizations orthogonal to n, producing two identical transverse speeds. So we expect one distinct vL and one vT.

3.2 Start from the mathematical model (PDE form)

Governing elastodynamics (operator notation) 
u = u(x,t) ∈ R^3
ε(u) = (1/2)(∇u + (∇u)^T)
σ = C : ε
ρ ∂^2u/∂t^2 = ∇·σ   (no body force)

Why each step is valid: The PDE is linear because we assume small strain (ε linear in u) and a linear constitutive law (σ linear in ε). Homogeneity (constant C, ρ) allows Fourier/plane-wave separation because the coefficients do not vary with x.

Component form (to connect operators to indices)

Using Einstein summation over repeated indices:

ε_kl = (1/2)(∂u_k/∂x_l + ∂u_l/∂x_k)
σ_ij = C_ijkl ε_kl
(∇·σ)_i = ∂σ_ij/∂x_j
PDE: ρ ∂^2u_i/∂t^2 = ∂/∂x_j ( C_ijkl ε_kl )

Symmetries: ε_kl = ε_lk, and for elastic materials C_ijkl = C_jikl = C_ijlk = C_klij. These ensure Γ is symmetric later.

3.3 Plane-wave ansatz and operator substitutions

Assume a monochromatic plane wave:

Plane wave and substitution rules 
u(x,t) = U exp(i(k·x − ωt))
∇u → i k ⊗ U exp(i(k·x − ωt))
∂/∂t → −iω
∂^2/∂t^2 → −ω^2

Why valid: In a linear PDE with constant coefficients, complex exponentials are eigenfunctions of derivative operators. Physically, real waves are the real part; mathematically, complex representation simplifies algebra (linearity guarantees equivalence).

3.4 Reduce the PDE to the Christoffel eigenvalue problem

We compute strain for the plane wave. In components: 

u_i = U_i e^{i(k·x − ωt)}
∂u_i/∂x_j = i k_j U_i e^{i(k·x − ωt)}
ε_kl = (1/2)(i k_l U_k + i k_k U_l) e^{i(k·x − ωt)}
      = (i/2)(k_l U_k + k_k U_l) e^{...}

Stress is then:

σ_ij = C_ijkl ε_kl
     = (i/2) C_ijkl (k_l U_k + k_k U_l) e^{...}

Now take divergence: 

(∇·σ)_i = ∂σ_ij/∂x_j = i k_j σ_ij
= i k_j * (i/2) C_ijkl (k_l U_k + k_k U_l) e^{...}
= -(1/2) k_j C_ijkl (k_l U_k + k_k U_l) e^{...}

Use the minor symmetry C_ijkl = C_ijlk to show the two terms are equal: 

Term A: k_j C_ijkl k_l U_k
Term B: k_j C_ijkl k_k U_l

Rename dummy indices in Term B: swap k ↔ l inside C using C_ijkl = C_ijlk,
and swap dummy labels in the product:
k_j C_ijlk k_l U_k  = k_j C_ijkl k_l U_k

So Term A = Term B, hence their sum is 2 * (k_j C_ijkl k_l U_k).

Therefore:

Christoffel reduction 
(∇·σ)_i = − k_j C_ijkl k_l U_k e^{i(k·x − ωt)}.

PDE: ρ (−ω^2) U_i e^{...} = − k_j C_ijkl k_l U_k e^{...}

Cancel e^{...} and define v = ω/|k|, n = k/|k|:

C_ijkl n_j n_l U_k = ρ v^2 U_i

(Γ(n) − ρ v^2 I) U = 0,
Γ_ik(n) := C_ijkl n_j n_l
Math Insight: what kind of object is this?

The plane-wave problem is a finite-dimensional symmetric eigenvalue problem. For each direction n on the unit sphere S², Γ(n) is a 3×3 real symmetric matrix. Its eigenvalues λα(n) are direction-dependent effective stiffnesses, and the dispersion relation is ρ vα² = λα(n). This is the anisotropic-wave analog of a Sturm–Liouville eigenproblem: symmetry → orthogonal eigenvectors and real eigenvalues.

3.5 Build Γ(n) for a cubic crystal

For a cubic crystal aligned with axes x,y,z along [100],[010],[001], the stiffness in Voigt form is: 

[σ11]   [C11 C12 C12  0   0   0 ][ε11]
[σ22]   [C12 C11 C12  0   0   0 ][ε22]
[σ33] = [C12 C12 C11  0   0   0 ][ε33]
[σ23]   [ 0   0   0  C44  0   0 ][2ε23]
[σ13]   [ 0   0   0   0  C44  0 ][2ε13]
[σ12]   [ 0   0   0   0   0  C44][2ε12]

A standard (and widely used) result for cubic crystals is that the Christoffel matrix components are:

Christoffel matrix for cubic symmetry 
Let n = (n1,n2,n3) be the unit propagation direction.

Γ11 = C11 n1^2 + C44 (n2^2 + n3^2)
Γ22 = C11 n2^2 + C44 (n1^2 + n3^2)
Γ33 = C11 n3^2 + C44 (n1^2 + n2^2)

Γ12 = (C12 + C44) n1 n2
Γ13 = (C12 + C44) n1 n3
Γ23 = (C12 + C44) n2 n3

Γ is symmetric: Γij = Γji
Why those Γ entries make sense (symmetry + scaling)
  • Γ must be quadratic in n because it contains njnl.
  • Cubic symmetry demands invariance under permutations of axes (x↔y↔z), yielding identical functional form for Γ11, Γ22, Γ33.
  • Off-diagonal terms must be proportional to ninj; the only cubic combination with correct symmetry is (C12 + C44)ninj.

3.6 Specialize to the [111] direction

The crystallographic direction [111] corresponds to the unit vector: 

n = (1,1,1)/√3  ⇒  n1 = n2 = n3 = 1/√3
So: n1^2 = n2^2 = n3^2 = 1/3, and n1 n2 = n1 n3 = n2 n3 = 1/3.

Plug into the cubic Γ formulas.

  • Diagonal entries (all equal):
Γ11 = C11*(1/3) + C44*((1/3)+(1/3)) = (1/3)C11 + (2/3)C44
Similarly Γ22 = Γ33 = (1/3)C11 + (2/3)C44
  • Off-diagonal entries (all equal):
Γ12 = (C12 + C44)*(1/3)
Γ13 = (C12 + C44)*(1/3)
Γ23 = (C12 + C44)*(1/3)

Therefore Γ has the special “all diagonals equal, all off-diagonals equal” form: 

Let a := (1/3)C11 + (2/3)C44
Let b := (1/3)(C12 + C44)

Γ = [ a  b  b
      b  a  b
      b  b  a ]

3.7 Solve the eigenvalue problem (no magic jumps)

We need eigenvalues of Γ. This matrix can be written as: 

Γ = (a−b) I + b J
where I is identity and J is the all-ones matrix (J_ij = 1).

Key facts (verify quickly): 

J has eigenvector e = (1,1,1) with eigenvalue 3:
J e = (sum of components of e) * (1,1,1) = 3*(1,1,1)

Any vector orthogonal to e (i.e., components sum to 0) is an eigenvector of J with eigenvalue 0:
If U·e = 0 ⇒ U1+U2+U3=0 ⇒ J U = 0.

Since Γ = (a−b)I + bJ, the eigenvalues are: 

For longitudinal-like eigenvector e = (1,1,1):
λ_L = (a−b) + b*(3) = a + 2b

For any transverse eigenvector U with U1+U2+U3=0 (two-dimensional subspace):
λ_T = (a−b) + b*(0) = a − b

Now substitute a and b and simplify carefully.

3.8 Compute λL and λT explicitly

a + 2b = [(1/3)C11 + (2/3)C44] + 2*[(1/3)(C12 + C44)]
       = (1/3)C11 + (2/3)C44 + (2/3)C12 + (2/3)C44
       = (1/3)C11 + (2/3)C12 + (4/3)C44
       = (1/3)(C11 + 2C12 + 4C44)
a − b = [(1/3)C11 + (2/3)C44] − [(1/3)(C12 + C44)]
       = (1/3)C11 + (2/3)C44 − (1/3)C12 − (1/3)C44
       = (1/3)C11 − (1/3)C12 + (1/3)C44
       = (1/3)(C11 − C12 + C44)

Finally, the Christoffel eigenvalue relation is ρ v² = λ. Hence:

Final Answer (boxed)
Wave speeds along [111] in a cubic crystal 
Propagation direction: n = (1,1,1)/√3.

Longitudinal (U ∥ n):
v_L = sqrt( (C11 + 2 C12 + 4 C44) / (3 ρ) ).

Transverse (two degenerate shear polarizations U ⟂ n, U1+U2+U3=0):
v_T = sqrt( (C11 − C12 + C44) / (3 ρ) ).

Degeneracy note: Along [111], cubic symmetry makes the two shear modes equivalent, so they share the same vT.

3.9 Sanity checks

Units / dimensional analysis
  • Cij has units Pa = kg·m⁻¹·s⁻².
  • C/ρ → (kg·m⁻¹·s⁻²)/(kg·m⁻³) = m²·s⁻².
  • √(C/ρ) → m·s⁻¹, correct for speed.
Limiting case: isotropic check

For an isotropic material embedded in cubic form: C11 = λ + 2μ, C12 = λ, C44 = μ. 

v_L^2 = ( (λ+2μ) + 2λ + 4μ )/(3ρ) = (3λ+6μ)/(3ρ) = (λ+2μ)/ρ
v_T^2 = ( (λ+2μ) − λ + μ )/(3ρ) = (3μ)/(3ρ) = μ/ρ

Matches the standard isotropic speeds. ✅

Polarizations (explicit examples)

Longitudinal eigenvector is e = (1,1,1) (parallel to [111]). Any two independent vectors orthogonal to e span the shear subspace, e.g. 

U_T1 = (1, −1, 0)   (sum = 0)
U_T2 = (1, 1, −2)   (sum = 0)

Both give the same eigenvalue λT because Γ is isotropic within the plane perpendicular to [111] (a symmetry result for cubic crystals along that axis).

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpret the formulas (what each term controls)

  • C11 contributes to both vL and vT because it governs normal stress/strain along principal axes.
  • C12 couples normal strains in different directions; it increases vL (via +2C12) but decreases vT (via −C12).
  • C44 is shear stiffness; it strongly affects vL (via +4C44) because longitudinal motion along [111] still induces shear components in the cube axes, and it also raises vT (via +C44).
  • ρ always slows waves: v ∝ 1/√ρ.

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative sensitivity)

  • If C44 increases (stiffer shear), both vL and vT increase, with vL typically more sensitive because of the 4C44 term.
  • If C12 increases, vL increases but vT decreases (opposite trends).
  • Density scaling is universal: doubling ρ reduces speeds by factor √2.

4.3 Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

A compact alternative is a variational/Rayleigh quotient viewpoint: for a unit polarization U, 

ρ v^2(U) = U · Γ(n) U / (U · U)

The extremal values of this quotient over all U are the eigenvalues of Γ(n). For [111], symmetry suggests testing U ∥ (1,1,1) and any U with sum zero; this immediately yields λL and λT because Γ has only two invariant subspaces (along e and perpendicular to e).

Mathematical physics highlight

The mapping n ↦ Γ(n) defines a matrix-valued quadratic form on S². Mechanical stability implies Γ(n) is positive definite for all n, so eigenvalues λ>0 and speeds are real. Along high-symmetry axes, group-theory symmetry reduces the representation of Γ to blocks, causing eigenvalue degeneracy (here: twofold shear degeneracy).

4.4 Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why do we get an eigenvalue problem instead of one speed? A: Because stress–strain response depends on polarization in anisotropy; different polarizations “see” different effective stiffness.
  • Q: What ensures real wave speeds here? A: Γ(n) is symmetric (elastic tensor symmetries) and positive definite (stability), giving real positive eigenvalues.
  • Q: Why are there two transverse modes? A: In 3D, the subspace orthogonal to n is 2D. For [111] in cubic symmetry, those two are degenerate.
  • Q: In the isotropic limit, what does vT depend on? A: Only the shear modulus μ and density: vT=√(μ/ρ).
