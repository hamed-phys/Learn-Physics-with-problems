Effective Shear Constant in a Cubic Crystal

Goal: show that the “shear constant” C′ equals (C11 − C12)/2 by applying a specific strain state εxx = −εyy = e/2 (others zero) and matching the elastic energy density to U = (1/2) C′ e².

Small-strain tensor: ε = ½(∇u + (∇u)T) Cubic stiffness: C11, C12, C44 Energy density: quadratic form in ε

Quick Summary

  • What it’s about: identifying an effective shear modulus for a tetragonal shear strain in a cubic crystal.
  • Key physics idea: in linear elasticity, elastic energy density is quadratic in strain; symmetry fixes the form.
  • Governing equations: ε = ½(∇u + ∇uT), σ = C:ε, and U(ε) = ½ ε:C:ε.
  • Applied strain state: εxx= e/2, εyy= −e/2, all others 0 (volume-preserving pure distortion).
  • Computation result: U = (C11 − C12) e² / 4.
  • Match form: U = ½ C′ e² ⇒ C′ = (C11 − C12)/2.
  • Meaning: C′ measures resistance to a specific “square-to-rectangle” distortion; it is tied to stability/soft modes in alloys.
  • Result type: fully symbolic; no numbers needed, so a plot adds little here.

PART 0 — Concept Primer

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • Displacement field u(x): vector field (m) giving how each material point moves.
  • Small-strain tensor ε: dimensionless, symmetric 2nd-order tensor:
    ε = ½(∇u + (∇u)^T), i.e. ε_ij = ½(∂u_i/∂x_j + ∂u_j/∂x_i)
    Key equation (copyable)
  • Stress tensor σ (Pa): relates to traction and internal forces.
  • Stiffness tensor C (Pa): 4th-order elastic constants; for a cubic crystal it reduces to three independent values: C11, C12, C44 (all in Pa).
  • Elastic energy density U (J/m³): strain energy per unit volume stored in elastic deformation.

0.2 Physical meaning (intuition)

  • C11: resistance to normal strain along a principal cubic axis (stretch/compress along x, y, z).
  • C12: coupling between normal strains in different directions (Poisson-like coupling under cubic symmetry).
  • C44: resistance to simple shear (off-diagonal strain components εxy, εyz, εzx).
  • C′ = (C11−C12)/2: resistance to a tetragonal shear (a shape change that makes x longer while y shorter, keeping volume unchanged).
Key idea
In linear (small-strain) elasticity, U(ε) is a quadratic form. If you prescribe a particular strain pattern, the energy reduces to U = ½ (effective modulus) × (amplitude)². That effective modulus is what we call here the “effective shear constant.”
Validity conditions
Small strains (|ε| ≪ 1), homogeneous material, temperature fixed, no plasticity, no damage. Crystal axes aligned with the coordinate system when using the cubic stiffness form.

0.3 Key laws/principles and why they apply

  • Constitutive law (Hooke’s law for anisotropic solids): σ = C:ε (linearity in ε).
  • Energy density in linear elasticity: for a stable elastic material,
    U(ε) = ½ ε : C : ε = ½ Σ_{i,j,k,l} ε_ij C_ijkl ε_kl
    The symmetry of ε and of C (minor/major symmetries) ensures this scalar is well-defined and quadratic.
  • Mechanical equilibrium: ∇·σ + f = 0. For a homogeneous prescribed strain in a body with compatible boundary conditions and no body forces, ∇·σ = 0 is satisfied (σ is uniform), so energy-based identification is consistent.

0.4 Cubic crystal energy density (the form you “plug into”)

For a cubic crystal, symmetry collapses the stiffness tensor to three constants. In Cartesian axes aligned with the cubic axes, the energy density can be written directly in terms of strain components (using tensor shear strains εij, not engineering γij):

U = ½ C11(εxx² + εyy² + εzz²) + C12(εxx εyy + εyy εzz + εzz εxx) + 2 C44(εxy² + εyz² + εzx²)
Key equation (copyable)
Optional note: why the factors look like this (tensor vs engineering shear)
Many texts define engineering shear strain γxy = 2εxy. If you rewrite energy in terms of γ, the coefficients in the shear term change. Here we keep the clean tensor form: the shear contribution is 2 C44 εxy², etc.

0.5 Mini intuition examples (very light algebra)

  • Pure uniaxial strain along x: εxx=a, others 0 ⇒ U = ½ C11a².
  • Simple shear in xy: εxy=s, others 0 ⇒ U = 2 C44s² = ½ (4C44) s² (effective modulus depends on amplitude definition).
  • Tetragonal shear (today’s case): εxx=+e/2, εyy=−e/2 ⇒ energy depends on C11 and C12, not on C44.

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Coordinate alignment: the cubic form for U is valid when x,y,z are along cubic axes.
  • Shear conventions: εxy vs γxy=2εxy changes factors of 2.
  • Energy matching: identify the “amplitude” e exactly as the problem defines it (here: εxx=e/2).
  • Don’t confuse C′ with C44: both can be called “shear-like,” but they correspond to different distortion modes.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Why this matters in real engineering

  • Phase-transforming alloys & shape memory (NiTi, Heuslers): softening of tetragonal shear (small C′) is often a precursor to martensitic transformations, impacting actuation, fatigue life, and hysteresis.
  • Microelectronics & thin films: epitaxial strain in cubic semiconductors (Si, Ge, III-V) produces anisotropic distortions; knowing which modulus controls which distortion improves stress-engineering and reliability.
  • Crystal plasticity & forming (metals): elastic anisotropy affects springback and residual stress. C′ influences how grains elastically distort under multi-axial loads, altering dimensional control in manufacturing.

Why engineers care (performance, safety, cost)

  • Reliability: low shear stiffness modes can localize stress/strain, promoting crack initiation at interfaces or grain boundaries.
  • Manufacturability: anisotropic elastic response influences warpage in wafers, distortion in heat treatment, and tolerance stack-up.
  • Efficiency: mechanical energy storage/loss and resonant behavior in MEMS/NEMS depend on direction-dependent elastic constants.

Measurement / validation methods (brief)

  • Ultrasonic wave speeds: measure longitudinal/transverse acoustic velocities along symmetry directions to extract C11, C12, C44 (and thus C′).
  • Brillouin light scattering: optical probe of acoustic phonons in thin films.
  • Resonant ultrasound spectroscopy / nanoindentation + modeling: infer anisotropic stiffness from resonance spectra or indentation response.

Design trade-offs & non-idealities

  • Temperature dependence: elastic constants vary with T; near transformations, C′ can drop sharply.
  • Texture/polycrystals: manufacturing creates preferred orientations, so “effective” moduli differ from single-crystal values.
  • Defects & precipitates: alter local stiffness and can pin soft modes, changing macroscopic response.
From lab to product
In product-scale simulations (FEA), single-crystal constants are often homogenized (Voigt/Reuss/Hill averages) for polycrystals, and temperature-dependent fits C(T) are used. Still, identifying mode-specific constants like C′ helps engineers interpret which deformation patterns are “soft,” and where warpage or instability might occur.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (no solving yet)

1.1 Restate the problem

For a cubic crystal with elastic constants C11, C12, C44, apply the strain state εxx = e/2, εyy = −e/2, and all other strain components zero. Show that the energy density takes the form U = (1/2) C′ e² with C′ = ½(C11 − C12).

1.2 Given, unknowns, and what must be proved

  • Given: cubic elastic energy density U(ε) (or equivalently stiffness constants C11, C12, C44).
  • Given strain: εxx= +e/2, εyy= −e/2, εzz=0, εxy=εyz=εzx=0.
  • Unknown: effective modulus C′ for this mode.
  • Prove: C′ = (C11 − C12)/2.

1.3 Mathematical model (variables, domain, coordinates, conditions)

Mathematical Physics Model
  • Domain: a homogeneous elastic solid, idealized as a continuum occupying a region Ω ⊂ ℝ³ (often taken as “large” so boundary effects are negligible in energy density).
  • Coordinates: Cartesian (x,y,z) aligned with cubic crystal axes.
  • Unknown field: displacement u(x) = (ux,uy,uz).
  • Strain operator: ε[u] = ½(∇u + (∇u)T) (a linear differential operator in u).
  • Constitutive law: σ = C:ε (linear mapping in ε; C has cubic symmetry).
  • Equilibrium PDE: ∇·σ = 0 in Ω (no body forces). For uniform imposed strain, σ is uniform ⇒ ∇·σ = 0 automatically.
  • Boundary/compatibility: we imagine boundary displacements that enforce the prescribed constant strain (affine displacement u = E x with E symmetric such that ε = E). This ensures ε is compatible: ε comes from a displacement field.

1.4 Relevant principles and why others don’t matter here

  • Energy method is ideal because the hint explicitly targets U and asks for a quadratic match.
  • We do not need to solve the equilibrium PDE or compute stresses explicitly; the strain is prescribed and homogeneous.
  • We do not need dynamics (no d/dt terms) or wave equations; this is purely static elasticity.

1.5 Assumptions (explicit)

Assumptions
  • Small strain, linear elasticity (neglect O(ε³) and higher energy terms).
  • Material is cubic, homogeneous, and axes are aligned to crystal symmetry directions.
  • Strain is uniform (no gradients), so energy density is spatially constant.
  • No thermal, electric, or plastic effects included.

1.6 Approaches (compare 2–3)

  • (A) Direct energy substitution (best): plug the strain components into the cubic energy density and simplify. Fast, minimal ambiguity.
  • (B) Stress–strain then energy: compute σ from σ=C:ε, then use U = ½ σ:ε. Same result; slightly longer but a good consistency check.
  • (C) Eigenmode / quadratic form view: treat U as a quadratic form in the 6D strain space; identify C′ as an eigenvalue associated with the deviatoric mode (εxx=−εyy). Elegant, more abstract.

Chosen approach: (A) Direct energy substitution, because the problem’s hint and goal are exactly “find C′ such that U = ½ C′ e².”

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (roadmap only)

  1. Write the cubic energy density U(ε) as a quadratic form in strain components (tool: symmetry-reduced expression).
  2. Insert the prescribed strain state (goal: reduce U to a scalar function of e).
  3. Compute each term carefully: squares εxx², εyy² and cross-term εxxεyy.
  4. Simplify algebra to get U = constant × e² (goal: isolate the coefficient multiplying e²).
  5. Match to the target form U = ½ C′ e² (tool: coefficient comparison in a polynomial).
  6. State the final C′ and interpret physically (what distortion it measures).
  7. Sanity checks: dimensional analysis (Pa), limiting case (if C11=C12 then this mode costs no energy), and sign/stability (C′>0 for stability).
Common mistakes
  • Using εxx=e (instead of e/2) and getting a factor-of-4 error.
  • Dropping the C12 cross-term or using the wrong sign (εxxεyy is negative here).
  • Mixing engineering shear γ with tensor shear ε in the C44 term (not needed here anyway since shear strains are zero).

PART 3 — Full Solution (DETAILED + TEACHING)

3.1 Physical intuition first

The imposed strain stretches the crystal in x while compressing it in y by the same amount, with εzz=0. Because the distortion is “normal-strain-only” (no off-diagonal shear), we expect the energy to involve C11 and C12, not C44. Also, since the deformation amplitude is e, linear elasticity implies energy scales like e².

3.2 Start from the governing energy density

In linear anisotropic elasticity, the strain energy density is U(ε) = ½ ε : C : ε. For a cubic crystal aligned with (x,y,z), the symmetry-reduced form is:

U = ½ C11(εxx² + εyy² + εzz²) + C12(εxx εyy + εyy εzz + εzz εxx) + 2 C44(εxy² + εyz² + εzx²)
Key equation (copyable)

3.3 Insert the prescribed strain state

Given (define the amplitude parameter e exactly as stated):

εxx = +e/2 εyy = −e/2 εzz = 0 εxy = εyz = εzx = 0
Key equation (copyable)

3.4 Compute the needed algebra step-by-step (no jumps)

Step 1: Squares of normal strains

εxx² = (e/2)² = e²/4 εyy² = (−e/2)² = e²/4 εzz² = 0 So: (εxx² + εyy² + εzz²) = e²/4 + e²/4 + 0 = e²/2
Intermediate algebra (copyable)

Step 2: Cross-terms among normal strains

εxx εyy = (e/2)(−e/2) = −e²/4 εyy εzz = (−e/2)(0) = 0 εzz εxx = (0)(e/2) = 0 So: (εxx εyy + εyy εzz + εzz εxx) = −e²/4
Intermediate algebra (copyable)

Step 3: Shear terms

εxy = εyz = εzx = 0 ⇒ (εxy² + εyz² + εzx²) = 0 ⇒ shear-energy contribution (2 C44 × ...) = 0
Intermediate algebra (copyable)

3.5 Substitute back into U and simplify

Substitute the computed sums into the cubic energy expression:

U = ½ C11(εxx² + εyy² + εzz²) + C12(εxx εyy + εyy εzz + εzz εxx) + 2 C44(εxy² + εyz² + εzx²) = ½ C11 (e²/2) + C12 (−e²/4) + 0 = (C11 e²)/4 − (C12 e²)/4 = (C11 − C12) e² / 4
Derivation (copyable)

3.6 Match to the target form U = ½ C′ e²

We want U written as U = ½ C′ e². From (C11 − C12) e² / 4, compare coefficients of e²:

(C11 − C12) e² / 4 = ½ C′ e² Divide both sides by (½ e²): C′ = (C11 − C12)/2
Coefficient matching (copyable)
Final Answer
For a cubic crystal under εxx = −εyy = e/2 (all other εij = 0), the energy density is U = (C11 − C12) e² / 4 = ½ C′ e², so the effective shear constant is: C′ = ½ (C11 − C12).
Copy final answer (plain text)

3.7 Sanity checks

Dimensional analysis
C11, C12 are in Pa; e is dimensionless; thus U ~ Pa × 1 = J/m³. Correct.
Limiting cases & stability
If C11 = C12, then C′=0 and this distortion costs no energy (a “soft mode”). For elastic stability in cubic crystals, one requirement is C′ > 0 (equivalently C11 > C12).
Optional deeper math: why matching coefficients is valid
Because linear elasticity gives a quadratic energy in the strain amplitude. Here all strain components scale linearly with e, i.e. ε(e) = e·ε̂ where ε̂ is a fixed strain pattern (ε̂xx=1/2, ε̂yy=−1/2). Then U(e) = ½ (e ε̂):C:(e ε̂) = ½ e² (ε̂:C:ε̂). The scalar (ε̂:C:ε̂) is exactly the effective modulus for that mode. Therefore writing U = ½ C′ e² is always possible for any single-parameter strain path in linear elasticity.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (THEORY AROUND THE RESULT)

4.1 Interpret the final formula physically

  • C′ depends only on (C11−C12) because the distortion uses only diagonal strains and preserves volume (trace ε = εxx+εyy+εzz = 0).
  • Why “shear”? Although there is no off-diagonal shear strain, this is a deviatoric shape change (a shear in the sense of changing shape at constant volume). In crystal elasticity it’s often called tetragonal shear.
  • Stability meaning: C′ > 0 means energy increases with e², so the crystal resists this distortion. Small C′ indicates proximity to a structural instability.

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Increase C11 (holding C12 fixed) ⇒ C′ increases ⇒ the crystal stiffens against tetragonal distortion.
  • Increase C12 (holding C11 fixed) ⇒ C′ decreases ⇒ the coupling between orthogonal strains “softens” this mode.
  • Changing C44 does nothing here because the applied strain has εxy=εyz=εzx=0.

4.3 Math Insight (interpret the core equation as a mathematical object)

Math Insight: quadratic form & eigenmodes
The mapping ε ↦ U(ε) is a positive-definite quadratic form on the 6D vector space of symmetric strain tensors. The strain pattern ε̂ = diag(1/2, −1/2, 0) is one specific direction in that space. The effective modulus is the Rayleigh quotient-like quantity
C′ = (ε̂ : C : ε̂)
(up to the exact amplitude convention), which is why identifying C′ by matching U = ½ C′ e² is mathematically rigorous.

4.4 Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Compute stress first using σ = C:ε for cubic symmetry:

σxx = C11 εxx + C12(εyy + εzz) σyy = C11 εyy + C12(εxx + εzz) σzz = C11 εzz + C12(εxx + εyy) (and σxy = 2 C44 εxy, etc.)

With εzz=0, εxx=+e/2, εyy=−e/2: σxx = (C11 − C12)e/2, σyy = −(C11 − C12)e/2, σzz = 0. Then energy density U = ½ σ:ε = ½(σxx εxx + σyy εyy) = (C11 − C12)e²/4 ⇒ C′=(C11 − C12)/2.

Optional: connect to deviatoric/volumetric decomposition (continuum viewpoint)
Any strain can be decomposed into volumetric (hydrostatic) part and deviatoric (shape-change) part: ε = (1/3)(tr ε) I + ε_dev, with tr ε_dev = 0. Here tr ε = 0, so the entire strain is deviatoric. In isotropic elasticity, deviatoric energy is controlled by the shear modulus μ. In a cubic crystal, there are multiple “shear-like” deviatoric moduli; C′ is one of them, distinct from C44.

4.5 Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why doesn’t C44 appear? A: Because εxy, εyz, εzx are zero, so the shear term contributes zero energy.
  • Q: What is tr(ε) for this strain? A: tr(ε)=εxx+εyy+εzz=e/2−e/2+0=0 (volume-preserving).
  • Q: If C′ is very small, what does that suggest? A: The crystal is “soft” against tetragonal distortion; it may be near a structural instability or phase transition.
  • Q: Where did the factor 1/2 in C′ come from? A: From the amplitude convention εxx=e/2; energy scales with εxx², giving factors of (1/2)².
