Stability Criteria for a Cubic Crystal (Elastic Constants)

We derive the thermodynamic/elastic stability conditions for a cubic crystal with one atom in the primitive cell against small homogeneous deformations, by requiring that the strain-energy density is positive for every nonzero strain. This is a classic “quadratic form is positive definite” problem with a clear physical meaning: any small strain must cost energy.

PART 0 — Concept Primer (Theory Before Solving)

0.1 Core definitions (symbols + SI units)

  • Displacement field: u(x) [m].
  • Small (infinitesimal) strain tensor: ε = (1/2)(∇u + (∇u)T) (dimensionless). Components: ε_ij = (1/2)(∂u_i/∂x_j + ∂u_j/∂x_i).
  • Stress tensor: σ [Pa].
  • Stiffness tensor: C_ijkl [Pa], with major/minor symmetries for elastic materials: C_ijkl = C_jikl = C_ijlk = C_klij.
  • Strain-energy density: U [J/m³] = [Pa].
  • Voigt notation: maps symmetric ε_ij to a 6-vector ε⃗ and C_ijkl to a 6×6 matrix C.

0.2 Physical meaning (intuition)

In linear elasticity, a stable crystal behaves like a multidimensional spring: any small distortion must raise the energy. If you can find a nonzero strain that lowers the energy (or costs zero energy), the material is unstable (or marginal) to that deformation: it will spontaneously distort, or it has a soft mode.

Key physics Stability against small homogeneous strain

Elastic stability (static): For every nonzero homogeneous strain ε, the strain energy density must satisfy U(ε) > 0. This is the elastic analog of “potential energy has a strict minimum at equilibrium”.

0.3 Key laws/principles and validity conditions

  • Small-strain kinematics: |∇u| ≪ 1 so higher-order terms in strain are neglected.
  • Hooke’s law (linear constitutive relation): σ_ij = C_ijkl ε_kl.
  • Energy density (hyperelastic linear form): U = (1/2) σ_ij ε_ij = (1/2) C_ijkl ε_ij ε_kl.
  • Homogeneous deformation: ε_ij is constant in space (no gradients of strain), so we are checking local/material stability, not buckling or wave instabilities.
  • Cubic symmetry: in the crystal coordinate axes aligned with cubic symmetry directions, only C11, C12, C44 are independent.

0.4 Common models/approximations and why they’re used

  • Linear elasticity: keeps only quadratic energy in small strains; simplest model capturing stiffness and stability near equilibrium.
  • Continuum approximation: treats the crystal as a continuous medium; valid for long-wavelength deformations compared to lattice spacing.
  • Symmetry reduction: cubic symmetry drastically reduces tensor components, enabling analytic stability inequalities.

0.5 Mini intuition examples (minimal algebra)

  • Pure shear: if a shear strain costs energy proportional to C44, then stability requires C44 > 0.
  • Uniform compression: energy must rise with volumetric strain; this leads to a condition equivalent to bulk modulus K > 0.
  • Tetragonal distortion: stretch in x and shrink in y at fixed volume; energy cost is tied to C11 − C12.

0.6 What to watch for (pitfalls)

  • Coordinate system: the simple cubic form of the stiffness matrix assumes axes aligned with crystal symmetry directions.
  • Engineering vs tensor shear strain: in Voigt notation, shear components often use γ = 2ε. Be consistent or you’ll miss factors of 2.
  • Positive definite vs semidefinite: stability requires strictly positive energy for all nonzero strains (no zero-energy modes).
  • “C11 > 0” alone is not enough: coupled normal strains require combined inequalities.

PART 0B — Industry & Applied Physics Perspective

Why this matters in real engineering

Elastic stability criteria are not just academic inequalities. They are screening rules used in materials engineering to ensure a crystal phase is mechanically viable under small loads, processing stresses, and thermal mismatch. If the criteria fail, the material tends to distort into a lower-symmetry phase, crack under residual stresses, or exhibit soft elastic modes.

Applications Where cubic elasticity shows up
  • Semiconductor manufacturing: Si, Ge, many III-V crystals—wafer stress, epitaxy mismatch, dislocation formation.
  • Metal alloys: many metals are FCC/BCC at certain temperatures—phase stability during heat treatment.
  • Ceramics & ionic crystals: cubic oxides/fluorites—thermal shock resistance and residual stress in coatings.
Why engineers care Performance & reliability
  • Safety: prevent spontaneous distortions (martensitic-like instabilities) that can degrade structural integrity.
  • Yield: stable elastic constants reduce warpage and cracking in wafers and thin films.
  • Design margins: informs allowable stress states and helps calibrate FEM material models.

Measurement/validation methods in practice (brief)

  • Ultrasonic/acoustic velocity measurements: measure sound speeds along symmetry directions → extract C11, C12, C44.
  • Brillouin light scattering: optical probe of phonons in crystals and thin films.
  • Nanoindentation + inverse modeling: infer effective elastic constants (with caution about anisotropy and texture).
  • First-principles (DFT) elasticity: compute elastic tensor by energy/strain fits; stability criteria are a sanity check for predicted phases.

Design trade-offs and constraints

  • Temperature dependence: elastic constants soften with temperature; stability can be lost near phase transitions.
  • Defects and microstructure: real crystals have dislocations, grain boundaries, and anisotropic textures that modify effective stiffness.
  • Processing stresses: residual stresses can push material toward unstable strain directions if margins are small.
From lab to product Adapting the ideal model

In products, engineers often use effective elastic constants (polycrystal averages, texture-weighted anisotropy) and add nonlinear or temperature-dependent corrections. Still, the cubic Born inequalities remain foundational as a quick mechanical feasibility filter.

PART 1 — Problem Analysis (No Solving Yet)

1.1 Problem restatement (in plain words)

A cubic crystal (one atom per primitive cell) is stable against small homogeneous deformations if the elastic energy density is positive for all combinations of strain components. Determine what inequalities this imposes on the elastic stiffness constants.

1.2 Given, unknowns, and target

  • Given: cubic symmetry; linear elastic energy density is a quadratic form in strains.
  • Unknowns: constraints (inequalities) on C11, C12, C44 (or equivalent constants).
  • Must find/prove: necessary and sufficient conditions so that U(ε) > 0 for all nonzero homogeneous strain tensors ε.

1.3 Relevant principles/laws (and why)

  • Linear elasticity energy: U = (1/2) C_ijkl ε_ij ε_kl. Applies because “small” deformations are explicitly assumed.
  • Tensor symmetries: ensure the quadratic form is defined by a symmetric operator on the strain space (important for positive definiteness).
  • Cubic symmetry reduction: reduces the general 21 independent elastic constants to 3 for cubic crystals, simplifying the quadratic form.

Not needed here: equations of motion (ρ ∂²u/∂t² = ∇·σ) or boundary conditions for a specific body, because we only check homogeneous strains (a purely material stability question).

1.4 Assumptions (explicit)

Assumptions What we assume and what it implies
  • Small strains: keep terms up to second order in strain; energy is quadratic.
  • Homogeneous deformation: ε is constant in space; no strain gradients.
  • Elastic equilibrium reference state: unstressed (or stress-free) reference; stability is about the curvature of energy at that state.
  • Cubic symmetry axes: coordinate axes align with cubic crystallographic axes so the standard cubic stiffness matrix applies.

1.5 Possible approaches (compare)

  • Approach A — Eigenvalues of stiffness matrix: Write the 6×6 Voigt stiffness matrix for cubic symmetry; require all eigenvalues > 0.
    Pros: gives clean physical modes (bulk, tetragonal shear, simple shear). Cons: requires some linear algebra.
  • Approach B — Sylvester’s criterion: For a symmetric matrix, positive definiteness ⇔ all leading principal minors are positive.
    Pros: purely algebraic. Cons: bookkeeping; must choose consistent ordering.
  • Approach C — Test strategically chosen strains: Use physically motivated strain families (pure shear, hydrostatic, tetragonal) and prove sufficiency by decomposing energy.
    Pros: very physical and transparent. Cons: must argue the chosen modes span strain space.

1.6 Best approach choice

We will combine Approach A and C: write the cubic stiffness matrix, identify its eigenmodes (hydrostatic + deviatoric + shears), and read off the eigenvalues. This is both mathematically rigorous (positive definite operator ⇔ positive eigenvalues) and physically illuminating (each inequality corresponds to a concrete deformation mode).

PART 2 — Strategy & Tips (Roadmap Only)

  1. Model the deformation: define the small strain tensor ε_ij and assume it is spatially uniform (homogeneous).
  2. Write the energy density: U = (1/2) C_ijkl ε_ij ε_kl; use symmetry to express it in Voigt form.
  3. Use cubic symmetry: write the 6×6 stiffness matrix with only C11, C12, C44.
  4. Reduce the problem to linear algebra: show U = (1/2) ε⃗^T C ε⃗, a real symmetric quadratic form.
  5. Diagonalize conceptually: identify orthogonal strain modes: (i) hydrostatic, (ii) two independent deviatoric normal modes, (iii) three shear modes.
  6. Read off eigenvalues: compute the energy for each mode; the coefficients multiplying squared mode amplitudes are eigenvalues (up to convention factors).
  7. Impose positivity: require each eigenvalue > 0 → get inequalities on C11, C12, C44.
  8. Sanity checks: relate inequalities to bulk modulus and shear moduli; check dimensions (Pa) and physical interpretation.
Common mistakes Quick tips
  • Mixing tensor shear ε_ij with engineering shear γ_ij = 2ε_ij without tracking factors of 2.
  • Forgetting that positivity must hold for all combined strains, not just single-component tests.
  • Assuming C11 > 0 automatically implies stability (it does not).
  • Using a rotated coordinate system: the cubic matrix form is simplest only in symmetry-aligned axes.

PART 3 — Full Solution (Detailed + Teaching)

3.1 Physical intuition (before calculating)

A stable cubic crystal must resist: (i) pure shear (sliding planes), (ii) volume change (compression/expansion), and (iii) shape change at constant volume (e.g., stretch in x and compress in y). We expect three independent “stiffnesses” for these distinct mode families — matching the three independent constants C11, C12, C44.

3.2 Mathematical model (variables, domain, coordinates, conditions)

Mathematical model What we are solving
  • Domain: a representative material point / small neighborhood in a cubic crystal (continuum limit).
  • Coordinates: Cartesian axes {x1,x2,x3} aligned with cubic crystallographic axes.
  • Unknown field: displacement u(x), but we restrict to homogeneous strain so ε is constant.
  • Strain operator: ε = sym(∇u) = (1/2)(∇u + (∇u)^T).
  • Constitutive law: σ = C : ε (double contraction), i.e. σ_ij = C_ijkl ε_kl.
  • Energy density functional: U(ε) = (1/2) ε : (C : ε).
  • Stability condition (goal): U(ε) > 0 for all symmetric ε ≠ 0.
  • Boundary/initial conditions: not needed for this local homogeneous-stability check (no PDE solving). If desired, homogeneous strain corresponds to affine displacement u(x)=A x with ε = sym(A).

3.3 From tensor energy to a symmetric quadratic form

Start from the linear-elastic energy density:

Key equation Strain energy density
U(ε) = (1/2) C_ijkl ε_ij ε_kl

Why this step is valid: for small strains, the Taylor expansion of the internal energy density about equilibrium keeps the first non-vanishing term, which is quadratic in strain. The linear term vanishes at a stress-free equilibrium. Tensor symmetry ensures the quadratic form is symmetric (real).

Because ε_ij = ε_ji, the strain has 6 independent components. We package them using Voigt notation:

Definition Voigt strain vector (tensor-shear form)
ε⃗ = [ ε11, ε22, ε33, 2ε23, 2ε13, 2ε12 ]^T

We choose the common convention with 2εij in the shear slots so that the energy keeps the compact matrix form (1/2) ε⃗^T C ε⃗ with the standard tabulated stiffness matrix entries. (If you use [ε11,ε22,ε33,ε23,ε13,ε12] instead, the shear blocks carry different factors; the final inequalities are unchanged.)

Key equation Quadratic form statement
U = (1/2) ε⃗^T C ε⃗  ,   with C = C^T (real symmetric)

Math meaning: We are requiring the symmetric linear operator C on a real 6D vector space to be positive definite. For symmetric matrices, this is equivalent to: all eigenvalues are positive (or, equivalently, Sylvester’s criterion).

Math Insight The core equation as a mathematical object

U(ε) is a quadratic form on the vector space of symmetric strains. Stability requires that this quadratic form defines an inner product (up to a constant), i.e. U(ε) > 0 for every nonzero ε. In linear algebra terms: C must be SPD (symmetric positive definite).

3.4 Cubic stiffness matrix

For a cubic crystal in its principal symmetry axes, the stiffness matrix is:

Cubic symmetry Stiffness matrix in Voigt notation
C =
[ C11  C12  C12   0    0    0
  C12  C11  C12   0    0    0
  C12  C12  C11   0    0    0
   0    0    0   C44   0    0
   0    0    0    0   C44   0
   0    0    0    0    0   C44 ]

Why this is valid: cubic symmetry constraints plus tensor symmetries force all normal-direction couplings to take the above form, leaving only three independent parameters. The matrix is manifestly symmetric.

3.5 Decompose strain space into orthogonal mode families

Because the matrix is block diagonal between normal strains (first 3 components) and shears (last 3), we can analyze them separately. This is a linear algebra simplification: if C = diag(C_normal, C_shear), then U = (1/2)(ε_normal^T C_normal ε_normal + ε_shear^T C_shear ε_shear). Positivity for all ε requires positivity on each block.

(A) Shear block

The shear block is C_shear = C44 I_3. Therefore, for any shear vector s ∈ ℝ^3,

Shear energy Immediate condition
U_shear = (1/2) s^T (C44 I) s = (1/2) C44 |s|^2

Since |s|^2 > 0 for any nonzero shear, we must have:

Stability Shear criterion
C44 > 0

Physical interpretation: C44 is a shear modulus for shear on {100} planes in ⟨010⟩ directions (in cubic axes). If C44 ≤ 0, the material would prefer to shear spontaneously.

(B) Normal-strain block

The normal block is the 3×3 matrix:

Normal block 3×3 matrix
C_normal =
[ C11  C12  C12
  C12  C11  C12
  C12  C12  C11 ]

This matrix has a well-known eigenstructure because it is “constant on diagonal, constant off-diagonal”. We can find eigenmodes by symmetry:

  • Hydrostatic (volumetric) mode: proportional to v1 = (1,1,1).
  • Deviatoric normal modes: any vector orthogonal to v1, e.g. v2 = (1,-1,0), v3 = (1,1,-2).

Why these are valid choices: cubic symmetry implies invariance under permutation of x,y,z directions. The subspace spanned by (1,1,1) is invariant, and so is its orthogonal complement (trace-free normal strains). Therefore the matrix must act as a scalar (multiple of identity) on each irreducible subspace.

3.6 Compute eigenvalues explicitly (no magic jumps)

Hydrostatic eigenvalue

Apply C_normal to v1=(1,1,1):

Eigen-step Action on hydrostatic vector
C_normal v1 =
[ C11*1 + C12*1 + C12*1
  C12*1 + C11*1 + C12*1
  C12*1 + C12*1 + C11*1 ]
= [ C11 + 2C12
    C11 + 2C12
    C11 + 2C12 ]
= (C11 + 2C12) v1

So the hydrostatic eigenvalue is: λ_bulk = C11 + 2C12. Positivity requires C11 + 2C12 > 0.

Deviatoric normal eigenvalue

Take v2 = (1,-1,0), which satisfies v2·v1=0. Compute:

Eigen-step Action on (1,−1,0)
C_normal v2 =
[ C11*1 + C12*(-1) + C12*0
  C12*1 + C11*(-1) + C12*0
  C12*1 + C12*(-1) + C11*0 ]
= [ C11 - C12
    C12 - C11
    0 ]
= (C11 - C12) (1, -1, 0)
= (C11 - C12) v2

Thus any deviatoric normal mode has eigenvalue λ_dev = C11 − C12 (degeneracy 2). Positivity requires C11 − C12 > 0.

Optional deeper note: why the deviatoric eigenvalue is doubly degenerate

The space of normal strains is 3D. The hydrostatic direction spans a 1D invariant subspace. Its orthogonal complement (trace-free normal strains) is 2D and invariant under cubic symmetry. A symmetric operator commuting with all symmetry operations must act as a scalar on each irreducible subspace, so the 2D subspace shares one eigenvalue (degeneracy 2).

3.7 Collect conditions for positive definiteness

The full stiffness matrix eigenvalues are:

  • Shear eigenvalues (3×): C44
  • Deviatoric normal eigenvalues (2×): C11 − C12
  • Hydrostatic eigenvalue (1×): C11 + 2C12

Therefore, the stiffness operator is positive definite iff all these are positive:

Final answer Cubic crystal stability criteria (Born)
C44 > 0
C11 − C12 > 0
C11 + 2C12 > 0

3.8 Sanity checks

Units check Dimensional analysis

Strain ε is dimensionless, so U has units of C, i.e. Pa (J/m³). Inequalities compare elastic constants to zero, consistent dimensionally.

Physical meaning Moduli interpretation

Define bulk modulus K = (C11 + 2C12)/3. The condition C11 + 2C12 > 0 ⇔ K > 0. The condition C11 − C12 > 0 corresponds to a positive tetragonal shear modulus.

Limiting cases What happens if a criterion fails?
  • If C44 → 0+, shear becomes extremely soft; near-instability to simple shear.
  • If C11 − C12 → 0+, the crystal is on the verge of a tetragonal distortion (common near cubic→tetragonal transitions).
  • If C11 + 2C12 → 0+, bulk modulus tends to zero: volume change costs almost no energy (physically implausible for stable solids).
Optional deeper note: connection to continuum equations (operator notation)

In full elastostatics, equilibrium (no body forces) is ∇·σ = 0, with σ = C : ε and ε = sym(∇u). Substituting gives a linear PDE system for u: ∇·(C : sym(∇u)) = 0. Positive definiteness of C implies coercivity of the associated energy functional E[u] = ∫ U(sym(∇u)) dV, which under standard boundary conditions ensures a unique minimizer (well-posedness). For our homogeneous-strain problem, we are checking the local coercivity condition directly.

PART 4 — Deeper Understanding (Theory Around the Result)

4.1 Re-interpret the inequalities term-by-term

  • C44 > 0: all three independent simple shear modes cost energy: U ∝ C44 (2ε23)^2, U ∝ C44 (2ε13)^2, U ∝ C44 (2ε12)^2.
  • C11 − C12 > 0: deviatoric “tetragonal” normal strains (shape change without net volume change) cost energy.
  • C11 + 2C12 > 0: hydrostatic compression/expansion costs energy; equivalent to K > 0.

4.2 Parameter dependence (qualitative)

  • Increasing C44 stiffens shear response and raises shear-wave velocities.
  • Increasing C11 − C12 stabilizes against cubic→tetragonal distortions (important near structural phase transitions).
  • Increasing C11 + 2C12 increases bulk modulus, reducing compressibility.

4.3 Alternative derivation idea (brief but meaningful)

Instead of eigenvalues, you can apply Sylvester’s criterion directly to the symmetric 6×6 matrix. For cubic symmetry, the shear minors immediately give C44 > 0. For the 3×3 normal block, Sylvester’s criterion yields: C11 > 0, det[[C11,C12],[C12,C11]] = C11^2 − C12^2 > 0 ⇒ C11 − C12 > 0, and the full determinant (C11 − C12)^2 (C11 + 2C12) > 0 ⇒ C11 + 2C12 > 0. The final inequalities match the eigenvalue method (as they must).

Optional deeper note: energy decomposition into invariants

For cubic crystals, the quadratic energy can be written in terms of volumetric strain (trace) and deviatoric/shear parts. Let tr(ε) = ε11+ε22+ε33 and define deviatoric normal components by subtracting tr(ε)/3. One can rewrite U = (1/2)(C11+2C12) (tr(ε))^2/3 + (1/2)(C11−C12) * (deviatoric-normal norm)^2 + (1/2)C44*(shear norm)^2 (up to convention factors). Positivity of each squared-norm coefficient yields the same criteria.

4.4 Concept check (self-test with answers)

  • Q: Why isn’t C11 > 0 sufficient for stability?
    A: normal strains are coupled by C12; some combined strains can reduce energy unless the coupled eigenvalues are positive.
  • Q: What deformation tests C11 + 2C12 > 0?
    A: hydrostatic strain ε11=ε22=ε33≠0 (uniform volume change).
  • Q: What deformation tests C11 − C12 > 0?
    A: tetragonal deviatoric strain like ε11=δ, ε22=−δ, ε33=0, with zero trace.
  • Q: If C11 = C12, what happens?
    A: the deviatoric-normal eigenvalue vanishes; there is a zero-energy tetragonal mode → instability/marginal stability.
